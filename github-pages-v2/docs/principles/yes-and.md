# Yes, And

> *Accept your partner's reality, then add to it.*

![Playful infographic explaining the improv principle “Yes, And”](../assets/infographics/yes-and.jpg)

## What it means

"Yes, And" is the foundational mindset of improv, rolling two steps into one. First, you accept whatever your scene partner says or does as absolute truth in your shared imaginary world. Then, you contribute a new piece of information, emotion, or action to grow that reality together. 

## The mechanics

*   **Listen first:** You cannot agree to an offer you didn't hear or notice.
*   **Accept fully:** Treat your partner's words and physical mimes (like handing you a heavy box) as undeniable facts. You don't argue with them.
*   **Add one brick:** Contribute a single new detail—a feeling, a relationship, or a physical action—that builds directly on what was just established.
*   **Keep it moving:** By constantly affirming and adding, the scene moves forward instead of spinning in circles.

## The skill it builds — Agreement & Building

The mindset of "Yes, And" is trained through the concrete skill of **Agreement & Building**. You aren't just politely nodding; you are actively practising the habit of affirming an offer and laying down the next brick. To build this muscle, improvisers practise specific moves:

*   **Verbal affirmation:** Literally starting sentences with the words "Yes, and..." during exercises to force the brain out of its natural habit of arguing or saying "No."
*   **Physical agreement:** Mirroring or interacting with a partner's mimed object. If they hand you a heavy box, you let your arms sag under the weight before opening it.
*   **Adding specific details:** Upgrading vague agreements ("Yes, I like it") to concrete contributions ("Yes, and this neon green paint really brightens up the dungeon").

## See it in play

A: "Grandma, your soup is delicious."
B: "Thank you, dear—it's the secret ingredient your grandfather hated."
A: "Well, I'm glad he's gone, because this extra garlic is exactly what I needed to fight off the vampires."

## Try this (2 minutes)

**The "Yes, And" Party Planning.** Plan an imaginary party with a partner. Take turns suggesting ideas, and you *must* start every single reply with the literal words "Yes, and..." (e.g., "Let's have a bouncy castle." → "Yes, and we can fill it with jelly beans!" → "Yes, and the jelly beans can be spicy!"). Notice how quickly the ideas escalate when nothing is rejected.

## Watch out for

*   **The sneaky "Yes, but..."** It sounds agreeable, but it quietly cancels your partner's idea ("Yes, but I forgot the soup"). *Quick fix:* Swap "but" for "and." Build on their reality instead of backpedalling.
*   **"Yes" with nothing added.** Agreeing without contributing forces your partner to do all the work ("Yes, it is soup"). *Quick fix:* Always attach one new detail (an emotion, a memory, or an action) to your agreement so your partner has something to play with.
