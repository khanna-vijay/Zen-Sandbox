# Sit, Stand, Lie, Lean

> A physical constraint game where players must constantly justify their changing postures in a scene.

## Overview
Four players perform a grounded scene under a strict physical rule: at any given moment, one player must be sitting, one standing, one lying down, and one leaning against a surface. Whenever a player changes their posture, the other three must instantly adjust their own physical states to maintain the exact distribution of the four postures, justifying every shift within the context of the story.

## What It Trains
- **Domain:** D3 — The Scene
- **Principle(s):** Show, Don't Tell; Base Reality First; Group Mind
- **Skill(s):** Justification; Peripheral Awareness; Physicality & Space Work; Active Listening
- **Technique(s):** Justify the absurd; Stage-picture exercises; Character Walks/Centers
- **Focus:** mixed

**Objective:** To develop physical justification, peripheral awareness, and active listening by seamlessly integrating sudden physical shifts into a coherent, emotionally grounded narrative.

## Setup
Place a few chairs on stage and identify a wall or sturdy object for leaning. Four players stand on stage. Obtain a simple relationship or location suggestion from the group to establish a base reality.

## How to Play
1. Four players enter the performance space and immediately adopt the four distinct physical postures: one sitting, one standing, one lying down, and one leaning.
2. Begin a scene based on a simple relationship or location suggestion, establishing a grounded base reality.
3. At any point, a player may choose to change their physical posture (for example, the lying player stands up).
4. The moment a player initiates a posture change, the remaining players must dynamically adjust their own postures so that the group always maintains exactly one person sitting, one standing, one lying, and one leaning.
5. Every physical transition must be fully justified by the characters within the context of the scene's narrative, rather than treated as an arbitrary game mechanic.
6. Players must maintain high peripheral awareness, tracking the physical states of their scene partners without breaking eye contact or dropping their emotional commitment.
7. Continue the scene, allowing the physical shifts to drive the emotional stakes and narrative progression until a natural conclusion is reached.

## Facilitation Notes
- Coaching cue: 'Justify the movement, not the rule!' Remind players to avoid meta-dialogue like 'Why are you lying down?' and instead use narrative reasons like 'My back is killing me after that hike.'
- Pitfall: Players focus so much on the physical mechanics that the scene's emotional reality disappears. Fix: Side-coach them to focus on their relationships and let the physical shifts heighten their emotional states.
- Coaching cue: 'Feel the space.' Encourage players to use their peripheral vision to track their partners' movements rather than staring directly at them to see who is doing what.
- Pitfall: Lag time during transitions where multiple players end up in the same posture. Fix: Encourage immediate, decisive physical choices; if two people end up in the same posture, the one who is slower must instantly adapt to the remaining open posture.

## Variations
- Emotional Postures: Tie each physical posture to a specific emotional state (e.g., lying down is despair, standing is dominance, leaning is casual indifference, sitting is anxiety). When players switch postures, they must also adopt the corresponding emotion.
- Blind Transitions: Players must close their eyes for three seconds when a transition is initiated, relying purely on spatial memory and sound to find their new postures, then open their eyes and justify the resulting arrangement.

## Debrief
- How did the physical constraints affect the emotional stakes of your scene?
- What strategies did you use to maintain peripheral awareness of your scene partners while staying present in the dialogue?
- How did justifying an absurd or sudden physical shift open up new narrative possibilities that you wouldn't have thought of otherwise?

## Safety & Inclusion
Ensure the lying posture is accessible to all players; if a player has mobility limitations, the postures can be adapted (e.g., sitting, standing, raising hands, crossing arms) to accommodate physical comfort and safety. Ensure the floor is clean and free of hazards.

## Why It Works
This game forces players to practice 'Show, Don't Tell' by making physical choices the primary driver of the scene. By requiring immediate justification for sudden physical changes, it bypasses the analytical mind and taps into spontaneous, organic storytelling. The physical restriction builds strong group mind and peripheral awareness, as players must constantly monitor the entire stage picture.
