# Boundary Cartography

> Navigate unspoken personal limits in real-time scenes using active check-ins and empowered choices.

## Overview
Two players improvise a scene while secretly holding a specific, minor personal boundary. As the scene unfolds, players must actively read their partner's subtle physical and verbal cues, employ explicit out-of-character check-ins when approaching sensitive territory, and prioritize personal safety over comedic agreement. The result is a highly supportive environment where setting boundaries is treated as a generative, collaborative act rather than a scene-killer.

## What It Trains
- **Domain:** D2 — The Partner
- **Principle(s):** Consent & Boundaries; Yes, And; Truth Over Pandering
- **Skill(s):** Boundary Navigation; Active Listening; Offer Reception
- **Technique(s):** Check-ins; Cut calls; Negotiating physical contact
- **Focus:** skill_drill

**Objective:** To build practical competency in real-time boundary navigation and active consent, training players to prioritize personal truth over people-pleasing while maintaining a collaborative scene.

## Setup
Arrange the group in a circle or semi-circle with a clear performance space in the center. No props are required. The facilitator should establish a supportive, low-stakes atmosphere before play begins, emphasizing that safety and self-advocacy are the primary measures of success.

## How to Play
1. Step 1: Select Players and Secret Boundaries. Invite two players to step into the performance space. Instruct each player to secretly select one minor, non-traumatic personal boundary to uphold during the scene, such as avoiding direct eye contact for more than three seconds, avoiding whispering, or avoiding physical touch.
2. Step 2: Receive the Suggestion. Provide the players with a simple, grounded relationship or scenario suggestion, such as neighbors sorting recycling or coworkers organizing a breakroom.
3. Step 3: Begin the Scene. The players begin improvising the scene, working to build a cohesive narrative while actively protecting their secret boundary and looking for signs of their partner's boundary.
4. Step 4: Employ Subtle Redirection. If a player's boundary is approached, they should first attempt to redirect the action using in-character choices, such as stepping back, changing the subject, or using defensive body language.
5. Step 5: Initiate a Meta-Check. If a player wants to initiate an action that might cross a boundary, they must pause the scene briefly and ask explicitly: 'Meta-check: [proposed action] okay?'
6. Step 6: Respond to the Check-In. The receiving partner must pause and respond with one of three options: 'Yes, and' (accepting the action), 'Hold, but...' (declining but offering an alternative), or 'Hold, no' (declining completely).
7. Step 7: Resume Play. Once the meta-check is resolved, the players immediately resume the scene, integrating the negotiated agreement into their characters' reality.
8. Step 8: Call 'Cut' if Needed. If at any point a player feels genuinely uncomfortable, or if a boundary is crossed, they must call 'Cut!' to immediately end the scene. The facilitator will instantly pause the action and offer support.

## Facilitation Notes
- Coaching on Boundary Selection: Ensure players choose boundaries that represent mild discomfort or specific physical/verbal constraints rather than deep personal trauma. Examples include 'no pointing fingers' or 'no standing directly behind me'.
- Normalize the Meta-Check: Remind players that pausing for a 'Meta-check' is a sign of high-level skill, not a failure of flow. Side-coach with 'Great check-in!' to reinforce this behavior.
- Celebrate the 'Cut' Call: If a player calls 'Cut!', treat it as a successful application of the game's mechanics. Never treat a stopped scene as a failure; praise the player for their self-advocacy.
- Address the Urge to Pander: Watch for moments where a player visibly hesitates but agrees to something uncomfortable just to keep the scene going. Gently point this out during the debrief as a natural habit to unlearn.

## Variations
- The Open Map: Play the same structure, but players state their boundaries openly before the scene starts, focusing purely on the mechanics of respecting known limits.
- The Physicality Scale: Restrict the secret boundaries strictly to physical proximity and touch, forcing players to master spatial awareness and physical consent.

## Debrief
- For both players: What was your secret boundary, and how did you attempt to signal or protect it during the scene?
- How did it feel to use the 'Meta-check' mechanic? Did it disrupt your flow, or did it give you more confidence to play boldly?
- Did you feel any internal pressure to ignore your boundary for the sake of 'Yes, And'? How did you navigate that tension?
- Observers, what subtle physical or verbal cues did you notice that signaled a boundary was being approached?

## Safety & Inclusion
This exercise is highly sensitive and requires explicit, enthusiastic consent from all participants to play. Establish a firm rule that no player is ever forced to participate in the active space. Ensure the 'Cut!' command is treated with absolute respect, and remind players that they have complete agency over their bodies and emotional output at all times.

## Why It Works
This game works because it reframes boundary setting from a defensive, scene-stopping interruption into an active, collaborative mechanic of play. By practicing the 'Meta-check' and 'Hold' responses, players learn that saying 'no' to a specific action does not mean saying 'no' to the scene itself, thereby preserving the spirit of 'Yes, And' while protecting personal safety.
