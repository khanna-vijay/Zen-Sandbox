# The Plot Pivot

> Transform a stable scene instantly by embracing a sudden narrative disruption with immediate, justified action.

## Overview
Two players establish a grounded, low-stakes base reality before a facilitator-injected disruption upends their world. Players must instantly accept this new reality, pause to internalize the emotional impact, and pivot their characters' objectives to drive the story forward. It is a high-energy exercise in narrative agility, moving scenes from stable platforms to high-stakes action.

## What It Trains
- **Domain:** D3 — The Scene
- **Principle(s):** Yes, And; Base Reality First; Serve the Story
- **Skill(s):** Offer Reception; Narrative Architecture; Stakes / The 'Want'; World-Building; Justification
- **Technique(s):** If this is true, what else is true?; Platform/Tilt; C.R.O.W. (Character, Relationship, Objective, Where); Justify the absurd
- **Focus:** narrative

**Objective:** To master the 'Platform and Tilt' technique by establishing a solid base reality and seamlessly integrating a sudden narrative disruption. Players learn to elevate stakes, redefine character objectives, and use immediate, justified action to advance the story.

## Setup
Designed for a virtual meeting platform with cameras on. Two active players keep their cameras on, while the rest of the group turns their cameras off to act as the audience. The facilitator has a pre-prepared list of narrative disruptions ('tilts') ready to read aloud or drop into the chat.

## How to Play
1. Select two players to turn their cameras on and begin a grounded, low-stakes scene, focusing on establishing a clear relationship, setting, and routine activity.
2. Allow the players 60 to 90 seconds to build a believable, everyday base reality without introducing major conflicts or high drama.
3. At a stable moment, the facilitator calls out 'Pivot!' and delivers a sudden, significant external event or revelation that directly impacts the characters.
4. Upon hearing the disruption, both players must immediately accept the new information as absolute truth within their scene's reality.
5. Players take a silent, two-second physical and emotional pause on camera to visibly process the shock, fear, excitement, or gravity of the news.
6. Each player instantly re-evaluates their character's primary objective: what does my character desperately want or need to do right now because of this event?
7. Players resume the scene by taking immediate, justified action—either physical movement within their camera frame or a direct verbal choice—rather than merely talking about or questioning the event.
8. The scene continues as players explore the consequences of this new reality, building rising action until the facilitator calls 'Scene' once a clear narrative arc is established.

## Facilitation Notes
- Ensure the initial platform is truly mundane; if players start with high drama, the pivot loses its impact. Side-coach them to keep it simple early on.
- The two-second pause is crucial. It prevents players from making glib, intellectual jokes and forces them to find an authentic, grounded emotional reaction.
- Watch out for 'talking heads' syndrome after the pivot. If players just stand there discussing the news, side-coach: 'Show me what you are doing about it right now!'
- Vary the types of pivots you introduce: some should be external threats, while others should be relational or object-based.

## Variations
- Conflicting Objectives: The facilitator private-messages a different pivot to each player, forcing them to negotiate two sudden, clashing realities simultaneously.
- Silent Pivot: The facilitator drops the disruption into the chat window silently, requiring players to keep an eye on the chat and seamlessly integrate it without an auditory interruption.
- Player-Driven Pivot: An off-camera audience member types a disruption in the chat, and the active players must integrate it the moment they notice it.

## Debrief
- How did taking a physical pause change your emotional commitment to the new reality compared to reacting instantly?
- What was the difference between talking about the disruption and taking immediate action to address it?
- How did establishing a strong, mundane platform make the pivot feel more satisfying and dramatic?

## Safety & Inclusion
Because sudden disruptions can sometimes touch on sensitive themes, establish a 'red card' or 'skip' protocol before playing. If a facilitator-introduced pivot makes a player uncomfortable, they can simply say 'Skip' or turn off their camera, and the facilitator will immediately provide an alternative, low-stress pivot without question.

## Why It Works
This game works because it operationalizes the 'Platform and Tilt' narrative structure. By forcing a clear separation between the platform (the mundane setup) and the tilt (the catalyst), players experience how a story naturally accelerates. The mandatory pause bypasses the analytical brain, allowing players to access genuine emotional justification, which makes the subsequent high-stakes actions feel earned and believable.
