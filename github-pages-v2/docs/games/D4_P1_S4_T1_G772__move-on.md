# Keep and Move On

> A high-energy editing drill where scenes pivot instantly on a single, preserved detail.

## Overview
A fast-paced ensemble game where players line up on the backline while two performers initiate a scene. At any moment, the facilitator calls out a specific element to preserve, prompting the active players to clear and two new players to instantly launch a fresh scene based on that single detail. It builds rapid-fire editing skills, active listening, and seamless ensemble hand-offs.

## What It Trains
- **Domain:** D4 — The Ensemble
- **Principle(s):** Group Mind; Commit 100%; The First Thought Is a Gift; Make Your Partner a Genius
- **Skill(s):** Pacing & Rhythm; Support Work; Unfiltered Spontaneity; Active Listening
- **Technique(s):** Edits (Sweep, Tag-Out, Sound/Light); First Thought drills; Last Word Response
- **Focus:** skill_drill

**Objective:** To develop group mind and sharp pacing through rapid, external edits. Players practice active listening to identify strong offers and learn to initiate instantly with high commitment, supporting the momentum of the entire ensemble.

## Setup
An open stage space with a clear backline area. Players stand in a horizontal line at the back of the stage, facing forward. No props or chairs are needed. The facilitator stands in the audience area to call out the edits.

## How to Play
1. Gather the group of 5 to 12 players into a vibrant backline at the rear of the playing space, ensuring everyone is physically engaged and ready to move.
2. Ask for a simple suggestion (like an everyday object or location) to inspire the first scene.
3. Two players immediately step forward from the backline and begin a fully committed, two-person scene based on the suggestion.
4. The remaining players on the backline watch and listen intently, tracking the details, relationships, and dialogue of the active scene.
5. After 30 to 60 seconds of play, the facilitator calls out: 'Move on! Keep [Element]!' where the element is a specific word, relationship, physical action, or concept from the current scene.
6. Upon hearing the edit, the two active players immediately run back to the backline, and two new players must instantly step forward into the space.
7. The new duo immediately initiates a brand-new, unrelated scene that centers on or incorporates the preserved element.
8. Repeat this cycle, keeping the energy high and the transitions seamless, ensuring every player gets multiple opportunities to initiate and support.

## Facilitation Notes
- Side-coaching cue: 'Stay on your toes!' Remind players on the backline to keep their weight forward, avoiding crossed arms or leaning against walls, to ensure instant physical transitions.
- Pitfall: Hesitation when stepping forward, leaving an empty stage. Fix: Coach the ensemble that any two players can step forward; if there is a pause, the nearest players must jump in without overthinking.
- Side-coaching cue: 'Isolate the element!' Ensure the new scene is completely fresh and not a continuation of the previous characters or storyline, unless the preserved element was specifically the character.
- Pitfall: The facilitator waits too long to edit. Fix: Call the edit as soon as a juicy, specific, or funny detail is uttered. Keep the pacing brisk to maintain high energy.

## Variations
- Player-Led Edits: Instead of the facilitator calling the edits, players on the backline yell 'Move on, keep...' and step forward to replace the active players.
- Emotional Carryover: The facilitator preserves an emotional state (e.g., 'Keep the underlying resentment') rather than a literal plot point or object.
- Tag-Out Hybrid: A single player from the backline tags out one of the active players while shouting 'Move on, keep [element]!', leaving one original player to adapt to the new context.

## Debrief
- How did it feel to initiate a scene with a pre-determined element versus starting from scratch?
- What did you notice about the rhythm of the edits? When did the transitions feel most satisfying?
- How does keeping your physical energy high on the backline affect your mental readiness to play?

## Safety & Inclusion
Ensure the stage floor is clear of tripping hazards, as players will be entering and exiting quickly. Encourage players to be mindful of physical spacing during rapid transitions to avoid collisions.

## Why It Works
This game works because it strips away the pressure of invention by providing a concrete starting point for each new scene. By forcing rapid, external edits, it trains the ensemble to develop a shared rhythm (Group Mind) and teaches players to treat every offer as an immediate gift, bypassing the analytical mind.
