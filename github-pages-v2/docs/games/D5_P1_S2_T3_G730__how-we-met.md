# The Origin Story

> Reconstruct a real couple's meeting story in real-time using immediate, playful audience feedback.

## Overview
In this high-energy narrative game, a real-life couple from the audience sits on stage armed with a bell and a horn. Performers improvise the story of how the couple first met, making bold narrative guesses that the couple instantly validates or refutes. The players must rapidly adapt to these auditory cues, justifying incorrect guesses and building on the truths to uncover the actual history.

## What It Trains
- **Domain:** D5 — The Audience
- **Principle(s):** The Audience Is the Final Scene Partner; Serve the Story; Yes, And
- **Skill(s):** Audience-Energy Management; Narrative Architecture; Offer Reception; Justification
- **Technique(s):** Breaking the 4th Wall / Direct Address; Story Spine; Endowment-acceptance; Justify the absurd
- **Focus:** mixed

**Objective:** To develop audience-energy management and narrative agility by treating audience feedback as an active, immediate scene partner that shapes the story's direction.

## Setup
Set up two chairs on stage for a real-life couple from the audience. Provide them with two distinct sound makers: a bell (representing 'truth/correct') and a horn (representing 'fiction/incorrect'). The performing cast of 4 to 6 players stands ready on the sides of the stage, prepared to enter the scene.

## How to Play
1. Invite a volunteer couple from the audience to sit in the designated chairs on stage.
2. Hand one partner the bell and the other partner the horn, explaining the rules clearly to both the couple and the audience.
3. Ask the couple only for their names and how long they have been together, keeping all other details of their meeting story a secret.
4. Begin the scene with two performers playing the couple at the moment or day they first met, making an immediate, specific narrative offer.
5. The couple must immediately react to every factual claim: ring the bell if it aligns with reality, or honk the horn if it is incorrect.
6. If the horn honks, the performers must instantly accept the 'no' and justify it, pivoting the narrative to a new explanation.
7. If the bell rings, the performers must build on that established truth, deepening the narrative and moving the story forward.
8. Other players can edit, tag in, or play supporting characters to help uncover the full, true story of how they met, culminating in the moment they officially became a couple.

## Facilitation Notes
- Side-coach players to make highly specific offers rather than vague questions; vague offers give the couple nothing to react to.
- Pitfall: Players getting stuck in a 'horn loop' by guessing random things repeatedly. Fix: Coach them to accept the horn as a gift, justify the mistake, and pivot to a completely different angle.
- Encourage the performers to look at the couple and break the fourth wall occasionally, acknowledging their reactions and treating them as the ultimate authority of the scene.
- Ensure the couple feels celebrated, not mocked; the humor should come from the performers' wild, incorrect guesses and desperate justifications, not at the couple's expense.

## Variations
- The Solo Narrator: One player acts as a narrator telling the story, while the other players act out the scenes based on the narrator's guesses and the couple's feedback.
- The Interview Hybrid: Start with a brief, 30-second interview to get one highly unusual detail, then use the bell and horn to discover the rest of the context.
- Reverse Origin: Start at the proposal or a recent anniversary and work backward in time to the initial meeting, using the bell and horn to verify the history.

## Debrief
- How did the immediate auditory feedback from the bell and horn change your pacing and decision-making?
- What strategies did you use to justify a 'horn' (incorrect guess) without stalling the momentum of the scene?
- How did breaking the fourth wall to check in with the couple enhance the audience's investment in the story?

## Safety & Inclusion
Always ensure the audience volunteers are fully consenting and comfortable being on stage. Never pressure a couple to join. Instruct the performers to keep the content respectful, avoiding overly personal, invasive, or sensitive romantic/sexual assumptions. If the volunteers seem uncomfortable at any point, the host should gently step in and pivot.

## Why It Works
This game works because it gamifies the core improv principle of 'Yes, And' by turning 'No' (the horn) into a playful narrative constraint. By giving the audience direct, physical tools to influence the scene, it breaks the fourth wall and transforms the audience from passive observers into active, vital scene partners whose real lives dictate the comedy.
