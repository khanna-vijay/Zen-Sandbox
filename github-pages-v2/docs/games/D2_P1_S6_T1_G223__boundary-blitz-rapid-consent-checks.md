# Boundary Blitz

> Practice real-time boundary navigation and explicit consent check-ins through rapid, low-stakes scene snippets.

## Overview
A fast-paced training drill where players perform short scene snippets and pause for immediate, structured verbal check-ins. Using a simple color-coded system, participants practice identifying, articulating, and adjusting to personal boundaries in real time. The experience normalizes safety communication, proving that personal comfort always takes precedence over narrative momentum.

## What It Trains
- **Domain:** D2 — The Partner
- **Principle(s):** Consent & Boundaries; Truth Over Pandering
- **Skill(s):** Boundary Navigation; Active Listening; Offer Reception
- **Technique(s):** Check-ins; Cut calls; Negotiating physical contact
- **Focus:** skill_drill

**Objective:** To build muscle memory for active boundary navigation, normalize explicit verbal check-ins, and practice adjusting scene dynamics in real time to prioritize partner safety over theatrical agreement.

## Setup
A clear, open playing space with room for two active players and a circle of observers. No props are required, though a whiteboard or poster displaying the color-coded system (Green, Yellow, Red) can be helpful for quick reference.

## How to Play
1. Pair up two players to step into the center of the space while the rest of the group observes.
2. Provide a simple, open-ended relationship or situational prompt to initiate a short scene.
3. Instruct the players to begin improvising, focusing on physical proximity, emotional intensity, or physical contact.
4. After 30 to 45 seconds of play, or when a physical or emotional boundary is approached, call out 'Check-In!' to pause the action instantly.
5. Upon the pause, both players must immediately state their current comfort level using the traffic light system: Green (fully comfortable), Yellow (approaching a boundary, needs adjustment), or Red (boundary crossed, must stop).
6. If a player calls 'Yellow' or 'Red,' require them to briefly and clearly state the specific boundary (e.g., 'Yellow: I felt crowded when you stepped closer' or 'Red: I do not want to be touched on my shoulders').
7. If a 'Yellow' is called, guide the players to negotiate a quick adjustment (e.g., 'I will step back two feet') and resume the scene for another 30 seconds to practice the modified boundary.
8. If a 'Red' is called, immediately end the scene with no questions asked, validate the boundary call, and rotate in a new pair of players.
9. Rotate players regularly so everyone practices both initiating scenes and articulating boundaries.

## Facilitation Notes
- Normalize the Stop: Actively celebrate 'Red' and 'Yellow' calls during the debrief to frame boundary setting as a skill of strength and self-awareness, not a narrative failure.
- Demand Specificity: If a player gives a vague 'Yellow' (e.g., 'It just felt weird'), gently coach them to identify the exact trigger, such as physical proximity, vocal volume, or touch.
- Watch for Pandering: Intervene if a player clearly looks uncomfortable but calls 'Green' to keep the scene going; remind the group that personal truth always overrides theatrical agreement.
- Keep Snippets Brief: Ensure the scenes do not run too long before the check-in; the goal is rapid, low-stakes practice, not building a complete narrative arc.

## Variations
- Pre-Scene Soft Boundaries: Before starting a snippet, players state one specific 'soft boundary' (e.g., 'no sudden loud noises') to practice proactive boundary setting.
- Silent Non-Verbal Cues: Run the same exercise but have players use physical gestures (like a raised hand or a step back) to signal a 'Yellow' or 'Red' mid-scene without waiting for the facilitator's call.
- High-Intensity Escalation: For advanced players, intentionally prompt scenes with high emotional stakes (e.g., a dramatic confrontation) to practice maintaining boundaries under intense theatrical pressure.

## Debrief
- How did it feel to prioritize your personal comfort over the momentum of the scene?
- What challenges did you face when trying to articulate a 'Yellow' boundary clearly and quickly?
- If you received a 'Yellow' or 'Red' from your partner, how did you manage your immediate reaction to ensure they felt heard and safe?
- How can we carry this habit of active, explicit check-ins into our regular, unpaused long-form scenes?

## Safety & Inclusion
This exercise is highly safety-sensitive. Establish a firm, non-judgmental environment before starting. Emphasize that players have absolute autonomy over their bodies and emotional limits. No player should ever be questioned, pressured, or debated regarding a 'Red' or 'Yellow' call. If a participant is uncomfortable participating in the active space, they may participate as a dedicated 'Check-In' caller from the audience.

## Why It Works
This game works because it disrupts the traditional improv pressure of 'always say yes' by introducing a structured, socially sanctioned pause. By gamifying the check-in process, it lowers the social friction of saying 'no' or 'not like this.' It builds cognitive muscle memory, transforming boundary navigation from an abstract safety policy into an active, physicalized performance skill.
