# The Special Edition Feature

> Structure a complete narrative using the familiar, playful framing of a home-video release.

## Overview
The Special Edition Feature is a structured longform format that bridges shortform playfulness and longform narrative depth. Players construct a fictional movie complete with a high-energy trailer, character introductions, a main feature film, and post-show bonus features like bloopers and deleted scenes. This framework helps players understand pacing, editing, and narrative architecture by breaking a long story into distinct, manageable segments.

## What It Trains
- **Domain:** D4 — The Ensemble
- **Principle(s):** Serve the Piece; Serve the Story
- **Skill(s):** Format Literacy; Pacing & Rhythm; Narrative Architecture
- **Technique(s):** Longform vs. shortform mechanics; Edits (Sweep, Tag-Out, Sound/Light); Story Spine
- **Focus:** narrative

**Objective:** Develops format literacy and narrative architecture by contrasting rapid-fire, shortform-style framing (trailers, bloopers) with the sustained, patient scene work required for a longform narrative.

## Setup
An open performance space with a clear stage area and an off-stage bench for players not in the current scene. No props are required. One player can act as the Director or Host to guide the menu transitions, or the ensemble can self-edit.

## How to Play
1. Get a suggestion from the audience for a movie title or a genre that has never been made.
2. Phase 1 - The Trailer: One player steps forward as the voiceover narrator, describing a bombastic movie trailer while other players jump up to perform rapid, high-energy, three-to-five-line micro-scenes that illustrate the narrator's prompts.
3. Phase 2 - The Cast Introduction: Players line up briefly on stage, stepping forward one by one to introduce their character's name and a signature physical or verbal trait, establishing the ensemble cast.
4. Phase 3 - The Feature Film: The ensemble performs a continuous, multi-scene narrative (approximately ten to fifteen minutes) based on the established characters and trailer, using sweep edits or tag-outs to transition between scenes.
5. Phase 4 - The Special Features: After the movie reaches its climax and resolution, the host or ensemble returns to the main menu to showcase bonus content.
6. Execute 'Deleted Scenes': Players perform scenes that were mentioned in the movie but never shown, or scenes that expand on a minor character's backstory.
7. Execute 'Bloopers': Players replay a key moment from the movie but intentionally break character, flub lines, or physicalize a comedic mistake, immediately freezing when the director yells 'Cut!'
8. Execute 'Director's Commentary': Two players sit to the side acting as the director and lead actor, while other players silently mime a scene from the movie; the commentators explain the behind-the-scenes drama or artistic choices.

## Facilitation Notes
- Coaching Cue: During the trailer, keep scenes incredibly brief. No more than three exchanges before the narrator cuts back in to keep the momentum high.
- Pitfall: Players might exhaust all their plot ideas during the trailer. Fix: Remind them that the trailer is a teaser of high-concept moments, not a chronological summary of the plot.
- Coaching Cue: In the Feature Film phase, slow down. The trailer was fast, but the movie needs relationship, platform, and patience to succeed.
- Pitfall: The Special Features can feel disconnected. Fix: Ensure every deleted scene or blooper directly references a specific line, character, or event established during the main movie.

## Variations
- The Interactive Menu: Let the audience press buttons on an imaginary remote control to select which special feature they want to see next.
- Foreign Language Dub: Run a special feature where the movie is played in a gibberish language with two other players translating it live.
- The Sequel Pitch: End the show with a trailer for the sequel, based on a suggestion of what happens next to the surviving characters.

## Debrief
- How did the fast-paced trailer help or hinder your character choices in the main movie?
- What did you notice about the shift in pacing between the trailer, the main feature, and the special features?
- How does having a rigid structural frame (like a DVD menu) actually give you more creative freedom during the scenes?

## Safety & Inclusion
Ensure that during the Bloopers phase, physical safety is maintained; physical comedy should be highly controlled and mimed rather than actually reckless. If players choose to do Director's Commentary about behind-the-scenes drama, keep the fictional conflict lighthearted and focused on artistic pretension rather than personal attacks.

## Why It Works
This format works because it gamifies the daunting task of longform narrative. By breaking the performance into distinct, recognizable media tropes (trailers, features, bonus content), players can easily shift their performance energy. The high-speed trailer warms up the group's collaborative agreement, the main feature challenges their narrative patience, and the special features allow them to playfully patch any plot holes or celebrate mistakes.
