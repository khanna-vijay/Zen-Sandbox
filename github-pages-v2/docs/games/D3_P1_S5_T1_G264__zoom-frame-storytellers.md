# Frame-by-Frame Worldbuilders

> Construct rich, collaborative worlds using virtual backgrounds and physical framing as your primary narrative tools.

## Overview
A virtual-first storytelling game where players build a continuous narrative using their webcams as dynamic stages. By combining pre-selected virtual backgrounds with physical positioning within their camera frames, participants co-create scenes with minimal dialogue. The facilitator acts as a live director, spotlighting players to guide the audience's focus through an evolving visual tapestry.

## What It Trains
- **Domain:** D3 — The Scene
- **Principle(s):** Show, Don't Tell; Yes, And; Group Mind
- **Skill(s):** World-Building; Narrative Architecture; Active Listening; Offer Reception; Physicality & Space Work; Peripheral Awareness
- **Technique(s):** C.R.O.W. (Character, Relationship, Objective, Where); Yes, And… sentence games; Thread-tracking drills
- **Focus:** mixed

**Objective:** To master the 'Show, Don't Tell' principle by establishing C.R.O.W. (Character, Relationship, Objective, Where) through visual choices, physical scale, and environmental backgrounds rather than relying on verbal exposition.

## Setup
Conducted on a video conferencing platform with virtual backgrounds enabled. Before starting, players should gather 3 to 5 diverse background images (e.g., a forest, a futuristic lab, a cozy kitchen, a dark alley). All players start in Gallery View with cameras on.

## How to Play
1. The facilitator designates one player to initiate the story as the Scene Starter.
2. The Scene Starter selects a virtual background to establish the 'Where' and adopts a distinct physical posture and facial expression to establish their 'Character'.
3. The Starter delivers a single, brief line of dialogue to anchor the scene, then freezes in their pose.
4. The next player must visually 'Yes, And' the previous offer by selecting a complementary or contrasting background (e.g., moving from outside a castle to inside the throne room) and adjusting their physical position in their frame.
5. This second player uses physical scale (e.g., leaning close to the camera to appear dominant, or stepping back to appear small and intimidated) to establish their 'Relationship' and 'Objective' relative to the first player.
6. The second player delivers a single line of dialogue to solidify their visual offer, then freezes.
7. The facilitator actively directs the narrative by using the platform's spotlight feature to highlight active players, switching between Speaker View for intimate exchanges and Gallery View for ensemble moments.
8. The game continues sequentially, with each player building upon the visual and narrative context established by the player immediately before them.

## Facilitation Notes
- Coaching Cue: 'Play with your frame!' Remind players that moving closer to the lens increases their status and intensity, while moving away lowers their status and shows vulnerability.
- Pitfall: Players relying too heavily on verbal explanation. Fix: Side-coach with 'Show us the environment first, then speak. Let the background do the talking.'
- Coaching Cue: 'Match the environment.' Encourage players to choose backgrounds that logically connect to the previous scene, creating a sense of physical movement through a shared world.
- Pitfall: Technical lag or slow background switching. Fix: Have players pre-load their images into their platform settings beforehand so they can swap them with a single click.

## Variations
- Silent Cinema: Run the entire game with absolutely zero dialogue, relying entirely on physical expressions, framing, and background transitions to convey the narrative.
- The Split-Screen Duet: Pair two players up. They must coordinate their backgrounds and physical framing to look like they are sharing a single, continuous physical space (e.g., one player looking off-screen to the right, the other looking off-screen to the left as if making eye contact).
- Rapid-Fire Location Hop: The facilitator calls out sudden environmental shifts (e.g., 'Gravity loss!' or 'Sudden storm!'), forcing all players to instantly swap backgrounds and adapt their physical postures to the new condition.

## Debrief
- How did changing your physical distance from the camera affect the perceived relationship and power dynamic between characters?
- What strategies did you use to make your virtual background feel like a physical space you were actually inhabiting rather than just a flat image?
- How did relying on visual offers change the way you listened and prepared your next contribution compared to a purely verbal scene?

## Safety & Inclusion
Ensure players are aware of their physical surroundings to avoid tripping or bumping into furniture when moving away from their cameras. If a player cannot use virtual backgrounds due to hardware limitations, allow them to use physical household props or hand-drawn signs held up to the camera to represent their environment.

## Why It Works
By stripping away the crutch of heavy dialogue, this game forces players to embody their choices. Utilizing the camera frame as a physical boundary teaches players to manipulate status and proximity, while the virtual backgrounds instantly establish the 'Where' of C.R.O.W. This visual-first constraint sharpens active listening, as players must closely observe their peers' physical cues and environmental offers to build a cohesive, shared reality.
