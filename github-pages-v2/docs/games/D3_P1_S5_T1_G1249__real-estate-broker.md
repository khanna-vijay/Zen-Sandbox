# The Property Tour

> Build a rich, physical environment together through active declarations and immediate justification.

## Overview
In this exercise, one player acts as a real estate agent showing a prospective buyer or buyers through an unusual property. Players physically define the environment through precise object work and bold declarations, completely avoiding questions to ensure active world-building.

## What It Trains
- **Domain:** D3 — The Scene
- **Principle(s):** Show, Don't Tell; Base Reality First; Yes, And; Make Your Partner a Genius
- **Skill(s):** World-Building; Justification; Physicality & Space Work; Offer Reception; Active Gifting
- **Technique(s):** C.R.O.W. (Character, Relationship, Objective, Where); Object work; Endowment-acceptance; Endowment-gifting drills; Justify the absurd
- **Focus:** skill_drill

**Objective:** To master physical world-building and establish C.R.O.W. elements early in a scene by using declarative statements, physical justification, and consistent spatial memory.

## Setup
An open, moderate-sized playing space. Two to four players stand on stage. The facilitator or audience provides a suggestion for an unusual or highly specific type of property (such as a villain's lair, a submarine apartment, or a treehouse mansion). No physical props are used.

## How to Play
1. Assign one player to be the real estate agent and the remaining players to be the prospective buyers.
2. The agent begins the tour by physically entering the space, establishing the entrance (such as opening a heavy vault door or climbing a rope ladder) and welcoming the buyers.
3. Players must interact with the environment physically, using precise object work to define the size, weight, and location of doors, furniture, and features.
4. All dialogue must be declarative; players are strictly forbidden from asking questions (for example, instead of asking 'Is this a fireplace?', say 'I love this massive stone fireplace').
5. Every physical offer or declaration must be accepted and justified by the other players (for example, if a buyer points out a random trapdoor, the agent must immediately explain why it is there).
6. Once an object is placed or defined in the space, it remains fixed in that exact physical location; players must navigate around it or interact with it consistently throughout the tour.
7. The scene concludes once the agent has shown at least three distinct areas or rooms, and the buyers make a decision about the property based on the established C.R.O.W. elements.

## Facilitation Notes
- Side-coach players to avoid the 'question trap.' If a player asks 'What is this?', freeze the scene and have them rephrase it as a bold statement: 'This is a giant golden statue!'
- Watch for spatial consistency. If a player walks through a previously established table, gently remind them of the object's location to preserve the reality of the space.
- Encourage players to establish their relationship (C.R.O.W.) early. Are they a wealthy couple, a demanding boss and assistant, or a supervillain and their henchman?
- Remind the agent to justify any bizarre features introduced by the buyers, turning 'mistakes' or weird choices into brilliant selling points.

## Variations
- The Haunted Listing: The property is actively haunted or dangerous, forcing players to physically react to shifting environments while maintaining their professional composure.
- The Time-Travel Agency: The agent is showing properties across different historical eras or futuristic dimensions, requiring rapid adaptation to sci-fi or historical object work.
- Hidden Motives: Give the agent and buyers secret objectives (such as the agent needing to hide a body in the house, or the buyer being an undercover health inspector).

## Debrief
- How did banning questions change the speed and confidence of your world-building?
- What strategies did you use to remember where objects were placed in the physical space?
- How did justifying your partner's unexpected additions help define the relationship and the environment?

## Safety & Inclusion
Ensure the physical space is clear of actual tripping hazards. Encourage players to be mindful of their physical boundaries and mobility levels when defining vertical spaces (like ladders or steep stairs), adapting the physical environment to be accessible to all participants.

## Why It Works
By banning questions, players are forced to make active, high-stakes offers that immediately build the 'Where' of the scene. Justification transforms random physical choices into logical narrative elements, while consistent object work establishes a shared physical reality that makes the imaginary world feel tangible to both the players and the audience.
