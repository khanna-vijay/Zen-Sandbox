# The Unspoken Quest

> Anticipate and fulfill your partner's hidden needs through deep observation and proactive, unspoken support.

## Overview
A two-player scene work exercise where one player harbors a secret, character-driven objective they can only hint at, while their partner must decode these subtle cues and actively resolve the need. It shifts players away from explicit verbal negotiation into a space of high-fidelity empathy, active listening, and intuitive collaboration. The result is a deeply connected scene where partners make each other look brilliant by anticipating needs before they are spoken.

## What It Trains
- **Domain:** D2 — The Partner
- **Principle(s):** Make Your Partner a Genius; Yes, And; Assume Competence
- **Skill(s):** Active Gifting; Active Listening; Single-Partner Empathy & Mirroring; Offer Reception; Status Modulation; Physicality & Space Work
- **Technique(s):** Give them the answer; Endowment-acceptance; Endowment-gifting drills; Object work
- **Focus:** connection

**Objective:** To develop the ability to read subtext, body language, and indirect offers, training players to proactively 'give their partner the answer' and make them look like a genius without requiring explicit verbal instruction.

## Setup
Two active players stand in the performance space, while the rest of the group acts as observers. The facilitator privately assigns a simple, physical, or situational secret objective to Player A (e.g., 'You need to find a quiet place to read a book' or 'You need to find your lost keys to leave'). Player B is instructed that their sole purpose is to discover Player A's hidden need and proactively fulfill it. A basic relationship and location are established.

## How to Play
1. The facilitator privately assigns a secret, character-driven objective to Player A (The Seeker) while Player B (The Catalyst) is out of earshot.
2. The facilitator establishes a shared setting and relationship for both players (e.g., coworkers in a breakroom, roommates cleaning up).
3. The scene begins with both players interacting naturally within the environment, using object work and dialogue.
4. Player A subtly communicates their secret objective using non-verbal cues, physical tension, pacing, and indirect verbal hints, but is strictly forbidden from explicitly stating their goal.
5. Player B closely observes Player A's physical behavior, vocal tone, and spatial choices, treating every action as a meaningful clue.
6. Player B makes active, supportive offers to test their theories and proactively 'gives the answer' by providing physical or situational solutions (e.g., handing over a set of keys, suggesting they move to a quieter room).
7. Neither player may explicitly ask or state what the secret objective is; all communication must remain subtextual and integrated into the scene's reality.
8. Player A must accept Player B's helpful offers, using them to either achieve their goal or gently steer Player B closer to the true objective if the first attempt misses.
9. The scene concludes organically once Player B successfully helps Player A achieve their secret objective, or when the facilitator calls edit.

## Facilitation Notes
- Side-coach Player B to make bold, concrete offers rather than asking open-ended questions like 'What's wrong?' or 'What are you looking for?'
- If Player A is struggling to convey the objective, remind them to use their environment and physical tension (e.g., checking pockets, sighing, looking at exits) rather than just waiting.
- Pitfall: The scene stalls because Player B is afraid of guessing wrong. Fix: Remind Player B that a 'wrong' helpful offer is still a gift that helps Player A clarify their direction.
- Encourage both players to maintain the reality of the scene; the pursuit of the secret goal should feel like a natural character motivation, not a charades game.

## Variations
- Virtual Adaptation: Play in a video call where Player A uses on-camera framing, eye contact, and desktop object work to hint at their objective, while Player B utilizes verbal endowment.
- Emotional Needs: Instead of a physical objective, Player A is given a secret emotional need (e.g., 'I need to feel validated about my intelligence' or 'I need to be comforted after a bad day').
- Double Blind: Both players are given secret, complementary objectives by the facilitator, and both must discover and fulfill each other's needs simultaneously.

## Debrief
- To the Catalyst: What specific physical or verbal cues first tipped you off to your partner's secret objective?
- To the Seeker: How did it feel when your partner offered a solution that was close but not quite right? How did you adapt?
- For the group: How does 'giving the answer' proactively change the trust and connection between two scene partners compared to waiting to be asked?

## Safety & Inclusion
Ensure secret objectives do not involve high-stress triggers, physical boundary violations, or non-consensual touch. Remind players that physical objectives should be solved through simulated object work or verbal endowment rather than actual physical contact unless explicitly consented to beforehand.

## Why It Works
This game works because it strips away the crutch of explicit verbal negotiation, forcing players to rely on somatic empathy and active listening. By requiring the Catalyst to 'give the answer' through proactive gifting, it builds a deep sense of mutual support and trust. It teaches improvisers that their partner's subtle, seemingly random choices are actually valuable offers waiting to be validated and made brilliant.
