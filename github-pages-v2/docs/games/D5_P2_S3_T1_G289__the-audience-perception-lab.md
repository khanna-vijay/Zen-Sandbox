# The Sightline Lab

> Train your physical and vocal projection using targeted feedback from the room's most challenging seats.

## Overview
The Sightline Lab is an active diagnostic exercise where performers run short scenes while peers occupy designated blind spots or challenging vantage points around the room. These observers act as sensory spotters, providing objective, location-specific feedback on what was actually seen and heard. This structured feedback loop helps players bridge the gap between their internal performance intentions and the physical reality of the audience's experience.

## What It Trains
- **Domain:** D5 — The Audience
- **Principle(s):** The Audience Is the Final Scene Partner; Play for the Back Row
- **Skill(s):** Room Reading; Stage Presence & Clarity; Physicality & Space Work; Vocal Craft
- **Technique(s):** Cheating out; Projection; Make the choice readable; Energy-calibration
- **Focus:** skill_drill

**Objective:** To develop physical and vocal projection, master the technique of cheating out without breaking the scene's reality, and build an intuitive spatial awareness of how movement and voice carry to the furthest corners of a performance space.

## Setup
Mark 3 to 5 specific Hotspots in the room using tape, chairs, or cones. These spots must represent challenging viewing angles: the extreme back corner, a side-angle seat, a spot with a partially obstructed view, or the very back row. Prepare index cards and pens for the observers. Divide the group so 2-3 players are on stage, and the remaining players are assigned to the Hotspots as Spotters.

## How to Play
1. Assign 2 to 3 players to the stage and place the remaining participants in the designated Hotspots around the room, handing each Spotter an index card and pen.
2. Instruct the Spotters that their sole job is to record objective, sensory observations from their specific seat, focusing strictly on visibility, audibility, and physical clarity (such as 'I lost your voice when you turned left' or 'The hand gesture was clear, but the facial expression was hidden').
3. Give the onstage performers a simple, low-stakes scene prompt (such as a basic relationship and location) to ensure they can focus on physical and vocal delivery rather than complex plotting.
4. Begin the scene, running it for 2 to 3 minutes. During play, performers must actively work to 'cheat out' their bodies, project their voices, and make their physical choices readable to all Hotspots.
5. Freeze the scene and immediately ask each Spotter to read their objective notes aloud, moving systematically from the closest/easiest vantage point to the most challenging back-row spot.
6. Have the performers listen to the feedback in silence without defending their choices, then give each performer 30 seconds to state one adjustment they will make in their next round.
7. Rotate roles so that the onstage performers become Spotters in different Hotspots, and a new group takes the stage, repeating the process until everyone has played both roles.

## Facilitation Notes
- Strictly police the feedback: Stop Spotters immediately if they offer narrative critiques (such as 'You should have agreed to the vacation') and redirect them to sensory data (such as 'From my angle, I couldn't hear the word vacation').
- Remind performers that 'cheating out' does not mean staring at the audience; it means angling the pelvis and shoulders at a 45-degree angle toward the house while maintaining eye contact with their scene partner.
- Watch for 'vocal dropping' at the ends of sentences. Performers often start a line with great projection but let the volume trail off as they finish their thought.
- If a performer is physically blocked by their partner, side-coach the blocked player to 'find the window'—adjusting their position to be visible between other actors or set pieces.

## Variations
- The Emotional Zoom: Spotters focus exclusively on facial expressions and micro-movements, reporting whether the specific emotional shifts of the characters were readable from the back row.
- The Whisper Challenge: Performers must play an entire high-stakes scene in a whisper or low volume, relying entirely on extreme vocal resonance and physical articulation to make every word clear to the back row.
- The Blind Spot Guess: Performers play a scene with empty Hotspots. Afterward, they must guess which of their physical or vocal choices failed to reach those empty zones before the facilitator reveals the actual blind spots.

## Debrief
- What physical adjustments felt unnatural to you on stage but were reported as highly effective by the Spotters?
- How does knowing someone is sitting in the worst seat in the house change your physical relationship with your scene partner?
- What is the difference between shouting to be heard and projecting to be heard?

## Safety & Inclusion
Ensure that Hotspots are physically accessible to all participants. If a spotter has hearing or visual impairments, calibrate the feedback cards to focus on the sensory channels they are monitoring, or pair them up to analyze how different physical adjustments translate across different abilities.

## Why It Works
This game works because it replaces vague performance notes with localized, objective data. By experiencing the frustration of not seeing or hearing a scene from a bad seat, players develop immediate empathy for the audience. This empathy translates into an active, internalized habit of 'room reading' and physical adjustment, turning the abstract concept of the fourth wall into a dynamic, physical boundary that must be negotiated constantly.
