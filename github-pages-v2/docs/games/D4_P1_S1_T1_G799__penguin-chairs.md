# Glacial Chairs

> A high-stakes game of silent coordination, spatial awareness, and slow-motion chair defense.

## Overview
In this high-energy ensemble game, players work as a silent, unified group to protect a shifting empty chair from a slow-moving seeker. By tracking the entire room's layout, players must strategically vacate their own seats to fill the void, creating a dynamic, constantly evolving puzzle of movement.

## What It Trains
- **Domain:** D4 — The Ensemble
- **Principle(s):** Group Mind; Commit 100%
- **Skill(s):** Peripheral Awareness; Physicality & Space Work; Support Work
- **Technique(s):** Stage-picture exercises
- **Focus:** connection

**Objective:** To develop deep peripheral awareness, non-verbal ensemble communication (Group Mind), and physical commitment to spatial composition.

## Setup
Scatter chairs randomly throughout a large, open room, ensuring there is exactly one chair per participant. Position the chairs so they face in various random directions. All players except one begin seated.

## How to Play
1. Designate one player to start as the Seeker, who begins standing at the perimeter of the room, leaving their own chair vacant.
2. The Seeker's goal is to sit in any empty chair in the room, but they must move with a restricted, slow-motion gait (knees locked together, taking small, shuffling steps).
3. The seated players must work together to prevent the Seeker from sitting down by occupying the vacant chair before the Seeker can reach it.
4. When a seated player stands up to fill the empty chair, they immediately create a new empty chair behind them, shifting the Seeker's target.
5. Players are not allowed to run, and once a player leaves their chair, they cannot return to that same chair; they must commit to finding a new one.
6. The Seeker can pivot at any time to target any newly vacated chair that becomes closer or easier to reach.
7. The round ends when the Seeker successfully sits in an empty chair. The player who left that chair empty or was caught in transition becomes the new Seeker.

## Facilitation Notes
- Side-coach players to look at the whole room, not just the Seeker. Encourage them to use their peripheral vision to spot empty chairs behind them.
- Watch out for hesitation: if a player starts to stand up and then sits back down, remind them of the rule that once you commit to leaving, you must go.
- If the seated players are winning too easily, challenge them to play in absolute silence, relying only on eye contact and breath to coordinate.
- If the Seeker is winning too quickly, remind the seated players to anticipate the Seeker's path and move earlier rather than waiting until the last second.

## Variations
- Blind Spots: Arrange some chairs to face completely away from the center of the room, forcing those seated players to rely entirely on the movement cues of others.
- Double Seeker: Introduce two Seekers and two empty chairs, doubling the cognitive load and requiring the ensemble to split their focus.
- Emotional Chairs: Assign a specific emotion or relationship dynamic to the movement, requiring players to maintain character while defending the chairs.

## Debrief
- How did you communicate with your teammates without speaking or pointing?
- What happened when someone hesitated or changed their mind halfway through a move?
- How did keeping your eyes on the entire room (peripheral vision) change your success rate?

## Safety & Inclusion
Ensure the floor is completely clear of tripping hazards. Players with mobility limitations can adjust the Seeker's slow-motion gait to a pace that is safe and comfortable for everyone, or the Seeker's speed can be calibrated to match the group's physical needs.

## Why It Works
This game forces players out of their individual heads and into a collective consciousness. Because the target is constantly shifting, players must maintain constant peripheral awareness of the entire stage picture, anticipating needs and supporting their teammates before a crisis occurs.
