# The Digital Scene Canvas

> Weave immersive virtual worlds using digital whiteboards, custom backgrounds, and collaborative physical-verbal choices.

## Overview
A virtual-first improv game where players collaboratively construct a scene's environment, characters, and stakes in real-time. By combining verbal dialogue, rapid virtual background changes, and text-based whiteboard additions, participants build a rich, multi-modal base reality. One player acts as the Scene Architect, translating group suggestions into a shared visual canvas that serves as the physical stage.

## What It Trains
- **Domain:** D3 — The Scene
- **Principle(s):** Yes, And; Show, Don't Tell; Base Reality First; Group Mind
- **Skill(s):** Active Listening; Offer Reception; World-Building; Support Work
- **Technique(s):** Yes, And… sentence games; C.R.O.W. (Character, Relationship, Objective, Where); Playing architecture/objects
- **Focus:** mixed

**Objective:** To master the C.R.O.W. framework (Character, Relationship, Objective, Where) in a virtual space, training players to show rather than tell by integrating visual, environmental, and verbal offers.

## Setup
Players join a video conferencing platform with gallery view enabled. Each player prepares a small folder of diverse virtual backgrounds (e.g., interiors, outdoor landscapes, abstract textures). The facilitator or a designated player (the Scene Architect) shares a blank digital whiteboard screen visible to all participants.

## How to Play
1. Designate one player as the Scene Architect, who will manage the shared digital whiteboard, and have all other players turn on gallery view and prepare their virtual backgrounds.
2. The Scene Architect initiates the world by drawing or typing a single, evocative object in the center of the whiteboard (e.g., 'a dusty treasure chest' or 'a humming control panel').
3. The first player enters the scene by changing their virtual background to establish their character's location relative to the object, delivering an opening line that establishes their Character and Where.
4. The second player enters by changing their virtual background to a complementary location, delivering a line that establishes their Relationship and Objective with the first player, directly building on the initial offer.
5. Immediately after speaking, the active player types a single, concise visual addition into the chat (e.g., 'add a rusty key on the table'), which the Scene Architect immediately draws or types onto the shared whiteboard.
6. Subsequent players take turns in sequence, each changing their virtual background to reflect their character's perspective or movement, delivering a 'Yes, And' line of dialogue, and submitting a new whiteboard addition via chat.
7. The Scene Architect continuously updates the whiteboard based on chat inputs, creating a dynamic, evolving set that players must physically react to and reference in their dialogue.
8. After two to three full rounds of visual and verbal contributions, the Scene Architect calls for a final round where players deliver their concluding lines and background shifts to bring the narrative to a satisfying resolution.

## Facilitation Notes
- Coaching Cue: 'Interact with the canvas!' Remind players to treat the whiteboard drawings as real, physical props in their virtual space, using pantomime to touch or use them.
- Pitfall: The Scene Architect gets overwhelmed. Fix: Have a non-playing facilitator act as the Architect, or keep whiteboard drawings extremely simple (using basic shapes, stamps, or text labels instead of detailed sketches).
- Coaching Cue: 'Establish C.R.O.W. early.' Ensure the first two players clearly define who they are, where they are, and what they want, using their background choices to do the heavy lifting.
- Pitfall: Players take too long choosing backgrounds. Fix: Instruct players to select the first background that loosely fits the vibe rather than searching for the perfect image; imperfection breeds comedy and spontaneous justification.

## Variations
- The Silent Canvas: Run the entire game in complete silence, relying solely on background changes, whiteboard drawings, and physical pantomime to tell the story.
- Background Roulette: Players must change their background to a completely random image from their folder on their turn, and then immediately justify how their character ended up in that new location.

## Debrief
- How did having a visual, evolving whiteboard change how you conceptualized the physical space of the scene?
- In what ways did the virtual backgrounds help you establish your Character and Where without needing to explain them verbally?
- How did we manage the cognitive load of tracking dialogue, backgrounds, and whiteboard updates simultaneously?

## Safety & Inclusion
Ensure all players have pre-loaded, accessible backgrounds to prevent technical exclusion. If a player's device does not support virtual backgrounds, they can participate by holding up physical drawings, household objects, or using descriptive text in the chat to represent their environment.

## Why It Works
By forcing players to align their verbal offers with visual background changes and whiteboard updates, the game operationalizes 'Show, Don't Tell.' It prevents the 'talking heads' syndrome of virtual improv by anchoring players to a shared, evolving physical reality (the canvas), which demands active listening and multi-sensory offer reception.
