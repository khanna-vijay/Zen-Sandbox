# Boundary Compass

> Navigate personal boundaries in real-time scenes using active check-ins and empowering cut calls.

## Overview
This exercise integrates real-time consent negotiation directly into active scene work. Players navigate scenes while secretly holding specific personal boundaries, practicing subtle in-character check-ins to maintain safety. When a boundary is reached, players use a dedicated pause mechanic to halt the action, transforming boundary management into a collaborative, low-stakes learning moment.

## What It Trains
- **Domain:** D2 — The Partner
- **Principle(s):** Consent & Boundaries; Truth Over Pandering
- **Skill(s):** Boundary Navigation; Active Listening
- **Technique(s):** Check-ins; Cut calls; Negotiating physical contact
- **Focus:** connection

**Objective:** To develop practical boundary navigation and active listening skills, empowering players to prioritize personal safety and truth over scene momentum by confidently executing and responding to cut calls.

## Setup
An open, comfortable playing space. Prepare a set of index cards ('Boundary Cards'), each listing a specific personal boundary (e.g., 'Maintain at least two feet of physical distance,' 'No physical touch initiated by others,' 'Avoid direct, prolonged eye contact,' 'No high-volume shouting'). A bell, chime, or distinct sound maker for the facilitator.

## How to Play
1. Distribute one secret Boundary Card to each participant; players must keep their assigned boundary private from their scene partners.
2. The facilitator sets up a simple, low-stakes scenario involving two to three players (e.g., coworkers organizing a desk, neighbors discussing a property line).
3. Players begin the scene, working to build a natural relationship while actively monitoring their own secret boundary and observing their partner's physical and verbal cues.
4. If a player wishes to initiate physical contact or enter close proximity, they must perform an in-character 'soft check-in' (e.g., 'May I help you carry that?' or 'Is it alright if I sit right next to you?').
5. The receiving player responds in-character, either accepting, redirecting, or declining the offer based on their secret card or personal comfort.
6. If a player's boundary is crossed, or if they experience genuine personal discomfort, they must immediately call out 'Cut!' to pause the action.
7. Upon hearing the 'Cut!' call, the facilitator sounds the chime, and all players instantly freeze and step out of character.
8. The facilitator guides a brief, non-judgmental debrief: the caller shares their boundary, the partner reflects on the cues they noticed or missed, and the group discusses alternative choices.
9. The facilitator chooses either to rewind the scene to a moment just before the cut to practice a safer navigation path, or to reset with new players and cards.

## Facilitation Notes
- Frame the 'Cut!' call as a gift of clarity and a success for the ensemble, never as a failure or a disruption to the creative flow.
- Ensure the debrief remains strictly objective and non-judgmental; focus on the impact of the actions rather than analyzing or defending the player's intent.
- Watch for players who try to 'power through' their secret boundary to save the scene; pause the scene yourself if you notice them violating their card's instruction.
- Encourage realistic, subtle boundaries rather than extreme or highly dramatic ones, keeping the focus on everyday interpersonal navigation.

## Variations
- Open Boundaries: Instead of secret cards, players openly state one boundary before the scene starts, practicing explicit, conscious respect of known limits.
- Non-Verbal Navigation: Run the scenes entirely in gibberish or silently, forcing players to rely solely on physical distance, body language, and non-verbal check-ins.
- Dynamic Boundaries: Players do not use cards but must tune into their actual, real-time comfort levels in the moment, calling 'Cut!' whenever a genuine personal boundary is approached.

## Debrief
- How did it feel to balance the creative demands of the scene with the absolute requirement to uphold your boundary?
- What subtle physical or verbal cues did you notice your partner giving off before a boundary was reached?
- How did calling 'Cut!' affect your sense of agency and safety on stage?
- In what ways did performing a 'soft check-in' actually deepen the connection and realism between the characters?

## Safety & Inclusion
This game directly addresses physical and emotional safety. Ensure all participants understand they have absolute authority over their own bodies and comfort. If a player is uncomfortable with any boundary card they draw, they may exchange it immediately with no questions asked. Participation in any physical contact must always be opt-in.

## Why It Works
By gamifying the enforcement of boundaries, this exercise dismantles the traditional improv pressure to always say 'yes' at the expense of personal comfort. It builds muscle memory for active consent by rewarding players for pausing the action, proving that safety and trust are the true foundations of brave, connected play.
