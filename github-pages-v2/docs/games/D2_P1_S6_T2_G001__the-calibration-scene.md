# The Calibration Scene

> Make consent the dramatic engine by explicitly negotiating physical and emotional boundaries in real-time.

## Overview
A two-player scene study where improvisers must explicitly ask for and receive verbal consent before initiating physical contact, spatial intrusion, or deep emotional disclosure. By making the negotiation of boundaries vocal and transparent, players learn to prioritize personal and character comfort over traditional scene momentum.

## What It Trains
- **Domain:** D2 — The Partner
- **Principle(s):** Consent & Boundaries; Truth Over Pandering; Yes, And
- **Skill(s):** Boundary Navigation; Active Listening
- **Technique(s):** Check-ins; Cut calls; Negotiating physical contact
- **Focus:** skill_drill

**Objective:** To develop advanced boundary navigation and active listening skills, teaching players to value truth over pandering by using structured pause-and-reset calls to align artistic choices with personal safety.

## Setup
Two players stand in the performance space while the rest of the group acts as active observers. No props are needed. The facilitator prepares a list of high-stakes, close-proximity relationships.

## How to Play
1. Select two players and assign them a relationship and a scenario that naturally invites physical proximity or emotional vulnerability.
2. Instruct the players to begin a standard scene, but with one absolute rule: any action that alters physical distance, initiates touch, or introduces intense emotional vulnerability must be preceded by an explicit, out-of-character verbal request for consent.
3. The initiating player must ask clearly and specifically, such as, 'As my character, I want to place my hand on your shoulder. As a player, are you comfortable with that?'
4. The receiving player must respond with absolute honesty, prioritizing their actual comfort as a performer and their character's logical boundaries over keeping the scene running smoothly.
5. The receiving player can answer in four ways: a direct 'Yes', a direct 'No' (which forces the initiator to pivot), a conditional 'Yes, but only if...', or a clarifying 'I am not sure, what is your intention?'
6. Introduce the 'Calibration Call': either player can call 'Cut!' at any point to pause the scene, adjust boundaries, correct a misread signal, or admit they pandered to the scene's momentum instead of honoring a boundary.
7. When a 'Calibration Call' is made, the players briefly discuss the boundary adjustment out-of-character, reset the physical staging to just before the pause, and resume play with the new boundary established.
8. Run the scene for five to seven minutes, encouraging multiple calibration pauses, then rotate to a new pair of players.

## Facilitation Notes
- Actively encourage players to call 'Cut!' early and often, framing the pause as a creative tool that enhances the scene rather than a failure or interruption.
- Watch for players who hesitantly say 'yes' just to keep the scene moving, and side-coach them to remember that 'No' is a highly dramatic and valid choice.
- Provide relationships that require physical or emotional negotiation but avoid highly traumatic setups, focusing instead on everyday intimacy or professional proximity.
- If you notice a player tensing up or hesitating but not calling a pause, gently step in as the facilitator and call a calibration check-in for them.

## Variations
- Silent Negotiation: Play the same game but replace verbal consent requests with highly exaggerated, deliberate non-verbal cues, such as offering an open palm and waiting for eye contact before moving closer.
- The Shadow Negotiator: A third player stands behind each actor to tap them on the shoulder and whisper consent checks or boundary adjustments that the actor must then vocalize.

## Debrief
- How did it feel to explicitly ask for permission instead of relying on implicit agreement or traditional 'Yes, And' rules?
- Did you experience a moment where you wanted to say 'No' but felt the urge to pander to the audience or the scene? How did you handle it?
- How did treating the 'Cut' call as a collaborative adjustment tool change your relationship with mistakes or boundary crossings?
- How can we bring this level of active listening and boundary awareness into scenes where we do not explicitly speak our consent out loud?

## Safety & Inclusion
This exercise is highly safety-sensitive. Establish a clear, universal stop signal before starting. Ensure players know they have absolute autonomy over their physical bodies and can veto any action without needing to justify it. Remind the group that a boundary crossed in character can feel real to the player; prioritizing the human over the performance is mandatory.

## Why It Works
By forcing implicit physical and emotional negotiations into explicit verbal agreements, this game dismantles the toxic misconception that 'Yes, And' requires sacrificing personal comfort for comedic or dramatic payoff. The 'Calibration Call' reframes boundary setting as a collaborative, generative act that actually deepens trust and artistic freedom.
