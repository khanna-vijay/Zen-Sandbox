# The Unified Space Harold

> A high-wire long-form structure merging the thematic depth of a Harold with single-location continuity.

## Overview
The Unified Space Harold is an advanced long-form format that blends the three-beat thematic structure of a traditional Harold with the strict spatial unity of a monoscene. The entire performance takes place in a single location in real-time, with transitions occurring seamlessly through character entrances and exits rather than blackout edits.

## What It Trains
- **Domain:** D4 — The Ensemble
- **Principle(s):** Serve the Piece; Group Mind; Serve the Story
- **Skill(s):** Format Literacy; Pacing & Rhythm; Thematic Synthesis; Narrative Architecture; World-Building
- **Technique(s):** Harold; Longform vs. shortform mechanics; Edits (Sweep, Tag-Out, Sound/Light); Weave the threads; C.R.O.W. (Character, Relationship, Objective, Where)
- **Focus:** mixed

**Objective:** Develops advanced format literacy, spatial awareness, and thematic synthesis by forcing players to maintain narrative continuity and character tracks within a single, unchanging environment.

## Setup
An empty stage representing a single, defined location. A line of chairs or a back line for off-stage players. No physical props. An audience is present to provide a suggestion of a location.

## How to Play
1. Secure a suggestion of a specific, active location from the audience (e.g., a 24-hour diner, a museum gallery, a ski lift platform).
2. Perform an opening that explores the physical layout, atmosphere, and thematic possibilities of the location, establishing the 'rules' of the space.
3. Initiate Beat 1 consisting of three distinct scenes (A, B, and C). Each scene must take place in a different area of the chosen location, transitioning seamlessly via 'French scenes' (as one character exits, another enters to start the next scene, maintaining physical continuity).
4. Execute the first Group Game, which must manifest as a collective event or shared activity natural to the location (e.g., a sudden power outage, a tour group passing through).
5. Begin Beat 2, returning to the three storylines. Because all characters inhabit the same physical space, players should begin to cross-pollinate, allowing characters from Scene A to observe, overhear, or briefly interact with characters from Scene B or C.
6. Execute the second Group Game, further heightening the thematic elements of the space or introducing a community-wide disruption.
7. Perform Beat 3, where the boundaries between the three storylines completely dissolve. The narrative arcs, relationships, and environmental pressures collide into a unified, chaotic, or poignant climax within the space.
8. Conclude the piece organically when the location's overarching story reaches a natural resolution or a final, satisfying image.

## Facilitation Notes
- Side-coaching cue: 'Keep the space alive!' Remind off-stage players to watch the physical layout so they do not walk through imaginary walls or furniture established in earlier scenes.
- Pitfall: Players treating transitions like standard edits (clapping or wiping). Fix: Coach players to use physical entrances and exits to trigger transitions. A character leaving a scene should leave a 'hook' or a moment of solo stage time for the next character to enter.
- Side-coaching cue: 'Cross-pollinate!' Encourage players to let their characters overhear conversations from other scenes, as they are all in the same room.
- Pitfall: The location becomes boring or static. Fix: Introduce environmental shifts (weather, time of day, mechanical failures) to force physical adaptation from the cast.

## Variations
- The Real-Time Clock: The entire piece takes place in exact real-time (e.g., a 25-minute show equals 25 minutes in the world of the scene), strictly forbidding any temporal jumps.
- The Silent Witness: One player remains on stage as a silent background character (e.g., a janitor, a sleeping passenger) for the entire duration, acting as a physical anchor for the space.

## Debrief
- How did restricting ourselves to a single location change how we developed our characters' relationships?
- What strategies did we use to transition between scenes without relying on traditional edits or sweeps?
- How did the physical environment itself become a character in our piece?

## Safety & Inclusion
Since players are sharing a single, often crowded physical space for the entire performance, establish clear boundaries regarding physical contact and staging. Ensure the imagined layout of the space leaves clear, safe pathways for physical movement and exits.

## Why It Works
By stripping away the ability to edit scenes with a sweep or a time-jump, the format forces players to rely on organic, character-driven transitions. It merges the thematic complexity of the Harold with the deep, slow-burn character work of a monoscene, teaching players to 'serve the piece' by finding connections within a shared physical reality.
