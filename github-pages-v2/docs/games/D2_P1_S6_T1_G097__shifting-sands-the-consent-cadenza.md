# Dynamic Calibration

> Navigate emotional and physical boundaries in real time through structured, active check-ins and adjustments.

## Overview
A high-stakes, low-physicality scene-work drill where players practice continuous boundary negotiation. By integrating explicit check-in protocols and immediate adjustment mechanics, players learn to prioritize personal comfort over scene momentum. The experience builds deep trust, allowing players to explore emotional depth safely.

## What It Trains
- **Domain:** D2 — The Partner
- **Principle(s):** Consent & Boundaries; Truth Over Pandering; Vulnerability
- **Skill(s):** Boundary Navigation; Emotional Fluidity; Active Listening
- **Technique(s):** Check-ins; Cut calls; Negotiating physical contact
- **Focus:** skill_drill

**Objective:** To develop advanced boundary navigation and active listening skills, specifically training players to use explicit check-ins and immediate adjustments to honor personal comfort over scene demands.

## Setup
Three to five players stand in a small circle in an open space. No props or special staging are required. The facilitator establishes a supportive, low-stakes physical environment and reviews the safety protocols before play begins.

## How to Play
1. Establish a non-verbal check-in gesture, such as tapping the collarbone, and review the three-tier status system: Green (fully comfortable), Yellow (proceed with caution/modify specific element), and Red (stop current direction immediately).
2. Define the Pause and Pivot mechanic: any player can call 'Pause and Pivot' to instantly freeze the scene, state a boundary, and request a specific adjustment without penalty or judgment.
3. Establish the Touch Protocol: any physical contact beyond incidental brushing must be verbally requested and explicitly agreed to out-of-character before occurring.
4. Cast two active players to begin a scene based on a high-stakes emotional prompt, while the remaining players observe.
5. At any point during the scene, the facilitator or an active player can call 'Status Check' or use the non-verbal gesture to pause the action.
6. Upon a Status Check, both active players must immediately state their current color status (Green, Yellow, Red) and briefly explain their comfort level regarding the scene's emotional or physical trajectory.
7. If a player reports Yellow or Red, the players must immediately adjust the scene's tone, topic, or physical proximity to accommodate the boundary before resuming.
8. If a player calls Pause and Pivot, the scene halts, the player states the necessary adjustment, and the scene resumes from that exact moment with the adjustment implemented.

## Facilitation Notes
- Actively monitor non-verbal cues: Watch for physical tensing, nervous laughter, or sudden disengagement, and proactively call a Status Check if you sense a player is overriding their comfort.
- Enforce immediate compliance: Ensure that any Pause and Pivot or Yellow/Red status is honored instantly without debate, justification, or intellectualizing from the other player.
- Normalize boundary setting: Praise players who call for adjustments or state Yellow/Red comfort levels, reinforcing that protecting one's boundaries is a highly skilled improv choice.
- Address the Yes-And trap: Remind players that agreement in improv does not mean sacrificing personal safety or comfort; Truth Over Pandering always overrides scene continuity.

## Variations
- Silent Calibration: Play the scene normally, but players must signal their status shifts (Green to Yellow) purely through the established non-verbal gesture, prompting the partner to adjust without verbalizing the check-in.
- Blind Boundaries: Before the scene, players privately write down one emotional or physical boundary on a slip of paper; they must actively navigate the scene and respect these boundaries as they naturally emerge.

## Debrief
- How did it feel to prioritize your own comfort over the perceived success of the scene?
- What physical or emotional cues helped you realize you needed to call a Status Check or Pause and Pivot?
- How did receiving a Yellow or Red status from your partner affect your choices as an improviser?
- In what ways did explicit verbal negotiation for physical touch change the trust level between you and your partner?

## Safety & Inclusion
This game is highly safety-sensitive. The facilitator must establish a strict, non-judgmental environment where boundary-setting is celebrated as a creative win. Players have absolute autonomy over their physical and emotional boundaries, and any request to pause, adjust, or stop must be met with immediate, unquestioning compliance.

## Why It Works
By gamifying the mechanics of consent, this drill removes the social stigma of saying 'no' in an improvisational setting. It replaces the pressure to constantly agree with a structured framework for negotiation, proving that scenes can pivot and thrive even when strict boundaries are established.
