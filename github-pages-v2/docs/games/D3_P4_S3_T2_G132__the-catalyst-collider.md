# The Catalyst Collider

> Transform unexpected disruptions into the structural pillars of a deeply connected, evolving narrative.

## Overview
The Catalyst Collider is an advanced narrative game where a scene's trajectory is repeatedly disrupted by external, highly disparate prompts introduced by the facilitator. Players must move beyond merely acknowledging these inputs, treating them instead as pivotal, reality-shifting events that retroactively reframe and deepen the entire story.

## What It Trains
- **Domain:** D3 — The Scene
- **Principle(s):** Serve the Story; Base Reality First; Yes, And; Fail Joyfully
- **Skill(s):** Narrative Architecture; Justification; Game Identification; Active Listening; Offer Reception; Stakes / The 'Want'
- **Technique(s):** Platform/Tilt; If this is true, what else is true?; Justify the absurd; C.R.O.W. (Character, Relationship, Objective, Where); Endowment-acceptance
- **Focus:** narrative

**Objective:** To develop advanced narrative architecture by training players to use sudden, disruptive tilts as structural opportunities to re-evaluate, justify, and synthesize a cohesive story arc.

## Setup
Three active players stand in the performance space while the remaining players observe. The facilitator stands at the edge of the stage with a set of pre-written Catalyst Cards containing highly specific, disruptive narrative shifts, along with a few random physical objects kept handy.

## How to Play
1. 1. Obtain a mundane, everyday suggestion from the audience to establish a solid, grounded base reality.
2. 2. Begin the scene by focusing heavily on character relationships, physical environment, and clear individual objectives.
3. 3. After ninety seconds, the facilitator silently delivers the first Catalyst Card to one player, who must organically introduce this new reality into the scene.
4. 4. All players immediately apply the 'If this is true, what else is true?' principle, adjusting their characters' relationships and objectives to accommodate this new truth.
5. 5. After another minute, the facilitator introduces a second, more disparate catalyst, such as a physical object or a sudden environmental shift.
6. 6. The players must weave this second catalyst into the existing, already-altered narrative, finding a logical or thematic connection between all elements.
7. 7. The facilitator delivers a final, high-impact catalyst that challenges a core assumption of the scene, prompting the players to drive the story to a satisfying, unified resolution.

## Facilitation Notes
- Pacing is critical: Ensure the players have fully established their base reality and relationships before introducing the first catalyst.
- Avoid the gag trap: Side-coach players who treat catalysts as cheap, isolated jokes, reminding them to make the disruption central to their character's stakes.
- Encourage retroactive justification: Remind players to look backward and connect new catalysts to statements or actions made earlier in the scene.
- Keep the physical environment active: If a physical object is introduced, ensure players use object work to give the item weight, history, and high stakes.

## Variations
- The Silent Catalyst: The facilitator introduces catalysts purely through non-verbal environmental cues, such as lighting shifts, sound effects, or physical props.
- Audience-Driven Collisions: Off-stage players write the catalyst cards before the game starts and take turns tossing them onto the stage.
- Genre Shift: One of the catalysts forces the entire scene to instantly adopt a specific theatrical style, requiring players to justify their current situation through that lens.

## Debrief
- How did the introduction of the first catalyst change your character's primary objective or want?
- What strategies did you use to connect two completely unrelated catalysts so they felt like they belonged in the same story?
- How did establishing a strong, mundane base reality at the beginning help you survive the chaotic shifts later on?

## Safety & Inclusion
Because catalysts can introduce sudden, high-stakes shifts, players should establish a non-verbal 'skip' signal if a catalyst accidentally touches on a personal boundary. The facilitator should ensure catalyst cards avoid triggering themes and focus instead on absurd, environmental, or stylistic disruptions.

## Why It Works
This game operationalizes the Platform and Tilt technique in an escalating, structured format. By forcing players to constantly ask 'If this is true, what else is true?', it prevents scenes from stalling. The external disruptions act as a narrative engine, demanding immediate justification and active listening, which ultimately teaches players that any choice can be woven into a satisfying story if they commit to serving the narrative over their individual plans.
