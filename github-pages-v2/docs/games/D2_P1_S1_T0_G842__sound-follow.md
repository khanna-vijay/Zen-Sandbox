# Sonic Beacon

> Navigate the space blindly by tracking and trusting your partner's unique vocal beacon.

## Overview
In this exercise, players work in pairs where one partner is blindfolded or has their eyes closed, and the other acts as a sonic guide. By emitting a consistent, distinct sound, the guide safely navigates their partner through a shared space, establishing a deep level of trust, non-verbal consent, and heightened auditory awareness.

## What It Trains
- **Domain:** D2 — The Partner
- **Principle(s):** Consent & Boundaries; Make Your Partner a Genius
- **Skill(s):** Active Listening; Boundary Navigation; Peripheral Awareness
- **Technique(s):** Cut calls
- **Focus:** connection

**Objective:** To develop deep active listening, establish physical trust, practice boundary navigation, and cultivate peripheral awareness of the shared environment.

## Setup
An open room cleared of tripping hazards. Players pair up and spread out across the space. No props are required, though blindfolds can be used if players prefer them over simply closing their eyes.

## How to Play
1. Divide the group into pairs and designate one person as the Guide and the other as the Navigator.
2. Instruct the Navigator to close their eyes or put on a blindfold and stand still.
3. The Guide selects a distinct, easily repeatable non-verbal sound (such as a click, hum, or whistle) and demonstrates it once to their partner.
4. When the Guide emits their sound, the Navigator begins walking slowly and directly toward the source of the sound.
5. If the Guide stops making the sound, the Navigator must immediately freeze in place.
6. The Guide must move dynamically around the room, using their sound to lead the Navigator along a safe path, constantly monitoring the physical boundaries of the room and other pairs.
7. If a collision is imminent, the Guide must immediately use the verbal safety word STOP to freeze their partner instantly.
8. After a few minutes of play, have the pairs pause, check in with each other, and swap roles so both experience guiding and navigating.

## Facilitation Notes
- Encourage Guides to start close to their partners and gradually increase the distance as trust and calibration improve.
- Remind Guides that they are fully responsible for their partner's physical safety; they must maintain peripheral awareness of other pairs in the space.
- If Navigators feel anxious, advise them to take smaller, slower steps, and remind Guides to keep their sound continuous and reassuring.
- Pitfall: Guides moving too fast or making erratic sounds. Fix: Side-coach Guides to keep their tempo steady and predictable.
- Pitfall: Navigators peeking or rushing. Fix: Remind them that the goal is sensory connection, not speed; encourage them to focus entirely on the texture of the sound.

## Variations
- Variable Volume: The Guide adjusts the volume of the sound to indicate speed—louder means move faster, softer means slow down.
- Obstacle Course: Place soft obstacles (like chairs or cushions) in the space that the Guide must navigate the Navigator around.
- Group Beacon: One Guide leads a small line of two or three blindfolded Navigators who are not holding hands but must all follow the same sound.

## Debrief
- How did it feel to surrender visual control and rely entirely on your partner's sound?
- What specific adjustments did the Guides make to ensure their partners felt safe and respected?
- How did your peripheral awareness change when you couldn't use your eyes?
- How does this level of care and active listening translate to supporting your partner in an improv scene?

## Safety & Inclusion
Establish a clear, universal verbal 'STOP' command before beginning. Ensure participants are comfortable with having their eyes closed in a moving environment; offer the alternative of keeping eyes cast down at the floor with a soft focus if closing them entirely causes anxiety or disorientation. Ensure the floor is completely clear of cords or slipping hazards.

## Why It Works
This game strips away visual cues, forcing players to rely entirely on auditory feedback and mutual trust. By making the Guide responsible for the Navigator's physical safety, it physically manifests the principle of 'making your partner look good' (and keeping them safe). It builds a non-verbal contract of consent where movement is directly tied to the partner's active signaling.
