# Sonic Dial

> A music-driven long-form format where every scene is inspired by and physically contains the soundtrack.

## Overview
In this high-energy long-form format, players perform a series of interconnected scenes where music is an active, diegetic element of the scene's reality rather than mere background atmosphere. A dedicated sound operator or live musician controls the soundtrack, and changes in the music serve as the primary editing mechanism, prompting players to transition seamlessly into new environments and narratives.

## What It Trains
- **Domain:** D4 — The Ensemble
- **Principle(s):** Serve the Piece; Base Reality First
- **Skill(s):** Pacing & Rhythm; Format Literacy; World-Building
- **Technique(s):** Edits (Sweep, Tag-Out, Sound/Light); Montage; C.R.O.W. (Character, Relationship, Objective, Where)
- **Focus:** mixed

**Objective:** To develop ensemble pacing, format literacy, and physical world-building by using diegetic music as both a scenic anchor and an editing tool.

## Setup
A performance space with a clear stage area and an audience area. A sound station equipped with a laptop, soundboard, or live musical instruments is positioned with a clear view of the stage. Prepare a curated playlist of diverse musical genres, tempos, and moods.

## How to Play
1. The sound operator starts the game by playing a distinct, recognizable piece of music to set the initial mood.
2. The ensemble listens to the music's tempo, genre, and emotional quality, using it as immediate inspiration to establish a base reality where this music is physically playing.
3. Players enter the stage and initiate a scene, explicitly acknowledging or interacting with the music as a real, physical element of their environment.
4. The music continues to play until a character within the scene interacts with it to stop or alter it, at which point the sound operator cuts the audio and the scene continues in silence.
5. To edit the scene and transition to a new one, either an onstage player initiates a change or the sound operator fades in a completely new track.
6. As the new track begins, the current scene sweeps or fades out, and new players immediately enter the stage to establish a fresh scene inspired by the new audio.
7. The cycle repeats, building a montage of scenes that explore different relationships, environments, and rhythms dictated by the shifting soundtrack.

## Facilitation Notes
- Side-coach players to let the music dictate their physical speed and emotional baseline before they speak.
- Watch out for players treating the music as non-diegetic background noise; remind them that the characters must hear the music and have a relationship with it.
- Encourage the sound operator to listen closely to the scene's energy and use sudden genre shifts to force an edit if a scene is dragging.
- Remind players that they can initiate edits physically by pretending to turn a dial, put on headphones, or change a CD, which the sound operator must instantly support.

## Variations
- The Volume Dial: The sound operator dynamically changes the volume of the track during the scene, forcing the actors to adjust their vocal projection and physical proximity to match.
- Musical Tag-Outs: Players can tag into a scene by changing the music, taking over the physical space but shifting the genre to transform the reality.
- The Broken Record: The sound operator loops a tiny, repetitive segment of a song, forcing the actors to loop their physical actions or dialogue in tandem.

## Debrief
- How did having the music physically present in the scene's reality change your physical choices and pacing?
- What was the difference in energy between scenes that kept the music playing versus scenes where you turned the music off?
- How did the sound operator and the onstage players collaborate to find the perfect moment for an edit?

## Safety & Inclusion
Ensure volume levels are kept at a safe, comfortable level for all participants, especially those with sensory sensitivities. Provide a clear visual cue, such as a raised hand, for players to request an immediate volume reduction if needed.

## Why It Works
It forces players to ground their scenes in a shared sensory reality while outsourcing the pacing and editing to an auditory cue. This relieves the cognitive load of deciding when to edit, allowing the ensemble to focus entirely on physical world-building and rhythmic alignment.
