# Style Rollercoaster

> A fast-paced scenic game where players maintain their core narrative while shifting through dramatic genres.

## Overview
In this high-energy game, three to four players initiate a grounded scene based on a simple location suggestion. At key moments, the facilitator pauses the action to inject a new theatrical or cinematic genre suggested by the audience. The players must instantly adapt their physical and vocal choices to this new style while keeping their original characters, relationships, and storyline intact.

## What It Trains
- **Domain:** D3 — The Scene
- **Principle(s):** Base Reality First; Commit 100%; Yes, And
- **Skill(s):** World-Building; Justification; Emotional Fluidity; Offer Reception; Stage Presence & Clarity
- **Technique(s):** C.R.O.W. (Character, Relationship, Objective, Where); Justify the absurd; The Emotional Dial (1→10); Endowment-acceptance; Make the choice readable
- **Focus:** comedy_game

**Objective:** Develops the ability to establish a robust base reality (C.R.O.W.) and maintain narrative continuity while dynamically shifting emotional intensity, physicality, and theatrical style.

## Setup
An open performance space with three to four players on stage and a facilitator standing off-stage or near the audience. No props or set pieces are required.

## How to Play
1. The facilitator asks the audience for a mundane, everyday location to inspire the start of the scene.
2. Two or three players step forward and begin performing a grounded, realistic scene, focusing heavily on establishing their Characters, Relationships, Objectives, and Where (C.R.O.W.).
3. Once the base reality is clearly established, the facilitator calls out 'Freeze!' to temporarily pause the action.
4. The facilitator asks the audience for a distinct theatrical genre, cinematic style, or literary tone (e.g., Shakespearean tragedy, sci-fi, film noir).
5. The facilitator calls 'Unfreeze!' and the players immediately resume the scene, filtering their performance through the newly designated style.
6. Players must continue the exact same storyline and maintain their established relationships, using the new genre's tropes to heighten their emotional and physical choices.
7. Every 45 to 60 seconds, the facilitator repeats the freeze-and-shift process, introducing a total of three to four different genres over the course of the scene.
8. The players work together to bring the narrative to a satisfying resolution during the final genre, and the facilitator calls 'Scene!' to conclude.

## Facilitation Notes
- Side-coaching cue: 'Keep the story moving! Do not reboot the scene; just change the style of how you tell it.'
- Common Pitfall: Players completely abandon their characters or location when a new genre is introduced (e.g., suddenly teleporting to outer space during a sci-fi shift). Fix: Remind players that the 'Where' and 'Who' remain identical; only the stylistic filter changes.
- Side-coaching cue: 'Commit 100% to the physical and vocal demands of the genre immediately upon unfreezing!'
- Encourage players to use strong physical offers—like striking a melodramatic pose or adopting a stealthy stance—to instantly signal the genre shift to their partners and the audience.

## Variations
- Emotional Rollercoaster: Instead of theatrical genres, the facilitator calls out specific, intense emotions (e.g., paranoia, extreme joy, deep grief) that the characters must suddenly adopt.
- Seamless Transitions: Play without freezing the action; the facilitator calls out the new genre on the fly, requiring players to morph their performance smoothly without pausing.
- Director's Cut: The facilitator calls out specific famous film directors or historical eras instead of broad genres, challenging players to adapt to more niche stylistic conventions.

## Debrief
- How did establishing a strong, grounded C.R.O.W. at the beginning help you navigate the sudden genre shifts?
- What physical or vocal choices did you discover when forced to commit to a genre you wouldn't normally play?
- How did you balance staying true to the original plot while fully embracing the stylistic tropes of each genre?

## Safety & Inclusion
Ensure that genre suggestions from the audience are kept respectful and do not rely on cultural, national, or identity-based stereotypes. If a sensitive or inappropriate genre is suggested, the facilitator should quickly and warmly pivot to an alternative choice.

## Why It Works
This game works because it separates the 'what' of a scene (the narrative platform) from the 'how' (the stylistic delivery). By forcing players to maintain a consistent storyline through radical stylistic shifts, it demonstrates that a solid base reality can support extreme comedic choices, teaching players to trust their platform rather than constantly inventing new plot points.
