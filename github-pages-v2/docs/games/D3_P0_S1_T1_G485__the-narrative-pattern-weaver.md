# The Pattern Weaver

> Spot the emerging comedic engine, call it out, and jump in to escalate it.

## Overview
An active observation and intervention drill where off-stage players watch a scene to identify recurring behaviors, unusual choices, or the core game. Once spotted, an observer pauses the action, names the pattern aloud, and tags in to immediately heighten that dynamic.

## What It Trains
- **Domain:** D3 — The Scene
- **Principle(s):** Yes, And
- **Skill(s):** Game Identification; Heightening & Exploration; Active Listening; Justification; Support Work
- **Technique(s):** Finding & Playing the Game; If this is true, what else is true?; The 'ladder' (escalating beats); Justify the absurd; Tap-ins
- **Focus:** skill_drill

**Objective:** To train improvisers in real-time game identification, active listening, and the immediate, disciplined heightening of established comedic or narrative patterns.

## Setup
Two players start a scene in the center of the space. The remaining players stand on the sidelines as Weavers. No props or special staging required.

## How to Play
1. Two players step into the performance space and initiate a standard, relationship-focused scene, establishing a clear platform.
2. The remaining players stand on the sidelines, acting as Weavers who actively listen and watch for emerging behavioral loops, unusual reactions, or repetitive dynamics.
3. As soon as a Weaver identifies a clear pattern or game of the scene, they call out 'Weave!' to freeze the action.
4. The Weaver steps forward and clearly articulates the observed pattern to the players and the room in one concise sentence.
5. The Weaver then tags out one of the active players, taking their place in the scene.
6. The scene immediately unfreezes, and the newly entered Weaver must deliver a line or action that directly heightens, escalates, or explores the specific pattern they just articulated.
7. The scene continues with the new pairing, with the remaining off-stage Weavers continuing to watch for the next evolution or a new pattern to Weave and tag in again.

## Facilitation Notes
- Coaching Cue: Look for the unusual thing that happens twice. That is your thread!
- Pitfall: Weavers make vague observations like 'They are arguing.' Fix: Coach them to identify the how and why of the argument, such as 'Every time one makes a point, the other physically steps backward.'
- Coaching Cue: When you tag in, don't start a new story. Pour gasoline on the fire you just named.
- Pitfall: The remaining player gets confused by the meta-interruption. Fix: Remind the remaining player to play their character's established perspective, accepting the Weaver's articulation as absolute truth.

## Variations
- Targeted Weaving: The facilitator assigns a specific type of pattern to look for, such as physical patterns, emotional shifts, or linguistic habits.
- Subversion Weaver: Instead of heightening the pattern, the entering Weaver must strategically subvert it to see how the remaining character reacts when their expected loop is broken.
- Silent Weaving: The Weaver tags in and demonstrates the heightening physically or verbally without naming it first, forcing the remaining player to adapt purely through play.

## Debrief
- How did naming the pattern out loud change your confidence in playing it?
- What was the difference between a vague observation and an actionable, specific pattern?
- How did it feel to have your scene interrupted, and how did you maintain your character's reality afterward?

## Safety & Inclusion
Since this game involves sudden physical tag-outs and verbal interruptions, ensure players establish clear physical boundaries before starting. Encourage verbal tags instead of physical contact if preferred.

## Why It Works
By forcing players to step out of the scene's fiction to analyze its structure, it demystifies the game of the scene. The immediate tag-in creates an instant feedback loop, transforming intellectual analysis into physical, spontaneous play.
