# The Mischievous Mouthpiece

> Justify the outrageous, sabotaging statements spoken by an invisible, sentient object in your mouth.

## Overview
In this comedic three-player scene, one player's character has an invisible, sentient object (like a piece of gum or a loose tooth) in their mouth that speaks aloud to sabotage them. The unsuspecting scene partner attributes these rude or bizarre statements directly to the host player, who must desperately justify the absurd remarks to save face. It is a high-energy exercise in rapid justification and maintaining a grounded base reality under pressure.

## Setup
Three players stand in the performance space. No props are required. Player A (the Host) and Player C (the Partner) stand center stage. Player B (the Saboteur) stands slightly behind Player A, acting as the voice of the object in Player A's mouth.

## How to Play
1. Assign the roles: Player A is the Host, Player B is the Saboteur (the voice of the gum/object), and Player C is the Partner.
2. Establish a high-stakes, polite base reality scene between Player A and Player C (e.g., a job interview, meeting the future in-laws, or a performance review).
3. Player A and Player C begin interacting normally. Player A should occasionally mimic chewing or adjusting something small in their mouth when not speaking.
4. At key moments of polite tension, Player B (the Saboteur) speaks aloud in a distinct, mischievous voice, making a rude, provocative, or highly inappropriate statement.
5. Player C must treat this statement as if it came directly out of Player A's mouth, reacting with appropriate shock, confusion, or offense.
6. Player A cannot deny saying the words; they must immediately accept that those words left their lips and rapidly justify why they said them to preserve the relationship.
7. Player B continues to inject sabotaging lines, forcing Player A into increasingly complex and hilarious justifications while Player C holds them accountable to the scene's stakes.

## Facilitation Notes
- Coaching cue: 'Don't deny it, justify it!' Remind Player A that they cannot say 'I didn't say that' or 'That was my gum talking.' They must fully own the words as their own.
- Pitfall: The Saboteur speaks too frequently, leaving no room for the scene to develop. Fix: Coach Player B to wait for moments of high politeness or tension to drop their sabotaging lines for maximum comedic impact.
- Coaching cue: 'Keep the partner in the dark.' Player C must never break the reality of the scene by acknowledging the physical player standing behind Player A; they must genuinely believe Player A is just being bizarrely eccentric.
- Pitfall: Player A panics and explains the gimmick to Player C. Fix: Remind Player A that the comedy comes from trying to pass off the absurd statements as their own intentional, if highly unusual, thoughts.

## Variations
- The Helpful Helper: Instead of an evil saboteur, the object is an overly enthusiastic, terrible 'wingman' trying to help Player A secure a date or a job, but giving awful, highly specific advice that the Host must justify.
- The Internal Organ: The voice is not in the mouth but is the character's heart, stomach, or liver, speaking their raw, unfiltered physical desires (e.g., 'I want to steal their wallet' or 'I am incredibly bored').

## Debrief
- How did it feel to have your conversational control stripped away, and how did that force you to listen more closely?
- What strategies did you use to justify the most absurd statements without breaking the scene's reality?
- How does this game demonstrate the power of 'Yes, And' when dealing with an unexpected or difficult offer?

## Safety & Inclusion
Ensure the Saboteur (Player B) keeps their sabotaging comments appropriate for the group's boundaries, focusing on social awkwardness, bizarre confessions, and eccentricities rather than genuinely offensive or triggering language.

## Why It Works
This game forces the Host to practice extreme justification. By treating every disruptive line as an absolute truth that they must own, players learn that any offer—no matter how bizarre or destructive to the character's goals—can be integrated into a coherent narrative if the performer commits to finding a logical reason behind it.
