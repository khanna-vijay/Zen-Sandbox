# Name the Monster

> Confront and label an unexpected threat together, one word at a time.

## Overview
In this paired exercise, players collaborate word-by-word to build a simple scene, introduce a bizarre threat, and explicitly name it before escaping. It is a fast-paced, spontaneous narrative game that highlights how clear labeling can instantly focus a scene's conflict. Players experience the thrill of shared storytelling where neither knows what the threat will be until it is spoken into existence.

## What It Trains
- **Domain:** D3 — The Scene
- **Principle(s):** The First Thought Is a Gift; Yes, And; Serve the Story
- **Skill(s):** Unfiltered Spontaneity; Active Listening; Narrative Architecture
- **Technique(s):** First Thought drills; Platform/Tilt
- **Focus:** narrative

**Objective:** To practice the Platform/Tilt narrative technique by establishing a stable baseline, introducing a clear disruption, and explicitly naming the conflict to drive the story forward.

## Setup
Players stand in pairs, facing each other in a clear space. No props or materials are required.

## How to Play
1. Divide the group into pairs and have partners stand facing each other.
2. Begin speaking a story together, alternating exactly one word at a time to build a sentence.
3. Establish a mundane platform by describing a simple location and activity (e.g., 'We', 'are', 'walking', 'in', 'the', 'deep', 'woods').
4. Introduce a sudden disruption or monster into the scene (e.g., 'Suddenly', 'a', 'giant', 'shadow', 'appears').
5. Explicitly name the monster with a specific, imaginative title (e.g., 'It', 'is', 'the', 'Screaming', 'Marshmallow', 'Beast').
6. React to the named threat immediately by fleeing or finding safety, maintaining the one-word-at-a-time structure.
7. End the scene once the characters have successfully escaped or reached a natural narrative conclusion.

## Facilitation Notes
- Side-coach players to establish the 'where' and 'what' (the platform) before introducing the monster, ensuring the disruption has maximum impact.
- If players hesitate or overthink their words, call out: 'First thought, best thought! Keep the rhythm going!'
- Ensure the monster is named explicitly. If they just say 'a monster,' side-coach: 'What is its specific name?'
- Pitfall: Players running away before naming the threat. Fix: Remind them that the conflict must be clearly labeled before it can be resolved.

## Variations
- The Confrontation: Instead of running away, players must choose to fight, tame, or make friends with the named monster.
- The Internal Threat: Apply the same structure but make the 'monster' an internal feeling, secret, or relationship tension that must be named.
- Three-Word Phrases: To ease the cognitive load for absolute beginners, allow players to alternate short phrases instead of single words.

## Debrief
- How did explicitly naming the monster change the clarity and focus of your scene?
- What did you notice about your ability to listen when you had to build the platform one word at a time?
- Why is it helpful in improv to clearly label the 'problem' or 'tilt' in a scene rather than keeping it vague?

## Safety & Inclusion
Ensure players remain stationary or move mindfully within their personal space when 'running away' to prevent physical collisions in a crowded room.

## Why It Works
By forcing players to alternate word-by-word, the game bypasses individual planning and demands deep active listening. The 'monster' serves as a literal manifestation of a narrative 'tilt.' Naming the monster teaches players that identifying the conflict clearly is the fastest way to align their shared reality and move the story forward.
