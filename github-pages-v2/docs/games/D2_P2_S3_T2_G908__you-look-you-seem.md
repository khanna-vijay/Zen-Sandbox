# You Look, You Seem

> Deepen scene connection by naming and instantly amplifying your partner's real-time emotional states.

## Overview
A two-player scene-work exercise where one player is restricted to labeling their partner's emotional state using 'You look...' or 'You seem...' prompts. The other player must immediately lean into and amplify that named emotion, creating a highly connected, relationship-driven scene. Over the course of the scene, the roles swap, keeping both players deeply attuned to physical and vocal shifts.

## What It Trains
- **Domain:** D2 — The Partner
- **Principle(s):** Yes, And; Make Your Partner a Genius; Vulnerability; Base Reality First
- **Skill(s):** Active Listening; Single-Partner Empathy & Mirroring; Active Gifting; Emotional Fluidity; World-Building
- **Technique(s):** Meisner Repetition; Emotional-echo drills; Endowment-gifting drills; The Emotional Dial (1→10); C.R.O.W. (Character, Relationship, Objective, Where)
- **Focus:** connection

**Objective:** To develop deep partner empathy, active listening, and emotional fluidity by treating a partner's physical and vocal cues as the primary source of scene information.

## Setup
Players stand in pairs facing each other in a clear space. No props or special staging are required. Designate one player as Player A (the Free Speaker) and the other as Player B (the Emotional Mirror).

## How to Play
1. Begin a standard two-person scene with Player A initiating a line of dialogue and establishing a basic activity or relationship.
2. Player B must respond only by using the sentence starters 'You look...' or 'You seem...' followed by a single, clear emotion they observe in Player A (e.g., 'You look anxious' or 'You seem thrilled').
3. Player A must immediately accept this emotional label as absolute truth, instantly amplifying that emotion in their body, voice, and subsequent dialogue.
4. Player B continues to observe Player A's physical and vocal shifts, offering new 'You look/seem' statements as the emotional landscape naturally evolves.
5. Player A continues to speak freely, justifying their heightened emotional state within the context of the scene's reality.
6. Midway through the scene (approximately 1-2 minutes in), the facilitator calls out 'Swap!'.
7. Upon the swap, the characters and scene context remain exactly the same, but the roles reverse: Player A can now only speak using 'You look/seem' prompts, while Player B is freed to speak normally and must embody the emotions labeled by Player A.

## Facilitation Notes
- Coaching cue: 'Label the actual physical offer.' Encourage players to look at their partner's real posture, facial expressions, and micro-movements rather than inventing an emotion out of thin air.
- Pitfall: Using intellectualized or plot-heavy labels (e.g., 'You look like you want to go to the grocery store'). Fix: Remind players to stick strictly to core emotions or physical states (e.g., 'You look exhausted,' 'You seem defensive').
- Coaching cue: 'Embody it fully!' Ensure the free speaker doesn't just agree verbally but physically shifts their posture, breathing, and vocal tone to match the named emotion.
- Encourage players to allow the emotion to change naturally. It is perfectly fine to repeat the same emotion if it is still active, but be ready to pivot the moment a new micro-expression appears.

## Variations
- Silent Echoes: Play the same game but without the verbal 'You look/seem' prompt; players must mirror and amplify each other's emotional shifts purely through physical and vocal mimicry.
- The Dial: The mirroring player can add an intensity modifier, such as 'You look slightly annoyed' or 'You seem absolutely furious,' forcing the partner to calibrate their emotional volume.
- Integration: Run a standard scene where players are free to speak normally, but must occasionally drop in a 'You look/seem' observation to ground the scene back in emotional reality whenever the plot becomes too intellectual.

## Debrief
- How did it feel to have your partner name your emotion? Did it make you feel supported or restricted?
- What physical cues did you find yourself noticing that you might normally overlook in a scene?
- How did focusing on emotional states rather than plot affect the relationship between the characters?

## Safety & Inclusion
Because this game requires close observation of physical and emotional states, remind players to respect personal boundaries. Players should focus on emotional states (e.g., 'You look tense') rather than commenting on their partner's physical appearance or body shape. Ensure players feel safe expressing vulnerability in a supportive, non-judgmental environment.

## Why It Works
This game works because it operationalizes the core principle of 'Yes, And' at an emotional level. By forcing one player to actively read their partner's physical offers and the other to instantly validate and expand upon those observations, it bypasses intellectual plotting and anchors the scene in a shared, felt reality. It builds trust by making the partner's actual state the most important element of the scene.
