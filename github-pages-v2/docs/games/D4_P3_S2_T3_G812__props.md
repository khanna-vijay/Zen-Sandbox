# Living Props

> Transform the ensemble into dynamic physical objects, vehicles, and environments to support a scene.

## Overview
Two actors initiate a high-stakes narrative scene while the remaining ensemble members stand ready at the sides. Whenever the scene partners mention, interact with, or require an object, vehicle, or environmental element, the off-stage players instantly rush in to physically manifest those items. The result is a fast-paced, highly physical collaboration where the environment itself is alive and responsive.

## What It Trains
- **Domain:** D4 — The Ensemble
- **Principle(s):** Commit 100%; Show, Don't Tell; Serve the Piece
- **Skill(s):** Physicality & Space Work; World-Building; Support Work
- **Technique(s):** Object work; Walk-ons; Playing architecture/objects
- **Focus:** skill_drill

**Objective:** To develop active ensemble support, physical commitment, and spatial awareness by training off-stage players to instantly serve the scene as physical architecture and objects.

## Setup
A large, open playing space. Two players start in the center of the stage to begin the scene. The remaining players (the 'prop closet') stand in a semi-circle or along the back and sides of the stage, poised to jump in at a moment's notice.

## How to Play
1. Two main actors begin an active, high-energy scene, establishing a clear relationship and a dynamic scenario that naturally invites physical action.
2. The off-stage players watch and listen intently, ready to physically support the scene's environment.
3. As soon as a main actor names, implies, or interacts with an object, vehicle, or environmental hazard (such as a helicopter, a heavy vault door, or a turbulent river), off-stage players must immediately run onto the stage to physically construct it.
4. Off-stage players can work individually or combine their bodies to form larger, complex structures or moving machinery.
5. The main actors must immediately accept and interact with the physicalized objects, matching their physical play to the shape, scale, and movement of the ensemble's creation.
6. Once an object or environment is no longer needed or the scene moves to a new location, the players forming the object melt back to the sidelines, keeping the stage clear for the next initiation.
7. The scene continues with rapid transitions, encouraging the main actors to explore diverse settings that challenge the ensemble's physical creativity.

## Facilitation Notes
- Side-coaching cue: 'Don't wait for permission! If they mention a key, become the key or the lock immediately.'
- Pitfall: Off-stage players hesitate, leaving the main actors hanging. Fix: Encourage 'imperfect commitment'—it is better to run in and make a simple shape instantly than to design a perfect machine in your head.
- Side-coaching cue: 'Main actors, use what they give you!' If the ensemble builds a tiny car, the actors must cram themselves into a tiny space.
- Pitfall: The living props start talking or taking over the narrative. Fix: Remind the ensemble that their role is silent, physical support; they are the set and props, not new characters.

## Variations
- Silent Environment: The main actors are not allowed to speak; they must use pure pantomime to indicate what objects they need, forcing the ensemble to read physical cues.
- Emotional Objects: The physical props must reflect the emotional state of the scene (e.g., if the characters are angry, the door slams violently or the chair feels spiky).
- Transforming Vehicle: The entire scene takes place inside or on a single complex vehicle that constantly morphs as the journey progresses (e.g., a car that turns into a submarine, then a rocket).

## Debrief
- How did it feel as a scene partner to have your verbal suggestions instantly physicalized by the ensemble?
- What strategies did the off-stage players use to quickly coordinate and build complex objects together?
- How does physicalizing the environment change the stakes and energy of a scene compared to just miming objects?

## Safety & Inclusion
Since this game involves rapid physical movement and close body contact to form objects, establish clear boundaries regarding physical touch before starting. Players should communicate non-verbally or use light touch only with consent. Offer low-impact options where players can represent objects using just their hands, posture, or vocal sound effects without needing to bend, lift, or make direct physical contact.

## Why It Works
This game shifts the focus from individual performance to collective support. By turning the ensemble into the physical fabric of the scene, it reinforces the principle of 'Show, Don't Tell' and forces off-stage players to listen actively. It strips away the fear of 'what do I do next' for the main actors, as they are constantly gifted with rich, physical offers to react to, while teaching the ensemble to selflessly serve the piece.
