# Reality Weavers

> Co-create and instantly adapt to a rapidly shifting, player-controlled digital environment in real time.

## Overview
A high-energy virtual improv game where players collaboratively build and alter their physical scene using a shared digital canvas. Instead of just describing their surroundings, players use shared screen controls to instantly inject visual assets, forcing the ensemble to immediately 'Yes, And' the new visual reality. It transforms the virtual stage from a static backdrop into an active, unpredictable character in the scene.

## What It Trains
- **Domain:** D3 — The Scene
- **Principle(s):** Show, Don't Tell; Yes, And; Group Mind
- **Skill(s):** World-Building; Offer Reception; Active Gifting; Support Work
- **Technique(s):** Endowment-acceptance; Endowment-gifting drills
- **Focus:** mixed

**Objective:** Develops advanced world-building and 'Show, Don't Tell' skills by integrating immediate, player-driven visual changes into the narrative, fostering deep offer reception and group mind.

## Setup
Players join a virtual meeting platform. One player is designated as the 'Console Operator' (CO) and shares a pre-loaded digital slide deck containing a vast library of environmental assets (landscapes, objects, weather effects, abstract textures). The CO enables remote control/shared control permissions. All other players rename themselves to reflect their character's name and role (e.g., 'Dr. Aris - Botanist').

## How to Play
1. The Console Operator (CO) shares their screen, displaying a blank starting slide (the 'Canvas') and prepares the asset library on a secondary screen or hidden slide.
2. Two to three active players step into the scene verbally, establishing their characters, relationships, and an initial broad setting.
3. At any point, any player (active or observing) can make an 'environmental offer' by signaling the CO (using a specific reaction emoji or a verbal cue like 'Visual Cue!').
4. The CO immediately grants temporary remote control of the shared screen to the signaling player.
5. The player with control quickly copies an asset (an image, icon, or texture) from the library slide and pastes/drags it onto the active Canvas slide, or uses annotation tools to draw a change.
6. Once the visual asset is placed, the CO instantly reclaims screen control to keep the technical transition seamless.
7. All active players must immediately and without hesitation 'Yes, And' this new visual element, reacting to it physically and verbally as if it just materialized in their physical space.
8. The scene continues to evolve dynamically as players rotate control, layering new visual elements, zooming in on objects, or shifting the entire location by swapping the background slide.

## Facilitation Notes
- Coaching Cue: 'React first, explain later!' Encourage players to gasp, duck, or reach out to the new visual asset before they verbally label it.
- Pitfall: Technical lag or fumbling with the asset library. Fix: Keep the asset library highly organized with clear categories (e.g., 'Sci-Fi', 'Nature', 'Objects') and limit the selection to 20-30 high-impact images to prevent decision paralysis.
- Coaching Cue: 'Make the environment a character.' Remind players that a sudden visual change (like a giant eye appearing) is an active narrative offer that demands a response, not just background decoration.
- Pitfall: The Console Operator getting overwhelmed. Fix: Have a co-facilitator manage the participant renaming and hand-raising signals so the CO can focus entirely on granting and reclaiming screen control.

## Variations
- The Blind Build: The active players close their eyes while an off-screen player places a new visual asset. On 'eyes open,' the active players must instantly justify the new element in their scene.
- Atmospheric Shift: Instead of adding physical objects, players can only use color overlays or lighting filters to change the emotional tone or weather of the scene.
- The Prop Master: One player is designated solely as the 'invisible hand,' dropping random, unexpected visual assets onto the canvas that the active players must immediately utilize as props.

## Debrief
- How did having a tangible, immediate visual change alter your physical and emotional choices compared to purely verbal world-building?
- What strategies did you use to seamlessly integrate an unexpected visual asset without breaking the narrative flow?
- How did sharing control of the physical stage affect the power dynamics and collaborative spirit of the ensemble?

## Safety & Inclusion
Ensure all visual assets in the library are pre-screened to be inclusive, accessible, and free of triggering imagery. For players with visual impairments, the Console Operator or a designated narrator should instantly and vividly describe any visual changes as they occur so they can fully participate in the scene.

## Why It Works
By externalizing the imagined space into a shared, mutable digital artifact, this game forces players to move beyond verbal exposition and practice true 'Show, Don't Tell.' The physical act of manipulating the stage creates a high-stakes feedback loop where visual offers must be instantly justified, sharpening the ensemble's active gifting, support work, and collective group mind.
