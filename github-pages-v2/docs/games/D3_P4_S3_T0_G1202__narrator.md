# The Storyteller's Lens

> Co-create a rich story through the dynamic interplay of physical action and spoken narration.

## Overview
One player acts as an off-stage narrator while two or more players act out the scene on stage. The narrator describes the characters' inner thoughts, subtext, and environment, while the actors physically manifest these details and advance the plot. It is a collaborative dance of offer and acceptance where the spoken word and physical action constantly feed into each other.

## What It Trains
- **Domain:** D3 — The Scene
- **Principle(s):** Serve the Story; Yes, And; Make Your Partner a Genius
- **Skill(s):** Narrative Architecture; Active Listening; Offer Reception; Active Gifting; Support Work
- **Technique(s):** Endowment-acceptance; Endowment-gifting drills; Story Spine
- **Focus:** narrative

**Objective:** To develop narrative architecture and active gifting by balancing external narration with physical scene work, ensuring both elements serve the story.

## Setup
A performance space with a designated stage area for the actors and a standing spot on the side (downstage left or right) for the narrator. No props or chairs are required.

## How to Play
1. Select one player to be the Narrator and two to three players to be the Actors on stage.
2. Obtain a simple suggestion from the group, such as an everyday activity or an unusual location, to inspire the start of the scene.
3. The Narrator begins by setting the scene, describing the environment, the atmosphere, or the initial physical state of the characters.
4. The Actors immediately physicalize the Narrator's descriptions, using object work and body language to bring the narrated details to life.
5. Actors speak dialogue and initiate actions, which the Narrator must then accept as truth, adding depth by narrating the characters' internal feelings or the unseen consequences of their actions.
6. The Narrator must avoid over-talking; they should pause frequently to let the Actors play out beats, speak to one another, and discover physical comedy or drama.
7. The scene progresses with this continuous hand-off: the Narrator gifts details to the Actors, and the Actors gift physical actions and dialogue back to the Narrator, building a cohesive narrative arc.
8. The scene concludes when the Narrator delivers a final, wrapping narration that brings the story to a satisfying resolution, or when the facilitator calls edit.

## Facilitation Notes
- Coaching Cue: 'Show what is told, tell what is shown.' Remind actors to immediately physicalize the narrator's words, and remind the narrator to describe the subtext of what the actors are physically doing.
- Pitfall: The Narrator puppeteers the actors too much (e.g., 'Then John did a backflip'). Fix: Encourage the narrator to focus on internal states, sensory details, and environmental changes rather than micro-managing physical movements.
- Pitfall: The Actors wait passively for the narrator to speak. Fix: Remind actors that they have full agency to initiate dialogue and action; the narrator is there to support and expand, not to write every line.
- Coaching Cue: 'Leave space.' Encourage the narrator to let scenes breathe. Silence from the narrator is an invitation for the actors to explore.

## Variations
- The Genre Shift: The narrator adopts a specific literary or cinematic style (e.g., Film Noir, Shakespearean, Children's Book) which the actors must match in tone.
- Tag-Team Narrators: Two narrators stand on opposite sides of the stage, taking turns to narrate, representing different perspectives or conflicting inner thoughts of the characters.
- The Subtext Translator: The actors speak normal, polite dialogue, but the narrator steps in to reveal their true, petty, or dramatic inner thoughts after each line.

## Debrief
- How did it feel to have your character's inner thoughts revealed by an external voice? Did it make choices easier or harder?
- For the Narrators: How did you balance giving inspiring offers with leaving enough space for the actors to play?
- How did this game demonstrate the principle of 'making your partner a genius'?

## Safety & Inclusion
Ensure the narrator does not narrate physical actions that are unsafe, uncomfortable, or violate the personal boundaries of the actors on stage. Actors always have the right to modify a narrated physical action to keep themselves safe and comfortable.

## Why It Works
This game works because it separates the burden of narrative progression from the burden of physical execution. By splitting these roles, players can focus deeply on active listening and offer reception. The narrator provides the structural architecture (the 'why' and 'where'), while the actors provide the emotional heart and immediacy (the 'who' and 'what'), creating a highly collaborative narrative engine.
