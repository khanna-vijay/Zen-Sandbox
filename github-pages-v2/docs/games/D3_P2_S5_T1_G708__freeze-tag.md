# Freeze Tag

> Interrupt physical poses to instantly launch brand-new scenes using immediate, physical C.R.O.W. justification.

## Overview
Two players begin an active, physical scene. At any moment, an off-stage player yells 'Freeze!', causing the active players to lock their physical postures. The caller steps in, taps one player out, assumes their exact physical pose, and initiates a completely new scene that justifies these physical shapes as a fresh base reality.

## What It Trains
- **Domain:** D3 — The Scene
- **Principle(s):** Base Reality First; The First Thought Is a Gift; Follow the Follower
- **Skill(s):** World-Building; Justification; Unfiltered Spontaneity; Support Work
- **Technique(s):** C.R.O.W. (Character, Relationship, Objective, Where); Justify the absurd; First Thought drills; Tap-ins
- **Focus:** comedy_game

**Objective:** To rapidly establish a clear base reality (C.R.O.W.) using physical shapes as the primary inspiration, fostering unfiltered spontaneity and immediate justification.

## Setup
An open playing space with a designated stage area. The remaining players line up along the back wall (the 'back line') acting as active observers ready to jump in. No props or chairs are used.

## How to Play
1. Two players step forward and assume distinct, highly physical, and asymmetrical poses (often prompted by letters of the alphabet or physical suggestions from the group).
2. The two players begin improvising a scene, immediately establishing their Character, Relationship, Objective, and Where (C.R.O.W.) based on their starting physical shapes.
3. At any point during the scene—ideally when a striking physical shape or high-energy moment occurs—a player from the back line shouts 'Freeze!'
4. The active players must instantly lock their physical bodies in place, holding their exact positions like statues.
5. The player who called 'Freeze' enters the stage, taps one of the frozen players on the shoulder to dismiss them, and assumes that exact physical posture.
6. The entering player immediately initiates a brand-new scene, using the frozen physical shapes of both players to define a completely different base reality.
7. The remaining original player must instantly adapt to this new reality, accepting the new C.R.O.W. parameters and playing the new scene with high energy.
8. The cycle repeats with back-line players continuously freezing the action, swapping in, and launching new scenarios.

## Facilitation Notes
- Side-coaching cue: 'Don't plan, just freeze!' Encourage players to shout 'Freeze' before they have a conscious idea of what the new scene will be, relying on the physical shape to inspire them once they step into it.
- Pitfall: Players entering the scene and explaining why they are in the pose rather than just living in the reality. Fix: Coach them to start with a direct, active line of dialogue that establishes the C.R.O.W. immediately (e.g., 'Hold still, this surgery is delicate!' instead of 'Why are my hands up like this?').
- Side-coaching cue: 'Physicalize!' Remind the active players to keep moving and using their bodies during their scenes so that the back line has interesting, dynamic shapes to freeze.
- Pitfall: Hesitation from the back line, leading to scenes running too long. Fix: Set a rule that scenes should only last 30 to 45 seconds before a freeze is required, keeping the energy high and fast-paced.

## Variations
- Blind Freeze: The back-line players stand with their backs turned to the stage. When they shout 'Freeze!', they spin around, look at the frozen shapes, and must immediately run in to take over a position.
- Tag-Team Freeze: The person who shouts 'Freeze' does not enter themselves, but instead calls out the name of another back-line player who must instantly run in, take the pose, and start the scene.
- Object-Focus Freeze: The entering player must specifically justify the physical pose as holding or interacting with a complex, imaginary object, emphasizing physical world-building.

## Debrief
- How did it feel to step into a physical shape without having a pre-planned scene idea?
- What strategies helped you instantly establish a clear C.R.O.W. (Character, Relationship, Objective, Where) for the new scene?
- How does physical movement and body posture make it easier to discover a character's voice and attitude?
- What did you notice about the relationship between physical commitment and the comedic payoff of a scene?

## Safety & Inclusion
Since this game involves physical contact (tapping a player on the shoulder to swap out), establish a clear boundary beforehand. Players can agree to tap the floor, clap near the player, or verbally say 'I take your spot' instead of physical touch. Ensure the playing space is clear of tripping hazards as players will be moving quickly onto the stage.

## Why It Works
Freeze Tag bypasses the analytical mind by forcing players to physicalize first and justify second. By stepping into a pre-existing physical shape, the improviser's body dictates the character's status, emotion, and environment before their brain can overthink. This perfectly trains the 'Base Reality First' principle, as the physical posture serves as an undeniable, concrete truth that must be accepted and built upon ('Yes, Anded') immediately.
