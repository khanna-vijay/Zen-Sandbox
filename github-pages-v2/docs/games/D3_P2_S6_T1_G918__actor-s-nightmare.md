# Actor's Nightmare

> One actor reads verbatim from a script while the other justifies every bizarre line.

## Overview
A two-player scene where one performer reads lines verbatim from a pre-existing script while the other improvises freely. The improvising player must treat every scripted line as a logical, meaningful contribution, building a coherent base reality around the absurd juxtaposition. It is a high-energy exercise in radical acceptance and creative justification.

## What It Trains
- **Domain:** D3 — The Scene
- **Principle(s):** Yes, And; Make Your Partner a Genius; Base Reality First
- **Skill(s):** Active Listening; Offer Reception; World-Building; Justification
- **Technique(s):** Endowment-acceptance; C.R.O.W. (Character, Relationship, Objective, Where); Justify the absurd
- **Focus:** mixed

**Objective:** To develop advanced justification skills, active listening, and the ability to establish and maintain a solid base reality under highly unpredictable constraints.

## Setup
Two players stand in the performance space. One player is handed a physical script, book, or printed text (such as a scene from a classic play, a technical manual, or a comic book) that they have not pre-read. The other player has no props. The facilitator establishes a simple, mundane starting relationship and location.

## How to Play
1. Assign roles: Player A is the 'Scripted Actor' and Player B is the 'Improviser'.
2. Provide Player A with the physical script. They must read their lines exactly as written, in order, without skipping, editing, or altering any words.
3. Establish a mundane, grounded base reality (e.g., two coworkers organizing a supply closet, or a parent and child washing a car) to give the scene a stable foundation.
4. Player B initiates the scene with a normal, grounded line of dialogue and physical action to set the context.
5. Player A responds by reading their first line from the script, delivering it with emotional commitment and physical action rather than reading it flatly.
6. Player B must immediately accept the scripted line as absolute truth, justifying why Player A said it within the context of their current relationship and activity.
7. Continue the scene with Player B improvising and Player A reading the next sequential line from their text whenever it is their turn to speak.
8. End the scene once a satisfying narrative resolution or a peak moment of comedic justification is reached, typically after two to three minutes.

## Facilitation Notes
- Coaching cue: 'Don't just read it, act it!' Encourage the scripted player to find physical actions and emotional subtext for their lines, rather than treating the script as a barrier.
- Pitfall: The improviser ignores the weirdness of the script. Fix: Remind the improviser to actively listen and explain why that specific, bizarre line makes perfect sense in this exact moment.
- Coaching cue: 'Ground the reality first.' If the scene starts too wacky, the justification loses its impact. Keep the physical environment and relationship highly relatable.
- Pitfall: The scripted player reads ahead or cherry-picks lines. Fix: Insist they read the very next line on the page, regardless of how ill-fitting it seems.

## Variations
- Double Nightmare: Both players have different scripts and must justify each other's random lines without any improvised dialogue.
- Genre Swap: Use a highly specific non-theatrical text, like a technical manual, a cookbook, or a children's fairy tale, instead of a traditional play script.
- Physical Script: The scripted player must also execute any physical stage directions printed in the script, which the improviser must also justify.

## Debrief
- How did having a grounded base reality help make the absurd scripted lines feel believable?
- What strategies did the improviser use to justify lines that seemed completely irrelevant to the scene?
- For the scripted player, how did committing to physical action and emotion change how the lines were received?

## Safety & Inclusion
Ensure the printed text used does not contain offensive, triggering, or highly sensitive language. If a line feels inappropriate, players should have permission to skip to the next line or call 'line' for a replacement.

## Why It Works
It forces the improviser to practice radical acceptance. Because they cannot control what their partner says, they must abandon any preconceived agenda and listen deeply. The humor and narrative satisfaction come from the brain's ability to find patterns and logical connections between completely unrelated ideas, reinforcing the power of a strong base reality.
