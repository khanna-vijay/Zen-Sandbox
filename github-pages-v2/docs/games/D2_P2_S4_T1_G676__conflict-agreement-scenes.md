# The Agreement Pivot

> Transform high-stakes, conflict-prone scenarios into deep moments of mutual agreement and connection.

## Overview
Two players are placed in a scenario that typically triggers immediate theatrical conflict, such as a car accident or a firing. Instead of arguing, they must actively receive every offer and find a surprising, collaborative point of agreement. This shift prioritizes relationship-building and shared reality over cheap combativeness.

## What It Trains
- **Domain:** D2 — The Partner
- **Principle(s):** Yes, And; Make Your Partner a Genius; Base Reality First; Serve the Story
- **Skill(s):** Active Listening; Offer Reception; World-Building; Justification
- **Technique(s):** Yes, And… sentence games; Endowment-acceptance; C.R.O.W. (Character, Relationship, Objective, Where); Justify the absurd
- **Focus:** connection

**Objective:** Develops advanced offer reception and 'Yes, And' skills by training players to bypass defensive instincts and find connection in high-stakes situations.

## Setup
Two chairs placed center stage. The remaining participants sit as an active audience. No props or materials are required.

## How to Play
1. Invite two players to the stage and assign them a classic high-conflict prompt, such as a manager firing an employee.
2. Instruct the players that they must accept the premise of the conflict but immediately pivot to mutual agreement and support.
3. The first player initiates the scene by delivering the high-stakes offer clearly and directly.
4. The second player receives the offer with total agreement, validating the first player's perspective instead of defending themselves.
5. Both players continue the scene by building on this shared reality, using 'Yes, And' to discover a deeper bond or a collaborative solution.
6. The scene concludes once a strong, cooperative relationship is established and the initial crisis has been transformed into a connection.

## Facilitation Notes
- Side-coach with: 'Agree with the reality, but love the person.' This helps players separate the bad news from their relationship.
- Watch out for passive compliance where one player simply submits. Remind them to 'Yes, And' by adding new information that justifies the agreement.
- If players stall, coach them to find a shared external focus, like blaming a poorly designed intersection after a car crash.
- Encourage players to keep the stakes high; agreement does not mean the situation is suddenly easy, it means they are facing it together.

## Variations
- The Literal Yes-And: Players must begin every single line of dialogue with the phrase 'Yes, and...' to strictly enforce the agreement mechanic.
- The Disarming Pivot: One player is secretly instructed to try to start a conflict, while the other player must disarm them using radical agreement.

## Debrief
- How did it feel to bypass the instinct to argue when given a high-conflict prompt?
- What happened to the depth of the relationship when you chose agreement over conflict?
- How does agreeing with a difficult offer make your partner look like a genius?

## Safety & Inclusion
Some high-conflict prompts can touch on sensitive personal experiences. Allow players to veto or adjust prompts instantly with no explanation needed.

## Why It Works
Conflict in improv often leads to defensive blocking. By forcing agreement in a high-conflict setup, players must listen deeply and collaborate on a creative justification, building a strong base reality and prioritizing connection.
