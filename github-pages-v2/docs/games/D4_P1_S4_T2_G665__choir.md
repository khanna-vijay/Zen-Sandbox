# Improvised Choir

> Create a harmonious, spontaneous song by building layers of rhythm, sound, and shared focus.

## Overview
A group of players stands before the audience as a formal vocal ensemble. Starting from a single suggestion, they build a layered, rhythmic, and melodic song from scratch, beginning with a unified sound and gradually branching into complex, interlocking vocal patterns. The experience is highly collaborative, requiring deep listening and a shared sense of timing.

## What It Trains
- **Domain:** D4 — The Ensemble
- **Principle(s):** Group Mind; Follow the Follower; The Audience Is the Final Scene Partner; Commit 100%
- **Skill(s):** Pacing & Rhythm; Peripheral Awareness; Vocal Craft; Stage Presence & Clarity
- **Technique(s):** Timing exercises; Thread-tracking drills; Vocal characterization; Projection
- **Focus:** connection

**Objective:** Develops group mind, peripheral awareness, and vocal pacing by training players to listen to the collective soundscape and find where their voice fits best.

## Setup
Players stand in a semi-circle or a straight line facing the audience. The space should be clear of obstacles. No props or instruments are needed.

## How to Play
1. The ensemble enters the stage with high energy, warm smiles, and strong eye contact, presenting themselves as a professional, highly coordinated choir.
2. The facilitator or a player solicits a simple suggestion from the audience, such as a mundane object, an emotion, or a location.
3. The entire group begins by establishing a unified, simple vocal rhythm or sound pattern based on the suggestion, chanting or singing it in perfect unison.
4. Once the initial unison pattern is locked in and stable, individual players begin to branch off one by one to introduce complementary vocal layers, such as a bassline, a melodic counterpoint, percussion sounds, or harmony.
5. Players practice 'follow the follower,' adjusting their volume, tempo, and pitch to support whatever new vocal choices emerge from their peers.
6. One or two players may step forward to sing short, improvised solo verses or spoken-word poetry over the established vocal backing track.
7. The ensemble maintains strong peripheral awareness, using physical cues like breathing together or subtle head nods to transition between sections or shift the song's dynamics.
8. To conclude the song, the group naturally senses a climax or resolution, coming back together into a final, unified chord or repeating the original unison chant before taking a synchronized bow.

## Facilitation Notes
- Side-coaching cue: 'Listen more than you sing. Find the empty space in the rhythm rather than crowding the sound.'
- Pitfall: Players all try to sing lead melodies at the same time, creating sonic chaos. Fix: Remind them to hold a steady, repetitive 'loop' or bassline to give others a solid foundation to play over.
- Side-coaching cue: 'Use your eyes! Look at your fellow choir members to sync up your breaths and transitions.'
- Pitfall: The song goes on too long without a clear ending. Fix: Coach the group to listen for a natural peak, and have one player clearly signal a final, sustained note that everyone joins.

## Variations
- Conducted Choir: One player acts as the conductor, using hand gestures to control volume, tempo, and who sings or stays silent.
- Genre Shift: The choir must perform the song in a specific musical style suggested by the audience, such as opera, gospel, heavy metal, or synth-pop.
- Gibberish Hymn: The choir sings entirely in an invented language, focusing purely on emotional tone, melody, and vocal texture rather than literal words.

## Debrief
- How did it feel to transition from absolute unison to individual, layered parts?
- What non-verbal cues did you use to stay in sync with the rest of the group?
- How did listening to the 'empty spaces' change the quality of the song?
- How does treating the audience as your final scene partner change your physical presence on stage?

## Safety & Inclusion
Ensure players are comfortable with vocal projection. Offer options for non-verbal vocalizations, such as rhythmic clapping, chest-patting, or humming, for those who may feel anxious about singing or have vocal fatigue.

## Why It Works
This game strips away narrative complexity and forces players to rely entirely on rhythm, tone, and mutual support. By starting in unison, it establishes an immediate 'group mind' connection, making it easier to hear where a new musical layer is needed.
