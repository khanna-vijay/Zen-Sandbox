# Pass Yes

> A rapid-fire circle game of agreement, reversal, and shared rhythmic momentum.

## Overview
Players stand in a circle and pass the word 'Yes' in a single direction to build a fast, unbroken rhythm. At any moment, a player can say 'No' to instantly reverse the flow, forcing the group to maintain focus and adapt to sudden shifts. The experience is high-energy, requiring intense concentration and a shared group pulse.

## What It Trains
- **Domain:** D4 — The Ensemble
- **Principle(s):** Commit 100%; Yes, And; Group Mind
- **Skill(s):** Unfiltered Spontaneity; Active Listening; Peripheral Awareness; Pacing & Rhythm
- **Technique(s):** Thread-tracking drills; Timing exercises
- **Focus:** connection

**Objective:** To develop group mind, active listening, and rapid pacing by training players to react instantly to auditory cues and maintain a collective rhythm.

## Setup
Have all players stand in a circle facing inward. Ensure there is enough space for everyone to see and hear each other clearly. No props or materials are required.

## How to Play
1. Gather the group into a standing circle, facing inward.
2. Designate one player to start the game by turning to their left, making eye contact, and saying 'Yes' in a clear, crisp voice.
3. The receiving player must immediately turn to their left and pass the 'Yes' to the next person, establishing a steady, clockwise rhythm.
4. Once the basic flow is established, introduce the reversal mechanic: any player, when passed a 'Yes', can choose to say 'No' instead.
5. Saying 'No' immediately reverses the direction of the pass, sending the turn back to the person who just spoke.
6. The player who received the 'No' must quickly adapt and pass a 'Yes' back in the opposite direction to keep the chain moving.
7. Encourage the group to eliminate any pauses or lag time between words, aiming for a seamless, rapid-fire exchange.

## Facilitation Notes
- Side-coaching cue: 'Keep the pulse alive! Don't let the ball drop between the Yes and the No.'
- Common Pitfall: Players anticipating their turn and speaking before the previous person has finished. Fix: Remind them to wait for the actual auditory cue before responding, practicing true active listening.
- Side-coaching cue: 'Commit fully to your choice. If you say No, say it with conviction and immediately look at the person you are sending it back to.'
- Common Pitfall: Hesitation when a 'No' is said, causing the rhythm to stall. Fix: Encourage players to embrace mistakes, laugh them off, and immediately restart the pulse without overthinking.

## Variations
- Blind Pass: Have all players close their eyes. They must rely entirely on auditory cues and spatial awareness to pass the 'Yes' and 'No' around the circle.
- Emotional Shift: Instruct the group to pass the 'Yes' and 'No' using a specific emotion (e.g., joyful, suspicious, terrified) which can change whenever a 'No' is spoken.
- Sound and Motion: Accompany the 'Yes' with a physical clap or gesture directed at the receiver, and the 'No' with a defensive block gesture.

## Debrief
- How did the rhythm change when we introduced the 'No' option, and how did you adapt to that uncertainty?
- What did it feel like when we closed our eyes? How did your listening shift?
- How does this game illustrate the concept of 'Group Mind' and supporting your partner's impulse?

## Safety & Inclusion
This game is highly accessible as it requires minimal physical movement. For players with visual impairments, the eyes-closed variation can actually level the playing field; ensure everyone speaks clearly and projects their voice so auditory cues are easy to track.

## Why It Works
By limiting the vocabulary to just two words ('Yes' and 'No'), the game strips away the cognitive load of inventing content. This allows players to focus entirely on timing, rhythm, and connection. The sudden reversal mechanic forces players out of anticipation and into a state of pure presence, building the collective reflex known as Group Mind.
