# The Negotiated Scene

> Build compelling scenes by pausing to explicitly negotiate physical and emotional boundaries in real time.

## Overview
A structured scene-work exercise where players temporarily step out of character to explicitly negotiate physical contact, intense emotional demands, or major relationship shifts. By normalizing brief, out-of-character check-ins, players learn to co-create narratives that respect personal boundaries while maintaining dramatic momentum. The resulting scenes are deeply grounded in mutual trust and authentic, high-stakes choices.

## What It Trains
- **Domain:** D2 — The Partner
- **Principle(s):** Consent & Boundaries; Yes, And; Truth Over Pandering
- **Skill(s):** Boundary Navigation; Active Listening; Offer Reception
- **Technique(s):** Check-ins; Cut calls; Negotiating physical contact; Yes, And… sentence games
- **Focus:** connection

**Objective:** To develop advanced boundary navigation and active listening skills by integrating explicit, out-of-character check-ins directly into the mechanical flow of a scene, prioritizing player safety and truth over people-pleasing.

## Setup
A clear performance space with two chairs. The remaining players sit in an audience arc to observe. No props or materials are required.

## How to Play
1. Two players step forward to begin a standard, character-driven scene based on a simple relationship or location suggestion.
2. At any point during the scene, if a player wants to initiate physical contact, make a heavy emotional demand, or introduce a major relationship shift, they must pause the action.
3. To pause, the initiating player steps out of character, makes direct eye contact with their partner, and asks an explicit check-in question (e.g., 'May I hug you?' or 'Is it okay if my character demands to know your secret?').
4. The receiving partner must respond honestly, blending their personal comfort with their character's perspective, using one of three options: 'Yes, and...', 'Yes, but...', or 'No, thank you.'
5. If the response is 'Yes, and...', the partner enthusiastically accepts, and the scene immediately resumes with the action integrated.
6. If the response is 'Yes, but...', the partner proposes a modification (e.g., 'Yes, but let's do a shoulder pat instead' or 'Yes, but my character will act resistant'), and they briefly negotiate before resuming play.
7. If the response is 'No, thank you' or 'I'm not comfortable with that,' the initiator must immediately accept the boundary without question or hesitation, and pivot the scene in a new creative direction.
8. At any point, any player or the facilitator can call a full 'Cut' to stop the scene entirely if a boundary is crossed or if genuine discomfort arises.

## Facilitation Notes
- Reframe 'No' as a Gift: Remind players that a boundary is not a block; it is a clear creative constraint that forces more interesting, authentic narrative choices.
- Normalize the Clunkiness: The transition between in-character play and out-of-character negotiation will feel awkward at first. Encourage players to embrace the pause rather than rushing through it.
- Monitor the Pivot: Watch the initiator after a 'No, thank you.' Ensure they do not punish the partner's character or whine about the boundary in-character; the pivot must be respectful and constructive.
- Model the Check-In: Run a quick, low-stakes demonstration with a volunteer before starting the full exercise to show how fast and simple the out-of-character check-in can be.

## Variations
- The Silent Signal: Instead of verbal check-ins, players use a specific non-verbal gesture (like an open palm) to signal a pending physical or emotional escalation, prompting a silent nod or shake of the head from their partner.
- The Multi-Player Contract: Run the exercise with three or four players in the scene, requiring the initiator to check in with all affected parties before executing a group action.

## Debrief
- How did it feel to step out of character to ask for permission, and did it disrupt your creative flow as much as you expected?
- For those who said 'No, thank you,' how did it feel to prioritize your personal comfort over the traditional improv instinct to always say yes?
- For those who received a 'No,' how did that boundary inspire a more creative or unexpected narrative pivot?
- How did this explicit negotiation process affect your overall sense of trust and safety with your scene partner?

## Safety & Inclusion
Establish a clear, universal 'Cut' signal before playing. Emphasize that personal physical and emotional safety always overrides the fiction of the scene. Ensure players know they can decline any physical contact or emotional theme without needing to explain or justify their decision.

## Why It Works
This game works because it dismantles the toxic misconception that 'Yes, And' requires absolute compliance with every physical or emotional offer. By formalizing the check-in as a mandatory game mechanic, it removes the social friction of setting boundaries, proving that explicit consent actually deepens trust and unlocks richer, more authentic dramatic choices.
