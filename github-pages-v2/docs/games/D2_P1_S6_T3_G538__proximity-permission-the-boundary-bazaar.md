# The Boundary Bazaar

> In-character negotiation meets physical boundaries to build trust, safety, and authentic scene work.

## Overview
A structured skill drill where players navigate physical proximity and contact by drawing scenario cards that demand close interaction. Players must explicitly negotiate physical boundaries in-character, balancing their character's truth with their personal comfort. The experience builds a shared vocabulary of consent, transforming boundary-setting from a scene-blocker into a rich narrative offer.

## What It Trains
- **Domain:** D2 — The Partner
- **Principle(s):** Consent & Boundaries; Truth Over Pandering
- **Skill(s):** Boundary Navigation; Active Listening; Offer Reception
- **Technique(s):** Check-ins; Cut calls; Negotiating physical contact
- **Focus:** skill_drill

**Objective:** To develop practical fluency in negotiating physical contact and setting boundaries in real-time. It trains players to prioritize authentic character comfort (Truth Over Pandering) and navigate physical proximity safely without stalling the scene's momentum.

## Setup
Arrange a moderate-sized playing space with a few physical props (two chairs, a blanket, a box, a rope) to suggest confined environments. Prepare a deck of 'Boundary Cards' containing scenarios that require physical closeness or contact.

## How to Play
1. Two players step into the performance space, and the facilitator establishes a basic relationship and setting.
2. One player draws a Boundary Card and reads the physical challenge aloud to the group.
3. Before acting, both players take a silent moment to determine their character's authentic comfort level with this physical challenge.
4. The initiating player makes an in-character, explicit verbal request for physical contact or proximity based on the card's prompt.
5. The receiving player responds in-character, either granting permission with specific parameters or setting a clear boundary based on their character's comfort.
6. If a boundary is set, the initiating player must actively navigate it by offering a creative compromise, alternative action, or respectful pivot that honors the boundary.
7. Throughout the scene, players use active listening and can initiate brief, in-character check-ins to ensure ongoing consent as physical dynamics shift.
8. At any point, any player or the facilitator can call Cut! to immediately halt the scene for personal safety or discomfort, which is met with supportive validation.

## Facilitation Notes
- Establish Psychological Safety First: Explicitly state before the drill that Cut is an absolute safety valve, and using it is a celebrated act of self-care, not a failure.
- Distinguish Player vs. Character: Coach players that while they as performers might be comfortable with contact, their character might not be. Honor the character's truth to drive the drama.
- Avoid Stilted Dialogue: Encourage players to weave consent and check-ins naturally into their character's voice rather than dropping character entirely, unless calling a personal Cut.
- Celebrate Boundaries as Offers: When a player says no or sets a limit, side-coach the partner to treat that limit as a gift that inspires creative, non-contact solutions.

## Variations
- Non-Verbal Cues: Play the same setup but negotiate boundaries entirely through physical spacing, eye contact, and breath, without explicit verbal asks.
- The Escalation Scale: Start with low-stakes physical proximity (sharing a small space) and gradually draw cards with higher physical contact requirements as trust builds.

## Debrief
- How did it feel to explicitly ask for physical permission while remaining in character?
- When a boundary was set, how did you pivot the scene without feeling like you blocked the momentum?
- What is the difference between your personal comfort as a player and your character's comfort, and how did you manage both?
- How did active listening help you read your partner's physical comfort before they even spoke?

## Safety & Inclusion
This game involves physical proximity and potential contact. Establish a clear, non-judgmental environment. Ensure the Cut rule is fully understood. Players must always have the right to opt out of any card or physical interaction without explanation. Physical props like ropes or boxes must be used safely to avoid tripping hazards.

## Why It Works
By gamifying the negotiation of physical space, it strips away the stigma of saying no in improv. It teaches that boundaries do not stop a scene; instead, they provide concrete parameters that force players to listen deeply, respect their partners, and find highly creative, authentic narrative paths.
