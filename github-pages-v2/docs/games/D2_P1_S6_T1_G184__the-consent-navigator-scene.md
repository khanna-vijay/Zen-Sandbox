# The Consent Navigator

> Real-time coaching to master physical boundaries, active check-ins, and authentic choices in every scene.

## Overview
A structured training drill where a dedicated third player acts as an active Navigator, pausing the scene to guide players through real-time boundary negotiations and check-ins. It transforms safety protocols from a pre-show lecture into an active, playful, and deeply integrated performance skill.

## What It Trains
- **Domain:** D2 — The Partner
- **Principle(s):** Consent & Boundaries; Truth Over Pandering
- **Skill(s):** Boundary Navigation; Active Listening
- **Technique(s):** Check-ins; Cut calls; Negotiating physical contact
- **Focus:** skill_drill

**Objective:** To build practical, in-the-moment skills for navigating physical and emotional boundaries, executing active check-ins, and prioritizing authentic personal comfort over the pressure to please the audience or partner.

## Setup
Three to five players stand in a moderate open space. Two players step forward to perform a scene, while one player is designated as the Consent Navigator. The remaining players observe.

## How to Play
1. Assign roles: two active players in the scene, one player as the Consent Navigator, and any remaining players as active observers.
2. Establish a simple, realistic scene premise that naturally invites physical proximity or emotional vulnerability.
3. Begin the scene. The players improvise normally, but must remain highly attuned to their own physical comfort and emotional boundaries.
4. The Consent Navigator actively monitors the scene, calling out 'Pause and Check' every 30 to 60 seconds, or whenever physical contact or high emotional intensity approaches.
5. Upon a 'Pause and Check', the scene freezes and the Navigator asks direct, non-judgmental questions to one or both players regarding their comfort, physical intentions, or authenticity.
6. The players must answer the Navigator with absolute honesty, prioritizing their actual comfort over keeping the fictional narrative going.
7. If a player expresses discomfort or a boundary, the Navigator guides both players to verbally negotiate an alternative choice before resuming the scene.
8. Any player, or the Navigator, can call 'Cut' at any point to stop the scene entirely if a boundary is crossed or if a player feels frozen.
9. Rotate roles after 3 to 5 minutes so every participant experiences being an actor, a Navigator, and an observer.

## Facilitation Notes
- Frame the 'Cut' as a Win: Explicitly instruct players that calling 'Cut' or expressing a boundary is a successful execution of the game, not a failure of the scene.
- Coach the Navigator's Tone: Ensure the Navigator asks questions neutrally and supportively, avoiding an accusatory tone.
- Combat Pandering: Watch for players who reflexively say 'I'm fine' with tense body language, and side-coach them to honor their actual feelings.
- Keep Negotiations Brief: Ensure the in-scene negotiations are direct and functional so the momentum of the exercise is maintained.

## Variations
- Silent Signals: Instead of verbal pauses, the Navigator uses simple hand gestures to prompt players to initiate their own verbal check-ins without the Navigator speaking.
- The Internal Navigator: Play the scene without an external Navigator; players must proactively pause the action themselves to check in and negotiate boundaries.

## Debrief
- How did it feel to have an external person pause the scene to ask about your physical and emotional comfort?
- Did you notice any moments where you felt internal pressure to agree to something uncomfortable just to keep the scene going?
- As the Navigator, what physical or non-verbal cues did you look for to decide when to intervene?
- How can we carry this practice of explicit, low-stakes negotiation into our regular, unmoderated scene work?

## Safety & Inclusion
Establish a hard stop signal before playing. Because this game explicitly deals with physical boundaries and consent, players must have absolute agency to opt out of any physical contact or topic without explanation.

## Why It Works
By externalizing the internal process of boundary assessment, this game acts as a cognitive scaffold. It slows down the rapid-fire decision-making of improv, giving players the physical and mental space to practice active consent, transforming boundary navigation from an emergency reaction into a proactive, collaborative tool.
