# Hats

> Justify every physical reach and defensive dodge while wearing hats in a grounded scene.

## Overview
Two players wear physical hats and perform a grounded, realistic scene. The underlying tension is that either player can end the scene by successfully grabbing the other's hat, but every single reach, flinch, or defensive block must be instantly justified within the reality of the scene. This creates a high-stakes environment where physical comedy meets rigorous narrative logic.

## What It Trains
- **Domain:** D3 — The Scene
- **Principle(s):** Base Reality First; Make Your Partner a Genius
- **Skill(s):** Justification; Active Listening; Offer Reception; Physicality & Space Work
- **Technique(s):** Justify the absurd; Object work; Endowment-acceptance
- **Focus:** mixed

**Objective:** To develop the skill of justification (specifically justifying absurd or sudden physical movements) while maintaining a strong base reality and active listening.

## Setup
Two players stand in the performance space, each wearing a physical hat (ideally of similar size and secure fit). No other props are needed. The facilitator establishes a simple, grounded suggestion for a relationship and location.

## How to Play
1. Two players put on hats and step into the scene, establishing a grounded, realistic relationship and environment.
2. The scene proceeds normally, but with an underlying high-stakes rule: if one player successfully grabs the other's hat, the scene immediately ends.
3. A player may attempt to grab the other's hat at any point, but the movement must be integrated naturally into their character's actions or justified immediately after.
4. If a player flinches, ducks, blocks, or makes any defensive movement to protect their hat, they must instantly justify that physical reaction within the context of the scene.
5. If a player attempts a grab and misses, or if a grab is successfully blocked, the scene continues, but both players must immediately justify the physical interaction.
6. The scene ends instantly the moment one player successfully removes the other's hat from their head.

## Facilitation Notes
- Side-coach players to focus on the base reality first. If they immediately go for the hats, the scene loses its comedic and dramatic tension.
- Watch out for 'ghost grabs' where players swipe without any narrative reason. Remind them: 'Every reach must have a story.'
- If a player flinches defensively without a reason, pause and ask them to justify it: 'Why did you duck just now?'
- Ensure players do not get overly aggressive or rough. The physical contact should be light and focused on the hats, not tackling or wrestling.

## Variations
- The Grace Period: Introduce a mandatory 30-second window at the start of the scene where no hat grabs are allowed, forcing players to establish a deep relationship and environment first.
- The Emotional Hat: Each hat represents a specific emotion. If your hat is touched or threatened, your emotional intensity must spike.

## Debrief
- How did the physical threat of the hat grab affect your commitment to the base reality?
- What were some of the most creative justifications you used to explain a sudden reach or flinch?
- How does justifying an accidental or sudden movement make the scene feel more cohesive and realistic?

## Safety & Inclusion
Since this game involves physical reaching and potential contact near the face/head, establish clear boundaries before playing. Players must agree to keep physical contact light, avoid aggressive swiping, and respect personal space. If a player has neck or mobility issues, they can play with a 'virtual' hat or a shoulder-tap alternative.

## Why It Works
By linking a high-stakes physical objective (grabbing the hat) with the strict rule of justification, players learn to treat every physical action as a meaningful offer. It prevents mindless physical comedy by forcing players to ground absurd movements in a logical, character-driven reality, reinforcing the principle of making your partner look like a genius.
