# The Affliction

> Detect a secret physical symptom and spread the cure through collective, synchronized movement.

## Overview
In this subtle ensemble warm-up, players mingle with secret roles: one is 'afflicted' with a physical tic, while the rest hold the 'cure.' Through keen observation and peripheral awareness, players must identify the symptom and mirror it, gradually bringing the entire group into physical unison.

## What It Trains
- **Domain:** D4 — The Ensemble
- **Principle(s):** Group Mind; Make Your Partner a Genius
- **Skill(s):** Peripheral Awareness; Active Listening
- **Focus:** connection

**Objective:** To develop peripheral awareness, active listening, and group mind by tuning into subtle physical shifts in the room and responding collaboratively without explicit verbal direction.

## Setup
Prepare small slips of paper equal to the number of players. Write 'Afflicted' on one slip and 'Cure' on all the others. Distribute them face-down. Players look at their slips privately and stand in a loose circle in a clear room.

## How to Play
1. Distribute the secret slips of paper so that exactly one player is 'Afflicted' and everyone else is a 'Cure'.
2. Instruct players to begin walking around the space at a moderate pace, making soft eye contact with others as they pass.
3. The 'Afflicted' player must introduce a very subtle, repetitive physical movement (e.g., a light collar-tug, a specific head tilt, or a double-blink) while continuing to walk.
4. The 'Cure' players must use their peripheral vision and active listening to detect who is carrying the symptom without staring directly or pointing.
5. When a 'Cure' player spots the symptom, they must subtly adopt the exact same physical movement themselves, 'absorbing' and validating the behavior.
6. As more players notice the movement spreading, they also adopt it, creating a chain reaction of physical mirroring.
7. The game concludes when the entire group is moving in perfect, silent unison, having successfully identified and integrated the affliction into a shared group state.

## Facilitation Notes
- Coaching cue: 'Keep your eyes soft. Don't hunt for the symptom; let it enter your field of vision naturally.'
- Pitfall: The Afflicted player makes their symptom too obvious (e.g., loud coughing or dramatic flailing). Fix: Remind them that the game is about subtlety and high-level awareness, not comedy.
- Coaching cue: 'Once you adopt the movement, try to synchronize your timing with the people around you to build a single, shared rhythm.'
- Pitfall: Players immediately call out the Afflicted player verbally. Fix: Emphasize that this is a non-verbal exercise of physical alignment, not a detective game of accusation.

## Variations
- Reverse Affliction: One player is the 'Cure' and everyone else is 'Afflicted' with the same symptom. The lone 'Cure' must gradually calm the room down by introducing a grounding movement.
- Vocal Affliction: Instead of a physical movement, the symptom is a subtle vocal sound or sigh, requiring deep auditory peripheral awareness.
- Multi-Symptom: In larger groups, have two 'Afflicted' players with different symptoms. The group must eventually merge the two movements into a single hybrid gesture.

## Debrief
- How did it feel to rely on your peripheral vision rather than direct focus to spot the symptom?
- What did it take to transition from individual movement to a synchronized group rhythm?
- How does this level of physical sensitivity translate to supporting your scene partners on stage?

## Safety & Inclusion
Ensure players are mindful of physical boundaries while walking. If any player has mobility limitations, encourage symptoms that are easily adaptable for everyone (e.g., facial expressions, breathing patterns, or small hand gestures) so that full group unison remains accessible.

## Why It Works
By removing verbal communication, players are forced to rely entirely on visual and physical cues. Mirroring a partner's subtle movement builds immediate empathy and connection, embodying the 'Make Your Partner a Genius' principle. The gradual spread of the movement creates a tangible, felt experience of Group Mind.
