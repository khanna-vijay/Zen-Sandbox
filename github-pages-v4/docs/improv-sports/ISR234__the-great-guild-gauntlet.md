# The Great Guild Gauntlet

![The Great Guild Gauntlet — infographic](img/ISR234.jpg){ .game-hero }

> Teams act as adventuring parties running a timed 3-minute quest to retrieve a magical MacGuffin while a Dungeon Master throws dynamic hazards their way.

## Overview
A fast-paced, competitive short-form game where teams act as adventuring parties. Guided by the Referee acting as a 'Dungeon Master' (DM), each team runs their own separate, timed 3-minute quest to retrieve a magical MacGuffin. The DM dynamically throws environmental hazards, eccentric NPCs, and boss monsters at them, forcing rapid-fire object work, character commitment, and teamwork.

## Setup
Requires two teams of 3 to 4 players. The Referee acts as the Dungeon Master (DM) and controls a stopwatch. The stage is bare, relying entirely on the players' pantomime and the DM's narration. Get a 'Fantastical Location' and a 'Magical MacGuffin' from the audience for Team A, and a new set of suggestions for Team B.

## How to Play
1. The Briefing: The DM gets a location (e.g., 'The Whispering Woods') and a MacGuffin (e.g., 'The Giggle-Spark Gem') from the audience for Team A.
2. The Party: Team A steps up. They will play the exact same characters for their entire 3-minute run, acting as a single, cohesive adventuring party.
3. The Gauntlet Begins: The DM starts the 3-minute clock and sets the scene. The players enter the environment and establish their characters and relationships.
4. Organic Pacing (Beginning, Middle, End): Instead of rigid stages, the DM dynamically paces the 3 minutes. The DM should introduce an environmental obstacle in the first minute, a strange NPC or puzzle in the second minute, and the Guardian of the MacGuffin in the final minute.
5. DM Interventions: The DM can freeze the action at any time by shouting 'Freeze!' to introduce sudden traps, mutate the environment, or demand a 'Skill Check' (e.g., 'Freeze! The floor turns to jelly. Roll for agility by showing us your best jelly-walk!').
6. Resolution: The team must work together, using the clues and items they've gathered, to defeat or bypass the Guardian and retrieve the MacGuffin before time runs out.
7. Team B's Turn: Once Team A finishes (or time expires), Team B gets a completely new location and MacGuffin from the audience and runs their own separate 3-minute Gauntlet.
8. Scoring: At the end of both Gauntlets, the audience votes by applause for the most entertaining quest, awarding 5 points to the winner.

## Coaching Notes
- Dynamic DM Pacing: The Referee actively side-coaches and controls the narrative flow, ensuring the scene has a clear beginning, middle, and climax within the time limit.
- Continuous Characters: Players maintain their specific fantasy archetypes throughout the entire run, allowing for running jokes and character development.
- The 'Empty Pack' Foul: Actively enforces strong object work. If a player holds a heavy broadsword like a feather, or forgets where a door was, the DM calls this foul and deducts 1 point.
- The 'Muddled Map' Foul: Enforces active listening. If players ignore a clue given by an NPC or drop the narrative thread, the DM calls this foul and deducts 1 point.
- Bonus Points: The DM can award a 1-point bonus for exceptional teamwork.

## Variations
- Mirror Dungeon: Both teams receive the exact same location and MacGuffin. Team B leaves the room (or wears soundproof headphones) during Team A's turn. The fun comes from watching how wildly different the two teams handle the exact same DM prompts.
- Audience Inventory: Before the clock starts, the DM asks the audience for three mundane objects (e.g., a spatula, a rubber duck, a stapler). The team must logically (or hilariously) use all three objects to defeat the final Guardian.

## Why It Works
The game develops rapid-fire object work, character commitment, and teamwork by forcing players to adapt to the DM's dynamic pacing and sudden interventions while maintaining a cohesive narrative.

## Safety & Inclusion
Physical safety is paramount during 'combat' scenes; all fighting must be purely pantomimed from a safe distance with no actual striking, lifting, or weight-bearing on other players. Ensure fantasy tropes avoid real-world cultural stereotyping. If a player is 'injured' in the game, they can magically heal or tap out to avoid playing out graphic or uncomfortable physical trauma.
