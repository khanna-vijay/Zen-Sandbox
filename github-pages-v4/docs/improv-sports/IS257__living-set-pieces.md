# Living Set Pieces

![Living Set Pieces — infographic](img/IS257.jpg){ .game-hero }

> Teams must creatively and seamlessly integrate designated audience members, who are embodying specific environmental elements, into their improvised narrative.

## Overview
Living Set Pieces is an improv game where teams must incorporate designated audience volunteers, acting as non-verbal environmental elements, into their scene. Players must actively discover these volunteers, endow them with a role, and seamlessly adapt their narrative to the volunteers' actual, often unexpected, responses. Teams earn points for creative integration and quick-witted adaptability to create a cohesive, funny story.

## Setup
The referee asks the audience for a single scene location and selects 3-5 enthusiastic audience volunteers to come to the front of the stage. The referee discreetly gives each volunteer a small, secret role (a simple, non-verbal action or sound representing a prop or environmental element, like a 'creaky door' or 'grumpy old cat'). Volunteers are instructed to only perform their assigned action/sound when directly prompted or clearly implied by a player, and then position themselves at the sides or back of the stage.

## How to Play
1. Two players from each team (or a designated number) step forward.
2. The referee announces the agreed-upon scene location and potentially a simple relationship or situation to kick things off.
3. Players initiate the scene, with the primary goal of identifying and incorporating at least two 'Living Set Pieces' into their portion of the scene.
4. A player approaches an audience volunteer, physically acknowledges them (e.g., points, mimes interaction), and endows them with a role that they think fits.
5. The audience volunteer responds with their secret, pre-assigned action or sound.
6. The player must immediately react and integrate this actual action into the scene, even if it contradicts their initial endowment.
7. Teams alternate turns, usually after a set time (e.g., 90 seconds to 2 minutes) or a 'Freeze!' call from the referee, continuing to build the scene and discover more pieces.

## Coaching Notes
- Pre-game Setup is crucial: Brief the volunteers on their limited, non-verbal participation to effectively manage audience input.
- Call a 'Neglect Foul' if a team consistently fails to attempt interaction with or integrate any Living Set Pieces into their scene.
- Call a 'Misuse Foul' if a player tries to force an audience member to do something drastically outside their assigned role, or completely ignores the actual action/sound performed.
- Call an 'Audience Ad-Lib Foul' if an audience volunteer steps outside their pre-assigned role with dialogue or excessive action, gently reminding them of their specific role.
- Award points for successful integration, creative discovery of the actual secret role, hilarious logical reactions, and seamless 'Yes, And' responses.
- Players must listen not only to their scene partners but also intently to the unexpected responses of the Living Set Pieces.

## Why It Works
It strongly tests 'Yes, And' skills, active listening, and adaptability, as players must immediately accept and build upon the actual action or sound provided by the audience volunteer, even if it contradicts their initial endowment. It also reinforces object work and scene building while managing external, unpredictable elements.

## Safety & Inclusion
The assigned roles are always innocuous objects or sounds, preventing potential for inappropriate contributions. The referee enforces a content foul for blue humor, swearing, or innuendo, applying it to players and using it as a gentle reminder for audience volunteers if they deviate from their family-friendly role.
