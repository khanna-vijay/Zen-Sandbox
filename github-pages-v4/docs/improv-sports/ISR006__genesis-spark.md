# Genesis Spark

![Genesis Spark — infographic](img/ISR006.jpg){ .game-hero }

> A team of improvisers acts out the epic, multi-era historical origin story of a mundane everyday object.

## Overview
In this competitive short-form game, a team of improvisers acts out the epic, multi-era historical origin story of a mundane everyday object. Moving away from static storytelling, players perform a series of fast-paced historical vignettes guided by a team Narrator, showing how the object evolved from a crude prototype to its modern form.

## Setup
Format: Competitive Short-Form. Staging: Narrator downstage corner, Actors center stage. Suggestion: A mundane, everyday object (e.g., a spatula, a paperclip).

## How to Play
1. The Referee gets a suggestion of a mundane, everyday object from the audience.
2. The playing team takes the stage. One player acts as the Narrator downstage, while the other three are the Actors upstage.
3. The team has exactly 3 minutes to tell the object's origin story across three distinct historical eras (e.g., Prehistoric, Medieval, Industrial Revolution).
4. The Narrator begins by setting the scene in the first era. The Actors immediately launch into a fully acted vignette showing the discovery or initial need for the object.
5. To transition, the Narrator calls out a time jump (e.g., 'Centuries later, during the Renaissance...'). The Actors must instantly freeze, clear, and initiate the new era.
6. SHOW, DON'T TELL: Players must prove they are listening by seamlessly evolving the object physically. The prototype established in Era 1 must be physically carried over and upgraded in Era 2, without any verbal recapping of the previous scene.
7. The game concludes when the final era perfectly (and absurdly) explains the object's modern form, name, and use before the 3-minute buzzer.
8. The Referee runs the clock and enforces clean play. After both teams have performed their 3-minute origin stories, the audience votes by applause to determine the winner.

## Coaching Notes
- Ensure the game relies on acted historical vignettes instead of a static line-relay.
- Focus on the physical 'show, don't tell' evolution of the object.
- Maintain player-driven pacing with the dedicated Narrator managing the high-energy, ticking-clock structure.
- The Referee may award bonus points for seamless physical evolution of the object between eras.

## Variations
- Director's Cut: For advanced teams, the Referee CAN interrupt to shout out the next historical era or genre (e.g., 'Cut to the Wild West!'), forcing the team to adapt instantly.
- Prop Box Genesis: The team is handed a random, bizarre physical prop. They must use this actual prop as the prototype and physically manipulate it differently in each era to explain the suggested object.

## Why It Works
It develops physical object work, active listening, and narrative pacing by forcing players to physically evolve an object across distinct eras using a 'show, don't tell' mechanic.

## Safety & Inclusion
Enforce a clean-content foul for any crude, blue, or inappropriate content to maintain an all-ages environment. When playing with historical eras, players must focus on time-period tropes (e.g., knights, cowboys, astronauts) and strictly avoid cultural, racial, or harmful stereotypes. Physical safety is paramount during fast transitions; actors must maintain spatial awareness.
