# Sonic Storytellers

![Sonic Storytellers — infographic](img/ISR243.jpg){ .game-hero }

> A fast-paced competitive scene interrupted by a Referee demanding instant vocal sound effects and seamless narrative justifications.

## Overview
In this fast-paced competitive short-form game, two teams collaborate on a shared scene that is frequently interrupted by the Referee's whistle. Whenever the whistle blows, the Referee shouts out a sound effect from a list pre-gathered from the audience. The active player must instantly perform that sound vocally and seamlessly justify its existence in the reality of the scene.

## Setup
Four players are on stage (two from the Red Team, two from the Blue Team). The Referee stands downstage center or to the side, equipped with a whistle and a notepad or clipboard. No physical props are used. The game is played in a standard open-scene format.

## How to Play
1. The Gather: Before the scene begins, the Referee asks the audience for a location to ground the scene. Then, the Referee rapid-fires the audience to collect 6 to 8 specific, challenging sound effects (e.g., chainsaw, dial-up modem, angry dolphin, laser beam) and writes them down on their notepad.
2. The Scene: The four players begin a standard, fully acted scene based on the audience's location suggestion. They establish characters, relationships, and physical action.
3. The Interruption: At any point during the scene, the Referee blows a loud, distinct whistle and immediately shouts one of the pre-gathered sounds from their list.
4. The Execution: The player who was currently speaking, or who is in the middle of a physical action, must immediately make that sound effect using their voice and body.
5. The Justification: Immediately after making the sound, that player must justify why the sound just happened within the reality of the scene (e.g., making a dial-up modem sound, then saying, 'I told you not to buy a smart-fridge from 1998!').
6. The Resumption: The scene continues seamlessly from that new reality until the Referee blows the whistle again. The game ends when the list of sounds is exhausted or a natural scene conclusion is reached.
7. Scoring: The Referee awards points on the fly: 1 point to a team for a recognizable, committed vocal sound, and 2 points for a brilliant, seamless narrative justification.
8. Fouls: The Referee can call fouls, awarding 1 point to the opposing team: 'Content Foul' for inappropriate content, 'Hesitation Foul' for stalling before making the sound, or 'Narrative Noise Foul' for making the sound but failing to justify it in the scene.

## Coaching Notes
- Pre-gathered sound list eliminates mid-game dead time and keeps energy high.
- Shifting from a one-word story to a full scene allows the narrative to survive frequent interruptions.
- Perfectly fits a competitive short-form match format with clear referee interventions, points, and fouls.
- Requires zero props, relying entirely on vocal Foley and physical mime.
- The audience's laughter and cheers help the Referee gauge the success of the justifications.

## Variations
- Sonic Relay (Story Format): Instead of a scene, the four players stand in a line and tell a story one sentence at a time. When the whistle blows, the active storyteller must make the called-out sound and incorporate it into their current sentence.
- Blind Sounds: Instead of the Referee reading from a list, the Referee points to a specific audience member when the whistle blows. The audience member makes a random noise, and the active player must mimic the noise exactly and then justify it.

## Why It Works
It tests vocal dexterity, rapid justification, and the ability to maintain scene momentum under chaotic constraints.

## Safety & Inclusion
The Referee must vet the initial list of sounds to ensure they are family-friendly and safe to perform. Players should be reminded not to strain their vocal cords on harsh or screaming sounds; a 'Safety Foul' can be playfully called by the Referee if a player is pushing their voice to a dangerous limit. Physical boundaries must be respected during scene work, especially when reacting to sudden 'dangerous' sounds like explosions or animal attacks.
