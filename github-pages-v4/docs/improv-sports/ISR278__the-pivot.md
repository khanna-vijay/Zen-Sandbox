# The Pivot

![The Pivot — infographic](img/ISR278.jpg){ .game-hero }

> A scene is interrupted exactly once to radically recontextualize a recent line or action, forcing players to instantly justify the new reality.

## Overview
A facilitator-led rehearsal exercise where a scene is interrupted exactly once to radically recontextualize a recent line or action. The facilitator provides a new, game-changing fact about what just happened. Players must instantly justify this new reality through immediate emotional and physical reactions rather than exposition.

## Setup
2 to 3 players and 1 facilitator. The facilitator gets a simple suggestion for a location or relationship to start the scene. No props are needed.

## How to Play
1. Players begin a grounded, realistic scene based on the suggestion, establishing their characters and environment.
2. Around the 60 to 90-second mark, the facilitator calls 'Freeze!'
3. The facilitator identifies one specific line or action that just occurred in the scene.
4. The facilitator provides a single, massive new fact that completely recontextualizes that line or action. For example, if a player just said 'I brought the paperwork,' the facilitator might say, 'The paperwork is actually a confession to a bank robbery.'
5. The facilitator calls 'Unfreeze!'
6. Players instantly resume the scene, accepting the new reality. Crucially, they must not explain the shift with clunky exposition like 'As you know, this is a confession.' Instead, they must show the new truth through immediate shifts in emotion, status, and physical action, such as suddenly sweating, closing the blinds, and whispering.
7. The scene continues for exactly one more minute to explore the fallout of this single pivot before the facilitator calls 'Scene.' Do not add a second pivot.

## Coaching Notes
- Ensure players do not resort to talking-head exposition; the 'win' is seamlessly and silently justifying the shift without breaking the reality of the scene.
- The facilitator must practice active listening, pulling the pivot directly from the players' generated dialogue or action.
- Push players out of their heads and into physical and emotional reactions.
- Strictly limit the exercise to one pivot per scene. One is enough to challenge the players without causing narrative collapse or descending into chaos.

## Variations
- Internal Monologue Pivot: Instead of the facilitator giving the new fact, the facilitator taps a player on the shoulder. That player speaks a one-sentence internal monologue revealing their true hidden motive for the last action, then the scene resumes.
- Genre Pivot: The facilitator recontextualizes the action by shifting the genre. For example, 'When you opened that umbrella, we are now in a sci-fi thriller and that umbrella is a plasma shield.'

## Why It Works
It practices extreme justification and 'Yes, And' under pressure, testing active listening and forcing players out of talking heads into immediate physical and emotional reactions.

## Safety & Inclusion
Because the pivot is imposed externally, the facilitator has total control over the content's safety. The facilitator must ensure the provided 'new facts' remain within the group's safety boundaries and avoid trauma-triggering tropes. A 'massive pivot' should mean a fun, dramatic shift (like turning a mundane office into a heist planning session), not an inappropriate or unsafe one. Keep all prompts clean and accessible.
