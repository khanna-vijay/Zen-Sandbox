# Relationship Current

![Relationship Current — infographic](img/IS138.jpg){ .game-hero }

> Pass an imaginary ball imbued with a clear attitude to spark instant, visceral reactions and shifting relational dynamics.

## Overview
Relationship Current is an adaptation of a simple circle pass-and-catch game that transforms into an exercise in embodying and exchanging relational dynamics. Players pass an imaginary ball, imbuing the throw with a specific attitude, and the receiver must instinctively react to this incoming current before transforming it and passing it on. This continuous flow fosters emotional spontaneity and deepens ensemble presence.

## Setup
Players stand in a large circle. The facilitator starts with an imaginary ball and explains that the ball is not just an object, but carries an invisible thread of relationship.

## How to Play
1. The first player receives the imaginary ball and chooses another player in the circle to throw it to.
2. As the player throws the ball, they must imbue the entire action of the throw (gaze, posture, energy, trajectory, and weight) with a single, clear, and felt attitude or relationship towards the receiver (e.g., angrily, lovingly, hesitantly, seductively).
3. The receiving player must immediately physically and vocally react to the incoming relationship conveyed by the pass, offering an authentic, intuitive response to what was just offered.
4. Once the player has received and reacted, they transform that relationship into a new or modified one.
5. They immediately throw the ball to a different player in the circle, imbuing their throw with this new relationship.
6. The game continues with the current of relationships flowing and transforming between players without breaks, unless the relationship becomes unclear or drops.

## Coaching Notes
- Point of Concentration: Let the relationship you hold for the receiver entirely shape the trajectory, weight, and timing of your throw, and let the incoming relationship entirely shape your reception.
- Ensure the throw physically manifests the relationship. For example, an angry throw might be fast and hard with a narrowed gaze, while a loving throw might be soft and floaty.
- Offer non-judgmental prompts if a player becomes intellectual or unclear, such as: 'What is the relationship now?', 'What is happening between you and them?', or 'Trust your first impulse.'
- Prioritize clear, felt impulses over 'good acting.' The fast pace prevents overthinking and fosters spontaneous, visceral responses.

## Variations
- Use a real, soft beanbag or ball for initial focus during the first few rounds, then remove it to transition to an imaginary ball once the group understands the mechanic.

## Why It Works
It develops emotional responsiveness and the physical embodiment of intangible relationships. By focusing on the 'how' of the pass, it prevents intellectualization, forces active listening of non-verbal cues, and builds a cohesive ensemble through continuous, play-based problem-solving.

## Safety & Inclusion
Encourage players to respect physical and emotional boundaries. When exploring intense emotions like anger or fear, ensure the physical actions and imaginary throws remain safe, playful, and do not cross into genuine aggression.
