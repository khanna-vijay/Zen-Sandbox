# Status Quake

![Status Quake — infographic](img/ISR293.jpg){ .game-hero }

> Players must instantly justify sudden plot twists and status shifts injected by a Director mid-scene.

## Overview
A judged narrative scene where the Director abruptly calls 'Quake!' to inject a sudden plot twist or status shift. Players must instantly justify the new reality, seamlessly weaving it into the ongoing story without losing their emotional commitment or the scene's base reality.

## Setup
2 to 3 players and a Director (or Judge). The Director stands offstage with a list of 'Quake Prompts' divided into two distinct categories: Plot Twists and Status Shifts. The host gets a suggestion of a mundane location and an everyday relationship from the audience.

## How to Play
1. Players begin a grounded, realistic scene based on the audience suggestion, focusing on establishing their characters, relationship, and base reality.
2. Around the 1-minute mark, the Director calls 'Quake!' and delivers EITHER a Plot Twist OR a Status Shift (e.g., 'Quake! Status Shift: Player A now desperately needs Player B's approval.').
3. Players must instantly accept and justify this shift, treating it as a natural, albeit surprising, evolution of the scene rather than a random gag. They must not drop their characters.
4. Around the 2.5-minute mark, the Director calls a second and final 'Quake!' using the opposite category. If they used a Status Shift first, they must now use a Plot Twist (e.g., 'Quake! Plot Twist: You realize the room is slowly filling with water.').
5. Players justify this final complication, using it to drive the scene to a climax and a satisfying narrative resolution by the 4-minute mark.
6. In a competitive short-form match, a panel of Judges awards 1 to 5 points based on narrative continuity, the emotional justification of the Quakes, and how well the players maintained the scene's reality without resorting to cheap jokes.

## Coaching Notes
- Push for radical acceptance and immediate justification of the Quakes.
- Encourage dynamic status play and emotional agility rather than just physical reactions.
- Remind players to maintain narrative resilience under pressure; they must not drop their characters or resort to cheap jokes.
- Directors must maintain a clear separation of plot and status mechanics when delivering the Quake prompts.

## Variations
- Audience Quakes: The audience writes the Quakes on slips of paper before the show, categorized into plot or status buckets, which the Director draws blindly.
- The Epicenter (Long-Form Drill): Play a 3-scene runner. After Scene 1, the audience votes on the most significant 'Turning Point' of the scene. Scene 2 begins in a new location but must build directly upon that turning point, with new Quakes introduced.

## Why It Works
It forces players to practice radical acceptance and immediate justification, building narrative resilience and emotional agility by challenging them to weave jarring twists into a grounded base reality.

## Safety & Inclusion
Explicitly avoid forced 'subservient', 'pet', or master/servant dynamics, which can easily cross personal boundaries and create unsafe power dynamics on stage. Status shifts should focus on emotional, social, or professional leverage (e.g., 'You realize they are your childhood hero,' or 'You desperately need them to co-sign a loan'). Players always retain the right to physically distance themselves, and the Director should immediately edit the scene if a prompt pushes a player into an uncomfortable physical or emotional space.
