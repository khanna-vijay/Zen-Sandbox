# Echo Chamber

![Echo Chamber — infographic](img/ISR075.jpg){ .game-hero }

> Players re-perform their immediately preceding line or action with a new, subtle behavioral filter layered on top.

## Overview
A facilitator-led training game where players re-perform their immediately preceding line or action with a new, subtle behavioral filter layered on top. It trains actors to justify sudden shifts in behavior, maintain physical control, and play complex subtext without breaking the scene's reality.

## Setup
2 players on stage, 1 facilitator. The rest of the ensemble acts as the audience/suggesters. No props are needed.

## How to Play
1. Two players initiate a grounded, realistic scene based on a simple suggestion (e.g., a location or relationship).
2. The facilitator calls 'Echo!' immediately after a player delivers a line or performs a distinct physical action.
3. The facilitator (or the observing ensemble) instantly provides a specific, behavioral 'filter' (e.g., 'as if you just remembered you left the oven on,' 'trying to discreetly scratch an itch,' or 'like you are about to sneeze').
4. The player who just acted must immediately re-perform their exact last line or action, incorporating the new filter. This acts as a 'take two' - the scene's timeline resets to that exact moment, replacing the original delivery.
5. The scene continues from this new reality. The scene partner must accept and react to this new, filtered delivery, actively justifying why the character is suddenly behaving this way within the context of the scene.
6. The facilitator can call 'Echo!' multiple times throughout the scene. If a player receives a second filter later in the scene, they must layer it on top of their first one (e.g., scratching an itch AND remembering the oven is on).
7. To prevent cognitive overload, players are limited to a maximum of TWO active filters. If a player receives a third filter, they drop their oldest one and keep the two most recent.
8. The scene concludes when the facilitator calls 'Scene!', usually after both players are juggling one or two filters and have successfully justified the resulting absurdity.

## Coaching Notes
- The facilitator acts as a side-coach, ensuring filters remain subtle behaviors or physicalities rather than sweeping genre changes.
- Encourage the scene partner to actively justify the bizarre shifts.
- Focus on subtle, behavioral subtext rather than broad, sweeping genre changes.
- Embrace the high potential for physical comedy as players juggle conflicting physical instructions.

## Variations
- Internal Monologue Echo: Instead of physical behaviors, the filters are secret internal thoughts (e.g., 'You secretly think they are an idiot' or 'You are desperately in love with them').
- Physical Isolation: The filters only apply to specific body parts (e.g., 'Your left arm is extremely heavy' or 'Your eyebrows are terrified').

## Why It Works
It trains emotional layering and physical control without breaking character, forcing players to actively justify bizarre, sudden shifts in behavior within the scene's reality.

## Safety & Inclusion
Ensure physical filters do not require players to perform actions that cause pain or discomfort. If a filter is too physically demanding or crosses a personal boundary, the player can simply drop it or the facilitator can instantly swap it out. Keep filters focused on everyday behaviors and minor physical quirks; strictly prohibit filters that mimic disabilities, neurodivergence, or medical conditions.
