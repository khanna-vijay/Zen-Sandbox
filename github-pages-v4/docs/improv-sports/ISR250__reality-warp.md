# Reality Warp

![Reality Warp — infographic](img/ISR250.jpg){ .game-hero }

> Two teams compete to build a single, continuously escalating scene by seamlessly justifying bizarre, pre-gathered audience suggestions called 'Warps.'

## Overview
Two teams compete to build a single, continuously escalating scene by seamlessly justifying bizarre, pre-gathered audience suggestions called 'Warps.' Guided by a referee who injects these Warps at 30-to-45-second intervals, players must instantly 'Yes, And' the impossible into their established reality. When the referee calls 'Freeze,' the opposing team tags in, inheriting the characters, the setting, and all the chaotic Warps that came before.

## Setup
Two teams (e.g., Red and Blue) stand on opposite wings. The Referee stands center or downstage with a clipboard or whiteboard. Before the game begins, the Referee asks the audience for a base Location and Relationship, plus a rapid-fire list of 4 to 6 'Warps' (e.g., a random object, a bizarre physical trait, a strange environmental change). The Referee writes these down so the scene won't need to stop later.

## How to Play
1. 1. The Kickoff: Two players from Team A take the stage and begin a scene based on the base Location and Relationship. They play a grounded scene for 30 to 45 seconds to establish a strong foundation.
2. 2. The First Warp: The Referee blows the whistle, yells 'WARP!', and reads the first pre-gathered suggestion from their list (e.g., 'The floor is now made of marshmallows!').
3. 3. Immediate Justification: Team A must instantly accept this new reality, physically react to it, and verbally justify why it makes perfect sense in their scene, without breaking character.
4. 4. Breathing Room: The scene continues for another 30 to 45 seconds, allowing the players to explore the new dynamic and find the comedy in the justification.
5. 5. The Second Warp: The Referee calls a second Warp from the list. Team A integrates this new element while maintaining the first Warp. (Limit to 2 Warps per team to prevent scene clutter and cognitive overload).
6. 6. Tag and Inherit: The Referee yells 'FREEZE!' Team A exits. Two players from Team B immediately take their exact physical positions. Team B must continue the exact same scene, playing the same characters, and maintaining all previously established Warps.
7. 7. Team B's Turn: Team B plays for 30 to 45 seconds before the Referee hits them with Warp 3, and later Warp 4, following the same pattern.
8. 8. Conclusion: The game ends after each team has done one or two rotations. The Referee awards final points and declares a winner.

## Coaching Notes
- The Referee awards 1 to 3 points for immediate, creative justification of a Warp.
- A 'Reality Rift' foul (-2 points) is called if players stall, ignore a Warp, or drop a previously established Warp after a tag-in.
- Standard competitive short-form match fouls (content foul for inappropriate content, groaner for bad puns) apply.
- Ensure the 30-to-45-second intervals are respected to allow the scene to breathe and the comedy to land before throwing the next Warp.

## Variations
- Blind Warps: Instead of gathering suggestions out loud, the Referee pulls pre-written audience suggestions from a bucket, making the Warps a surprise to everyone.
- Genre Warp: Instead of objects or endowments, the Warps are sudden shifts in film, television, or theatrical genres (e.g., 'Film Noir', 'children's educational puppet show').

## Why It Works
It forces immediate justification and strong 'Yes, And' under pressure. The tag-out and inherit mechanic heavily tests active listening and memory, while pre-gathered suggestions maintain high-energy pacing without stopping the scene.

## Safety & Inclusion
The Referee must actively curate the upfront list of Warps, filtering out any suggestions that rely on offensive tropes, unsafe physical actions, or non-family-friendly content. Players should adapt physical endowments (like 'walks on hands') to match their own mobility and comfort levels, focusing on the character choice rather than physical strain.
