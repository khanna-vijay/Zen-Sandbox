# Plot Patrol

![Plot Patrol — infographic](img/ISR064.jpg){ .game-hero }

> When players fall into common improv traps, the facilitator assigns bizarre physical tasks they must instantly justify while continuing the scene.

## Overview
Plot Patrol is a facilitator-led workshop exercise where an ensemble builds a continuous story, but with a twist: when players fall into common improv traps ( like standing around as 'talking heads' or stalling the plot), the facilitator doesn't stop the scene or deduct points. Instead, they call out the trap and instantly assign a bizarre, active physical task. The player must immediately perform and justify this task while continuing the scene, transforming bad habits into hilarious, high-energy narrative additions.

## Setup
3 to 4 players on stage, with the rest of the ensemble observing. One facilitator acts as the 'Patrol'. No props or special staging are required. The facilitator gets a single, mundane suggestion from the observing group to serve as the starting inspiration.

## How to Play
1. The facilitator gets a mundane suggestion (e.g., 'a rusty spoon', 'a slow elevator') and designates two players to start the scene.
2. Players begin a standard open scene, focusing on advancing the narrative, establishing characters, and staying physically active. Other players can tag in, enter, or exit as the story dictates.
3. The facilitator watches for three specific improv traps: 'Talking Head' (standing still and just talking), 'Echo Chamber' (repeating a partner's words without adding new information), and 'Narrative Nosedive' (introducing a random idea that completely ignores the established reality).
4. When a trap occurs, the facilitator calls it out and immediately assigns an active physical correction. For example: 'Talking Head! You are now aggressively washing a giant elephant!'
5. The scene DOES NOT STOP. The called-out player must instantly adopt the physical task, justify it within the reality of the scene, and continue their dialogue without breaking character.
6. Example Corrections: For 'Talking Head', assign complex object work (e.g., 'Bake a wedding cake', 'Defuse a bomb'). For 'Echo Chamber', assign a physical restriction (e.g., 'You must physically mirror your partner's exact movements until you add a new idea'). For 'Narrative Nosedive', assign a burden (e.g., 'Mime carrying a 100-pound boulder until you connect your random idea back to the main plot').
7. The facilitator should use these interventions sparingly. The goal is to correct the habit and inject energy, then step back to let the narrative build momentum.
8. The exercise concludes when the narrative reaches a natural resting point or the stage becomes delightfully, physically chaotic.

## Coaching Notes
- Active Corrections: Transforms negative feedback into a fun, generative physical offer.
- Momentum Preservation: Interventions don't stop the scene; they add to it, keeping the energy high.
- Targeted Skill Building: Directly addresses and cures 'talking heads', 'wimping/stalling', and 'derailing'.
- The 'reward' is the ensemble learning to self-correct in real-time and discovering how physical limitations can inspire brilliant narrative justifications.

## Variations
- Self-Patrol: For advanced ensembles, remove the facilitator. Players must call their own traps and assign themselves bizarre physical tasks the moment they realize they are stalling or standing still.
- Genre Patrol: The physical corrections must fit a specific genre chosen at the start. For example, if playing Sci-Fi, a 'Talking Head' must now 'recalibrate the warp drive' or 'fight off a face-hugger'.

## Why It Works
It directly addresses and cures 'talking heads', 'wimping/stalling', and 'derailing' by transforming negative feedback into a fun, generative physical offer. It teaches the ensemble to self-correct in real-time and discover how physical limitations can inspire brilliant narrative justifications.

## Safety & Inclusion
Physical tasks must be tailored to the mobility and comfort levels of the players. Facilitators should avoid assigning tasks that require unsafe movements (e.g., falling, heavy lifting, or running). Ensure players know they can modify or scale a physical assignment if it causes discomfort. Keep the tone highly supportive; the 'Patrol' is a fun, collaborative game mechanic, not a genuine punishment or harsh critique.
