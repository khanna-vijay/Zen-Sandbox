# The Organism

![The Organism — infographic](img/ISR166.jpg){ .game-hero }

> A non-verbal ensemble exercise where players move as a single entity, building silent scenes shaped by physical impulses and audience cues.

## Overview
A non-verbal ensemble exercise where players move as a single, leaderless entity, building silent scenes and shapes based purely on physical impulses. The observing group (audience or classmates) occasionally provides simple, unified physical cues that the players immediately adopt, turning abstract movement into grounded, collaborative scenes.

## Setup
An open, clear playing space. 6 to 12 players form a loose cluster on stage. The rest of the class or audience sits in the house to act as the 'Observer Group'. No props or set pieces are required.

## How to Play
1. The players begin in a loose cluster, moving slowly, silently, and aimlessly around the space.
2. Anyone can initiate a simple physical impulse or non-verbal sound (e.g., a foot tap, a sudden reach, a rhythmic breath).
3. Follow or Contrast: Other players must immediately react without thinking. They either copy and enlarge the movement (amplification) or do something completely different to change the picture (contrast).
4. Finding the Scene: As the group's movements synchronize, the facilitator side-coaches them to discover a real-world scenario in their abstract motion (Example: A collective reaching and swaying motion organically becomes a group of sailors pulling a heavy rope in a storm).
5. The Observer Shift: Every 1 to 2 minutes, the facilitator cues the observing group to offer a unified physical or vocal reaction (e.g., 'Audience, give me a heavy, exhausted sigh' or 'Audience, lean forward like you are watching something tiny').
6. Reacting to the Shift: The players immediately absorb this new physical feeling into their scene without stopping to discuss it.
7. Ending the Game: To prevent the exercise from dragging, the facilitator calls out, 'Find an ending in 10 seconds!' The group must intuitively find a way to freeze in a final, unified tableau as the countdown ends.

## Coaching Notes
- Use the audience as a 'weather system' for the players. Cue them to provide low-pressure, unified physical or vocal reactions (a gasp, a lean, a slow clap, a shiver) to instantly change the emotional tone or physical environment.
- Side-coach the players to transition smoothly from abstract movement to concrete narrative.
- Ensure players react instantly to the audience shift without stopping to discuss it.
- Remind players to strip away verbal crutches to force deep, full-body listening and ensemble connection.

## Variations
- Soundscape Organism: Focus entirely on non-verbal sounds, rhythms, and harmonies instead of movement. The audience provides a sound (like wind or a mechanical hum) that the players must build a vocal scene around.
- Split Organisms: Two groups of 4-5 players are on stage simultaneously. They must react not only to their own group's impulses and the audience, but also to the 'rival' organism sharing the space.

## Why It Works
It strips away verbal crutches to force deep, full-body listening and ensemble connection, training players to transition smoothly from abstract movement to concrete narrative.

## Safety & Inclusion
Players should move at their own physical capacity. 'Copying' a movement does not mean doing it exactly if it causes physical strain or requires going to the floor; it means capturing the essence, rhythm, or energy of the movement. There is no forced physical contact; the 'organism' can remain connected entirely by proximity and eye contact.
