# The Pendulum of Power

![The Pendulum of Power — infographic](img/IS071.jpg){ .game-hero }

> An evolving status-driven narrative game where characters' relative power is continuously and dramatically flipped by an external cue.

## Overview
Profound narrative development is achieved through a constantly evolving, abstract narrative constraint. Two improvisers continuously flip their characters' relative status in response to an external cue. Performers are challenged to immediately invent narrative justifications for these power shifts, ensuring their choices directly evolve the abstract status constraint and propel the overarching story.

## Setup
Two improvisers take the stage. The MC (or judge/referee) prepares a clear, distinct, and audible cue mechanism (e.g., a bell, sound effect, or loud verbal command like 'FLIP!'). The MC gets two audience suggestions: a relationship between the two characters (which inherently suggests an initial status dynamic) and a specific, unusual location. Finally, the MC explicitly states the initial status assignment (e.g., 'Player A, you are High Status. Player B, you are Low Status.').

## How to Play
1. The scene begins immediately with the characters establishing their relationship and the stated initial status dynamic within the suggested location.
2. At random intervals (e.g., every 30-60 seconds, or at the MC's discretion to enhance dramatic tension), the MC sounds the 'FLIP!' cue.
3. Upon the cue, the relative status between the two characters must immediately and dramatically reverse within 1-2 seconds. There is no negotiation or hesitation.
4. Performers must instantly integrate this status reversal into the ongoing scene, finding an internal or external narrative justification in that very moment for why the power dynamic has changed.
5. The narrative must continue, but now with the new status dynamic influencing character objectives, reactions, and dialogue. The stakes of the scene should visibly shift.
6. Players must 'Yes, And' not just verbal offers, but critically, the implied status offer from their partner based on the cue and their partner's immediate reaction.
7. The game continues for a set duration (e.g., 3-5 minutes), with multiple status flips occurring to build an often absurd yet compelling narrative.

## Coaching Notes
- Embrace Mistakes and Risk-Taking: Moving from a comfortable high-status position to a vulnerable low-status one is a risk. Wholeheartedly commit to these sudden reversals, even if they momentarily make the character 'look bad'.
- Collaborative Scene-Building: For a status flip to be effective, both performers must instantly recognize and respond in kind, actively supporting the new power structure through deep listening and mutual acknowledgment.
- Status Play: Embody and project high/low status instantly and convincingly through body language, vocal tone, and word choice. Differentiate subtle nuances between varying levels of high and low status.
- Being Changed: Allow your character's inner state, external behavior, and immediate objectives to be profoundly altered by each flip, rather than resisting or ignoring the change.
- Physicality: Strong physical reactions are demanded to immediately convey the new power dynamic. A character might literally shrink, puff up, change posture, or alter movement patterns.
- No Gagging: While humor will naturally arise, the primary goal is narrative progression through status play and character change, not cheap laughs at the expense of the story or scene partner.

## Why It Works
The relative status acts as a constantly evolving, abstract narrative constraint. Each status flip creates a new conflict, shifts character objectives, and pushes the narrative into unexpected directions. By requiring performers to invent immediate justifications for these power reversals, their scene choices become the primary driver of the constraint's evolution, ensuring the story organically weaves the changes into its fabric rather than stagnating.

## Safety & Inclusion
Ensure physical choices related to status (e.g., shrinking, puffing up, or moving quickly) are done safely without risking injury or physical boundary violations with the scene partner. Maintain emotional check-ins, as rapid power dynamic shifts can sometimes feel intense.
