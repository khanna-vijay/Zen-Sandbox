# Echo Impulse

![Echo Impulse — infographic](img/ISR120.jpg){ .game-hero }

> Pass a chain of intuitive physical reactions around a circle until a sudden cue forces two players to launch a grounded scene from their current abstract impulse.

## Overview
A fast-paced, 5-minute physical warm-up where players pass a chain of intuitive physical and emotional reactions around a circle, like a game of physical telephone. The exercise gets players out of their heads and into their bodies, culminating in a facilitator's cue that forces two players to use their current abstract physical impulse to instantly launch a grounded, relational scene.

## Setup
4 to 12 players stand or sit in a circle in an open space. A facilitator stands just outside the circle to side-coach and call the transitions. No props or audience suggestions are required.

## How to Play
1. The Seed: The facilitator gives the first player a simple, abstract sensory prompt (e.g., 'a sudden chill', 'the smell of burnt toast', 'a loud crash').
2. The First Reaction: Player 1 reacts instantly with a physical movement and a sound (no words). They should not overthink it; they just let their body react to the prompt for 1-2 seconds.
3. The Echo Chain: Player 2 immediately reacts to Player 1's movement and sound. Player 2 does NOT copy Player 1, nor do they react to the original prompt. They simply let Player 1's action trigger a new, distinct physical and vocal reaction in their own body.
4. Passing the Pulse: This chain reaction passes rapidly around the circle. Player 3 reacts to Player 2, Player 4 reacts to Player 3, and so on. The facilitator encourages speed, telling players to drop their intellect and just move.
5. The Scene Launch: After the impulse has traveled around the circle one or two times, the facilitator suddenly calls out 'Scene!' while a player is in the middle of their physical reaction.
6. Grounding the Abstract: The player who was just moving, along with the person next to them in the circle, immediately step into the center. The moving player must freeze in their current posture, retain their current emotional energy, and use it to speak the first line of a grounded, two-person scene.
7. Example: If Player 5's impulse is a heavy sigh while slumping their shoulders, and the facilitator calls 'Scene!', Player 5 keeps that exact posture, looks at Player 6, and initiates: 'I don't think I can assemble another IKEA chair today.' They then play out a brief 30-second scene before the facilitator resets the game with a new Seed.

## Coaching Notes
- Encourage speed and tell players to drop their intellect and just move to force them out of their heads.
- The facilitator guides the pacing, calling out the initial prompts and deciding exactly when to trigger the scene launch to maximize energy and surprise.
- Remind players not to copy the previous person's movement, but to let it trigger a completely new, distinct reaction in their own body.
- Demand intense focus, listening, and commitment to the present moment.

## Variations
- Character Echo: Instead of abstract sounds and movements, the facilitator gives Player 1 a specific character posture or physical tic. Players pass and exaggerate the physical trait around the circle until it morphs into a completely new character, which is then used to launch the scene.
- Emotion Echo: The facilitator seeds a pure emotion (e.g., 'pure joy', 'simmering rage'). Players pass the emotion, letting it naturally morph (e.g., joy turns to surprise, surprise turns to fear). When 'Scene!' is called, the players must justify their current emotional state in the scene's first line.

## Why It Works
It effectively forces players out of their heads and into their bodies through rapid-fire pacing, bridging the gap between abstract physical warm-ups and concrete, relational scene work.

## Safety & Inclusion
Players must maintain their own physical space; no impulse or reaction should involve touching, grabbing, or entering another player's personal bubble. The game is fully accessible for players with limited mobility or those who prefer to remain seated, as 'physical impulses' can be expressed entirely and effectively through facial expressions, vocalizations, and upper-body posture.
