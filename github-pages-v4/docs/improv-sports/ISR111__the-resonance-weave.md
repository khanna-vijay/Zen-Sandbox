# The Resonance Weave

![The Resonance Weave — infographic](img/ISR111.jpg){ .game-hero }

> Performers create a collective sound and movement piece driven entirely by the concrete, observable behaviors of the audience.

## Overview
An ensemble warm-up where performers create a collective sound and movement piece driven entirely by the concrete, observable behaviors of the audience or the rest of the workshop group. Performers mirror and amplify audience shifts until the abstract movement organically solidifies into a recognizable scene, tableau, or song, providing a clear payoff.

## Setup
An open room or stage. 5-10 performers stand in the center. The 'audience' (the rest of the class or workshop) sits around them in a semi-circle or full circle. A facilitator stands to the side to coach and call time.

## How to Play
1. The Baseline: Performers start in the center with a simple, unified, repetitive sound and movement (e.g., a slow sway and a low hum, or a rhythmic foot tap).
2. Concrete Observation: Performers keep their eyes open and focus entirely outward on the audience. They must look and listen for concrete, observable behaviors: a cough, shifting weight, crossing legs, eye contact, looking away, or a whisper.
3. The Ripple Effect: When a performer notices a specific audience behavior, they immediately incorporate it into the weave. If an audience member coughs, a performer might add a sharp, rhythmic vocalization. The rest of the ensemble immediately supports and mimics this new addition.
4. Escalation: As more audience behaviors are absorbed, the weave becomes more complex. The facilitator side-coaches to heighten the movements and sounds.
5. The Payoff (Finding the Form): After 2-3 minutes of abstract escalation, the facilitator calls out, 'Find the form!' The ensemble must take the current chaotic mix of sounds and movements and instantly justify them into a concrete reality, such as a specific scene, a frozen physical tableau, or a cohesive song.
6. The Ending: Once the scene, tableau, or song is clearly established and runs for 30-60 seconds, the facilitator calls 'And scene!' to end the exercise cleanly.

## Coaching Notes
- Side-coach to heighten the escalation: 'Amplify that leg cross!' or 'Turn that sneeze into a rhythm!'
- Remind performers to replace vague 'feeling the energy' with concrete, actionable observation of physical behaviors.
- Encourage performers to shift their focus entirely outward, reducing self-consciousness and getting them out of their heads.
- The audience acts as the passive engine of the game; their natural fidgets, reactions, and physical shifts provide the raw material for the performers.

## Variations
- The Solo Weave: A single performer does this exercise, mirroring the audience's physical shifts and eventually snapping into a grounded character monologue based on those movements.
- The Machine: Instead of an organic, fluid weave, the performers build a mechanical structure with interlocking parts. The speed, volume, and breakdown of the machine are dictated by the audience's volume and restlessness.

## Why It Works
It builds ensemble agreement, 'group mind,' and shared breathing while providing a clear bridge from an abstract physical warm-up to concrete scene work.

## Safety & Inclusion
Performers must be coached to be inspired by the audience's movements, not to mock or single out individuals maliciously. The facilitator should ensure physical safety during the abstract movement phase (e.g., no running blindly). The game is highly accessible, as the 'weave' can be adapted to be entirely seated, purely vocal, or purely physical depending on the ensemble's mobility.
