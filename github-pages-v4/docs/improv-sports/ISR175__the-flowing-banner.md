# The Flowing Banner

![The Flowing Banner — infographic](img/ISR175.jpg){ .game-hero }

> Move a 'banner' across the room as a cohesive, connected unit, fluidly adapting to moving obstacles without breaking formation.

## Overview
A non-competitive, facilitator-led ensemble exercise where a group must move a 'banner' across the room as a cohesive, connected unit. They must fluidly adapt their shape to navigate around moving obstacles (the other team) without breaking their formation, stopping their momentum, or speaking.

## Setup
Clear a large, open space (like a rehearsal room or stage). You will need one 'Banner'—this can be a piece of flowing fabric, a pool noodle, or a hula hoop. Divide the participants into two equal teams. Designate one side of the room as the Starting Base and the opposite side as the Destination.

## How to Play
1. Define the Support Circle: The active team clusters together. One player stands in the center holding the Banner. The rest of the team forms a 'Support Circle' around them. The group agrees on their connection type: physical (e.g., fingertips on shoulders), prop-based (everyone holds a large loop of rope), or spatial (everyone must remain exactly one arm's length from the center).
2. The Objective: The Banner Team must move continuously from the Starting Base to the Destination. The Banner must never stop moving forward, and the Support Circle must never break its chosen connection.
3. Concrete Interference Mechanics: The opposing team acts as 'Interference.' To do this safely and clearly, Interfering players act as predictable, moving geometry. An Interfering player picks a point on the opposite wall and walks toward it in a straight line at a steady, unvarying pace.
4. Morphing and Adapting: As Interfering players walk through the space, the Banner Team must stretch, compress, and morph their shape to let the Interference pass through or around them. The Banner Team cannot forcefully push through; they must yield and flow.
5. Rules of Interference: Interfering players must never run, chase, grab, or intentionally trap the Banner Team. They do not alter their path to block; they simply exist as moving obstacles (like crossing a busy sidewalk).
6. Flow Broken: The facilitator calls 'Reset!' if: a) The Support Circle breaks its connection, b) The Banner comes to a complete halt, c) Anyone on the Banner Team speaks, or d) A collision occurs. Upon a reset, the Banner Team returns to the Starting Base and tries again.
7. Escalation Phase 1 (Empty Room): The Banner Team practices moving across an empty room to establish their collective breathing and pacing.
8. Escalation Phase 2 (Slow-Motion Traffic): The Interfering Team walks across the space in straight lines, but in extreme slow-motion.
9. Escalation Phase 3 (Grid Traffic): The Interfering Team walks at a normal, steady pace, occasionally making sharp 90-degree turns to create a dynamic grid of moving obstacles.
10. Escalation Phase 4 (Two Banners): Both teams get a Banner and start on opposite sides of the room. Both teams must cross the space simultaneously, acting as the Interference for each other as their Support Circles intersect.

## Coaching Notes
- Side-coach throughout the exercise, offering prompts like 'Breathe together,' 'Keep the banner drifting,' and 'Yield to the space.'
- Emphasize that this is a non-competitive exercise; there are no points or winners. The focus is entirely on process, adaptation, and yielding.
- Encourage deep physical listening and non-verbal communication to maintain the group's collective breathing and pacing.

## Variations
- Invisible Banner: Play without a physical prop. The group must maintain their spatial relationship around an imaginary center point, relying entirely on shared focus.
- Soundscape Flow: The Banner Team creates a continuous, unified vocal hum. As the group compresses to avoid an obstacle, the pitch goes up; as they expand, the pitch goes down. If the sound breaks, the flow is broken.

## Why It Works
It cultivates 'group mind' and ensemble responsiveness by demanding deep physical listening and non-verbal communication. Players learn to yield and adapt to their environment rather than forcing their way through.

## Safety & Inclusion
Consent and accessibility are built into the setup. Groups explicitly choose their connection type (physical touch, holding a shared prop, or purely spatial distance) so no one is forced into unwanted contact. To prevent accidental collisions, Interfering players are strictly forbidden from running, grabbing, or altering their path to intentionally block; they must move at a steady, predictable pace.
