# Inner Dialogue

![Inner Dialogue — infographic](img/IS033.jpg){ .game-hero }

> Spontaneously reveal characters' hidden thoughts and true motivations, forcing performers to integrate this audible subtext into the ongoing scene.

## Overview
Inner Dialogue is a competitive short-form game where a designated Inner Voice Caller prompts performers to pause their overt scene dialogue and vocalize a brief, unfiltered inner thought. After expressing this subtext, the performer resumes the scene as if it wasn't spoken aloud. Crucially, their scene partners must subtly or overtly adapt their subsequent actions and dialogue to acknowledge the content of this newly revealed inner world.

## Setup
Two or three performers are on stage. The host requests audience suggestions for a relationship, a location, and an object that plays a role in the scene. A designated judge or the host acts as the 'Inner Voice Caller' and establishes a clear, distinct signal (e.g., a bell, the word 'Thought!', or a light cue) to trigger an inner monologue.

## How to Play
1. Performers begin the scene, establishing characters and initial stakes.
2. At any point, as a performer is speaking or about to speak, the Inner Voice Caller can activate their signal.
3. The signaled performer must immediately pause their overt dialogue, step slightly forward (or make another agreed-upon physical gesture), and vocalize an inner thought or alternative line their character is secretly thinking.
4. Ensure the inner thought is character-specific, brief (a single sentence or short phrase), and unfiltered, revealing subtext usually kept hidden.
5. After vocalizing the thought, the performer steps back to their original position and completes their interrupted line or delivers the intended next line as if the inner thought had not been spoken aloud.
6. The other performers must not directly acknowledge the act of the thought being spoken (e.g., no saying 'I heard you think that!'). Instead, they must subtly or overtly react to the content of that inner thought in their subsequent dialogue, actions, or emotional state.

## Coaching Notes
- The Inner Voice Caller can trigger inner thoughts at any frequency they deem appropriate, from every few lines to longer stretches of dialogue.
- Embrace mistakes and unexpected turns. An inner thought might be awkward or contradictory; performers must accept this 'gift' and integrate it seamlessly.
- Performers must be visibly and audibly changed by the revelation of their partner's inner thoughts. Shift your character's perspective, status, emotional state, or immediate objectives based on this new information.
- Practice active listening. Non-speaking performers are constantly challenged to listen intently to the inner thoughts and creatively weave them into their responses to make their partner look good.

## Variations
- Self-Initiated Inner Thought: Performers are given one opportunity per scene to self-initiate an inner thought using a distinct physical cue (e.g., touching their forehead), provided they haven't been prompted by the Caller recently.

## Why It Works
By exposing subtext, the game adds immediate layers of complexity to character motivations and relationships without explicit plot points. It forces performers to continuously consider what their character is not saying, demanding a high level of 'Yes, And' to integrate hidden agendas and fears into the collaborative scene-building.

## Safety & Inclusion
Ensure performers respect physical boundaries and emotional safety when revealing vulnerable or intense subtext. Establish a supportive environment where unexpected or awkward revelations are treated as gifts, not mistakes.
