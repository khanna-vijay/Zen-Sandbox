# Pulse Play

![Pulse Play — infographic](img/ISR119.jpg){ .game-hero }

> Actors perform a grounded scene while the audience provides continuous, concrete feedback to act as an emotional volume knob.

## Overview
Pulse Play is a non-competitive, facilitator-led theater game where actors perform a grounded scene while the audience provides continuous, concrete feedback using visual or auditory cues. The actors' primary focus remains firmly on their scene partner and the reality of the scene. Instead of changing the plot when the audience's attention wanes, actors use the feedback as a 'volume knob'—heightening their current emotional dynamic when engagement is high, and grounding themselves in their partner when connection drops.

## Setup
Requires 2-4 actors on stage, with the rest of the workshop acting as the audience. A facilitator leads the exercise. Provide the audience with Green and Red cards (or instruct them to use simple hand gestures like 'Thumbs Up' and 'Hands Flat'). No specific props or sets are required.

## How to Play
1. The facilitator establishes the feedback mechanic with the audience: Green cards (or Thumbs Up) mean 'We are highly engaged; heighten whatever you are doing right now.' Red cards (or Hands Flat) mean 'We are losing connection; ground yourselves and reconnect with your partner.'
2. Two actors take the stage and are given a simple, grounded relationship and location (e.g., 'Siblings cleaning out the garage').
3. The actors begin the scene. Their primary Point of Concentration is their scene partner and committing to the reality of the moment.
4. The audience continuously displays their cards or gestures based on their genuine engagement with the scene's emotional truth.
5. When actors see Green, they should lean into their current tactic—deepening the emotion, extending a silence, or raising the stakes of the conversation.
6. When actors see Red, they must NOT bail on the scene, invent a wacky plot twist, or force a joke. Instead, they must slow down, make eye contact with their partner, take a breath, and recommit to the relationship.
7. The scene concludes when a natural emotional resolution is reached, or the facilitator calls 'Scene.'

## Coaching Notes
- The facilitator side-coaches throughout, reminding actors: 'Stay with your partner,' 'Don't change the story, change the depth,' and 'Let the audience turn the volume knob, not the steering wheel.'
- Ensure actors don't sacrifice their scene partner for cheap audience reactions.
- Watch out for bailing: remind actors that Red means 'reconnect', not 'panic and destroy the narrative'.

## Variations
- The Sound of the Crowd: Instead of visual cards, the audience uses a low, continuous hum that grows louder for high engagement and fades to silence for disconnection. This is excellent for visually impaired players or highly physical scenes where actors cannot easily look at the audience.
- The Facilitator's Dial: For beginners, the facilitator acts as the sole feedback provider using a literal or mimed 'dial,' allowing actors to practice responding to feedback without the pressure of the entire room.

## Why It Works
It replaces vague 'vibe reading' with clear, externalized signals from the audience. It ensures a partner-first focus so actors don't sacrifice their scene partner for cheap audience reactions. It also provides anti-bailing training, teaching actors the vital skill of grounding and recommitting when a scene struggles, rather than panicking and destroying the narrative.

## Safety & Inclusion
Explicitly frame the Red cards/Hands Flat not as 'boos' or punishment, but as neutral, helpful navigational tools that mean 'reconnect.' Ensure the feedback mechanics are accessible; offer the auditory 'Sound of the Crowd' variation if visual cues are difficult for any participants to see or process. Keep all scene prompts and content clean, all-ages, and focused on emotional truth rather than shock value.
