# Phrase Heist

![Phrase Heist — infographic](img/ISR068.jpg){ .game-hero }

> A fast-paced scene challenge where the opposing team strategically drops bizarre, audience-provided phrases into the performance.

## Overview
Two teams compete in a fast-paced scene challenge. While one team performs, the opposing team acts as the 'Word Master,' strategically dropping bizarre, audience-provided phrases into the scene by ringing a bell between lines of dialogue. The performers must instantly and naturally justify the phrase, with the referee awarding points at the end of the scene based on seamless integration and comedic survival.

## Setup
Divide 4-8 players into two teams (e.g., Red Team and Blue Team). You need a Referee to host and judge, a bell for the Word Master, a large whiteboard or screen to display the 'Phrase Bank' visibly to players and the audience, and a clear stage area.

## How to Play
1. Phrase Bank Creation: Before the scene begins, the Referee asks the audience for 5 to 7 bizarre, highly specific, and clean phrases (e.g., 'Aggressively buttering the toast', 'A suspicious amount of mayonnaise'). Single words are not allowed. The Referee writes these on the whiteboard.
2. Scene Initiation: Two to three players from the Red Team take the stage. The Referee gets a scene suggestion from the audience (e.g., a location or relationship) and starts the scene.
3. The Heist: The Blue Team designates one player as the 'Word Master.' The Word Master stands to the side of the stage holding the bell, with a clear view of the whiteboard.
4. Dropping the Bomb: At any strategic point during the scene, strictly between lines of dialogue, the Word Master rings the bell and loudly, clearly reads one unused phrase from the board. They cross it off immediately.
5. Justification: The very next Red Team player to speak must instantly and naturally incorporate that exact phrase into their dialogue, justifying why their character would say it in the current reality of the scene.
6. Pacing: The Word Master aims to drop 3 to 4 phrases over the course of a 2 to 3-minute scene.
7. Scene End: The Referee calls 'Scene!' after 3 minutes or when 4 phrases have been used. The scene flows uninterrupted.
8. Scoring: At the end of the scene, the Referee awards up to 5 points to the performing team based on how seamlessly and hilariously they justified the phrases. If the performing team completely broke character or fumbled a phrase, the Referee can award 'stump points' (1-2 points) to the opposing team. Audience cheers heavily influence the final point allocation.

## Coaching Notes
- Active Off-Stage Engagement: The opposing team should remain highly engaged while off-stage, looking for the perfect moment to strike.
- Timing is crucial: The Word Master should time the phrases to create maximum comedic difficulty without completely destroying the narrative.
- Uninterrupted Flow: By moving scoring to the end of the scene and using a bell between lines, the scene maintains its momentum. Do not stop the scene to score individual phrases.
- Ensure the Word Master rings the bell strictly between lines of dialogue, rather than talking over players, so the prompt is heard clearly.

## Variations
- Physical Heist: Instead of spoken phrases, the bank consists of bizarre physical actions (e.g., 'Moves like a crab', 'Tries to catch an invisible fly'). When the bell rings, the next player to move must incorporate the action.
- Blind Heist: For an added layer of chaos, the phrases are written on slips of paper and drawn blindly by the Word Master right as they ring the bell, meaning even the opposing team doesn't know what's coming.

## Why It Works
Forces improvisers to immediately accept and justify absurd information, which is a core improv skill.

## Safety & Inclusion
The Referee must strictly filter audience suggestions to ensure all phrases are family-friendly, clean, and free of harmful tropes. The rule requiring the Word Master to ring the bell between lines (rather than talking over players) is crucial for accessibility, ensuring players with auditory processing differences can clearly hear the prompt without overlapping noise.
