# Image Impulses

![Image Impulses — infographic](img/ISR157.jpg){ .game-hero }

> Players use evocative image cards to trigger spontaneous, non-verbal physical and vocal reactions in a shared soundscape.

## Overview
A non-competitive, facilitator-led Theater Game where players use evocative image cards (like Dixit or art postcards) to trigger spontaneous, non-verbal physical and vocal reactions. By removing the pressure to be 'clever' or invent a narrative, this warm-up bypasses the intellect and trains raw impulse, emotional reaction, and ensemble listening.

## Setup
Players sit in a circle on chairs or the floor. Place a shuffled deck of highly evocative image cards face down in the center. Each player draws 3 to 5 cards to form a hand. The facilitator stands outside the circle to side-coach and guide the pacing.

## How to Play
1. The Catalyst (Player 1) selects a card from their hand that sparks an immediate feeling, placing it face-up in the center.
2. Without speaking, the Catalyst immediately expresses a physical action or vocal sound inspired by the card (e.g., a heavy sigh and slumped shoulders, or a sharp intake of breath and a reaching gesture).
3. Sustaining the Impulse: The Catalyst settles into a gentle, repeatable loop of their physical/vocal impulse, sustaining it at a low, manageable energy level.
4. Moving clockwise, the next player selects a card from their hand that intuitively resonates with the Catalyst's card, the Catalyst's impulse, or the room's energy.
5. Player 2 places their card next to the first, and adds their own physical/vocal impulse, sustaining it alongside Player 1 to begin building a collective, living tableau and soundscape.
6. This continues around the circle until everyone has played a card and joined the sustained soundscape.
7. The facilitator calls 'Fade,' allowing the group's energy to gently dissipate into silence. The role of Catalyst passes to the left, players draw a new card to replace the one they played, and a new round begins.
8. The Debrief: After 2 to 3 rounds, the facilitator stops the game for a structured discussion. Ask: 'What did you feel in your body?', 'How did your impulse change when you heard the person next to you?', and 'What was it like to react without needing to make sense?'

## Coaching Notes
- The facilitator guides the process, side-coaching with phrases like 'Don't think, just breathe and move,' 'Let the image move you,' and 'Listen to the sounds of the ensemble.'
- Remind players to sustain their impulse at a low, manageable energy level.
- Encourage players to bypass the intellect and avoid the pressure to be 'clever' or invent a narrative.

## Variations
- The Scene Bridge: To transition these abstract skills into scene work, have two players step into the center without cards. The facilitator prompts them to recall a specific physical posture or vocal tone they used during the warm-up. They adopt that impulse, make eye contact, and begin a two-person scene grounded entirely in that physical/emotional anchor.
- Blind Draw (Advanced): Instead of choosing a card from a hand, players draw the top card from the deck and flip it face-up, reacting instantly to whatever they see. This removes the 'selection' process and forces pure, immediate reaction.

## Why It Works
Bypasses the intellect to train raw impulse and emotional reaction. Low pressure for 'cleverness' or narrative makes it highly accessible for beginners. Uses physical image cards as strong, tangible anchors for abstract physical work. Builds ensemble awareness through a shared, sustained soundscape and tableau.

## Safety & Inclusion
Ensure the deck of cards is curated to remove any genuinely disturbing or triggering imagery. Set a clear boundary that players should not touch each other during their sustained impulses to maintain personal safety. For accessibility, players can remain seated the entire time, and responses can be entirely vocal (for players with limited mobility) or entirely physical (for neurodivergent players or those who prefer non-verbal expression).
