# Collective Current

![Collective Current — infographic](img/ISR110.jpg){ .game-hero }

> Performers attempt a mundane task while the audience uses hand gestures to dictate shifting physical realities.

## Overview
Collective Current is a facilitator-led ensemble exercise where performers attempt to complete a mundane, everyday task while the audience acts as the environment. Using specific hand gestures, the audience dictates shifting physical realities like heavy gravity or strong winds.

## Setup
Gather 4 to 8 performers in an open space. The facilitator stands where they can see both the performers and the audience. The facilitator teaches the audience four simple hand gestures that represent environmental shifts: Pointing Up for low gravity, Pointing Down for heavy gravity, Waving for strong wind, and Clapping for a strobe-light or choppy time effect. The facilitator then asks the audience for a mundane, multi-person activity, such as painting a room, setting up a campsite, or preparing a family dinner.

## How to Play
1. The performers begin the scene by engaging in the mundane activity in a normal, neutral environment.
2. After about 30 seconds, the facilitator points to a section of the audience and cues them to perform one of the environmental gestures.
3. The performers must instantly adapt their physicality to match the new environment while continuing to pursue their mundane objective.
4. The contrast between the ordinary task and the extreme physical constraint provides the comedy and the focus.
5. The facilitator continues to cue different audience gestures every 30 to 60 seconds, escalating the intensity or abruptly shifting the reality.
6. The exercise concludes after 3 to 5 minutes, either when the mundane task is somehow completed or when the physical escalation reaches a natural, high-energy peak.

## Coaching Notes
- This is a non-competitive improv exercise with no points or judges.
- Encourage high physical commitment and ensemble agreement.
- Remind players to ground the extreme physical comedy in their relatable, mundane objective.
- Use the audience actively to co-create the scene as environmental designers.

## Variations
- Emotional Current: Instead of physical environments, the audience gestures dictate the underlying emotional atmosphere of the room, such as extreme paranoia, overwhelming joy, or sudden lethargy.
- Conductor Current: A single audience member is brought up to act as the conductor, using a baton to shift the environment at their own pace without the facilitator's prompting.

## Why It Works
It builds extreme physical responsiveness and ensemble agreement by forcing players to pursue a simple objective under ridiculous, ever-changing constraints.

## Safety & Inclusion
Physical safety is paramount. The facilitator must explicitly instruct performers not to throw themselves to the floor or drop their full body weight. Heavy gravity means moving as if through thick molasses, not collapsing. Wind means leaning and struggling, not flying across the room. Performers with mobility restrictions can fully participate by focusing the environmental effects on their upper body, facial expressions, and vocal strain. The focus is on the illusion of the environment, not actual physical punishment.
