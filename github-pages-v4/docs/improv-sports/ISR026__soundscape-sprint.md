# SoundScape Sprint

![SoundScape Sprint — infographic](img/ISR026.jpg){ .game-hero }

> Embody three audience-suggested sounds and seamlessly weave them into a fast-paced, competitive scene.

## Overview
A high-energy, two-phase competitive short-form game where players first physically and vocally embody three distinct audience-suggested sounds. They then immediately improvise a scene that organically justifies and integrates those exact sounds into the narrative.

## Setup
Requires two teams of 2 to 3 players each and a Referee. No props or set pieces are needed. Played on a standard open stage. The Referee stands downstage to manage audience suggestions and pacing.

## How to Play
1. The Referee asks the audience for a location or scenario (e.g., 'A haunted pirate ship' or 'An alien diner').
2. The Referee explicitly solicits exactly three distinct sound effects from the audience, pointing to individuals one at a time to avoid chaotic shouting.
3. As each sound is suggested, the Referee assigns it to a specific player on the active team. That player steps forward and fully embodies the sound physically and vocally, locking it into their memory.
4. Once all three sounds are established and assigned, the Referee calls 'Scene!'
5. The players immediately drop their initial sound poses and begin an improvised scene in the suggested location.
6. During the scene, players must organically weave their assigned sounds into the narrative, justifying them through character actions, environmental hazards, or dialogue.
7. Players should aim to use their sounds multiple times, building a rhythmic and cohesive auditory environment.
8. The scene concludes after 60 to 90 seconds on a high comedic note. The Referee calls 'Scene!', and the opposing team takes their turn with a new location and three new sounds.
9. After both teams have played, the Referee calls for audience applause to determine the winner based on which team integrated and justified their three sounds most creatively, seamlessly, and hilariously.

## Coaching Notes
- The Referee acts as the bridge, carefully soliciting the three sounds one by one to ensure clarity.
- Encourage strong physical and vocal commitment right from the start when players first embody their sounds.
- Strictly limit the cognitive load by capping the sounds at exactly three, ensuring players can succeed without getting overwhelmed.
- Remind players to justify the sounds organically rather than just making noises randomly; the sound should become a rusty peg-leg, an alien mouse, etc.

## Variations
- Solo Sprint: One player is assigned all three sounds and must perform a solo scene, bouncing between characters or environmental interactions to trigger all three sounds themselves.
- Sound Effects Double: Players are paired up. One acts in the scene while their partner stands off-stage and provides their assigned sound effect whenever the acting player's physical choices trigger it.
- Genre Sprint: The scene phase must be performed in a specific audience-suggested movie or theater genre, forcing the sounds to fit that specific tone.

## Why It Works
It serves as an excellent exercise in post-hoc justification and 'Yes, And' skills. It forces strong physical and vocal commitment right from the start, and requires zero props or setup, making it highly accessible and fast to stage.

## Safety & Inclusion
Vocal safety is paramount; the Referee should side-coach players to project from the diaphragm rather than straining their vocal cords on harsh or screaming sounds. Physical embodiment of sounds must respect personal space, ensuring players do not unintentionally collide during the chaotic scene phase. A standard content foul applies to keep all content, including the sounds and their justifications, clean and family-friendly.
