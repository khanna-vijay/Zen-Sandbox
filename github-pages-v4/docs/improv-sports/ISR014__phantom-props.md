# Phantom Props

![Phantom Props — infographic](img/ISR014.jpg){ .game-hero }

> Players must maintain the exact physical dimensions, weight, and location of an imaginary object throughout a scene.

## Overview
A foundational object-work exercise where players must maintain the exact physical dimensions, weight, and location of an imaginary object throughout a scene. Stripped of competitive points, this drill uses facilitator side-coaching to build rigorous object permanence without sacrificing narrative momentum.

## Setup
A clear stage or classroom area. No props are used (purely mime). Two players take the stage while the rest of the ensemble observes.

## How to Play
1. The facilitator brings two players to the stage and gets a location suggestion from the observing ensemble.
2. The facilitator assigns exactly ONE imaginary object to the scene, giving it two distinct physical properties (e.g., 'a feather-light, razor-sharp sword' or 'a heavy, sticky bowling ball').
3. The players begin their scene. Their primary goal is to play a grounded, relational scene while flawlessly maintaining the physical reality, location, and properties of the assigned object.
4. The observing ensemble watches actively for 'Phantom Breaks' - moments where the object's size, weight, or location is forgotten (e.g., walking through the table the object was set on, lifting a 100-pound object effortlessly, or changing its shape).
5. If a break occurs, the facilitator (and ONLY the facilitator) calls 'Freeze!' to pause the scene. The facilitator gently points out the physical inconsistency.
6. The players rewind three seconds, correct the physical action to honor the object's properties, and continue the scene.
7. After 2-3 minutes, the facilitator calls 'Scene,' and a new pair takes the stage for a completely new scene with a new object.

## Coaching Notes
- The facilitator acts as the sole judge of physical reality to prevent the scene from devolving into bickering.
- Keep off-stage players highly engaged by having them actively watch for subtle physical inconsistencies.
- Remind players that their primary goal is still to play a grounded, relational scene; the object work should support the scene, not replace it.
- Ensure the 'freeze and fix' mechanic is used gently to build muscle memory for better space work, rather than as a punishment.

## Variations
- The Passing Circle (Warm-up): Before doing scenes, the ensemble stands in a circle and passes a single phantom object around. The facilitator calls out changing properties ('It is now boiling hot,' 'It is now 100 pounds') to drill physical reactions.
- Silent Phantom: The scene is played entirely in gibberish or silence, forcing the players to communicate the object's properties purely through exaggerated physical mime and eye contact.

## Why It Works
It forces players to track the exact location, weight, and dimensions of invisible items, building rigorous object permanence. By capping the scene at a single object, players can focus on cognitive focus and quality scene work rather than juggling too many rules. The corrective repetition builds muscle memory for better space work.

## Safety & Inclusion
Ensure the playing space is entirely clear of real obstacles so players do not trip while focusing on imaginary ones. Object properties can be adapted to the physical mobility of the players (e.g., focusing on texture, smell, or temperature rather than heavy lifting). Emphasize that 'freezing' to correct a mistake is a supportive learning tool, not a punishment.
