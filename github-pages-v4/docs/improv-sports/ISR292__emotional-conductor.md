# Emotional Conductor

![Emotional Conductor — infographic](img/ISR292.jpg){ .game-hero }

> Two players build a grounded scene while instantly adopting and justifying new emotional states called out by a facilitator.

## Overview
A facilitator-led workshop exercise where improvisers practice the essential skill of 'being changed.' Two players build a grounded scene while the facilitator periodically calls out specific, distinct emotions. The players must instantly adopt the new emotional state in their physicality and voice, and crucially, justify why this shift occurred within the scene's reality, using it to drive the narrative forward.

## Setup
Two players on stage. A facilitator stands downstage or in the house to side-coach and call out prompts. The rest of the workshop group observes.

## How to Play
1. The facilitator asks the group for a suggestion of a mundane location and a relationship (e.g., two siblings cleaning an attic).
2. The players begin the scene in a grounded, neutral emotional state, focusing on establishing the base reality, the environment, and their relationship.
3. After about 60 seconds, once the scene has had time to breathe and establish context, the facilitator calls out a specific emotion (e.g., 'Jealousy!', 'Euphoria!', 'Paranoia!').
4. Both players must instantly internalize this new emotion, visibly altering their posture, facial expressions, and vocal tone.
5. The players must immediately justify the emotional shift using the existing narrative. They cannot ignore the previous reality; they must find a reason why the current topic or their partner's last line suddenly triggered this specific emotion.
6. The scene continues in this new emotional state for another 60 seconds, allowing the players to explore how the emotion affects their relationship and objectives.
7. The facilitator calls out a new, contrasting emotion. The players shift and justify again.
8. After 3 to 4 emotional shifts, the facilitator calls 'Scene' and briefly debriefs with the players on which justifications felt most grounded.

## Coaching Notes
- The facilitator's role is to side-coach, ensuring players don't drop the narrative and that they fully commit to the physical and vocal changes.
- Encourage strong, immediate physical and vocal choices.
- Ensure players use emotional shifts to deepen the narrative rather than derail it.

## Variations
- Split Emotions: The facilitator points to Player A and calls 'Arrogance', then points to Player B and calls 'Insecurity'. Players must justify their contrasting states.
- The Emotional Dial: The facilitator calls an emotion and an intensity level from 1 to 10 (e.g., 'Anger at a 3... now Anger at an 8!').

## Why It Works
Forces players to actively practice 'being changed' and justifying new realities. It trains improvisers to use emotional shifts to deepen the narrative rather than derail it, encouraging strong, immediate physical and vocal choices.

## Safety & Inclusion
The facilitator should select playable, theatrical emotions (e.g., 'pride', 'suspicion', 'infatuation') and avoid trauma-triggering states (e.g., 'panic attack', 'suicidal despair'). Remind players that intense emotions like anger must be played safely—no physical aggression, throwing objects, or crossing a scene partner's physical boundaries. Players can always step out if an emotion feels unsafe to embody.
