# Impulse Transformer

![Impulse Transformer — infographic](img/ISR152.jpg){ .game-hero }

> Players receive a sound and movement, let it physically affect them, and transform it into a completely new impulse to pass along.

## Overview
A non-verbal circle warm-up where players pass a sound and movement, but instead of mimicking what they receive, they allow it to physically affect them and pass a completely new, transformed impulse to the next person. It trains full-body listening, emotional reactivity, and letting go of planned ideas.

## Setup
Players stand in a loose circle with enough room to move their arms and bodies freely. No props, stage, or audience are required. A facilitator leads from within or just outside the circle.

## How to Play
1. The facilitator asks the group to stand in a circle and make eye contact, explaining that they will pass a sound and movement across the space.
2. Player A initiates by sending a distinct sound and movement to Player B across the circle (e.g., a sharp clap and a loud 'Ha!').
3. Player B receives the impulse and lets it physically affect them, reacting naturally to the energy of what was sent (e.g., flinching backward from the sharp, aggressive energy).
4. Instead of passing the same clap and 'Ha!', Player B transforms their natural physical reaction into a completely new sound and movement (e.g., turning their backward flinch into a slow, sweeping arm motion and a soft 'Whoosh' sound), sending it to Player C.
5. Player C receives the 'Whoosh', reacts to its smooth energy (e.g., swaying gently), and transforms that sway into a new impulse (e.g., a heavy stomp and a low grunt) to send to Player D.
6. The chain continues randomly across the circle. Players must not plan their move in advance; they must wait to be changed by what they receive.

## Coaching Notes
- Side-coach players to avoid getting stuck in their heads, reminding them that their first, unthinking physical response is the correct one.
- Focus entirely on the ensemble's internal process, building trust, and practicing spontaneous, embodied reactions.
- Remind players that the goal is to be changed by their partner rather than executing a pre-planned idea.

## Variations
- Emotional Transformer: Instead of just physical energy, players pass and transform specific emotions. For example, receiving anger might make the next person react with fear, which they then transform into sadness to pass to the next player.
- Machine Speed: The facilitator side-coaches the speed of the game, moving from extreme slow-motion (allowing deep physical processing) to rapid-fire (forcing players to bypass their brains entirely and rely purely on reflex).

## Why It Works
It heavily emphasizes the crucial improv skill of being changed by your partner, encourages full-body listening, breaks players out of verbal ruts, and replaces intellectual planning with physical, spontaneous reactions.

## Safety & Inclusion
Ensure players maintain physical boundaries; impulses are passed across the space using eye contact and energy, not through physical contact. Encourage players with limited mobility to focus on facial expressions, vocalizations, and upper-body transformations. Remind the group that there are no 'wrong' reactions, which helps reduce performance anxiety and builds a safe environment for taking risks.
