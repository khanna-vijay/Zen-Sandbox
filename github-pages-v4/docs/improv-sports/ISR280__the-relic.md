# The Relic

![The Relic — infographic](img/ISR280.jpg){ .game-hero }

> Players must establish an abstract object's function entirely through physical interaction before they are allowed to verbally name it.

## Overview
A facilitator-led workshop exercise where 2-3 players build a scene around 1 or 2 abstract physical objects. Players must establish the object's function entirely through physical interaction and mime before they are allowed to verbally name it.

## Setup
Place 1 or 2 abstract, non-representational objects in the center of the playing space. Good examples include a gnarled piece of driftwood, a heavy smooth stone, or a strange metal bracket. Ensure the objects are safe to handle and do not have one obvious real-world function. The facilitator stands by to side-coach.

## How to Play
1. The facilitator places 1 or 2 abstract objects (The Relics) in the center of the space.
2. Two to three players step up and get a location or genre suggestion from the rest of the workshop (e.g., 'A submarine' or 'A wizard's tower').
3. The scene begins. Players must interact with the object to discover what it is within the context of the scene.
4. The Golden Rule: Players cannot verbally name or define the object until its function has been undeniably established through physical use and partner agreement.
5. Players must use 'Physical Yes-And' to build on each other's object work. If Player A treats the object as incredibly heavy and hot, Player B must treat it the same way when they take it.
6. Once the physical reality and function are firmly locked in, a player may finally name the object out loud (e.g., 'Careful, the reactor core is melting!') to help ground the narrative for the audience.
7. The facilitator calls 'Scene' after 2 to 3 minutes, or immediately following a strong narrative discovery or resolution.

## Coaching Notes
- Encourage the audience to snap or applaud when the players successfully establish and finally name the object, celebrating the moment of discovery.
- Use the constraint to eliminate 'talking head' scenes by making physical interaction the primary driver of the plot.
- Focus on training physical 'Yes-And' and non-verbal agreement.
- Allow the constraints to create a strong atmosphere of discovery and mystery.

## Variations
- Invisible Relic: Played entirely with space objects (mime) instead of physical props. The facilitator assigns a weight and texture, and players must maintain that illusion.
- The Assembly Line: A warm-up for larger groups where one abstract object is passed down a line. Each player must physically transform its function without speaking before passing it to the next person.

## Why It Works
This forces improvisers out of their heads and into their bodies, effectively eliminating 'talking head' scenes and training deep physical agreement.

## Safety & Inclusion
Ensure all physical objects are free of sharp edges, splinters, or breakable parts. For players with mobility limitations, the game can easily be played seated at a table using smaller, hand-held objects. Facilitators should emphasize that 'physical Yes-And' does not require acrobatics or strain; it is about clear, deliberate movement and respecting physical boundaries.
