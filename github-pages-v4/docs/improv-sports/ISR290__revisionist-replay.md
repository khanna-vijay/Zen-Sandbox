# Revisionist Replay

![Revisionist Replay — infographic](img/ISR290.jpg){ .game-hero }

> Perform a brief scene, then replay it twice while hilariously justifying wild new truths from the audience using the exact same dialogue and blocking.

## Overview
A fast-paced, competitive short-form game where players perform a brief, grounded scene, then replay that exact same scene two more times. Before each replay, the Referee gets a wild new 'truth' from the audience. Players must repeat their original dialogue and physical blocking as closely as possible, but hilariously justify every word and action to fit the new reality.

## Setup
2 to 3 players and a Referee to run the game and call fouls. Get a simple audience suggestion for an everyday location and a mundane physical activity (e.g., 'A laundromat, folding clothes').

## How to Play
1. The Base Scene: Players perform a grounded, 30 to 45-second scene establishing clear physical actions, distinct dialogue, and a definitive ending point. The Referee blows the whistle to stop the scene.
2. The First Revision: The Referee asks the audience to redefine one specific element that just happened (e.g., 'What were they actually folding?') to get a new truth (e.g., 'Parachutes that are currently on fire').
3. The First Replay: Players reset to their starting positions and replay the exact same scene from the top, hitting the same lines and physical blocking. They must act as though the audience's new truth was always a fact, using inflection and physical struggle to justify their original lines.
4. The Second Revision: The Referee blows the whistle at the end of the first replay and asks the audience for one final, character-driven twist (e.g., 'What was Player A's true secret motive for being there?').
5. The Final Replay: Players reset and replay the scene one last time, layering BOTH the first and second revisions into their performance. They must push their justifications to the comedic limit while strictly maintaining the original script.
6. Scoring: The Referee awards up to 5 points based on how cleverly the team justifies the original lines to fit the new truths without altering the core script. The Referee can call a 'Script Dropping Foul' if players completely abandon their original dialogue.

## Coaching Notes
- Encourage extreme 'Yes, And' justification to make the new realities fit the old script.
- Players need intense listening and memory retention to recall their exact lines and blocking from the base scene.
- Focus on high-energy repetition with clear comedic escalation.
- Embrace constraint-based creativity; the comedy comes from the struggle to make the original words fit the bizarre new context.

## Variations
- Genre Revision: Instead of a factual change, the audience provides a radically different genre (e.g., 'Horror' or 'Sci-Fi'), and the exact same lines must be justified within that genre's tropes.
- Inner Monologue Replay: A third player acts as the 'inner voice' during the replays, speaking the subtext of the new reality out loud while the main players stick strictly to the original script.

## Why It Works
It develops intense listening, memory retention, and extreme justification skills by forcing players to creatively adapt to severe constraints without changing their foundational script.

## Safety & Inclusion
Keep the initial physical actions grounded and safe so they can be easily repeated without injury. The Referee must actively filter audience suggestions for 'secret motives' to ensure they remain clean, family-friendly, and avoid non-consensual or inappropriate relationship dynamics.
