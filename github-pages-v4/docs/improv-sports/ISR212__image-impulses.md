# Image Impulses

![Image Impulses — infographic](img/ISR212.jpg){ .game-hero }

> Use evocative image cards or tactile objects to trigger spontaneous, non-verbal physical and emotional impulses.

## Overview
A non-competitive ensemble warm-up that uses evocative image cards or tactile objects to trigger spontaneous, non-verbal physical and emotional impulses. By removing the pressure to invent a narrative, players bypass intellectual planning and connect deeply with their bodies, voices, and each other.

## Setup
Players sit or stand in a circle. Place a deck of highly evocative image cards (e.g., art postcards or abstract illustration cards) face down in the center. Each player draws 3 to 5 cards. The facilitator establishes the primary focus: 'Respond instantly and physically to the stimulus, bypassing the intellect without trying to make logical sense.'

## How to Play
1. The Catalyst: One player volunteers to start as the Catalyst. They select one card from their hand that sparks a strong feeling, placing it face up in the center.
2. The First Impulse: Without speaking or explaining, the Catalyst immediately offers a brief, spontaneous physical action, posture, or non-verbal sound inspired by the card. This must be an abstract, felt response (e.g., a heavy sigh and a slumped posture), not a literal pantomime.
3. The Ripple Effect: Moving clockwise, the next player selects a card from their hand that intuitively resonates with the Catalyst's card, impulse, or the energy in the room. They place it down.
4. Integration: As they place their card, the second player adds their own physical or vocal impulse, integrating it with the Catalyst's ongoing energy. They do not try to tell a story; they simply add to the emotional 'canvas'.
5. Completion: The round continues until every player has contributed a card and an impulse, creating a collective, abstract physical and vocal atmosphere. The energy is held for a moment, then released.
6. Reset: The role of Catalyst passes to the left, players draw a replacement card, and a new round begins.

## Coaching Notes
- The facilitator actively side-coaches. If players start pantomiming literal actions (e.g., pretending to drive a car because a car is pictured), say: 'Abstract the movement,' 'Focus on the feeling, not the object,' or 'Let go of the story, just breathe and sound.'
- As an ensemble exercise, there are no points, judges, or audience suggestions. Guide the process by prioritizing the players' internal experience and ensemble connection over a polished product.
- Maintain a low-pressure environment to remove the anxiety of 'inventing' improv, focusing purely on reaction and presence.

## Variations
- Impulse to Scene: Once the group has built a collective physical and vocal atmosphere, the facilitator calls 'Scene!' Two players step into the center, maintaining their current physical and emotional state. They begin a spoken, two-person scene, allowing the narrative to emerge naturally from the established feeling rather than inventing a plot beforehand.
- Soundscape Only / Movement Only: Limit the impulses to purely vocal sounds (creating a choir of emotion) or purely silent physical movements (creating a moving sculpture) to isolate specific skills.

## Why It Works
By establishing a primary focus on bypassing the intellect, players respond physically and emotionally to a stimulus. This removes the anxiety of inventing improv and focuses purely on reaction, presence, and ensemble connection.

## Safety & Inclusion
Accessibility (Visual Impairment): Replace the image cards with a 'mystery bag' of varied tactile objects (e.g., a piece of velvet, a jagged rock, a squishy ball, a heavy metal gear). Players draw an object, feel its texture, weight, and temperature, and let that tactile sensation inspire their impulse. Physical Safety: Remind players to respect their own physical boundaries and those of others. Impulses should not involve forceful contact or unsafe movements. Emotional Safety: The abstract nature of the game can bring up unexpected emotions. The facilitator should ensure a supportive, non-judgmental environment where all authentic reactions are validated.
