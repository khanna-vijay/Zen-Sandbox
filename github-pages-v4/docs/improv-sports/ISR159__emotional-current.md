# Emotional Current

![Emotional Current — infographic](img/ISR159.jpg){ .game-hero }

> Pass an imaginary object imbued with a specific physical weight and a strong emotion, transforming it before passing it on.

## Overview
A facilitator-led ensemble warm-up where players pass an imaginary object imbued with a specific physical weight and a strong emotion. Upon receiving the object, a player instantly embodies its current physical and emotional state—vocalizing a sigh or gibberish sound to match—before physically molding it into a completely new object and emotion to pass to the next person.

## Setup
Players stand in a single circle with enough room to move their arms freely. The facilitator stands on the edge of the circle to side-coach. No props or stage setup are required.

## How to Play
1. The facilitator hands an imaginary object to one player, declaring its physical property and emotion (e.g., 'Here is a furious bowling ball' or 'Catch this melancholic feather').
2. The receiving player catches the object, instantly letting its weight, texture, and emotion affect their entire body. They must immediately make a vocal sound (a sigh, grunt, or short gibberish phrase) that externalizes this specific emotional and physical state.
3. While holding the object, the player visibly and deliberately molds it into something entirely new. The physical shape, weight, and emotion must shift simultaneously (e.g., the heavy, angry ball is stretched and fluffed into a light, joyful ribbon).
4. Once the transformation is complete, the player makes clear eye contact with anyone else in the circle and passes the new object. The style of the pass (a heavy heave, a delicate toss, a sharp slide) must clearly communicate the object's new physical properties.
5. The receiving player catches it, instantly taking on the new state and vocalizing it, then morphs it into a third state, and passes it on.
6. The current never stops. If a pass is dropped or the emotion is unclear, the receiver simply picks up whatever they think it is, feels it, and morphs it.

## Coaching Notes
- Flow over Perfection: There are no fouls or mistakes. If a pass is dropped or the emotion is unclear, simply side-coach the receiver to 'pick up whatever you think it is, feel it, and morph it.'
- Encourage players to utilize vocalization and gibberish to bypass intellectual overthinking and help externalize their internal state.
- Demand high physical commitment and active non-verbal listening. Players must carefully watch the pass to know what they are catching.
- Remind the group that the goal is achieving a seamless, continuous flow of energy and clear, uninhibited transformations.

## Variations
- Milling Current: Instead of standing in a circle, players walk randomly around the room. Passes must happen on the move, requiring heightened spatial awareness, peripheral vision, and strong eye contact to initiate a pass.
- Speed Current (No Morph): The object and emotion stay exactly the same, but the speed of passing increases. This tests the group's ability to maintain the exact same physical and emotional reality as a collective without letting the object 'degrade' over time.

## Why It Works
It isolates and trains two crucial improv skills simultaneously: object permanence (space work) and emotional embodiment. By eliminating competition and 'fouls', it fosters a highly safe, process-oriented, and supportive training environment.

## Safety & Inclusion
Players should be coached to pass objects safely, avoiding aggressive throwing motions directed at faces. The facilitator should monitor emotional choices, guiding players away from genuine trauma or distress and keeping the emotional range theatrical, playful, and safe. For players with limited mobility, the 'weight' and 'morphing' can be entirely adapted to upper-body movement, facial expressions, and vocalizations without needing to move around the room.
