# The Split-Focus Gauntlet

![The Split-Focus Gauntlet — infographic](img/ISR179.jpg){ .game-hero }

> Max out your cognitive load with a continuous physical task to bypass your inner critic and react intuitively.

## Overview
The Split-Focus Gauntlet is an advanced scene-work exercise designed to force performers out of their heads by maxing out their cognitive load. Players are assigned a continuous, demanding physical task that they must maintain flawlessly while simultaneously performing a standard three-minute improv scene. By occupying the brain's intellectual processing power with a physical point of focus, players bypass their inner critic and are forced to react intuitively, resulting in more authentic, grounded, and spontaneous scene work.

## Setup
Format: Training and Warm-Up. Space: An open stage or classroom area. No props are required, though real objects can be used for variations. The facilitator leads the exercise, assigns tasks, and side-coaches.

## How to Play
1. The facilitator brings two players to the stage and gets a basic suggestion for a scene, such as a relationship and a location.
2. Before the scene begins, the facilitator assigns each player a unique, continuous, deliberate physical task. These must be active environmental interactions, not bodily tics. Examples include: sorting a massive pile of imaginary laundry, untangling a giant knot of thick rope, balancing an imaginary book on the head, or continuously sweeping a large floor.
3. The facilitator instructs the players to begin their physical tasks in silence for ten seconds. This allows them to lock in the muscle memory, establish the weight and reality of the object, and center their focus entirely on the physical action.
4. The facilitator calls 'Action.' The players begin their scene, but they must never stop or pause their physical task. The physical task must always take priority over the dialogue.
5. After two to three minutes, the facilitator calls 'Scene.' The group briefly discusses how the cognitive load affected the performance, noting how the dialogue became more natural when the brain was distracted by the physical task.

## Coaching Notes
- The facilitator actively side-coaches during the scene. If a player freezes their body or drops their task to think of a clever line, the facilitator immediately calls out 'Keep sorting!' or 'Don't drop the rope!'
- The observable metric for being 'in your head' is the cessation of physical movement. This creates a strong, observable metric for the facilitator to see exactly when a player drops their focus or starts overthinking.
- The 'win' condition for the players is simply maintaining their physical task continuously without dropping it to think.
- This exercise generates highly physical, dynamic stage pictures rather than static talking-head scenes, so encourage players to fully commit to the physicality.

## Variations
- Shared Task: Instead of individual tasks, both players must accomplish a single continuous task together, such as moving a heavy imaginary sofa back and forth or folding a giant parachute, while performing a scene about something completely unrelated.
- Real Objects: For beginners or players who struggle with imaginary object work, use safe, physical props. Have them continuously toss a soft ball back and forth, or sort a real deck of cards into suits while doing the scene.

## Why It Works
It forces players out of their heads by maxing out cognitive load. By occupying the brain's intellectual processing power with a physical point of focus, it bypasses the intellectual censor, leading to more authentic and spontaneous emotional reactions.

## Safety & Inclusion
Crucial Safety Rule: Facilitators must only assign deliberate, environmental actions like painting a wall or sorting cards. Never assign bodily tics, twitches, or involuntary vocalizations. This prevents offensive caricatures and avoids mimicking neurodivergent stimming or conditions like Tourette's. For accessibility, physical tasks can be easily adapted for seated players or those with limited mobility by assigning tabletop tasks, such as sorting imaginary coins or knitting.
