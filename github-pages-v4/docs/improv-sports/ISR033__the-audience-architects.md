# The Audience Architects

![The Audience Architects — infographic](img/ISR033.jpg){ .game-hero }

> The audience acts as assistant referees, cheering to award points and using a unified 'Critique Cluck' to flag improv fouls.

## Overview
In this highly interactive competitive short-form game, the audience is deputized as assistant referees. They collaboratively build the scene's score by cheering to award points for strong improv choices, and use a unified 'Critique Cluck' sound to pause the action if they spot a foul. To keep the pace fast and safe, the referee heavily curates these interruptions, limiting them to three per game and rewarding players if the audience's critique is deemed invalid.

## Setup
Requires two teams of improvisers and one highly experienced referee. No props or set pieces are needed. Before the game begins, the referee teaches the audience two mechanics: loud cheers for positive reinforcement, and a collective 'Cluck-Cluck' sound to flag potential improv fouls (like bad puns, wimping, or stalling). The referee explicitly states the audience only has three 'Cluck' interruptions for the entire game, ensuring they use them wisely.

## How to Play
1. The referee gets a standard suggestion to start a scene featuring two players from one team.
2. As the scene progresses, the referee listens for massive, spontaneous audience cheers in response to great improv moves (e.g., a brilliant 'Yes, And', stellar object work, or a perfect callback).
3. When heard, the referee briefly blows the whistle, announces 'The Architects approve!', awards a point, and play continues seamlessly.
4. If the players commit a foul (Groaner, Delay, Wimp Out, etc.), the audience can initiate the 'Critique Cluck'. Instead of tracking individual sounds, the referee waits for a 'general consensus'—a noticeable wave of clucks from the crowd.
5. Upon hearing consensus, the referee blows the whistle, yells 'Freeze!', and pauses the scene. This consumes one of the audience's three allowed interruptions.
6. The referee approaches an audience member who clucked, maintaining strict control of the interaction. The ref asks, 'Architect, what specific improv foul did you witness?' to keep the focus on game mechanics rather than personal insults.
7. The referee makes a final ruling. If the foul is valid, the offending team loses a point. If the critique is invalid, nitpicky, or mean-spirited, the referee declares 'Invalid Cluck!' and awards a point to the performing team for their resilience.
8. The scene resumes immediately. Once the audience has used their three interruptions, the referee announces they are 'out of flags' and the remainder of the scene plays out with only positive point-scoring allowed.

## Coaching Notes
- The 'Invalid Cluck' rule is crucial; it turns a failed audience critique into a point for the players, discouraging spam and rewarding improvisers for staying confident under pressure.
- Use this game to teach the crowd the actual rules and vocabulary of competitive improv, such as fouls, wimping, and driving the scene.
- Maintain strict control of the audience interaction during a foul call to keep the pacing tight and the focus on the game mechanics.
- Rely on the three-interruption limit and the consensus trigger to self-regulate the pacing of the scene.

## Variations
- The Positivity Bell: Instead of calling fouls, the audience is taught a specific sound (like 'Ding!') to demand bonus points for specific, pre-determined actions, like a perfect rhyme or a brilliant physical choice.
- VIP Architect: For smaller crowds or corporate shows, select one specific audience volunteer to sit near the stage with a literal challenge flag. Only they can stop the scene to call a foul, acting as the sole guest referee.

## Why It Works
It empowers the audience by making them active participants in the scoring and officiating, while the built-in resilience reward protects players from overly harsh crowds and keeps the scene moving.

## Safety & Inclusion
The referee must heavily curate the audience interviews. If an audience member makes a mean-spirited comment about a player's appearance or personal traits, the referee must immediately shut it down with 'We critique the improv, not the improviser,' declare an Invalid Cluck, award the players a point, and move on. Additionally, if the audience clucks for a content foul, the referee should not ask the audience to repeat the offensive statement; they should simply acknowledge the foul, deduct the point, and pivot the scene.
