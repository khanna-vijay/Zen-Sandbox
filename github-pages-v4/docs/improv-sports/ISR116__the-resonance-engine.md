# The Resonance Engine

![The Resonance Engine — infographic](img/ISR116.jpg){ .game-hero }

> The observing group creates a continuous, audible pulse that dictates the speed, tension, and emotional intensity of the players' scene.

## Overview
A physical ensemble game where the observing group creates a continuous, audible pulse (like a collective hum or rhythmic tap) that dictates the speed, tension, and emotional intensity of the players performing a grounded, everyday scene. It trains deep listening and physical connection without the pressure of being witty.

## Setup
Designate 2 to 4 players to be on stage. The rest of the workshop group or audience acts as 'The Engine' and remains seated. No props or set pieces are needed. The facilitator acts as the conductor for The Engine.

## How to Play
1. Assign the players on stage a relatable, mundane physical activity to perform together silently (e.g., folding a massive parachute, cleaning up a messy kitchen, or assembling flat-pack furniture).
2. Instruct the seated group (The Engine) to create a baseline audible pulse. This should be a collective, continuous sound, such as a steady thigh-tap, a low hum, or a rhythmic snap.
3. The players begin their scene in silence. They must match their physical speed, muscle tension, and breathing to the exact tempo and volume of The Engine's pulse.
4. The facilitator acts as a conductor, using simple hand signals to direct The Engine to raise or lower the volume and tempo of the pulse.
5. As the pulse changes, the players must instantly adapt their physical movements and the emotional stakes of their mundane task. A slow, quiet hum means slow-motion, low-tension movement; a fast, loud tap means frantic, high-stakes, urgent movement.
6. Players must maintain intense physical awareness of each other, ensuring they react not just to the sound, but to how their scene partners are physically manifesting the sound.
7. Call 'Scene' or 'Freeze' after 2 to 3 minutes, or when the group has successfully navigated a full arc of tension from low to high and back down.

## Coaching Notes
- Remind players to drop the pressure to be verbal or witty; the focus is entirely on physical reaction and connection.
- Ensure players maintain intense physical awareness of each other, not just the sound.
- Use the audience actively; giving them a concrete, audible way to participate drives the action and makes them co-creators.
- Guide the Engine through a full arc of tension, from low to high and back down, to give the scene structure.

## Variations
- The Emotional Engine: Instead of just speed and tension, the facilitator directs The Engine to provide a sound with a specific emotional quality (e.g., a sinister hiss, a joyful sigh, a nervous chatter). The players must let this sound dictate the emotional relationship of their grounded scene.
- Player-Led Pulse: Reverse the dynamic. The Engine must closely watch the players. If a player organically speeds up their movement or increases their physical tension, The Engine must match that change with their sound, creating a continuous feedback loop of action and audio.

## Why It Works
It trains deep listening and physical connection by forcing players to react to an external auditory stimulus and their partners' physical manifestations of it, completely removing the pressure of verbal invention.

## Safety & Inclusion
Players should establish physical boundaries before starting, as high-tension movements can lead to accidental collisions. Emphasize that 'high tension' does not mean aggressive or unsafe physical contact. For sensory-sensitive participants, the facilitator must ensure the 'pulse' does not become overwhelmingly loud. Participants with mobility restrictions can easily participate as vital members of The Engine.
