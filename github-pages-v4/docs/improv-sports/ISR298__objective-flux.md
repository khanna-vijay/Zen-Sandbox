# Objective Flux

![Objective Flux — infographic](img/ISR298.jpg){ .game-hero }

> The Host verbally injects sudden emotional, status, or objective shifts mid-scene that players must instantly adopt and justify.

## Overview
A narrative long-form scene where the Host verbally injects sudden emotional, status, or objective shifts mid-scene. Players must instantly adopt and justify these new realities. This forces dynamic character arcs, tests their ability to 'be changed,' and prevents the story from flatlining.

## Setup
Two players on stage. A Host/Director off to the side with a microphone. A panel of Judges (if playing in a competitive narrative format). Ask the audience for a suggestion of an everyday, relatable location.

## How to Play
1. The Host takes the location suggestion and the two players begin a grounded, realistic scene.
2. Allow the players 1 to 2 minutes to firmly establish their characters, their relationship, and the base reality of the scene.
3. At a pivotal moment, the Host verbally calls out a direct, instantly playable shift for one of the players (e.g., 'Player A, you are now terrified of Player B'). The actors do not break character; they simply absorb the direction.
4. The targeted player must immediately manifest this new reality through their physicality, tone, and dialogue. Crucially, they must justify why they suddenly feel this way based on whatever was just said or done in the scene.
5. The partner must 'Yes, And' this sudden change, observing their partner's new behavior and reacting authentically to it.
6. The Host should only inject 1 to 2 shifts total per scene. This is critical: it gives the actors the necessary time to actually play, explore, and heighten the new reality rather than scrambling to keep up with constant interruptions.
7. The scene concludes when the narrative reaches a satisfying resolution or the emotional consequences of the final shift have peaked.

## Coaching Notes
- In a judged format, Judges score the scene from 1-5 based on narrative justification (how seamlessly the actors made the sudden shift make sense in the story), emotional commitment, and how generously the partner supported the change.
- Simplified, visceral prompts allow for instant, physical reactions rather than deep internal processing.
- Verbal callouts maintain scene momentum and flow, eliminating the clunkiness of physical cards.
- The audience provides the initial location and enjoys the dramatic irony of hearing the Host's instructions out loud.

## Variations
- Secret Flux: Instead of the Host calling it out live, players are handed a folded slip of paper before the scene with a simple shift (e.g., 'At some point, you realize you are in love with them'). They must initiate the shift organically without the audience knowing the prompt beforehand.
- Genre Flux: Instead of internal character shifts, the Host calls out sudden genre or stylistic changes (e.g., 'This is now a Noir detective film', 'You are now in a soap opera') that the players must instantly justify.

## Why It Works
It forces players to actively demonstrate 'being changed' in real-time and generates instant narrative twists that prevent scenes from flatlining. It heavily exercises character objectives and status dynamics.

## Safety & Inclusion
Host prompts must avoid dictating physical contact (e.g., avoid 'You want to kiss them' or 'You want to hit them'). Focus entirely on internal emotional states, status dynamics, or urgent needs (e.g., 'You are intimidated by them', 'You desperately need their approval', 'You are overwhelmed with guilt'). Players always retain the agency to justify the shift in a way that respects their own physical boundaries and comfort levels.
