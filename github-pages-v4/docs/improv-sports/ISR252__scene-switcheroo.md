# Scene Switcheroo

![Scene Switcheroo — infographic](img/ISR252.jpg){ .game-hero }

> A referee-controlled, fast-paced improv game of dynamic scene shifts.

## Overview
Two players perform a scene that is repeatedly hijacked by the Referee, who injects pre-gathered audience suggestions to instantly change the location, relationship, or objective. Players must seamlessly justify the sudden shift without dropping the narrative thread. It is a high-speed test of 'Yes, And' and rapid justification, played for points in a competitive short-form match.

## Setup
Two players from one team take the stage. The Referee acts as the host and timekeeper. Before the scene begins, the Referee asks the audience for a mundane starting activity (e.g., 'folding laundry'). Then, the Referee rapid-fire collects 4 to 5 'Switch Elements' from the audience (a mix of wild locations, bizarre relationships, and extreme objectives) and writes them down or memorizes them. The players hear these suggestions, but do not know when or in what order they will be deployed.

## How to Play
1. The Referee blows the whistle and yells 'GO!' The two players begin a grounded, realistic scene based on the initial mundane suggestion.
2. After 45 to 60 seconds, once the players have established their characters and the base reality, the Referee blows the whistle and shouts 'SWITCH!' followed immediately by one of the pre-gathered suggestions (e.g., 'SWITCH! You are now inside a volcano!').
3. The players must instantly adapt their physicality, dialogue, and narrative to make the new reality make sense within the context of what just happened.
4. The Referee allows the players another 45 to 60 seconds to explore and heighten this new reality before changing it again.
5. The Referee repeats the 'SWITCH!' command 2 to 3 more times, injecting the remaining pre-gathered suggestions at peak comedic moments.
6. After the final switch has been fully explored, the Referee blows the whistle and calls 'TIME!' (Total scene length should be roughly 3 to 4 minutes).
7. The Referee awards up to 5 points per switch based on how smoothly and creatively the players justified the change, using audience cheers to gauge success.
8. The opposing team then takes the stage for their turn with a fresh set of suggestions.

## Coaching Notes
- Players cannot use 'waking up from a dream' or 'we are actors on a set' as a crutch; they must logically (or absurdly) justify how the original activity turned into the new reality.
- Paced escalation is critical: giving players 45-60 seconds between switches ensures the scene has time to breathe and be funny, rather than just being chaotic.
- The Referee acts as a dynamic editor, controlling the rhythm and energy of the game. Pre-loaded suggestions keep the scene's momentum alive without stopping to ask the audience for new prompts.
- Fouls are strictly enforced: a 'Delay of Game' foul (-2 points) is called if players hesitate, argue, or ignore the switch. A 'clean-content' foul (loss of points and immediate scene stop) is called for any non-family-friendly content.

## Variations
- Blind Switcheroo: Instead of the Referee gathering the switches from the audience, the opposing team writes 4 wild switches on index cards. The Referee reads them blindly during the scene, surprising both the playing team and the audience.
- Genre Switch: Instead of changing the location or relationship, the Referee switches the cinematic or theatrical genre (e.g., 'SWITCH! Film Noir!', 'SWITCH! Soap Opera!'). Players must instantly adopt the tropes of that genre while maintaining the existing narrative.

## Why It Works
It forces players to exercise extreme mental agility and 'Yes, And' skills by rapidly justifying sudden shifts in reality while maintaining the narrative thread.

## Safety & Inclusion
The Referee strictly vets all audience suggestions upfront to ensure they are family-friendly and accessible. Players must maintain spatial awareness and avoid dangerous physical choices (e.g., sudden running or lifting) during transitions. Players must respect physical boundaries; if a relationship switches to something intimate (e.g., 'lovers'), it must be played through dialogue, eye contact, and status, never through forced physical contact.
