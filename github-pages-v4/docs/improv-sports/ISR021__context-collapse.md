# Context Collapse

![Context Collapse — infographic](img/ISR021.jpg){ .game-hero }

> Players must instantly justify their exact physical positions and emotions as the referee rapidly shouts out wildly different new contexts.

## Overview
A fast-paced competitive short-form game where two players begin a standard scene, but every 30 to 45 seconds, the referee shouts a completely new, unrelated context. Players must instantly continue the scene from their exact physical positions, justifying their current posture and emotions within the brand-new reality without missing a beat.

## Setup
Requires two players, a referee with a whistle, and a bare stage. Before the scene begins, the referee asks the audience for an initial mundane activity to start the scene, followed by a list of four to five wildly different, high-stakes contexts (e.g., defusing a bomb, trapped in a submarine, competing in a spelling bee). The referee keeps this list to use during the game.

## How to Play
1. The referee gets the initial suggestion and the list of four to five contexts from the audience.
2. The two players begin a scene based on the initial mundane suggestion.
3. After 30 to 45 seconds, once the scene has established a clear dynamic and physical picture, the referee blows the whistle and calls out the first new context.
4. Players must immediately continue the narrative from their exact current physical positions and emotional states. They do not restart the scene or repeat previous lines.
5. Players must seamlessly justify their current physical reality in the new context. For example, if a player is holding a mimed laundry basket and the context shifts to 'defusing a bomb,' that basket immediately becomes the bomb casing, and the next line of dialogue moves the bomb-defusing story forward.
6. Players must never explicitly acknowledge the shift or act confused; they must treat the new reality as if it has always been the reality.
7. The players explore this new reality for another 30 to 45 seconds, building a new mini-narrative before the referee blows the whistle to introduce the next context.
8. The game ends after all contexts have been used, culminating in a final resolution.
9. At the end of the game, the referee awards 5 points to the team that wins the audience's applause.

## Coaching Notes
- The referee can call standard fouls during play, such as a Delay Foul if a player hesitates or fails to justify the new context, or a content foul for inappropriate content.
- Encourage players to make immediate, bold physical and vocal choices.
- Remind players not to drop their physical mimed objects or change posture when the whistle blows; the magic is in the justification of the exact freeze-frame.
- The audience is highly engaged because they provided the list of contexts upfront and are waiting to see how the players will handle their specific suggestions, so lean into their anticipation.

## Variations
- Genre Collapse: Instead of situational contexts, the referee shifts the cinematic or theatrical genre (e.g., Noir, Sci-Fi, Soap Opera, Horror) every 45 seconds.
- Emotional Collapse: The shifts are extreme emotional states or relationship dynamics (e.g., bitter rivals, deeply in love, terrified of each other) that the players must instantly adopt while continuing the same narrative.

## Why It Works
It forces players to make immediate, bold physical and vocal choices, testing the core improv skill of instant justification and 'Yes, And'.

## Safety & Inclusion
The referee must actively filter audience suggestions to ensure all contexts are safe, family-friendly, and appropriate. Players should avoid sudden, reckless physical movements when a shift is called; the focus is on justifying the current posture, not throwing themselves around the stage. The game is highly accessible as it relies on verbal justification and acting choices, and can easily be played entirely seated or with mobility aids.
