# The Chrysalis

![The Chrysalis — infographic](img/IS174.jpg){ .game-hero }

> Improvisers navigate a scene where radical, externally imposed character and relationship transformations are triggered by drawing cards.

## Overview
The Chrysalis is a narrative game driven by radical, externally imposed character and relationship transformations. Players begin a standard scene, but at key intervals, they draw 'Chrysalis Cards' containing dramatic, often absurd prompts. Improvisers must immediately integrate and collaboratively justify these new realities, layering them to create a unique, emergent plot.

## Setup
Prepare a 'Chrysalis Deck' of cards, each containing a single, dramatic character or relationship transformation prompt (e.g., 'You are now convinced your partner is secretly a highly sophisticated AI', 'You just realized you can control the weather with your eyebrows'). The MC gets a single audience suggestion for a foundational scene premise (e.g., 'A first date'). Optionally, assign basic high-level character traits relevant to the suggestion before starting.

## How to Play
1. The two improvisers begin a scene based on the audience's suggestion, establishing their characters, location, and initial interaction.
2. At intervals throughout the scene (e.g., every 60-90 seconds), the MC cues a transformation by calling 'Chrysalis Event!' or playing a sound effect.
3. One improviser (or alternating improvisers) draws a card from the Chrysalis Deck.
4. The improviser must immediately integrate the card's prompt into the scene as a mandatory, non-negotiable reality shift.
5. If the card applies to the drawer (e.g., 'You are now...'), they clearly embody the new reality. If it applies to the partner (e.g., 'Your partner is...'), they endow their partner with this new reality. If it applies to both, both adapt immediately.
6. Improvisers must actively help their partner justify the new reality, retrospectively or proactively creating reasons for why this change is happening without stopping or breaking the scene.
7. Subsequent Chrysalis Cards layer new realities onto the scene, building upon or radically altering what came before.
8. The scene concludes after 3-4 minutes or when the MC calls 'Scene', leaving the characters in their new, bizarre reality.

## Coaching Notes
- Prioritize narrative development over gags. Treat the transformations as structural demands that redefine characters and stakes, not just opportunities for simple punchlines.
- Embrace 'Being Changed'. The core mechanic relies on cheerfully and creatively accepting external prompts that force radical adaptation.
- Focus on collaborative scene-building ('Making Your Partner Look Good'). When endowing a partner, do it gracefully; when endowed, enthusiastically 'Yes, And' the new reality.
- Pay attention to status shifts. Many cards will naturally alter the status dynamics (e.g., human becomes plant, friend becomes secret leader); play these new relationships authentically.
- Justify inventively. Make the absurd transformations make sense within the heightened reality of the scene to maintain narrative coherence.

## Why It Works
It tests the core skill of 'Being Changed' by forcing improvisers to radically adapt their character, motivations, and understanding of the scene's reality. It also applies 'Yes, And' at a meta-level, requiring players to accept fundamental shifts in identity while practicing inventive justification and status play.

## Safety & Inclusion
Ensure Chrysalis Card prompts avoid forcing non-consensual physical contact or crossing personal boundaries. Players should maintain physical safety and respect each other's limits during sudden character or status shifts.
