# The Catalyst Canvas

![The Catalyst Canvas — infographic](img/ISR287.jpg){ .game-hero }

> An off-stage Architect injects sudden, physical changes to the environment that performers must instantly physicalize and justify.

## Overview
A non-competitive workshop exercise where an off-stage Architect injects sudden, physical changes to the environment while a scene is in progress. Performers must instantly physicalize and justify these bizarre shifts without stopping the scene. This trains their ability to commit to the physical space and adapt to unexpected offers.

## Setup
2 to 3 players on stage and 1 player (or the facilitator) off-stage acting as the Environmental Architect. The Architect holds a bell, buzzer, or clicker and has a list of short Catalyst ideas ready. Ensure the stage is clear of tripping hazards.

## How to Play
1. Two or three players begin a grounded scene based on a simple location and relationship suggestion.
2. The off-stage Architect observes the scene and rings the bell when they want to challenge the players' physicality.
3. Without stopping or freezing the scene, the Architect loudly calls out a short, punchy environmental catalyst (e.g., 'The floor is sticky mud!', 'Gravity is halved!', 'The ceiling is shrinking!').
4. The on-stage players must instantly physicalize this new reality and justify it within the context of their scene, continuing their dialogue and character objectives.
5. Only ONE environmental constraint is active at a time. When the Architect rings the bell and calls a new catalyst, the previous constraint instantly vanishes and is replaced by the new one.
6. The Architect should space out the calls, giving players 30 to 60 seconds to fully explore and justify each physical constraint before introducing the next.
7. The scene ends after 3 to 4 minutes, or when a natural narrative conclusion is reached.

## Coaching Notes
- Live side-coaching without freezing the action keeps the scene's momentum alive.
- Limiting the game to a single active constraint prevents cognitive overload and physical chaos.
- The Architect role provides a structured way for off-stage players to practice directing and supporting scene work.
- Success is measured by the players' immediate physical commitment to the new environment and their ability to seamlessly justify the change without dropping their characters.

## Variations
- Emotional Canvas: Instead of physical environments, the Architect calls out sudden emotional shifts or genre changes (e.g., 'Everyone is deeply paranoid!', 'High-stakes action movie!').
- Prop Canvas: The Architect targets a specific object on stage rather than the whole environment (e.g., 'That chair is burning hot!', 'The broom is incredibly heavy!').

## Why It Works
It forces players to physically commit to the environment and actively justify bizarre changes, training their ability to adapt to unexpected offers while maintaining character objectives.

## Safety & Inclusion
Physical safety is paramount. Instruct players not to make choices that require unsafe falls or collisions. Ensure the stage is clear of actual tripping hazards. For players with mobility restrictions, the Architect should tailor catalysts to focus on upper-body physicality, sensory changes (e.g., 'The room smells like rotten eggs'), or object manipulation rather than full-body locomotion.
