# Narrative Flux

![Narrative Flux — infographic](img/ISR092.jpg){ .game-hero }

> An ensemble builds a story that is periodically reshaped by the audience choosing to Rewind, Fast-Forward, or Reframe the timeline.

## Overview
A long-form narrative game where an ensemble builds a story that is periodically reshaped by the audience. Acting as a live film director, the Host pauses the scene and asks the audience whether to 'Rewind,' 'Fast-Forward,' or 'Reframe' the story, getting a single suggestion to guide the shift. Players must instantly adapt to the new timeline, maintaining character truth while justifying the sudden narrative leap.

## Setup
4-6 improvisers playing as one team. A Host/Director who manages the timeline and audience interaction. A panel of Judges (if playing in a judged long-form match). The stage is bare, relying on space-work.

## How to Play
1. The Host gets a starting suggestion from the audience (e.g., a location, a relationship, or an unspoken secret) and the players begin a grounded, narrative scene.
2. The scene is allowed to breathe and develop for 3 to 4 minutes, establishing clear characters, status dynamics, and a core conflict.
3. At a pivotal moment or cliffhanger, the Host calls 'Freeze!' and turns to the audience, asking: 'Do we Rewind, Fast-Forward, or Reframe?'
4. The audience shouts their preference. The Host selects the loudest choice and asks for a one-word suggestion to inspire that specific shift.
5. If 'Rewind': The Host asks, 'What object or event triggered this?' (e.g., 'The Necklace'). The scene jumps back in time to that origin point.
6. If 'Fast-Forward': The Host asks, 'Where are they in five years?' (e.g., 'Prison'). The scene jumps ahead to the consequences of the frozen moment.
7. If 'Reframe': The Host asks, 'What is the hidden truth or genre of this scene?' (e.g., 'Sci-Fi' or 'Betrayal'). The scene stays in the present but the context radically shifts.
8. The Host announces the new reality. The players immediately resume the scene in this new context, justifying the gap and fully committing to the new reality.
9. This process repeats 2 to 3 times over a 10-15 minute piece, allowing each new timeline segment enough time to fully develop.
10. At the end of the piece, the Judges award a single score (1-5) based on narrative coherence, emotional commitment, and how brilliantly the players justified the timeline shifts.

## Coaching Notes
- Force players to aggressively justify and commit to new realities.
- Allow scenes to breathe, prioritizing strong theatrical craft and emotional stakes over rushed gags.
- The Host should streamline their role by using the simple, repeatable audience prompt rather than inventing complex options on the fly.
- In a competitive match, Judges score the overall piece 1-5 at the very end based on narrative arc, character consistency, and justification of the shifts, eliminating mid-game bookkeeping.

## Variations
- Genre Flux: Instead of time shifts, the audience only chooses new genres or directorial styles to reframe the existing scene every few minutes.
- Status Flux: The shifts focus entirely on character status (e.g., the Host asks the audience, 'Who suddenly gains all the power, and what is their weapon?').

## Why It Works
It gives the audience meaningful, structural control over the narrative direction while forcing players to aggressively justify and commit to new realities, prioritizing strong theatrical craft and emotional stakes.

## Safety & Inclusion
The Host must filter audience suggestions to ensure they are appropriate and avoid harmful tropes. Players should focus on emotional reactions and relationship dynamics rather than physicalizing dangerous or non-consensual situations during time jumps. Clear boundaries should be maintained regarding physical touch, especially when jumping into high-stakes or intimate moments.
