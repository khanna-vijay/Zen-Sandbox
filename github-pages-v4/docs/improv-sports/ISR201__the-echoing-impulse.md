# The Echoing Impulse

![The Echoing Impulse — infographic](img/ISR201.jpg){ .game-hero }

> Players pass an invisible internal impulse around a circle using deep eye contact and subtle physical shifts.

## Overview
A focused, non-verbal ensemble warm-up where players stand in a circle and pass an invisible internal impulse—a feeling, energy, or breath—to one another. Instead of pantomiming, players use deep eye contact and subtle, genuine physical shifts to receive the energy, let it affect them, and pass it on. It trains active listening, intuitive empathy, and authentic reactions over pre-planned acting.

## Setup
Players stand in a large circle facing inwards, with their arms relaxed at their sides. Ensure there is enough space between players so they can move freely, but keep them close enough to maintain clear eye contact. No props or chairs are needed. The facilitator stands just outside the circle to observe and side-coach.

## How to Play
1. The facilitator asks the group to stand in a neutral, relaxed position, take a collective breath, and make eye contact with one another.
2. One player begins by generating an internal impulse. This should be a sudden feeling, a specific breath pattern, or a shift in energy (e.g., a sudden wave of relief, a spark of curiosity, or a heavy sinking feeling).
3. The initiating player makes strong, unbroken eye contact with one specific person across the circle and 'sends' this impulse non-verbally, projecting the feeling through their gaze and subtle body language.
4. The receiving player maintains eye contact, takes a breath, and allows that impulse to physically affect them. They must not mime or 'act out' a scenario, but rather let the energy manifest as a genuine physical shift.
5. Once the receiving player has fully embodied the impulse, they let it organically shift or amplify within their own body, lock eyes with a new player, and send their new version of the impulse across the circle.
6. The game continues in a continuous flow, with the impulse bouncing around the circle. No talking is allowed.

## Coaching Notes
- Actively side-coach throughout, encouraging players to breathe, slow down, and trust their bodies rather than their brains.
- Guide players away from pantomime. If a player gets stuck in their head, say, 'Breathe it in, don't think it in.' If they mime, say, 'Drop the story, just give us the physical shift.'
- Emphasize concrete embodiment. A good embodiment looks like a sharp inhale, a relaxed spine, a clenched jaw, or a shift of weight, rather than broad, cartoonish acting like wiping a fake tear.
- Remind players that the focus is entirely on ensemble connection, process over product, and building a safe space for authentic, spontaneous reactions.

## Variations
- Impulse to Scene: The impulse is passed non-verbally 3 to 4 times. The final receiver lets the physical impulse trigger a spoken line of dialogue, and the sender steps into the center to begin a two-person scene based entirely on that energetic initiation.
- Sound and Movement: For a higher-energy variation, players are allowed to attach an abstract sound (not a recognizable word) to the physical impulse as they pass it, which the receiver must absorb, physicalize, and transform before passing.

## Why It Works
It trains active listening, intuitive empathy, and authentic reactions over pre-planned acting. Deep eye contact forces players to truly see and be seen by their scene partners before reacting, building profound ensemble trust.

## Safety & Inclusion
Emphasize that players should maintain physical boundaries; no touching is required or permitted during the pass. Eye contact can be intense or overstimulating for some neurodivergent players; facilitators should explicitly offer the modification to look at the bridge of the nose or the forehead instead. Keep the emotional impulses grounded and instruct players to avoid aggressive, violent, or threatening energy.
