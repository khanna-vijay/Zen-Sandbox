# Director's Remix

![Director's Remix — infographic](img/ISR002.jpg){ .game-hero }

> A host writes secret, game-changing directives on a whiteboard behind one player's back, forcing sudden narrative pivots that must be instantly justified.

## Overview
Director's Remix is a competitive challenge scene where a host writes secret, game-changing directives on a whiteboard behind one player's back. The player facing the board must immediately and boldly introduce this bizarre new reality into the scene, while their partner—who hasn't seen the board—must aggressively justify and 'Yes, And' the sudden narrative pivot. The audience is in on the secret, creating delicious dramatic irony as they watch the players scramble to rationalize the shifting reality.

## Setup
Requires 2 players on stage. A Host/Facilitator stands downstage or in the audience aisle with a large whiteboard and a thick, highly visible marker. Three Judges sit offstage with scorecards (1-5). The stage is positioned so Player A faces downstage (able to see the Host) and Player B faces upstage or profile (unable to see the Host).

## How to Play
1. The Host gets a standard scene suggestion from the audience (e.g., a location, a relationship, or an everyday activity).
2. The two players begin the scene, establishing a grounded base reality. Player A must ensure they are angled to see the Host, while Player B keeps their back to the Host.
3. While the scene flows, the Host writes a bold, game-changing directive on the whiteboard and holds it up for the audience and Player A to see (e.g., 'The floor is lava', 'You are both secretly spies', 'Player B is a ghost').
4. Player A must immediately and clearly introduce this new reality into the scene. They should NOT be subtle; they must make a strong, undeniable offer so their partner can easily understand the shift.
5. Player B, who has not seen the board, must instantly accept, justify, and 'Yes, And' this bizarre shift in reality, treating it as the most natural evolution of the story.
6. The Host wipes the board and writes 2-3 more directives throughout the scene. The Host can gesture for the players to physically switch stage positions so Player B becomes the one reading the board.
7. The scene concludes after a satisfying narrative arc is reached, or when the final directive has been successfully and hilariously justified.
8. At the end of the scene, the three Judges award scores from 1 to 5 based on the team's ability to instantly justify the narrative pivots, the clarity and boldness of the offers, and their commitment to the shifting reality without dropping character or blocking.

## Coaching Notes
- Dramatic Irony: Lean into the fact that the audience knows the secret prompt before the receiving player does; this builds great anticipation.
- Aggressive Justification: Remind players they must instantly rationalize absurd or sudden narrative shifts to save the scene's reality.
- Unbroken Flow State: Using a whiteboard eliminates the need to stop the scene, so encourage players to maintain momentum and pacing.
- Bold Endowments: Train the viewing player to make clear, undeniable offers rather than vague hints so their partner can catch on quickly.
- Judges should evaluate the team on their ability to instantly justify the narrative pivots, the clarity and boldness of the offers, and their commitment to the shifting reality without dropping character or blocking.

## Variations
- Emotional Remix: Instead of narrative facts, the whiteboard dictates sudden, extreme emotional shifts (e.g., 'You are terrified of the furniture') for the viewing player to adopt and the partner to justify.
- Genre Remix: The whiteboard dictates a sudden shift in theatrical genre or movie style (e.g., 'Film Noir', 'Sci-Fi', 'Soap Opera') that the viewing player must initiate through their dialogue and physicality.
- Three-Player Rotation: A three-person scene where the Host stands in the wings; whoever happens to be facing the wings gets the prompt, requiring hyper-awareness of stage picture and constant justification from the other two.

## Why It Works
It trains players to make clear, undeniable offers rather than vague hints, and forces aggressive justification to instantly rationalize absurd or sudden narrative shifts without breaking the scene's flow state.

## Safety & Inclusion
The Host must ensure whiteboard prompts are physically safe and content-appropriate (avoiding prompts that force inappropriate physical contact, cross boundaries, or rely on harmful stereotypes). If a player is visually impaired, the Host can use a discrete earpiece or a tap/whisper mechanic (briefly pausing the scene) instead of a whiteboard to deliver the secret directive.
