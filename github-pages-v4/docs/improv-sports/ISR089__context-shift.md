# Context Shift

![Context Shift — infographic](img/ISR089.jpg){ .game-hero }

> Players replay the final 15 seconds of a scene in a new genre, re-justifying the exact same actions to end on the exact same line.

## Overview
Players perform a scene that culminates in a specific audience-provided line of dialogue. Once the line is spoken, the host freezes the scene, gets a new genre or world-rule from the audience, and the players must rewind and replay the exact last 15 seconds of the scene leading up to that same line, completely re-justifying the action and dialogue in the new reality.

## Setup
2-3 experienced players, 1 Host/Director, and a panel of 3 Judges (competitive format). No props or specialized staging required.

## How to Play
1. The Host asks the audience for a single, standalone line of dialogue (e.g., 'I knew you'd bring that up,' or 'Why is it sticky?').
2. Two or three players start a scene from scratch, establishing relationships, status, and an environment. They play for about 60-90 seconds.
3. The players naturally steer the scene so that one player delivers the audience's line as a logical conclusion to the current action.
4. Immediately after the trigger line is spoken, the Host yells 'Freeze!'
5. The Host turns to the audience and asks for a simple, distinct shift, such as a movie genre or a weird rule for the world.
6. The Host yells 'Rewind 15 seconds!'
7. The players reset their physical positions to where they were about 15 seconds before the trigger line was spoken.
8. Players replay that exact 15-second sequence of dialogue and physical action, but filter it entirely through the new genre or rule.
9. The replay must culminate in the exact same trigger line, which now takes on a completely different meaning or justification based on the new context.
10. The Host can repeat this process 1-2 more times, freezing after the trigger line, getting a new suggestion, and forcing another 15-second replay.
11. The scene ends after the final replay of the trigger line, and the Judges award their scores from 1 to 5 based on narrative commitment, status play, and clever re-justification.

## Coaching Notes
- Keep audience prompts simple and accessible to avoid blank stares.
- Players must commit to remembering their exact physical positions and dialogue from the 15 seconds prior to the trigger line.
- Focus on narrative flexibility and status shifts to make the re-justification clear and satisfying.
- The cognitive load is high, so players should avoid overly complex physical actions in the initial scene.

## Variations
- Emotional Rewind: Instead of genres or world rules, the Host asks the audience for a specific, intense emotion for the scene (e.g., 'Paranoia,' 'Unbridled Joy').
- Physical Constraint Rewind: The Host asks for a physical limitation (e.g., 'The floor is lava,' 'Everyone is moving underwater') to challenge the players' physical justification.

## Why It Works
It isolates and gamifies the core improv skill of justification. By locking in the dialogue and physical actions, players are forced to rely entirely on context, status, and emotional shifts to make the scene make sense, creating a highly rewarding puzzle for both the performers and the audience.

## Safety & Inclusion
Because the game involves rapid shifts and physical resets, players should avoid dangerous lifts or stunts in the initial scene, as they will have to repeat them. The Host must filter audience suggestions to ensure genres or rules are appropriate, avoiding harmful tropes or stereotypes. Players with mobility restrictions can focus on verbal and emotional re-justification rather than strict physical repetition.
