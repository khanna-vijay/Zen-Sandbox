# Emotional Volleyball

![Emotional Volleyball — infographic](img/ISR114.jpg){ .game-hero }

> A high-energy, non-verbal warm-up where players play an imaginary game of volleyball, passing, setting, and spiking emotions instead of a ball.

## Overview
Players divide into two teams and play an imaginary game of volleyball using physical and emotional commitment. Instead of a physical ball, they pass, set, and spike 'feelings' across the net. The focus is entirely on ensemble connection, physical listening, and continuous emotional transformation.

## Setup
Divide players into two teams standing on opposite sides of an imaginary net. No props or specific stage setups are needed. A facilitator stands on the sideline to serve the ball and side-coach the action.

## How to Play
1. The facilitator 'serves' an invisible ball over the net to one team, calling out a starting emotion (e.g., 'Heavy Sadness!') and physically pantomiming a volleyball serve.
2. The receiving team must keep the ball in the air using classic volleyball moves (Dig, Set, Spike). Every touch must be accompanied by a full-body, non-verbal expression of an emotion.
3. The Dig (Absorb & Redirect): When receiving a hit from over the net, the player must physically register the impact of the incoming emotion, absorb it, and bump it up to a teammate. Example: The ball comes over as 'Rage.' The receiving player 'digs' by recoiling as if hit by a heavy weight, gasping, and popping it up as 'Startled Fear.'
4. The Set (Support & Heighten): The second player receives the ball and gently pushes it up for a teammate, heightening or subtly shifting the emotion. Example: The player receives 'Fear,' heightens it to 'Absolute Terror' with wide eyes and shaking hands, and sets it high in the air.
5. The Spike (Transform & Attack): The third player reaches up and smashes the ball over the net, completely transforming the emotion into a new, powerful state. Example: The player takes the 'Terror' and spikes it over the net as 'Arrogant Pride,' puffing out their chest and sneering as they swing.
6. The opposing team must now Dig the new emotion ('Arrogant Pride') and continue the volley with their own Dig, Set, and Spike.
7. If a player drops the ball, hesitates, intellectualizes the emotion (e.g., talking instead of feeling), or fails to physically show the change, the facilitator blows an imaginary whistle, calls 'Dead ball!', and serves a new emotion to restart.

## Coaching Notes
- Force players to physically commit and visibly register being changed by an offer.
- Encourage full-body listening and non-verbal communication.
- Watch out for players intellectualizing the emotion (e.g., talking instead of feeling); blow the whistle and restart if this happens.
- Remind players that there is no scoring or winning team; the focus is on continuous emotional transformation.

## Variations
- Slow-Motion Replay: The entire game is played in exaggerated slow motion. This forces players to sustain the physical toll of the emotions for longer and allows for deeper, more nuanced physical transformations.
- Gibberish Volleyball: Instead of being strictly non-verbal, players are allowed to yell a single gibberish word or phrase when they make contact with the ball, matching the vocal tone to the physical emotion.

## Why It Works
It forces players to physically commit and visibly register being changed by an offer, building full-body listening and non-verbal communication skills without the pressure of scene work.

## Safety & Inclusion
Physical safety is paramount: players do not need to actually dive, fall, or jump. A 'dig' can be mimed from a standing squat, and a 'spike' with a simple overhead reach, making it accessible for all mobility levels. Remind players to maintain spatial awareness to avoid collisions. Emotionally, keep the feelings theatrical and playful; players are always free to step off the court if an emotion feels too intense.
