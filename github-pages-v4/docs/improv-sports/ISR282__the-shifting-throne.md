# The Shifting Throne

![The Shifting Throne — infographic](img/ISR282.jpg){ .game-hero }

> A narrative long-form game where players' character status instantly shifts based on their interaction with ordinary stage props.

## Overview
A narrative long-form game where 2-3 improvisers' character status shifts based on their interaction with ordinary stage props. The audience secretly knows which prop holds High, Medium, or Low status. When a player touches a prop, a distinct audio cue tells them which status to instantly adopt, creating delicious dramatic irony and forcing players to actively practice the 'being changed' principle.

## Setup
You need 2-3 players and 3-5 neutral, safe-to-handle props (e.g., a hat, a rubber duck, a clipboard, a plastic flower) placed around the stage. The host needs three distinct noisemakers (e.g., a high-pitched bell, a wooden clapper, a low buzzer). You also need a low-tech way to show the audience the prop statuses, such as a whiteboard, large pre-written signs, or simply pointing and holding up fingers while the players' backs are turned.

## How to Play
1. Send the players offstage or have them turn their backs and cover their eyes.
2. The host assigns a fixed status (High, Medium, or Low) to each prop and reveals this only to the audience using a whiteboard or large signs. Crucially, these statuses are locked and do not change mid-scene.
3. Bring the players back and explain the audio cues: High Status = High-pitched Bell/Ding; Medium Status = Woodblock/Clap; Low Status = Low-pitched Buzzer/Honk.
4. Get a scene suggestion from the audience (e.g., a location or a relationship) and begin the scene.
5. Whenever a player touches a prop, the host immediately plays the corresponding audio cue.
6. Upon hearing the cue, the player must instantly justify a shift to that new status, maintaining their core character objective but altering their posture, tone, and tactics.
7. If a player puts down a prop, they maintain that status until they (or someone else) touch another prop. If two players touch the same prop, they both take on that status.
8. The scene concludes when a natural narrative resolution is reached or the status shifts create a perfect dramatic climax.

## Coaching Notes
- Focus on seamlessly justifying the sudden status shifts while maintaining emotional commitment and overall narrative coherence.
- Lean into the dramatic irony; the audience knows the 'traps' before the players trigger them.
- Elevate standard object work by attaching high stakes to prop interactions.
- Use the distinct audio cues to remove ambiguity, ensuring players know exactly how to react.
- In a competitive short-form match, a panel of judges scores the scene (1-5) based on justification, emotional commitment, and narrative coherence.

## Variations
- The Cursed Object (Beginner): Only one prop has a status (e.g., extreme high or extreme low). The rest are normal. Great for introducing the mechanic without overwhelming players.
- Emotion Objects (Alternative Focus): Instead of status, props are assigned distinct emotions (Joy, Rage, Sorrow) with corresponding cues, shifting the focus from power dynamics to emotional agility.

## Why It Works
It creates excellent dramatic irony by giving the audience secret information and forces players to actively practice the 'being changed' principle.

## Safety & Inclusion
Ensure all props are safe to handle, lightweight, and placed at accessible heights for all players regardless of mobility. Avoid fragile or sharp items. For Deaf or Hard of Hearing players, replace audio cues with distinct visual cues, such as colored lights or a side-coach holding up colored cards in their peripheral vision. Emphasize that status shifts should never be used to justify abusive or non-consensual physical behavior between characters.
