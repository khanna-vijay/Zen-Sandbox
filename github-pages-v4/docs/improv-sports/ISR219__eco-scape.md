# Eco-Scape

![Eco-Scape — infographic](img/ISR219.jpg){ .game-hero }

> One team physically embodies a dynamic environment while the opposing team improvises a scene within it, adapting to chaotic mid-scene events.

## Overview
Eco-Scape is a high-energy competitive short-form game where one team physically embodies a dynamic environment (the Eco-Builders) while the opposing team improvises a scene within it (the Story Weavers). To keep the scene grounded but chaotic, the Referee interrupts exactly once or twice to introduce an audience-suggested Environmental Flux (a major event) or Augment (an adjective for a set piece). Teams swap roles for a second round, and the audience votes on the ultimate winner.

## Setup
Two teams (e.g., Red and Blue) compete. The Referee asks the audience for a unique, active location (e.g., A submarine control room or A magical bakery). Team A is assigned as the Eco-Builders and takes the stage. Team B is assigned as the Story Weavers and waits in the wings.

## How to Play
1. The Referee gets a location from the audience and gives the Eco-Builders (Team A) 10 seconds to physically form the environment using their bodies. They must create distinct set pieces, atmospheric elements, or architecture.
2. The Story Weavers (Team B) enter and begin a 2-to-3 minute scene. They must actively interact with the living environment, treating the Builders as the objects they represent.
3. The Mid-Scene Flux: About halfway through the scene, the Referee blows the whistle, freezing the action. The Referee asks the audience for an 'Environmental Flux' (e.g., an earthquake, zero gravity, a flood). The Referee calls 'Unfreeze!' and both teams must instantly adapt their physicality and narrative to this new reality.
4. The Augment (Optional): If the scene needs a final spike of energy, the Referee may blow the whistle one more time, point to a specific Eco-Builder, and get an adjective from the audience (e.g., 'Sticky' or 'Possessed'). The Builder and Weaver must incorporate this trait for the remainder of the scene.
5. The Swap: The Referee calls 'Scene!' Teams then swap roles. Team B becomes the Eco-Builders, and Team A becomes the Story Weavers, using a brand new audience suggestion.
6. Scoring: After both teams have played both roles, the Referee calls for an audience cheer to decide which team performed best overall, awarding 5 points to the winner.

## Coaching Notes
- The Referee can call standard fouls (like a content foul for inappropriate content) or game-specific fouls (like the Blueprint Breaker if a Weaver ignores the environment, or a Safety Foul for weight-bearing).
- Encourage strong physical commitment, mime, and object work from both teams.
- Ensure the Story Weavers rapidly adapt their narrative to the Flux and Augment without completely derailing the core scene.
- Remind players that both teams are simultaneously engaged on stage, requiring constant spatial awareness.

## Variations
- Living Props: Instead of a full environment, the Builders become specific, highly active props that the Weavers must use to accomplish a complex task (e.g., cooking a five-course meal using human kitchen appliances).
- Genre-Scape: The environment, the scene, and the Flux event must all adhere to a specific audience-suggested movie genre (e.g., Sci-Fi, Western, or Film Noir).

## Why It Works
It tests physical commitment, spatial awareness, and the ability to adapt a narrative on the fly.

## Safety & Inclusion
Human furniture games carry inherent risks. Explicitly ban all weight-bearing; actors must support their own weight at all times (e.g., by wall-sitting). Establish strict physical boundaries before the show: no touching of the head, neck, or sensitive areas. Contact should be limited to hands, arms, and shoulders, or entirely mimed with close proximity. Eco-Builders always retain their bodily autonomy and can safely break their pose or step away if they feel uncomfortable or off-balance.
