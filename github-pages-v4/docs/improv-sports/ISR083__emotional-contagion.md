# Emotional Contagion

![Emotional Contagion — infographic](img/ISR083.jpg){ .game-hero }

> One improviser is assigned a strong emotion that must organically 'infect' the rest of the players through interaction.

## Overview
A facilitator-led workshop exercise where one improviser is assigned a strong emotion that must organically 'infect' the rest of the players through interaction. Stripped of competitive gimmicks, this exercise isolates and trains active listening, emotional commitment, and the foundational improv skill of allowing oneself to be genuinely changed by a scene partner.

## Setup
Format: Improv Exercise [Ensemble & Warm-Up]. Facilitator: 1 (leads the exercise and side-coaches). Materials: A simple deck of index cards with a different strong emotion written on each (e.g., Paranoia, Ecstasy, Despair, Suspicion, Awe), or just a mental list for the facilitator. Space: A clear stage or workshop floor.

## How to Play
1. Base Reality: Two or three players begin a grounded, relational scene based on a simple prompt from the facilitator (e.g., 'folding laundry together' or 'waiting for a bus').
2. The Infection: Once the base reality is established (usually 30-60 seconds in), the facilitator calls 'Freeze!'
3. Patient Zero: The facilitator selects one player and assigns them a strong emotion from the deck (e.g., 'You are now filled with profound awe'). This player becomes 'Patient Zero.'
4. The Resumption: The facilitator calls 'Go!' Patient Zero must immediately filter all their dialogue, physicality, and reactions through this new emotion. Crucially, they must not explicitly name the emotion (e.g., no saying 'I am so in awe right now').
5. The Contagion: The other players in the scene must actively listen and allow themselves to be subtly affected by Patient Zero's energy. Through continued interaction (eye contact, shared dialogue, mirroring), the emotion slowly 'infects' them.
6. Saturation: The scene partners gradually transition from their original emotional state until they are also fully embodying the contagious emotion.
7. The Next Wave: Once the emotion has completely saturated the scene and all players are playing at the height of that shared emotion, the facilitator can either call 'Scene!' to end it, or call 'Freeze!' to assign a brand new emotion to a different player, starting a new wave of contagion.

## Coaching Notes
- The facilitator side-coaches if necessary (e.g., 'Let it affect you,' 'Breathe with them,' 'Show us, don't tell us').
- The facilitator's role is to side-coach the players to ensure the scene breathes and the emotional transfer feels earned rather than rushed.
- Eliminates the 'talking head' problem by forcing improvisers to show their feelings physically and vocally rather than discussing them.

## Variations
- Secret Contagion: Instead of announcing the emotion out loud, the facilitator taps one player on the shoulder and shows them the emotion card. The other players (and the observing class) do not know what it is and must discover and 'catch' it purely through organic observation.
- The Antidote: Two players are given conflicting emotions simultaneously (e.g., Extreme Rage vs. Profound Zen). The scene becomes an emotional tug-of-war to see which player's emotion is stronger and ultimately infects the other.

## Why It Works
Forces players to actively listen and be visibly changed by their scene partners. Provides strong emotional stakes and clear character shifts without relying on plot. The contagion mechanic creates rich subtext, as players must justify why this mundane activity is suddenly taking on this specific emotional weight.

## Safety & Inclusion
Emotional intensity must never translate to physical aggression; players must maintain strict physical boundaries and not use emotions like 'rage' or 'terror' as an excuse to grab, corner, or intimidate a scene partner. Facilitators should curate the emotion cards to avoid trauma-triggering states, sticking to universal theatrical emotions (joy, frustration, suspicion, infatuation). The game is highly accessible, as the contagion can be played entirely through vocal tone, pacing, and facial expression, requiring no specific physical mobility.
