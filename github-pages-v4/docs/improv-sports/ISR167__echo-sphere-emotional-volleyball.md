# Echo-Sphere (Emotional Volleyball)

![Echo-Sphere (Emotional Volleyball) — infographic](img/ISR167.jpg){ .game-hero }

> Play an invisible game of volleyball using emotional impulses instead of a ball.

## Overview
A highly physical theater warm-up where players play an invisible game of volleyball using emotional impulses instead of a ball. Players must physically receive an emotion, let it visibly change them, and then pass it using specific volleyball moves (Set, Spike, Dig) to transform the energy before sending it to the next player.

## Setup
Split the group into two teams facing each other across an imaginary net (or a line of tape on the floor). No props are needed. The facilitator stands on the sideline to side-coach, monitor the pacing, and serve new 'balls' when the energy drops.

## How to Play
1. The Serve: The facilitator or a player starts by 'serving' an invisible ball infused with a specific, clear emotion and physical gesture (e.g., a heavy, sobbing underhand serve).
2. Receiving (Being Changed): The receiving player must physically 'catch' or intercept the ball, letting the incoming emotion visibly affect their body and face for a split second before they react.
3. The Pass (Transforming): The player cannot hold the ball. They must immediately pass it to someone else (on their team or across the net) using one of three moves, completely changing the emotion as they do.
4. Move 1 - The Set (Support/Heighten): A gentle upward push. Example: Receiving a 'frustrated' ball, the player sets it with a 'curious' upward flick of the wrists, raising their eyebrows and lifting the energy.
5. Move 2 - The Spike (Attack/Shift): A forceful downward strike. Example: Jumping up and slamming a 'joyful' ball down with sudden 'furious rage,' using a hard, aggressive exhalation.
6. Move 3 - The Dig (Save/Recover): A low, desperate scoop. Example: Reaching low to save a 'terrified' ball, scooping it up with a 'relieved, exhausted' sigh.
7. The Net (Escalation): Passing the ball to a teammate keeps the emotion personal and small. Hitting it over the net to the other team requires escalating the emotion, making it bigger, louder, or more universal.
8. Drops and Resets: If a player hesitates, talks, or fails to change the emotion, the ball is 'dropped.' The facilitator calls 'Reset!' and immediately throws a brand new emotional ball to a random player to keep the energy moving.

## Coaching Notes
- Act as an active coach, calling 'Reset!' when the ball is dropped and throwing a new emotional ball to keep the energy moving.
- Side-coach to encourage deeper physical commitment with prompts like 'Let it change your face!' or 'Show me the weight of that spike!'
- Ensure players rely entirely on non-verbal communication and physical embodiment, avoiding words.

## Variations
- Gibberish Sphere: Allow players to add a single gibberish sound to their pass, matching the new emotion. This helps players who struggle with purely physical expression connect breath to movement.
- Slow-Mo Replay: Play the entire game in exaggerated slow motion. This removes the panic of speed and forces players to focus on the micro-expressions of 'receiving' and 'transforming' the emotion.

## Why It Works
It actively trains the principle of 'being changed' by an offer before responding, and translates abstract emotional shifts into concrete, playable physical actions.

## Safety & Inclusion
Physical safety is paramount. Instruct players that 'Spikes' and 'Digs' are theatrical; no actual diving, jumping, or floor-work is required if it causes discomfort. The suggestion of the movement is enough. Ensure the space between the two teams is wide enough to prevent accidental collisions. Players with mobility restrictions can adapt the Set, Spike, and Dig to upper-body, hand, or facial gestures.
