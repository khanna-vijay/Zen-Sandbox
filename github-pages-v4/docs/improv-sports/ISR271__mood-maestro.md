# Mood Maestro

![Mood Maestro — infographic](img/ISR271.jpg){ .game-hero }

> Performers must instantly justify sudden emotional shifts dictated by an opposing team's Maestro.

## Overview
A fast-paced competitive short-form game where two players from one team perform a scene, while a player from the opposing team acts as the 'Maestro,' calling out sudden emotional or character shifts that the performers must instantly justify and integrate.

## Setup
Two players from Team A take the stage as performers. One player from Team B stands downstage or to the side as the 'Maestro,' equipped with a bell, whistle, or clear hand signal. The Referee gets a location and a mundane relationship from the audience.

## How to Play
1. Team A begins the scene based on the audience's location and relationship suggestions.
2. At any point, the Team B Maestro signals (e.g., rings a bell) and calls out a new emotion, attitude, or character archetype for one or both performers (e.g., 'Player 1, you are now fiercely jealous!' or 'Both of you are hiding a terrible secret!').
3. The performers must instantly adopt the new state, seamlessly justifying the sudden shift within the existing narrative without dropping the scene's reality.
4. The Maestro's commands must be emotional or character-based, not physical actions (e.g., 'You are arrogant' is allowed; 'Do a backflip' is not).
5. The Maestro should aim for 4 to 6 shifts over a 2-to-3-minute scene, pacing them to let the performers establish the new dynamic before changing it again.
6. The Referee calls 'Time!' at the end of the scene.
7. The teams then swap roles: Team B provides two performers for a new scene, and a player from Team A acts as the Maestro.
8. The Referee evaluates the scene as a whole at the end of the round, eliminating any mid-scene point tracking. After both teams have performed, the audience votes by applause for the team that best justified their emotional shifts and maintained the funniest scene. The winning team receives 5 points.

## Coaching Notes
- The Referee can call a 'Sabotage Foul' (-1 point) if a Maestro gives an impossible, physical, or inappropriate command.
- The Referee can call a 'Delay Foul' (-1 point) if performers ignore or fight the emotional shift.
- Encourage the Maestro to pace their shifts, allowing performers enough time to establish the new dynamic before changing it again.

## Variations
- Genre Maestro: Instead of emotions, the Maestro calls out distinct film, television, or theatrical genres (e.g., Film Noir, Soap Opera, Sesame Street).
- Physical Maestro: The Maestro assigns physical states or environmental conditions rather than emotions (e.g., 'The floor is made of ice,' 'You are both incredibly itchy,' 'Zero gravity').

## Why It Works
It forces players to practice rapid emotional justification and active listening, while the cross-team interaction keeps pacing fast and unpredictable.

## Safety & Inclusion
Maestros must focus on internal emotional states or broad archetypes, never dictating specific physical actions, forced physical contact between players, or unsafe movements. The Referee must strictly enforce family-friendly boundaries (the 'content foul') and immediately veto any commands that reference trauma, mental illness (e.g., 'having a panic attack'), or inappropriate content, replacing it with a safe, playable alternative.
