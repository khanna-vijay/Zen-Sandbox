# The Constraint Cascade

![The Constraint Cascade — infographic](img/IS180.jpg){ .game-hero }

> Skillfully weave an ever-growing stack of increasingly challenging and contradictory rules into a continuous scene.

## Overview
Players perform a continuous scene while a Referee introduces an ever-growing stack of increasingly challenging and often contradictory rules. Players must ingeniously integrate each new constraint without dropping any previous ones, creating escalating comedic chaos through their visible struggle and adaptation.

## Setup
Requires 3-4 players and 1 dedicated Host or Referee. You need a standard improv stage, a large whiteboard or flip chart with markers (clearly visible to players and audience), and a whistle or bell for the Referee.

## How to Play
1. The Referee asks the audience for a simple scene premise (e.g., 'a public park') and an initial relationship or core objective. Players begin a grounded, realistic scene based on this suggestion.
2. After 45-60 seconds, the Referee blows the whistle or rings the bell and introduces the first constraint (e.g., 'Every line must end with a question').
3. The Referee clearly writes this rule on the whiteboard. Players must immediately begin incorporating this rule into their ongoing scene.
4. At regular 45-60 second intervals, the Referee signals again and introduces a new, additional constraint, drawn from a prepared list or by asking the audience for a weird, specific rule.
5. The Referee writes the new rule below the previous ones. Players must immediately incorporate it without dropping or forgetting any previously established rules. All active rules layer cumulatively.
6. The Referee acts as a watchdog. If a player overtly breaks, forgets, or intentionally ignores an active rule, the Referee calls 'Foul!' and explains the infraction, resulting in a point deduction or a temporary sideline penalty.
7. Players continue the scene, attempting to navigate the increasingly complex and absurd web of constraints while maintaining character, narrative, and relationship.
8. The game ends after 5-7 rounds of constraints (around 5-7 minutes total) or when the scene reaches its peak of comedic chaos. The Referee blows the whistle a final time and calls 'Scene!'.

## Coaching Notes
- The Referee's art lies in introducing rules that create interesting comedic friction or direct contradiction with existing rules or character choices.
- The goal of the foul system is not to punish severely, but to encourage adherence and comedic struggle within the rules.
- Award points (e.g., 1-5 points) throughout the game for brilliant integration, contradiction navigation, commitment, and generating big laughs.
- Ensure the whiteboard is clearly visible so players can reference the active stack of rules at a glance.

## Variations
- Team vs. Individual Scoring: Play with teams where fouls deduct points from the team score, or individually where the 'most valiant player' is acknowledged at the end of the chaos.

## Why It Works
It forces improvisers to think on their feet, embrace absurdity, and find humor in the struggle. The escalating difficulty and cumulative layering demand intense listening and immediate adaptation.

## Safety & Inclusion
The Referee must filter audience suggestions to ensure constraints do not cross physical or emotional boundaries, keeping the struggle focused on safe, comedic theatrical challenges.
