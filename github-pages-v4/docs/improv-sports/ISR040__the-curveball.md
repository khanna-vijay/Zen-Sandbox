# The Curveball

![The Curveball — infographic](img/ISR040.jpg){ .game-hero }

> Two improvisers play a scene interrupted by exactly three radical, pre-determined plot twists.

## Overview
A fast-paced, competitive short-form game where two improvisers play a scene interrupted by exactly three radical, pre-determined plot twists. By gathering these 'curveballs' from the audience before the scene begins, the Referee eliminates dead air and builds anticipation. Players must instantly justify and integrate the new reality without dropping the scene's momentum, testing their ultimate 'Yes, And' reflexes.

## Setup
Requires two players (usually from the same team, playing for points), a Referee with a whistle, and an audience. No props or specialized staging are needed. The Referee acts as the game's timekeeper and twist-deployer.

## How to Play
1. Get the Base Scene: The Referee asks the audience for a standard scene initiation, such as a mundane location or a non-romantic relationship (e.g., 'At the DMV' or 'Two rival bakers').
2. Gather the Curveballs: Before the scene starts, the Referee asks the audience for exactly three radical twists. To keep it fast and focused, the Referee asks for specific categories (e.g., 'Give me a bizarre secret identity,' 'Give me a sudden environmental disaster,' 'Give me a dramatic genre shift').
3. Lock It In: The Referee repeats the base scene and the three specific curveballs so the audience and players know exactly what is coming, building anticipation.
4. Start the Scene: The two players begin their scene, establishing the base reality and their relationship.
5. Throw the Curveball: At a high point or a lull, the Referee blows the whistle and shouts, 'Curveball: [Insert Twist 1]!' (e.g., 'Curveball: The floor is now lava!').
6. Instant Integration: The players must immediately 'Yes, And' the twist, justifying it instantly without breaking character, pausing, or dropping the existing narrative.
7. Escalate and Conclude: The Referee deploys the remaining two curveballs one by one, allowing 30-45 seconds of narrative development between each. After the third twist is integrated and the scene reaches a climax, the Referee calls 'Scene!'
8. Scoring: The Referee awards up to 5 points based on the speed of integration, creative justification, and narrative cohesion.

## Coaching Notes
- Front-loaded suggestions completely eliminate mid-scene dead air, maintaining a 'fast and fierce' pace.
- Capping the game at exactly three twists ensures the narrative has room to breathe and develop between interruptions.
- The audience provides the base suggestion and the three curveballs upfront, creating dramatic irony as they wait for the twists to drop.
- Enforce standard fouls: Call a 'Delay of Game' foul (-1 point) if players stall, argue, or ignore the curveball.
- Enforce standard fouls: Call a 'content foul' (-5 points) for inappropriate content.

## Variations
- Blind Curveballs: The players leave the room or wear noise-canceling headphones while the Referee gathers the three curveballs from the audience. The twists become a complete surprise to the players, heightening the spontaneous reaction.
- Referee's Choice: For extremely fast-paced shows or smaller crowds, skip the audience curveball gathering entirely. The Referee simply invents three twists on the spot and throws them into the scene.

## Why It Works
Forces immediate acceptance and justification of radical, game-changing offers. It tests ultimate 'Yes, And' reflexes while creating dramatic irony and high audience engagement, as the crowd knows what twists are coming but not when.

## Safety & Inclusion
Physical safety is paramount; since twists might involve environmental hazards (e.g., 'gravity turns off'), players must maintain physical control and avoid dangerous acrobatics or lifting. The Referee must strictly filter curveball suggestions to ensure they are all-ages, free of harmful tropes, and do not force players into non-consensual physical contact. Players with mobility restrictions can interpret physical twists verbally or emotionally, and the Referee should tailor the twist categories to the abilities of the performers.
