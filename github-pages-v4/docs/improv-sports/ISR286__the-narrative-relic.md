# The Narrative Relic

![The Narrative Relic — infographic](img/ISR286.jpg){ .game-hero }

> At the climax of a high-stakes quest, players must instantly justify a bizarre, ambiguous physical prop to resolve their scene.

## Overview
A narrative-driven scene where improvisers are given a high-stakes quest. At the climax of the scene, a Judge or Host interrupts to introduce a bizarre, ambiguous physical prop (the 'Relic'). The players must instantly justify the Relic through physical manipulation and sensory reactions, weaving its sudden appearance into the resolution of their overarching quest.

## Setup
Requires 2-3 players and a Host/Judge. The Host prepares a hidden box of 2-3 'ambiguous props'—objects with no immediately obvious function (e.g., a strange piece of hardware, an abstract heavy sculpture, a bizarre thrift-store gadget). The audience provides three suggestions: a unique Location, a Character Relationship, and a high-stakes Problem or Quest.

## How to Play
1. 1. The Initiation (Minutes 0-2): Players begin the scene by establishing the base reality, their relationship, and the stakes of the audience-suggested quest. They play this as a grounded, narrative scene (comedic or dramatic).
2. 2. The Introduction of the Relic: At a moment of high tension, or right before the scene stalls (typically around the 2-minute mark), the Host/Judge calls 'Relic!' and walks on stage to hand one of the ambiguous props to a player, then immediately exits.
3. 3. Physical Prop Manipulation: Players must immediately interact with the prop. Note: This relies on tactile 'prop manipulation' of the real object, not pantomimed 'object work'. They must explore its weight, moving parts, texture, and shape.
4. 4. Sensory Justification: As they handle the Relic, players must state improvised sensory reactions ('It's burning hot!', 'It's vibrating!', 'This dial is incredibly stiff') and 'Yes, And' each other's physical cues to define what the object is.
5. 5. Narrative Weaving: The players must verbally interpret these physical clues and integrate them into the overarching quest. The Relic must become the key to solving (or tragically failing) their initial problem.
6. 6. The Resolution (Minutes 2-5): The scene has a strict 5-minute time limit. The players must use the Relic to reach a definitive narrative conclusion before the time runs out.
7. 7. Scoring: After the 5-minute scene, a panel of judges scores the scene from 1 to 5. Points are awarded for strong physical commitment to the prop, seamless narrative justification of the Relic, and reaching a satisfying narrative resolution within the time limit.

## Coaching Notes
- Eliminate pacing drag by having the Host inject the prop exactly when the narrative needs a catalyst.
- Ensure players rely on tactile prop manipulation of the real object rather than pantomimed object work.
- Encourage players to actively listen, observe, and 'Yes, And' physical offers in real-time.
- Remind players that the game is equally effective for dramatic, serious scenes and high-energy comedic scenes.

## Variations
- The Cursed Relic: The Host establishes a rule beforehand that the Relic actively alters the emotion or physical status of whoever is currently holding it, forcing players to pass it back and forth while trying to solve the quest.
- The Blind Relic: For advanced listening and sensory focus, the player receiving the Relic must close their eyes when it is handed to them. They must justify the object entirely by touch and sound, while their scene partner supports them visually.

## Why It Works
It eliminates pacing drag by injecting a catalyst exactly when needed, forcing players to actively listen, observe, and 'Yes, And' physical offers in real-time while blending tactile prop manipulation with overarching narrative goals.

## Safety & Inclusion
Props must be carefully curated to be safe to handle blindly or quickly (no sharp edges, toxic materials, fragile glass, or excessively heavy items). If a player has mobility or visual impairments, the Host can verbally describe the object's sudden appearance or place it directly and gently into their hands. Players should establish physical boundaries prior to the show to ensure safe passing and handling of the prop.
