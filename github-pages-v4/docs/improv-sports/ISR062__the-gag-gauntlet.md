# The Gag Gauntlet

![The Gag Gauntlet — infographic](img/ISR062.jpg){ .game-hero }

> A high-octane, competitive short-form game where two teams share a single continuous scene, tagging in to complete rapid-fire improv challenges called by the referee.

## Overview
It functions like an improv decathlon, testing different skills while forcing players to maintain a coherent narrative. Two teams share a single continuous scene, tagging in to complete rapid-fire improv challenges called by the referee.

## Setup
Two teams of 2 to 3 players each stand on the backline or wings. The referee stands downstage or to the side with a whistle. No props are used; all object work is mimed. The audience provides a single location suggestion to ground the scene.

## How to Play
1. The referee gets a location from the audience and invites one player from the Red Team and one player from the Blue Team to center stage.
2. The two players begin a standard scene in the suggested location. They are allowed 3 to 4 lines of dialogue to establish a solid base reality, character relationship, and platform.
3. The referee blows the whistle, freezing the scene. The referee announces a 'Gauntlet Challenge' and calls on a specific team (e.g., 'Red Team! Gauntlet Challenge: Emotion - Extreme Paranoia!').
4. A new player from the called team runs in and tags out their teammate, assuming their exact physical position. The scene unfreezes.
5. The newly tagged-in player must immediately incorporate the challenge into the scene while justifying the transition and advancing the narrative.
6. The players are given another 3 to 4 lines of dialogue to play out the challenge, ensuring the scene actually progresses rather than just becoming a momentary gag.
7. The referee blows the whistle again, freezing the action. The referee awards points if the challenge was successfully met, then announces a new challenge for the opposing team (e.g., '5 points to Red! Blue Team, Gauntlet Challenge: Physical - Moving through thick mud!').
8. A Blue Team player tags out their teammate, and the cycle continues. The game ends after a set time limit (usually 3 to 4 minutes) or after each team has faced an equal number of challenges.

## Coaching Notes
- Scoring is kept simple to reduce the referee's cognitive load: a flat 5 points are awarded for successfully completing a challenge and advancing the scene. 0 points are awarded if the player fails the challenge, drops the base reality, or stalls.
- The audience is encouraged to cheer loudly for successful integrations, which the referee uses to judge borderline successes.
- Standard competitive short-form fouls (clean-content call for inappropriate content, groaner for bad puns) apply and result in a standard point deduction.
- Ensure freeze-tag transition mechanics keep the stage picture clean and organized.
- Paced interruptions are key: allow a base reality to form before throwing in new variables.
- Use a 'decathlon' variety of challenges that tests multiple improv muscles (emotions, physicality, genres, gibberish, object work).
- The referee acts as an energetic event coordinator driving the pace.

## Variations
- The Gauntlet Relay: Instead of alternating teams in a shared scene, Team A gets 2 minutes to run through as many consecutive challenges as possible in their own scene. Then Team B gets 2 minutes to try and beat Team A's score.
- Blind Gauntlet: Instead of the referee calling the challenges, the tagging-in player must draw a challenge from a hat on their way to the center stage, forcing them to instantly adapt to whatever they pull.

## Why It Works
It tests a 'decathlon' variety of improv muscles (emotions, physicality, genres, gibberish, object work) while forcing players to maintain a coherent narrative and justify rapid transitions, ensuring the scene progresses rather than just becoming a momentary gag.

## Safety & Inclusion
Physical challenges must be accessible and scale to the abilities of the improvisers (e.g., 'move as if in zero gravity' rather than 'do a cartwheel'). Tag-outs should be done with a gentle tap on the shoulder. The referee must strictly enforce the clean-content foul to ensure all content remains family-friendly and safe for all ages.
