# Constraint Cascade

![Constraint Cascade — infographic](img/ISR268.jpg){ .game-hero }

> Two teams alternate scenes while juggling an escalating stack of audience-suggested rules.

## Overview
A fast-paced, competitive short-form game where two teams alternate playing their own distinct scenes. After each round, the Referee gathers a new, audience-suggested constraint (up to a maximum of three) that applies globally to both teams. Players must juggle their ongoing narrative while stacking these escalating rules, testing their cognitive flexibility and comedic timing.

## Setup
Requires two teams (2-3 players each), a Referee with a whistle, and a whiteboard or digital display to track constraints. Both teams start with 5 points. The Referee gets two distinct scene suggestions from the audience—one for Team A (e.g., 'A bakery') and one for Team B (e.g., 'A submarine').

## How to Play
1. Base Scenes: Team A plays their scene for 45 seconds. The Referee blows the whistle, and Team A freezes/exits.
2. Team B then plays their scene for 45 seconds. The Referee blows the whistle.
3. First Constraint: The Referee asks the audience for a physical or vocal constraint (e.g., 'Everyone must speak in questions'). The Referee writes this on the board.
4. Round 2: Team A resumes their scene, now applying the first constraint. After 45 seconds, the Ref blows the whistle. Team B resumes their scene, also applying the constraint.
5. Stacking Constraints: The Referee asks for a second constraint (e.g., 'Constant physical contact'), and teams play Round 3 applying BOTH constraints.
6. A third constraint is added (e.g., 'Must incorporate animal noises') for Round 4.
7. Fouls: If a player breaks a rule, the Ref blows the whistle, calls a foul, and deducts 1 point. The scene continues.
8. Scoring: The Referee can award +1 to +3 bonus points for exceptional justification of a bizarre constraint. Audience applause breaks any ties at the end.

## Coaching Notes
- The Rule of Three: Cap the constraints at exactly three. Adding a fourth usually causes total cognitive overload and scene collapse. Three provides the perfect peak of comedic struggle.
- The parallel scene structure ensures teams don't get confused trying to play each other's characters.
- Heavy reliance is placed on the Referee to pace the game and enforce rules.
- Be strict but playful with fouls. For example, if the constraint is 'Alliteration with the letter S', and Pat says 'Seven silly snakes slithered,' that is clean. If Kelly replies, 'Wow, so scary!' the Ref calls a foul on 'Wow' because it doesn't start with S.

## Variations
- Constraint Elimination: Start with 3 constraints already on the board. Remove one each round, ending with a fast-paced, unconstrained finale.
- Genre Cascade: Instead of physical/vocal rules, the constraints are stacking genres (e.g., Western, then Western + Sci-Fi, then Western + Sci-Fi + Musical).

## Why It Works
Parallel scenes prevent character and plot confusion while sharing global rules. The strict 3-constraint cap ensures the game remains playable and fun rather than collapsing into chaos, perfectly testing players' cognitive flexibility and comedic timing.

## Safety & Inclusion
The Referee strictly filters audience suggestions to ensure they are family-friendly and physically safe (rejecting constraints like 'must do backflips' or anything involving inappropriate touch). Players are encouraged to modify physical constraints for accessibility (e.g., 'jumping jacks' becomes 'rhythmic arm movements'). The standard content foul applies for any crude or inappropriate content.
