# Echo Chamber

![Echo Chamber — infographic](img/ISR099.jpg){ .game-hero }

> Players mutate a seed phrase like a game of Telephone, instantly justifying the new reality with every shift.

## Overview
An advanced classroom exercise where players build a scene around a single 'seed phrase.' Every time the phrase is repeated, the words must mutate slightly—like a game of Telephone—and the players must instantly justify the new phrase, shifting the scene's reality to match.

## Setup
2-3 players and a facilitator to side-coach. No props or special staging required. Best used in an intermediate or advanced improv class. The facilitator gets a short, slightly absurd 'seed phrase' from the class (e.g., 'The badger smells of elderberries').

## How to Play
1. Two players begin a scene. Within the first few lines, one player must naturally introduce the exact seed phrase.
2. Every subsequent time the phrase is spoken, the player must alter the words themselves slightly, using near-homophones, puns, or misheard variations (e.g., 'The batter smells of elderberries' or 'The badger sells old strawberries').
3. Once the phrase mutates, the scene partners must immediately accept the new phrase as the truth of the scene and justify it, allowing the narrative to organically shift.
4. Players manage the pacing themselves. There are no host interruptions or freezes; the scene flows continuously as the phrase evolves.
5. The facilitator calls 'Scene' after 3-4 minutes, or when the phrase has mutated so far from the original that it reaches a natural comedic peak.

## Coaching Notes
- The facilitator may gently side-coach by saying 'Justify!' if players ignore a mutation, or 'Mutate!' if they repeat the phrase exactly.
- Encourage players to focus on subtle phonetic shifts rather than completely changing the sentence structure.
- Remind players to let the narrative organically shift based on the new phrase rather than forcing the original plot.

## Variations
- Exact Words, New Context: Instead of mutating the words, players must say the exact original seed phrase, but change its meaning or context every time (e.g., shifting from literal to metaphorical, or changing the emotional tone).
- Tag-Out Telephone: Played with a larger group. Whenever the phrase mutates, an off-stage player tags out one of the improvisers to heighten the new reality.

## Why It Works
It forces players to practice rapid justification of bizarre statements and non-sequiturs. It also trains hyper-active listening, as players must catch subtle phonetic shifts, while eliminating cognitive overload by focusing entirely on a single mutating seed phrase.

## Safety & Inclusion
Facilitators should clarify that 'mutating' the phrase means using wordplay, puns, and phonetic shifts, not mocking accents, dialects, or speech impediments. Keep the content clean and ensure players do not use the mechanic to introduce inappropriate or unsafe themes.
