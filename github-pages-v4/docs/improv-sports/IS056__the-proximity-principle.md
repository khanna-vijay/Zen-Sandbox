# The Proximity Principle

![The Proximity Principle — infographic](img/IS056.jpg){ .game-hero }

> A scene where every step, retreat, and physical shift is a 'spatial declaration' that drives emotion, status, and narrative.

## Overview
The Proximity Principle is a competitive improv game where performers explicitly link their physical distance and relative positions on stage to their character's emotional state, status, and narrative progression. Every significant physical movement acts as a 'spatial declaration' that scene partners must verbally acknowledge or respond to. Any discrepancy between a character's words and their physical actions is treated as a compelling offer, challenging improvisers to use their bodies as a primary, reactive storytelling tool.

## Setup
Typically played with two or three performers. The audience provides a location, character relationships, and an initial conflict or situation. No specific markings on stage are needed, as meaning will emerge organically, but performers should utilize the full stage space.

## How to Play
1. Get a location, character relationships, and an initial conflict or situation from the audience.
2. Performers begin the scene, treating every significant physical movement, change in distance, or alteration of relative position as a 'spatial declaration' that communicates a change in emotion, status, intention, or relationship.
3. When a performer makes a spatial declaration, the next performer to speak must verbally acknowledge or physically respond to the perceived meaning of that move (e.g., accepting and building, questioning, or countering).
4. Enforce the 'Contradiction Clause': If a performer's dialogue contradicts their spatial declaration (e.g., saying 'I trust you' while backing away), the scene partner must acknowledge this contradiction and incorporate it into the narrative as a character revelation or internal conflict.
5. Continue using space to advance the plot, allowing specific spatial relationships (like standing back-to-back or side-by-side) to trigger significant narrative beats.

## Coaching Notes
- Encourage clear spatial declarations: closing distance (intimacy/aggression), increasing distance (fear/withdrawal), moving behind (subservience/stealth), moving above/below (status change), mirroring (empathy/mockery), or turning away (evasiveness).
- Remind players to be visibly and audibly affected by their partner's spatial moves, allowing it to alter their own character's emotions and subsequent spatial choices.
- Treat physical 'mistakes' or incongruities as dramatic gold. If a performer physically misses their intended emotional expression, it becomes a new offer revealing hypocrisy or internal struggle.
- Apply 'Yes, And' to every physical offer. A physical movement is an offer, and partners must accept its implication and build upon it verbally and physically.

## Why It Works
It makes spatial relationships an explicit engine for story, turning distance into tension and proximity into resolution or confrontation. By forcing improvisers to be hyper-aware of their bodies as narrative tools, it integrates the silent language of movement into spoken words, creating layers of meaning and shifting power dynamics.

## Safety & Inclusion
Players should establish boundaries regarding physical touch and extreme proximity before playing. Be mindful of aggressive spatial declarations, such as cornering or trapping a scene partner, ensuring all physical choices remain safe and consensual.
