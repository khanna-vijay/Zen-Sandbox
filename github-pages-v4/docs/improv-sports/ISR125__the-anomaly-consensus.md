# The Anomaly Consensus

![The Anomaly Consensus — infographic](img/ISR125.jpg){ .game-hero }

> Players collaboratively build a shared reality by intuitively integrating bizarre, uncontextualized anomalies introduced by a facilitator.

## Overview
An advanced ensemble training exercise where players collaboratively build a shared reality by intuitively integrating bizarre, uncontextualized anomalies introduced by a facilitator. Without verbally questioning the anomaly, players must immediately embody and act as if the stimulus perfectly belongs in their world. It trains radical agreement, deep listening, and physicalizing reality over talking about it.

## Setup
3 to 6 players in an open space, plus one Facilitator. The facilitator prepares a way to introduce 'anomalies.' For a low-tech setup, gather soft random objects (a shoe, a weird pillow, a crumpled script) and prepare to make abstract vocal noises or flick the room lights. For a high-tech setup, use a soundboard with abstract noises or a projector with abstract images.

## How to Play
1. Establish a Base Reality: Players begin a simple, grounded scene with a clear physical environment (e.g., a waiting room, a quiet dinner, an archeological dig).
2. Introduce the Anomaly: Every 30 to 60 seconds, the facilitator introduces a random, abstract stimulus (e.g., throwing a soft object into the center, playing a bizarre sound, or flashing the lights).
3. Immediate Embodied Agreement: Players must instantly integrate the anomaly into the scene without breaking character or asking 'What is that?' They must treat it as a normal or logical part of their current reality.
4. Non-Verbal Consensus: Players observe each other's physical reactions to define what the anomaly is. If one player treats a thrown shoe like a delicate, glowing artifact, the others must immediately adopt and support that reality.
5. Escalation: The facilitator continues to introduce new anomalies, forcing the ensemble to continuously adapt their reality without stopping to discuss it.
6. Debrief: After 3 to 5 minutes, call 'Scene.' The facilitator leads a discussion asking: 'How did you decide what the anomaly was?' and 'How did it feel to follow a physical choice instead of a verbal one?'

## Coaching Notes
- Observing players watch for the exact moment the 'group mind' clicks and consensus is reached, noting how players justify the bizarre stimuli.
- Forces deep listening and immediate, embodied agreement.
- Prevents players from talking about the problem instead of playing the reality.
- Highly novel use of sensory inputs to challenge experienced improvisers.
- Builds group mind through non-verbal consensus.

## Variations
- Low-Tech / Found Object: The facilitator uses only items found in the room (keys, a jacket, a chair) and their own voice to create anomalies, making it playable anywhere.
- The Human Anomaly: One improviser is designated as the 'Anomaly' and enters the scene doing abstract movements or sounds; the rest of the group must justify their presence seamlessly.

## Why It Works
It trains radical agreement, deep listening, and physicalizing reality over talking about it, building group mind through non-verbal consensus.

## Safety & Inclusion
Sensory Overload: Explicitly check in with players regarding auditory or visual sensitivities before playing. Avoid strobe lights, high-pitched tones, or excessively loud noises. Physical Safety: If throwing objects, use soft items (pillows, foam, crumpled paper) and never aim at players' heads or bodies. Ensure the space is clear of tripping hazards.
