# Pulse Play

![Pulse Play — infographic](img/ISR172.jpg){ .game-hero }

> Actors play a grounded scene while using concrete, observable audience reactions to dictate the pacing, stakes, and emotional shifts of the narrative.

## Overview
A non-competitive ensemble training game where actors play a grounded scene while using concrete, observable audience reactions (posture, ambient noise, focus) to dictate the pacing, stakes, and emotional shifts of the narrative. It trains actors to expand their awareness beyond the stage without breaking the fourth wall.

## Setup
Requires 2-4 actors on stage and an audience (the rest of the workshop/class) seated in front of them. No props or specialized space needed. The facilitator stands off-stage where they can see both the actors and the audience.

## How to Play
1. The facilitator gets a simple, mundane starting scenario (e.g., 'Folding laundry,' 'Waiting at the DMV').
2. The actors begin the scene, establishing their relationship and environment through standard object work and dialogue.
3. While playing the scene, actors must monitor the 'Pulse Metrics' of the audience using peripheral vision and hearing. These metrics are: Ambient Noise (shuffling, coughing, murmurs vs. dead silence), Posture (leaning in vs. slouching/leaning back), and Gaze (locked on stage vs. looking around).
4. When an actor senses a dip in the pulse (e.g., audience shuffling, leaning back), they must immediately introduce a bold shift to re-engage: raise the stakes, change the emotional tone, or reveal a secret.
5. When an actor senses a spike in the pulse (e.g., dead silence, leaning in), they must lean into whatever is currently happening: slow down, let the silence breathe, and deepen the current emotion.
6. Rule of First Action: If actors have conflicting interpretations of the audience's mood, whoever makes the first bold choice leads. If Actor A senses boredom and spikes the energy, Actor B must immediately 'Yes, And' that shift, even if B thought the audience was captivated.
7. Actors must never stare blankly at the audience or break the fourth wall. The pulse must be felt peripherally while maintaining eye contact with scene partners and the imagined environment.
8. The facilitator calls 'Scene' when a clear narrative arc is completed or the learning objective has been met.

## Coaching Notes
- The facilitator actively sidecoaches to keep actors out of their heads.
- Specific sidecoaching phrases include: 'Check the pulse through your periphery,' 'Don't look at them, hear the room,' 'They are leaning back, change the weather!', 'Hear that silence? Let it breathe,' and 'First to act, leads—follow your partner!'
- Ensure actors make bold emotional and narrative shifts instead of intellectualizing the scene.

## Variations
- The Planted Pulse: Before the scene, the facilitator secretly instructs the audience to shift their behavior at a specific cue (e.g., 'When I scratch my nose, everyone cross your arms and lean back'). This tests if the actors are truly paying attention to the metrics.
- Dial It Up: The facilitator calls out 'Pulse 1' (completely ignore the audience, play in a bubble) up to 'Pulse 10' (hyper-reactive to every single cough or movement), helping actors find the sweet spot of awareness.

## Why It Works
Provides concrete, observable metrics (noise, posture, gaze) for audience awareness rather than vague 'energy'. It forces actors to make bold emotional and narrative shifts instead of intellectualizing the scene, developing peripheral focus and deep listening skills.

## Safety & Inclusion
Ensure the audience knows they are just being themselves; they should not actively try to sabotage or heckle the actors. Actors are instructed not to aggressively confront or call out audience members. The game is highly accessible for visually impaired actors, as the 'pulse' can be read entirely through auditory cues (breath, shuffling, silence, laughter).
