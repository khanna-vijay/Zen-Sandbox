# The Shifting Story

![The Shifting Story — infographic](img/IS029.jpg){ .game-hero }

> An external narrator relentlessly contradicts the established reality of a scene, forcing players to instantly adapt and justify absurd new truths.

## Overview
The Shifting Story is an improvisational game where an external narrator relentlessly contradicts the established reality of a scene, introducing absurd new truths about characters, events, or the environment. Scene players must instantly adapt, embody, and creatively justify these conflicting facts as if they were always true. This challenges performers to apply advanced 'Yes, And' principles to radical shifts, fostering extreme flexibility and comedic problem-solving.

## Setup
Two improvisers begin a scene based on a single audience suggestion. One or two designated players stand off-stage, often near a judge's table or narrator's podium, serving as the omniscient, highly unreliable voice of the story.

## How to Play
1. Two improvisers begin a scene, establishing a clear 'who, what, and where' based on an audience suggestion.
2. At any point, the off-stage Narrator freezes the action by stating a pre-agreed trigger phrase, such as 'HOLD! But what they didn't know was...'
3. The Narrator introduces a new piece of information that directly contradicts, significantly alters, or dramatically recontextualizes a previously established fact, intention, relationship, or belief.
4. Upon hearing the twist, the scene players must immediately accept and embody this new truth as the absolute reality of the scene.
5. Players instantly pivot their dialogue, physical actions, emotional state, and character objectives to creatively justify the new information, as if it had always been the case.
6. The Narrator waits a reasonable interval (20-45 seconds) before interjecting again, allowing players to explore the new reality.
7. The scene continues with these dynamic narrative shifts until the host calls 'Scene!' after 3-5 minutes.

## Coaching Notes
- The Golden Rule of Contradiction: The new information cannot merely add new, consistent information; it must actively shift the existing reality.
- Impact over random: Narrators should aim for twists that create new opportunities for the scene players rather than just random, unrelated disruptions.
- Scene players cannot argue with the Narrator (in or out of character), question the new information, or attempt to ignore it.
- Players must retroactively explain past actions or statements to fit the new reality.
- Active Listening is crucial: Scene players must meticulously track the shifting reality, while Narrators must listen keenly to identify established facts to subvert.

## Variations
- Two Narrators: Two narrators alternate their interventions, clearly indicating when their turn is over (e.g., by stepping back) to prevent immediate double-contradictions.

## Why It Works
It forces improvisers to live entirely in the moment, making bold choices that celebrate the beautiful chaos when 'truth' itself becomes a pliable, collaborative fiction. It distinctly promotes narrative development through systematic disruption, actively encourages embracing mistakes as potent gifts, and demands intense collaboration to weave a coherent tale.

## Safety & Inclusion
Ensure contradictions do not force players into unsafe physical situations or cross personal boundaries. Narrators should avoid twists that rely on harmful stereotypes or non-consensual physical intimacy.
