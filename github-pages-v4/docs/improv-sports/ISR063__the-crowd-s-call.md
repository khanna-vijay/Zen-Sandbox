# The Crowd's Call

![The Crowd's Call — infographic](img/ISR063.jpg){ .game-hero }

> Observing improvisers act as a collective director, analyzing scenes in real-time to provide immediate side-coaching.

## Overview
A facilitator-led workshop exercise where observing improvisers (the 'crowd') actively analyze scenes in real-time. The facilitator pauses the action to ask the observers specific questions about improv technique, narrative elements, or what the scene needs next. This trains the ensemble's 'director's eye' while giving the players on stage immediate, constructive side-coaching.

## Setup
Best played in a classroom or rehearsal studio. Two to three players take the 'stage' area. The rest of the ensemble sits in the 'audience' (acting as the Crowd). A facilitator stands off to the side, ready to pause the action and direct questions. No props or chairs are strictly required, though standard rehearsal blocks can be used.

## How to Play
1. The facilitator gets a simple suggestion (e.g., a location or relationship) and two players begin an open scene.
2. At a pivotal moment (usually 30-60 seconds in), the facilitator calls 'Freeze!'
3. The facilitator turns to the observing ensemble (the Crowd) and asks a specific, technique-focused question. For example: 'Who established the base reality?', 'What is the core relationship here?', or 'What was the first gift offered?'
4. The Crowd answers aloud. The facilitator briefly validates or clarifies the answers to ensure the ensemble understands the concept being highlighted.
5. The facilitator then asks a forward-looking question to the Crowd: 'What does this scene need next?' (e.g., 'More physical stakes', 'A moment of silence', 'Heightening the emotion').
6. The facilitator calls 'Action!' and the players resume the scene, immediately incorporating the Crowd's feedback.
7. The facilitator may freeze the scene 1-2 more times to check in on progress, before calling 'Scene!' and rotating a new pair of players onto the stage.

## Coaching Notes
- Remind the observing ensemble that they are acting as a collective director and analytical engine, learning to spot improv mechanics in real-time.
- Use this exercise to remove the pressure of performance, focusing purely on skill identification and application.
- Encourage players on stage to seamlessly accept and integrate the side-coaching without breaking character or arguing.
- Do not penalize repetition (echoing), as repeating is often a valid tool for exploring the base reality and resting in the scene.

## Variations
- Performance Mode (Live Audience): If adapted for a live show, drop the technical jargon entirely. Ask the actual audience narrative or comedic questions (e.g., 'Who is the most petty?', 'Who should apologize first?', 'Who has the lower status?') and have them cheer to decide, driving the scene's next beat.
- The 'Waffle' Check: Instead of calling a foul, the facilitator asks the crowd to identify moments of hesitation or 'waffling'. If identified, the players rewind and replay the last 10 seconds making a strong, immediate choice.

## Why It Works
It trains the 'director's eye' for observing improvisers, allowing them to spot improv mechanics in real-time without the pressure of performing, while teaching players on stage how to seamlessly accept and integrate side-coaching.

## Safety & Inclusion
Ensure the critique focuses strictly on the scene's mechanics and not the players' personal abilities. Frame questions positively ('What gift was offered?') rather than negatively ('Who messed up?'). Players on stage must consent to being paused and analyzed; remind them this is a diagnostic tool for the whole class, not a punishment. The game can be played entirely seated, and mimed object work can be adapted to the mobility levels of the players.
