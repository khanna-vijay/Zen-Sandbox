# The Narrative Mood Ring

![The Narrative Mood Ring — infographic](img/IS006.jpg){ .game-hero }

> Navigate a shared scene through a rapidly shifting landscape of audience-dictated emotions, reinterpreting character objectives and narrative context with every mood change.

## Overview
Players navigate a shared scene through rapid, audience-dictated emotional shifts, forcing them to immediately reinterpret character objectives, dialogue, and the entire narrative context with every mood change. A referee orchestrates these transitions, soliciting emotions from the audience and scoring players on their seamless re-contextualization, deep emotional commitment, and ability to collectively rewire the scene's reality.

## Setup
Requires 3-5 players on a clear stage, a Referee to moderate and score, and an active audience. Optional: A pre-selected list of 10-15 distinct, evocative emotions (e.g., Euphoric, Suspicious, Heartbroken, Maniacal) visible to the audience to streamline suggestions.

## How to Play
1. The Referee gathers a basic scene suggestion from the audience, such as a location and a simple relationship between two initial characters.
2. Two players step onto the stage and begin improvising the scene, establishing the initial scenario, characters, and their basic objectives.
3. After approximately 30-45 seconds, the Referee interrupts the scene with a whistle or bell and asks the audience, 'What's the mood?'
4. An audience member provides a single, strong emotion. The Referee clearly announces the new mood (e.g., 'Players, you are now operating under the mood of... Suspicious!').
5. All players currently on stage must immediately adopt and fully embody this new mood, re-filtering their dialogue, body language, scene objectives, and established relationships through that new emotional lens.
6. The scene resumes immediately. Players not on stage should listen intently, and if a new player enters the scene, they must instantly adopt the prevailing mood.
7. The Referee allows the scene to progress for another 20-45 seconds under the current mood before calling another halt and eliciting a new emotional directive. This cycle repeats for 4-6 mood changes.
8. After the final mood has been played out for 30-60 seconds, allowing for a mini-climax or resolution within that mood, the Referee calls an end to the scene.

## Coaching Notes
- The Referee is crucial for managing the pace, clearly announcing mood shifts, and providing instant feedback through scoring.
- Award points for Seamless Transition (3-5 points), Deep Emotional Commitment (3-5 points), Narrative Re-Contextualization (3-5 points), and Humor/Dramatic Impact (1-3 points).
- Deduct points (Fouls) for Mood Breaking (laughing out of character) or Blocking Mood (failing to adopt the mood and hindering another player).
- Players must do more than just show the emotion; they must rewire the narrative logic and character intentions. The same line of dialogue should gain radically different subtext and meaning.
- Players must find ways for their original scene goals to still make sense (or comically not make sense) within the new emotional context, which might involve completely altering their immediate objective.

## Why It Works
It serves as a rapid-fire masterclass in subtext and re-contextualization. The external, audience-driven nature of the shifts forces players into instant, radical reinterpretation of their shared reality, developing emotional range, active listening, and collaborative storytelling under intense constraints.

## Safety & Inclusion
Ensure players respect physical boundaries and avoid aggressive or unsafe physical choices during intense emotional shifts (e.g., 'Vengeful' or 'Maniacal'). Players should modulate emotional intensity to maintain a safe and supportive environment for scene partners.
