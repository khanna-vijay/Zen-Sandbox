# Character Arcs

![Character Arcs — infographic](img/ISR054.jpg){ .game-hero }

> Two players pursue specific objectives while filtering every choice through a distinct character trait.

## Overview
Character Arcs is a non-competitive, facilitator-led ensemble exercise where two improvisers are each assigned one distinct character trait and one specific objective. Placed into a shared scene, they must authentically pursue their goals while maintaining their character filters. The exercise strips away the pressure of 'being funny' or winning points, focusing purely on deep character commitment, active listening, and the natural relationship dynamics that emerge when two distinct objectives collide.

## Setup
Requires two players and one facilitator. No props or special stage configuration are needed. The rest of the ensemble acts as the audience to provide suggestions and observe. The facilitator stands downstage or to the side to side-coach as necessary.

## How to Play
1. The facilitator brings two players to the stage and asks the ensemble for suggestions.
2. For Player A, the facilitator asks for ONE distinct character trait (e.g., 'Extreme germaphobe') and ONE simple scene objective (e.g., 'Borrow the other person's toothbrush').
3. For Player B, the facilitator asks for ONE distinct character trait (e.g., 'Overly affectionate') and ONE simple scene objective (e.g., 'Get a hug from the other person').
4. The facilitator assigns a neutral location, such as a shared bathroom or a bus stop, and starts the scene.
5. Both players enter the same scene, instantly creating relationship dynamics rather than playing solo showcases. They must pursue their assigned objective while filtering every choice through their assigned trait.
6. The scene concludes when an objective is met, a natural climax is reached, or after 2 to 3 minutes.
7. The facilitator then leads a brief debrief with the observing ensemble on how the traits affected the tactics used to achieve the objectives, when players dropped their character filters, and how the colliding objectives drove the scene's narrative.

## Coaching Notes
- Side-coach if a player drops their trait to achieve their objective, reminding them that the struggle is more important than succeeding.
- Prioritize character commitment and active listening over quick jokes or gag-driven play.
- Remind players that the clear, playable goal is there to anchor them if they feel lost in the scene.
- Ensure players are instantly creating relationship dynamics rather than playing solo showcases.

## Variations
- Secret Objectives: The character traits are announced out loud, but the facilitator gives the objectives to each player in secret (whispered or on slips of paper), forcing them to discover the other person's goal through active listening.
- Status Shift: Instead of a character trait, players are assigned a starting status (High or Low) alongside their objective, focusing the exercise on how status affects the tactics used to get what we want.

## Why It Works
It reduces cognitive load by limiting endowments to exactly one trait and one objective per player, providing a clear, playable goal that anchors improvisers and generates immediate, authentic relationship dynamics when the objectives collide.

## Safety & Inclusion
The facilitator must actively vet audience objectives to ensure they do not require crossing physical boundaries, simulating violence, or engaging in inappropriate content. Objectives should be relational or task-based (e.g., 'get them to agree with you' rather than 'kiss them'). Players are always empowered to use object work or distance to maintain personal boundaries, and the facilitator should stop the scene immediately if it becomes physically unsafe or uncomfortable.
