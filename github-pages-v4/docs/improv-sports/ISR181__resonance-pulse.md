# Resonance Pulse

![Resonance Pulse — infographic](img/ISR181.jpg){ .game-hero }

> A continuous chain of non-verbal sounds and involuntary physical reactions passed around a circle.

## Overview
A facilitator-led ensemble warm-up where players pass a continuous chain of non-verbal sounds and involuntary physical reactions around a circle. By isolating the specific skill of 'being changed' by an offer, this exercise bypasses the intellect to foster deep listening, vulnerability, and authentic emotional responsiveness.

## Setup
Gather 5 to 15 players in a circle, standing or sitting comfortably facing inward. Preliminary Warm-up: To help players feel comfortable making vulnerable, non-verbal noises in front of each other, the facilitator should first lead a 'Sigh Chorus'. Have everyone take a deep breath and let out a loud, vocalized sigh together 3 to 4 times, getting louder and sillier each time to break the ice and lower inhibitions.

## How to Play
1. Player A begins the game by making a single, authentic, non-verbal emotional sound (e.g., a sharp gasp of surprise, a heavy sigh of relief, a grunt of effort). No words or complex melodies are allowed.
2. Player B (the person to their left) receives the sound. Their only job is to let that sound physically impact them, allowing an immediate, involuntary physical impulse or shift in their body (e.g., a flinch, a dropped shoulder, a sudden straightening of the spine).
3. Immediately following that physical impulse, Player B emits a new non-verbal emotional sound that is directly inspired by how their body just reacted.
4. Player C then receives Player B's sound, allows their own involuntary physical reaction, and emits a new sound based on that feeling.
5. The pulse continues clockwise around the circle, building a continuous, flowing chain of sound-to-body-to-sound reactions without pausing for intellectual thought.

## Coaching Notes
- Point of Concentration (POC): Strictly focus on receiving the previous sound with your entire body, allowing it to generate a physical impulse before you make your own sound.
- Actionable Side-Coaching: The facilitator should actively call out prompts like 'Let it hit you!', 'Breathe into it!', 'React first, think later!', and 'What is the first sound that comes from that feeling?'
- Facilitator Tip - Correcting 'Performance': If a player is clearly faking, pantomiming, or 'acting' a theatrical reaction, gently side-coach them back to authenticity. Say things like, 'Make it smaller, just a breath or a blink,' 'Drop the character, just be you,' or 'Don't plan it, let the sound surprise you.' Encourage micro-expressions over big theatricality.

## Variations
- Impulse to Scene: To transition from abstract sounds to grounded dialogue, the facilitator lets the pulse go around the circle a few times, then calls 'Scene!' The next two players step into the center. Player A delivers a sound; Player B receives it, allows the physical impulse, but instead of a sound, speaks the very first line of dialogue that matches their physical state. They play a short 3-line scene rooted entirely in that emotional truth.
- Cross-Circle Pulse: Instead of passing the pulse sequentially in a circle, players must make strong eye contact and 'throw' their sound to anyone across the circle. This heightens focus, unpredictability, and ensemble awareness.

## Why It Works
It isolates the specific skill of 'being changed' by an offer, bypassing the intellect to foster deep listening, vulnerability, and authentic emotional responsiveness.

## Safety & Inclusion
This game is highly accessible, requiring zero props, space, or physical mobility. Facilitators must emphasize that the physical impulse can be entirely internal or a micro-movement (like a blink or a change in breathing), ensuring players with physical disabilities can fully participate. By stressing that there are no 'wrong' sounds or reactions, the game creates a low-stakes, judgment-free environment that protects players from performance anxiety.
