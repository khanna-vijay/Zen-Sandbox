# Narrative Flux

![Narrative Flux — infographic](img/ISR274.jpg){ .game-hero }

> Improvisers build robust story arcs by organically hitting secret narrative milestones during a scene.

## Overview
A diagnostic workshop exercise where improvisers practice building robust story arcs by organically hitting 3 to 4 specific narrative milestones during a scene. By removing points and visible scoreboards, it trains advanced improvisers to recognize and execute dramatic beats without cognitive overload or the pressure of competition.

## Setup
2-3 players on stage. A facilitator and the rest of the ensemble observing. A whiteboard or list of 3-4 selected 'Flux Triggers' (narrative milestones) visible ONLY to the observing ensemble and facilitator, not the players. A bell, chime, or a vocal 'Ding!' used by the facilitator.

## How to Play
1. The facilitator selects 3 to 4 'Flux Triggers' from a master list and writes them on a board facing the observing class (away from the players). Examples include: 'Relationship Shift', 'Self-Revelation', and 'Unexpected Opportunity'.
2. The players are given a standard suggestion (e.g., a location or relationship) and begin a 3-to-5 minute scene.
3. The players' goal is simply to play a grounded, narrative-driven scene. They know they are being evaluated on narrative milestones, but they do not know exactly which ones are active.
4. As the scene unfolds, the observing class and facilitator watch for the secret triggers. When a player organically achieves one of the milestones, the facilitator rings a bell or calls out 'Ding!'
5. The players do not stop the scene when the bell rings; they use it as positive reinforcement that they are on the right track and continue playing.
6. The scene concludes naturally after 3-5 minutes, or when all triggers have been hit.
7. The facilitator leads a brief debrief, revealing the triggers to the players and discussing how those specific milestones affected the story arc, character depth, and overall scene architecture.

## Coaching Notes
- Focus heavily on relationship, character development, and story architecture over cheap gags.
- The 'Unexpected Opportunity' trigger gamifies mistakes, teaching players to embrace accidents and weave them into the narrative.
- Drastically reduce cognitive load by limiting goals to just 3-4 clear milestones per scene.
- Remove the distraction of a visible scoreboard, keeping players present and in the moment.
- The observing ensemble acts as active listeners, often raising their hands when they spot a trigger to help the facilitator call it, keeping the whole room engaged.

## Variations
- Open Book: For newer improvisers, tell the players the 3-4 triggers before the scene starts. This allows them to actively practice aiming for specific narrative beats rather than discovering them blindly.
- Player's Choice: Each player secretly picks one trigger for themselves before the scene. The class has to guess what each player's secret goal was during the debrief.
- Director's Cut: If the scene stalls, the facilitator pauses the action, reveals one of the un-hit triggers to the players, and asks the class to brainstorm how the characters might organically reach that milestone from their current position.

## Why It Works
It trains advanced improvisers to recognize and execute dramatic beats without cognitive overload or the pressure of competition. The game gamifies mistakes and focuses heavily on relationship, character development, and story architecture over cheap gags.

## Safety & Inclusion
Emphasize that 'milestones' should never force players into non-consensual physical contact or trauma-dumping (e.g., 'Vulnerability' or 'Self-Revelation' means emotional honesty in character, not real-life distress). Ensure the 'Unexpected Opportunity' trigger is used for theatrical mistakes (dropped props, verbal flubs) and never used to highlight or mock genuine accessibility needs, neurodivergent traits, or physical limitations.
