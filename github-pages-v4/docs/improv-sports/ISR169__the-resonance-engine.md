# The Resonance Engine

![The Resonance Engine — infographic](img/ISR169.jpg){ .game-hero }

> An off-stage Chorus provides a continuous sound or rhythm that dictates the physical environment of the on-stage scene.

## Overview
A highly focused ensemble exercise where the off-stage group (the 'Chorus') provides a continuous, concrete sound or rhythm like a collective hum or synchronized breath. The on-stage players perform a grounded scene and must treat the Chorus's sound as the physical environment or emotional atmosphere. Players instantly adapt their movement and narrative as the Facilitator 'conducts' the Chorus to change volume, pitch, or speed.

## Setup
An open space with no props or set pieces required. Divide the workshop group in half: 3-4 people are the 'Players' on stage, and the rest form the 'Chorus' seated or standing around the playing space. A Facilitator stands where both the Players and the Chorus can see them.

## How to Play
1. The Facilitator assigns the Players a simple, recognizable group task to ground the scene (e.g., setting up a campsite, working in a busy kitchen, or painting a large room).
2. The Facilitator turns to the Chorus and assigns them a concrete, continuous mechanic. The default is a 'Collective Hum' (a low, sustained vocal note) or a 'Shared Breath' (audible, synchronized breathing).
3. The Facilitator acts as a conductor for the Chorus, raising their hands to increase volume/speed, or lowering them to decrease it. The Chorus begins their baseline hum or breath.
4. The Players begin their scene and attempt to accomplish their assigned task. They must allow the Chorus's sound to dictate the physical environment of the scene, translating the sound into gravity, weather, atmospheric pressure, or temperature.
5. As the Facilitator modulates the Chorus (e.g., making the hum louder, higher, or faster), the Players must instantly and intuitively adapt their physical movements and the narrative stakes to match.
6. For example, if the hum becomes a loud, rapid vibration, the Players might act as if gravity has intensified or a storm has hit, struggling physically while still trying to complete their mundane task.
7. The scene continues for 3-5 minutes, allowing the Players to find a rhythm of non-verbal connection and physical ensemble play. The Facilitator then calls 'Scene' and swaps the groups so everyone gets a turn to play and to be part of the Chorus.

## Coaching Notes
- Encourage hyper-focus on physical ensemble connection and non-verbal communication.
- Ensure players ground abstract physical movement in a recognizable narrative or task.
- Remind the Chorus that they are the engine of the game, actively co-creating the environment through concrete, guided vocal or physical mechanics rather than passive observation.

## Variations
- Silent Gestures: Instead of sound, the Chorus uses a repetitive, silent physical gesture (e.g., swaying, rubbing hands together, or tapping fingers). The Players must translate the speed and intensity of this visual cue into their scene's environment.
- Emotional Resonance: Instead of dictating the physical environment (like gravity or weather), the Chorus's sound dictates the internal emotional tension or status of the characters. A low hum might mean calm cooperation, while a high, fast hum drives the characters into frantic anxiety or joyous celebration.

## Why It Works
It encourages hyper-focus on physical ensemble connection and non-verbal communication, grounding abstract physical movement in a recognizable narrative or task, and transforms the audience from passive observers into an active, vital component of the scene's environment.

## Safety & Inclusion
Highly accessible. The Chorus mechanics (humming, breathing, or small hand gestures) can be performed seated and adapted for any mobility level. Players on stage are encouraged to focus on their own physical resistance and environmental mime, ensuring no forced physical contact is required between players. The Facilitator should ensure the assigned narrative tasks are everyday, all-ages activities.
