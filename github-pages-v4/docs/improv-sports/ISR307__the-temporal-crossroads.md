# The Temporal Crossroads

![The Temporal Crossroads — infographic](img/ISR307.jpg){ .game-hero }

> An offstage Voice of Fate interrupts the scene, forcing players into a high-pressure five-second vote to choose their narrative path.

## Overview
A narrative long-form scene is interrupted exactly twice by an offstage Voice of Fate. At these critical junctures, the Voice freezes the action and asks the audience for two divergent paths based on the players' immediate offers. The players then have a strict five-second countdown to unanimously vote for a path, with a split vote resulting in a dramatically harder consequence.

## Setup
Requires 2 to 4 players and one facilitator acting as the Voice of Fate on a microphone. Designate Stage Left as Path A and Stage Right as Path B. Ask the audience for a setting and a relationship with a built-in secret or tension to ground the scene. The scene is judged by a panel in a competitive improv format.

## How to Play
1. The players begin a grounded, relationship-based scene using the audience suggestion.
2. After 1 to 2 minutes, when a natural moment of tension or discovery occurs, the Voice of Fate calls 'Freeze!' over the microphone. All players must instantly freeze in place.
3. The Voice of Fate synthesizes the immediate action and asks the audience for two options. For example: 'Eldrin just opened the forbidden grimoire. Audience, what does he see? Option A or Option B?'
4. The Voice selects two distinct audience suggestions, labeling them Path A (Stage Left) and Path B (Stage Right).
5. The Voice counts down loudly: '5, 4, 3, 2, 1, Vote!' On 'Vote', all frozen players must simultaneously gesture (point, look, or nod) toward Stage Left or Stage Right.
6. If the vote is unanimous, the Voice announces the success: 'Path A chosen. Unfreeze!' Players immediately resume the scene, fully committing to that reality.
7. If the vote is split or players fail to gesture in time, the Voice announces: 'Fate intervenes!' The Voice then imposes a harder, more dramatic consequence that complicates the scene (e.g., combining the worst elements of both paths).
8. Allow the scene to develop for another 1 to 2 minutes before triggering the second and final interruption for the climax.
9. Repeat the audience suggestion and voting process. After this second crossroads, the players resolve the scene based on the final outcome.

## Coaching Notes
- The Voice of Fate must synthesize the action quickly and clearly to keep the interruption brief and maintain scene momentum.
- Ensure the imposed consequence for a split vote is narratively punitive but highly theatrical and fun to play.
- Judges in the competitive improv format should award points (1-5) based on the players' ability to seamlessly justify the chosen or imposed paths, their emotional commitment to the new reality, and the overall narrative arc.
- Limit interruptions to exactly two to preserve scene momentum and natural pacing.

## Variations
- Blind Crossroads: Players must close their eyes during the 5-second countdown and vote blindly. This drastically increases the chance of a split vote and a forced consequence, perfect for advanced teams.
- Emotional Crossroads: Instead of plot actions, the audience suggests two radically different emotional reactions for a character receiving news. The players vote on the emotional pivot rather than the narrative event.

## Why It Works
The strict five-second physical voting mechanic creates a thrilling forced consensus and tests group mind. Audience-generated options prevent the facilitator from becoming a bottleneck, while punitive but theatrical consequences for split votes drive dramatic escalation and force players to justify new realities.

## Safety & Inclusion
Voting gestures must be accessible; players can point, turn their head, or vocalize their choice if physical pointing is not possible. The Voice of Fate acts as a safety filter for audience suggestions, ensuring no options force players into non-consensual physical intimacy, dangerous stunts, or boundary-crossing content. Imposed consequences should increase narrative difficulty, not physical risk.
