# The Justification Gauntlet

![The Justification Gauntlet — infographic](img/ISR269.jpg){ .game-hero }

> A fast-paced workshop exercise that cures the fear of failure by turning improv mistakes into narrative gold.

## Overview
Players perform an open scene while a facilitator watches for fundamental missteps like denials or dropped props. Instead of stopping the scene to correct the error, the facilitator freezes the action, calls out a 3-5 word justification challenge, and forces the ensemble to instantly weave the mistake into the story as a brilliant, deliberate plot twist.

## Setup
3 to 4 players on stage. 1 facilitator standing downstage or in the house. No props or chairs needed. The rest of the group forms an audience to observe and provide suggestions.

## How to Play
1. Get a single suggestion from the workshop group to inspire the scene.
2. Players begin a standard, natural improv scene. There are no time limits or forced team rotations.
3. The facilitator watches closely for common improv missteps: denying a reality, inconsistent object work, dropping an accent, or ignoring a statement.
4. When a misstep occurs, the facilitator claps once and calls 'Freeze!'
5. The facilitator delivers a 3-5 word challenge to the other players on stage. Examples: 'Denial! Justify the lie.' or 'Bad mime! Prop is magical.' or 'Weak emotion! Extreme overreaction.'
6. The scene instantly unfreezes. The ensemble must immediately incorporate the challenge, treating the mistake as a vital, intentional part of the story.
7. The scene continues naturally until it reaches a conclusion or the facilitator calls 'Scene.'
8. The Hard Reset: If the scene crosses a content boundary or becomes unsalvageable, the facilitator calls 'Wipe!' Players immediately drop the scene and start a completely new one with a new suggestion, no questions asked.

## Coaching Notes
- Streamline coaching into punchy, 3-5 word actionable challenges to keep the pace fast.
- Remind players that as a non-competitive workshop exercise, there are no points, referees, or audience votes. The 'win' is successfully repairing the narrative and supporting scene partners.
- Remove the rigid structure of competitive games to focus purely on organic scene work.

## Variations
- Self-Justification: The player who made the mistake must be the one to immediately justify it, rather than relying on their scene partners.
- Genre Gauntlet: The facilitator dictates that the justification must fit a specific genre trope (e.g., 'Dropped prop! Sci-fi justification!').

## Why It Works
It trains rapid justification, active listening, and ensemble support by transforming mistakes into narrative gifts, eliminating the fear of failure.

## Safety & Inclusion
The 'Wipe' command serves as an immediate, stigma-free boundary reset. If any player feels uncomfortable, or if the scene veers into inappropriate territory, the facilitator uses 'Wipe' to instantly cleanse the stage. The exercise inherently promotes psychological safety by framing mistakes as necessary ingredients for the scene rather than failures.
