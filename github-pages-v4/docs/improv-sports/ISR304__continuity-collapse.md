# Continuity Collapse

![Continuity Collapse — infographic](img/ISR304.jpg){ .game-hero }

> Two improvisers build a grounded scene until a triggered collapse forces them to introduce and instantly justify bizarre, non-sequitur elements.

## Overview
Continuity Collapse is a fast-paced rehearsal exercise where players practice extreme justification. Two improvisers build a grounded scene until the facilitator triggers a collapse, forcing the next player to introduce a bizarre, non-sequitur element like a setting shift, rule change, or character alteration. By controlling the frequency of the chaos, this exercise trains improvisers to embrace wild offers without sacrificing the base reality.

## Setup
Two players on stage. A facilitator stands off-stage with a bell, clapper, or a clear vocal cue (like 'Collapse!'). Get a mundane suggestion from the group for a location and relationship. No props or set pieces are required.

## How to Play
1. Establish the Base: The two players begin a grounded, realistic scene based on the suggestion. They must spend the first 30-60 seconds establishing a solid Who, What, and Where before any disruptions occur.
2. The Trigger: The facilitator rings the bell or calls out the cue.
3. The Disruption: The next player to speak must immediately introduce a major, bizarre disruption to the scene. This must fall into one of three categories: Setting Shift (e.g., 'The floor is now made of marshmallows'), Rule Change (e.g., 'Gravity only works when we hold hands'), or Character Alteration (e.g., 'I just remembered I have crab claws for hands').
4. The Justification: The partner must instantly accept ('Yes, and...') and justify this new reality. For example, if Player A says, 'I am glad the breakroom fridge is now floating three feet off the ground,' Player B might justify it with, 'I told maintenance about the anti-gravity leak yesterday. At least it keeps the ants away.'
5. Re-grounding: The players continue the scene, treating the new bizarre element as a permanent, normal part of their base reality.
6. Repeat and Escalate: The facilitator triggers a collapse every 30-60 seconds, layering new disruptions on top of the old ones. The scene ends after 3-4 minutes when the reality has become delightfully absurd but remains fully justified.

## Coaching Notes
- Focus purely on the ensemble's ability to justify and support. There are no points or judges.
- Force players to instantly rationalize absurd offers rather than blocking, ignoring, or panicking over them.
- Use the bell mechanic to ensure a base reality is established and maintained between disruptions, preventing the scene from washing out into pure, unanchored chaos.
- Players must be hyper-aware of the exact disruption introduced so they can justify its specific details through active listening.
- If used in a casual warm-up showcase, empower the audience to yell 'Collapse!' to trigger the disruptions.

## Variations
- Group Collapse: Played with 3-4 improvisers. When the bell rings, the next line alters the reality for the entire environment, and the whole ensemble must work together to justify it simultaneously.
- Genre Collapse: Instead of random bizarre elements, the bell requires the next player to introduce a trope from a specific, randomly assigned genre (e.g., Sci-Fi, Western, Soap Opera) while maintaining the original characters and base reality.

## Why It Works
It trains improvisers to embrace wild offers without sacrificing the base reality, developing the skill of instant justification and active listening while preventing scenes from washing out into unanchored chaos.

## Safety & Inclusion
Physical Safety: Rule Changes or Setting Shifts (like 'gravity is reversed' or 'the floor is lava') must be played through mime and acting, not by climbing on unsafe furniture or engaging in risky acrobatics. Respectful Choices: When altering a character's history or identity, players should avoid assigning trauma, harmful stereotypes, or crossing personal boundaries. Keep the disruptions playful, absurd, and focused on the fictional world.
