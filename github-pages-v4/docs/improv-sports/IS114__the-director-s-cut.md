# The Director's Cut

![The Director's Cut — infographic](img/IS114.jpg){ .game-hero }

> To flawlessly integrate outlandish, on-the-spot directorial notes into a developing scene, showcasing extreme adaptability and comedic justification.

## Overview
The Director's Cut is an improv game where 2-3 Scene Players perform a developing scene based on audience suggestions, while 2-3 Directors stand ready to intervene. At any moment, a Director can shout CUT!, freezing the scene, and issue a specific, often outlandish, directive to one of the Scene Players. The designated Scene Player must instantly and seamlessly integrate and justify this new instruction into their next line or action, while other players yes, and the change.

## Setup
Requires 4-6 performers divided into 2-3 Scene Players and 2-3 Directors. Use a standard improv stage with minimal props. A referee or host can use a buzzer or whistle to signal CUT! if needed, though a Director's voice is usually sufficient. Audience interaction is essential for initial suggestions and scoring.

## How to Play
1. The host or referee solicits an audience suggestion for a location and a relationship (e.g., A competitive eating contest, between estranged siblings).
2. Two or three Scene Players step forward and immediately begin improvising a scene based on these suggestions to establish characters, stakes, and narrative.
3. The Directors stand slightly off-stage or in a designated area to observe, armed with the ability to cut the scene.
4. At any point, a Director can loudly shout CUT!, causing all Scene Players to immediately freeze their bodies and dialogue in place, mid-sentence or mid-action.
5. The Director addresses one specific Scene Player and gives a clear, specific, and often challenging or contradictory directorial note that must apply to their very next line or action.
6. The Director shouts ACTION!, and the Scene Players immediately unfreeze.
7. The designated performer must seamlessly integrate the directive into their next line or action, and the others must yes, and the sudden changes, incorporating them into the evolving reality.
8. The scene continues, with Directors calling CUT! again at any time. Performers must justify the directives within the scene's new context.
9. After approximately 3-5 cuts, the host/referee calls time. The audience applauds to score Best Integration, Most Creative Directive, and Overall Scene Fun in the style of a competitive short-form match.
10. Performers rotate roles for subsequent rounds, allowing everyone to experience both being a Scene Player and a Director.

## Coaching Notes
- Directors should give clear, specific, and challenging notes that alter the performer's character, objective, emotion, physical state, or the reality of the scene.
- Performers must not just do the directive, but justify it within the scene's new, often absurd, context. The humor comes from how well unexpected shifts are integrated.
- The game requires strong listening and yes, and skills from all players, both those receiving directives and those reacting to them.
- Keep the pacing fast with rapid transitions between freeze, directive, and resume to prevent scenes from stagnating.
- Encourage performers to instantly pivot through a wide array of emotions, physical states, and character traits.

## Variations
- Super Director: For a smaller cast, these roles can rotate or one performer can be a Super Director and a scene player at the same time.

## Why It Works
The game aligns with competitive improv traditions by providing clear constraints and objectives. The rapid transitions keep energy high, while the core challenge rewards quick thinking, character commitment, and the ability to justify absurd changes on the fly. It also plays with the meta-theatrical convention of a scene being directed.

## Safety & Inclusion
Directors should ensure their notes respect physical boundaries and avoid forcing players into unsafe physical actions or emotionally triggering content. Players always have the right to modify a directive if it feels unsafe.
