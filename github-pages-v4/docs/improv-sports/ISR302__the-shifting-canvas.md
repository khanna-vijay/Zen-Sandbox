# The Shifting Canvas

![The Shifting Canvas — infographic](img/ISR302.jpg){ .game-hero }

> A narrative long-form game where a Director cues a player to instantly deliver a bold, declarative truth that fundamentally heightens the existing reality.

## Overview
A narrative long-form game where improvisers build a grounded base reality, only to have a Director verbally cue a Canvas Shift 1-2 times per scene. The cued player must instantly deliver a bold, declarative truth that fundamentally heightens the existing relationship or situation, forcing the rest of the team into immediate, intense justification without destroying the established world.

## Setup
3-4 players on stage. 1 Director (or referee/facilitator) off-stage to call the shifts. Obtain a grounded suggestion from the audience, such as a mundane location or a relatable relationship (e.g., 'A quiet bakery' or 'Two siblings cleaning an attic'). No technical cues (lights/sound) are required.

## How to Play
1. Establish the Base Reality: Players begin a grounded, narrative scene based on the audience suggestion. They must spend the first 1 to 2 minutes building a solid 'Who, What, Where' so the reality has time to breathe and the audience invests in the characters.
2. The Call: At the Director's discretion (strictly limited to 1 or 2 times per scene, usually at moments of plateau or to spike tension), the Director verbally calls out 'Shift: [Player Name]'.
3. The Canvas Shift: The named player must immediately deliver a bold, declarative line of dialogue that reveals a massive new truth or secret about the current situation or relationship.
4. The Constraint: The Shift must heighten the existing reality, not erase it. (Bad Shift: 'We aren't in a bakery, we are actually on Mars!' / Good Shift: 'I didn't come to this bakery for a baguette; I came because I know you're the one who poisoned my father.').
5. Mandatory Acceptance and Justification: All other players must instantly accept this new truth as undeniable fact. They cannot deny, question, or act confused. They must immediately justify why this makes sense in the context of the established base reality.
6. Resolution: The scene continues, integrating the new truth and driving toward a narrative resolution based on the newly heightened stakes. If a second shift is called later, it must build upon the first.

## Coaching Notes
- Ensure the Shift heightens the existing reality rather than erasing it, eliminating the 'rug-pull' trope.
- Encourage bold, declarative choices from players rather than asking questions or waffling.
- Remind players they cannot deny, question, or act confused when a Shift occurs; they must instantly accept and justify.
- If scoring in a competitive short-form match, judge based on patience in establishing the base reality, boldness of the Shift, and seamlessness of justification.

## Variations
- Internal Monologue Shift: Instead of spoken dialogue, the Director calls 'Shift: [Player Name], Internal'. The player steps forward and delivers a secret motive or hidden truth directly to the audience. When they step back, the other players don't know the secret, but the audience does, creating massive dramatic irony.
- The Genre Shift (For Short-Form): A faster-paced variant where the Director calls a specific genre along with the player's name (e.g., 'Shift: Sarah, Film Noir'). The player must deliver a truth that instantly shifts the emotional stakes to match the new style, while keeping the location and characters intact.

## Why It Works
It balances patient, grounded scene-work with high-stakes narrative pivots, forcing players to practice immediate acceptance and intense justification without destroying the established world.

## Safety & Inclusion
Shifts must not introduce non-consensual physical intimacy, graphic violence, or trauma. Because the game relies on sudden narrative pivots, players must be mindful of their scene partners' boundaries. If a Shift crosses a personal boundary, players can use standard improv safety mechanics (like 'Tap Out' or a physical 'time out' gesture), at which point the Director will smoothly call 'Shift: [Different Player]' to pivot the narrative in a safe direction. The game is highly accessible as it relies entirely on verbal declarations and emotional reactions, requiring no specific physical mobility.
