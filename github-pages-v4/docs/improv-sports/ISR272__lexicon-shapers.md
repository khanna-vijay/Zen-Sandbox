# Lexicon Shapers

![Lexicon Shapers — infographic](img/ISR272.jpg){ .game-hero }

> Two improvisers build a scene using a strictly limited, visible vocabulary that expands only when a partner clearly accepts a newly pitched word.

## Overview
Lexicon Shapers is a focused classroom exercise where two improvisers build a scene using a strictly limited, visible vocabulary of 5 to 7 starting words. To expand their shared language, a player can introduce a new word into their dialogue. If their scene partner clearly accepts the new word, the facilitator adds it to the board. Capped at 10 words, this exercise forces performers to rely heavily on non-verbal communication, deep listening, and explicit emotional agreement to drive the narrative forward.

## Setup
Requires a facilitator, two players, and a highly visible writing surface (whiteboard, chalkboard, or large easel pad) with markers. The facilitator asks the class or ensemble for 5 to 7 diverse words (e.g., a mix of nouns, verbs, and adjectives like 'Apple', 'Run', 'Heavy', 'Why', 'Tomorrow'). Write these clearly on the board to create the starting 'Active Lexicon'.

## How to Play
1. The facilitator gets a simple relationship or location suggestion, and the two players begin a scene.
2. Players may ONLY speak words currently written on the Active Lexicon board. They can use any grammatical form of those words (e.g., if 'Run' is on the board, 'Running' or 'Ran' is acceptable).
3. Because the vocabulary is so limited, players must rely heavily on physical actions, gestures, facial expressions, and vocal tone to communicate their intent and build the scene.
4. Pitching a Word: At any point, a player may attempt to expand the vocabulary by speaking exactly ONE new word not on the board, embedding it clearly in their limited dialogue (e.g., 'Heavy apple... sad').
5. Accepting a Word: The scene partner must immediately acknowledge the new word for it to be added. They can do this verbally (by repeating the word) OR non-verbally (by showing a clear physical or emotional reaction that proves they understand and agree with the new concept).
6. Adding to the Lexicon: If the facilitator sees clear agreement, they write the new word on the board. The lexicon grows until it reaches a hard cap of 10 total words.
7. Rejecting a Word: If the partner ignores the new word or doesn't clearly react, the facilitator does not write it down. The pitched word is 'lost' and cannot be used again. The players must adapt and continue using only the approved board words.
8. The scene concludes when the 10-word cap is reached and the players find a natural emotional resolution, or after 3 to 4 minutes.

## Coaching Notes
- The facilitator acts as the active judge of whether a pitched word was successfully accepted by the partner.
- If a player struggles with rapid verbal recall, the facilitator can point to words on the board to help guide them.
- Remind the 'audience' (the rest of the class or ensemble) to observe the players' physical commitment and listening skills.
- Encourage players to fully commit to their physical storytelling and vocal tone, as these must carry the weight of the communication.

## Variations
- Genre Lexicon: The initial 5-7 words are pulled from a specific genre (e.g., Sci-Fi, Western, Noir), forcing the players to build the tropes of that genre primarily through physicality.
- Gibberish Bridge: Instead of English words, the starting lexicon consists of 5 specific gibberish sounds. Players must pitch new English words to slowly transition the scene from pure gibberish into a spoken language.

## Why It Works
It forces intense listening and explicit agreement (verbal or non-verbal) while elevating non-verbal communication, physical storytelling, and vocal tone. It removes the pressure of 'writing the script' to focus purely on connection and subtext.

## Safety & Inclusion
Because physical expression is heightened, remind players to maintain physical boundaries and avoid aggressive or unsafe movements. The facilitator should curate the starting words to ensure they are clean, accessible, and free of slurs or inherently triggering concepts.
