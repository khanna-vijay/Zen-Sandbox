# The Shifting Scribe

![The Shifting Scribe — infographic](img/IS084.jpg){ .game-hero }

> Two performers play out a scene while a third player, 'The Scribe,' dynamically dictates and alters the stylistic and literary rules governing their dialogue and actions.

## Overview
The Shifting Scribe is a collaborative storytelling game where two players perform a scene while a third player acts as The Scribe. The Scribe dynamically introduces and shifts literary or stylistic constraints, such as rhyming couplets or internal monologue. The performers must instantly adapt to these fluctuating rules, turning the storytelling process into an evolving meta-narrative.

## Setup
Three improvisers are needed: two main characters (A & B) and one Scribe (C). The Scribe stands slightly to the side or upstage, optionally with a symbolic prop like a quill and parchment or a tablet. Get a broad narrative premise or setting from the audience to begin.

## How to Play
1. Players A and B begin to establish their characters, relationship, and the initial scene based on the audience suggestion, playing it as a relatively 'normal' improv scene for approximately 30-60 seconds.
2. The Scribe interjects by stepping forward and delivering a narrative statement or subtle physical gesture to introduce the first literary or stylistic constraint (e.g., rhyming couplets, haiku, internal monologue, dramatic soliloquy).
3. Players A and B must immediately adopt and adhere to this constraint for their dialogue and character portrayal.
4. The Scribe actively observes the scene and can change the constraint at any point. This shift can be triggered by a narrative turning point, a player's struggle, or simply for dramatic effect.
5. To change the constraint, The Scribe interjects with another narrative statement making the new stylistic rule clear, and A and B must instantly adapt to the new form.
6. The Scribe can also interject briefly with narration that adds to the scene or directs the flow without necessarily changing the constraint.
7. The scene ends either on a timed cue from the host, or when The Scribe delivers a concluding narrative statement that wraps up the current segment or overall story arc.

## Coaching Notes
- The Scribe controls the pace of changes. Too many rapid changes can lead to chaos; too few might make the game less dynamic. Find a balance that allows players to settle into a constraint before challenging them with a new one.
- Leverage mistakes. If a player struggles with a rhyme or emotional tone, The Scribe can narrate this struggle as a 'break in the cosmic order' and use it as a springboard to shift to a new constraint.
- Players A and B must 'Yes, And' both The Scribe's constraint changes and their partner's adherence to them.
- The Scribe must listen to A and B's scene contributions, acknowledging them even as they impose new rules. It is a three-way trust exercise.
- Encourage players to let the constraints inspire physical choices, such as exaggerated gestures for melodrama or stillness for internal monologue.

## Why It Works
The continuous shifting of styles ensures the narrative remains dynamic and surprising, intensely testing players' ability to be changed, listen, and adapt their linguistic, physical, and emotional performance instantaneously.

## Safety & Inclusion
Ensure the constraints imposed by The Scribe are respectful and do not force players into uncomfortable or unsafe physical or emotional territory. Check in on physical boundaries if constraints inspire exaggerated physicality.
