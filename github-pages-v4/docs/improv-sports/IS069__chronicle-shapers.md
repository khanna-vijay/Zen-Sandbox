# Chronicle Shapers

![Chronicle Shapers — infographic](img/IS069.jpg){ .game-hero }

> A third player retroactively redefines a pivotal event in an ongoing scene, forcing the performers to instantly adapt to a dramatically impactful new truth.

## Overview
Chronicle Shapers is an improvisational game for three players, where two performers establish a scene while a third, the Shaper, observes. At pivotal moments, the Shaper retroactively redefines a recently established event, object, or line of dialogue, introducing a new, dramatically impactful truth as if it had always been the case. This compels the other two players to instantly accept and adapt to this altered reality, seamlessly continuing the narrative and demonstrating profound adaptability.

## Setup
Three improvisers are needed: Player A and Player B are the primary scene-makers, and Player C is The Shaper. The audience provides a suggestion for a relationship (e.g., estranged siblings, competing scientists) and a location (e.g., a deserted antique shop, the moon). No physical setup is required beyond the stage.

## How to Play
1. Opening Scene (2-3 minutes): Player A and Player B begin a scene based on the audience suggestions, establishing characters, stakes, and an initial conflict or situation while The Shaper observes intently from the side.
2. The 'SHAPE!' Call: At any point after the first 2 minutes, when The Shaper identifies a specific, impactful event, object, or line of dialogue that has just occurred, they call out 'SHAPE!'.
3. The Recontextualization: Immediately upon calling 'SHAPE!', The Shaper steps forward and makes a clear, declarative statement redefining the underlying truth or purpose of the specific event, object, or line.
4. Ensure the recontextualization is a significant alteration, retroactive (treated as if it always was the case), and dramatically viable (opening new narrative possibilities rather than simply negating the scene).
5. Adapt and Re-Build: Player A and Player B must instantly accept this new reality without questioning it or referring to the old truth, shifting their entire present and past in the scene to accommodate the Shaper's statement.
6. Continue the Scene: The performers continue the scene from the moment of the 'SHAPE!' call with the new recontextualization firmly in place, exploring its consequences. The Shaper retreats to the side.
7. Subsequent Shifts: The Shaper can call 'SHAPE!' again after another significant event, leading to further narrative pivots. The game continues for a set duration of 6-8 minutes total, typically featuring 2-3 shifts.

## Coaching Notes
- Narrative Development: The game forces profound narrative shifts. The initial 'mistake' or original intent becomes a foundational element for new narrative possibilities.
- Embracing Mistakes: Performers must cheerfully abandon their original interpretation and dive headfirst into the new reality, treating the 'wrong' path as the 'right' one all along.
- Players A & B: Must immediately accept and validate the Shaper's often dramatic recontextualization, building upon it seamlessly. Their willingness to surrender their original choices is key.
- The Shaper: Must listen intently and offer recontextualizations that deepen the existing scene and offer opportunities for the players, rather than derail it arbitrarily. Make the scene better, not just different.
- Being Changed: Performers must not only accept new information but actively embody the psychological, emotional, and practical changes implied by a retroactive shift in their reality.
- Status Play & Emotional Range: The new truth often inherently alters the status dynamics between characters and requires quick changes in emotional commitment.

## Why It Works
It forces profound narrative shifts, requiring players to build complex and evolving stories with unexpected twists. It tests essential improv skills like 'Being Changed', 'Yes, And', storytelling, pattern recognition, status play, and emotional range by making the players embrace total recontextualization.

## Safety & Inclusion
Ensure the Shaper's recontextualizations respect the physical and emotional boundaries of the performers, avoiding traumatic or non-consensual themes unless previously agreed upon.
