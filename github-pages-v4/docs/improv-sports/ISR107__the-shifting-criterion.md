# The Shifting Criterion

![The Shifting Criterion — infographic](img/ISR107.jpg){ .game-hero }

> A fast-paced, competitive short-form game where players perform a continuous scene while a Referee dynamically shouts out audience-provided constraints.

## Overview
By gathering 4-5 performance criteria upfront, ranging from genres to physical quirks, the Referee acts as a live director, forcing players to instantly adapt their playstyle without breaking the scene's reality. It is a high-energy test of listening, ensemble agreement, and theatrical versatility.

## Setup
2 to 4 players per team. A Referee with a whistle and a notepad. A bare stage. Played in two halves (Team A, then Team B).

## How to Play
1. The Referee asks the audience for a mundane, grounded scene suggestion (e.g., 'Waiting at the DMV' or 'Two roommates cleaning').
2. The Referee then asks the audience for 4 to 5 'Directorial Constraints'—these can be genres, emotions, or specific rules (e.g., 'Film Noir,' 'Extreme jealousy,' 'Speak in rhyming couplets,' 'Slow motion'). The Referee writes these down.
3. Team A takes the stage and begins the scene based on the mundane suggestion, playing it straight.
4. At any moment, the Referee blows the whistle and shouts one of the constraints (e.g., 'Shift: Film Noir!'). The players do not stop the scene; they must instantly justify and adapt their dialogue, physicality, and tone to match the new constraint.
5. The Referee dynamically calls out different constraints from the list every 15 to 30 seconds. They can return to previous constraints, switch them rapidly, or combine them for added difficulty (e.g., 'Shift: Film Noir AND Rhyming Couplets!').
6. After 2 to 3 minutes, the Referee calls 'Scene!' Team B then takes the stage, receives a new base suggestion and 4-5 new constraints from the audience, and plays their round.
7. Once both teams have played, the Referee asks the audience to vote by applause for the team that adapted most seamlessly and entertainingly. The winning team is awarded 5 points.

## Coaching Notes
- Mid-scene scoring is strictly avoided to preserve the scene's momentum.
- Constraints are called on the fly without stopping the action; players must actively listen and change their playstyle on a dime.
- The game relies entirely on the performers' physicality, vocal skills, and ensemble agreement, requiring zero props.

## Variations
- Rapid-Fire Gauntlet: Instead of 4-5 constraints, the Referee gathers 10 and rapid-fires them every 10 seconds, forcing chaotic, hilarious micro-shifts.
- Emotion Shift: A simpler version for beginners where the audience only provides 4-5 specific emotions, and the Referee calls them out to change the subtext of the scene.
- Workshop Mode: Played non-competitively in a class setting. A facilitator uses specific, targeted constraints (e.g., 'Raise your status,' 'Increase the stakes,' 'Focus on object work') to train players in applying immediate directorial notes.

## Why It Works
The audience's suggestions directly control the shifting mechanics of the scene, forcing players to actively listen and change their playstyle on a dime. It builds high adaptability and theatrical versatility while maintaining ensemble agreement.

## Safety & Inclusion
The Referee acts as a safety buffer, filtering out any audience constraints that are physically dangerous, offensive, or rely on harmful stereotypes. If a physical constraint is called, players must prioritize safe, controlled movements over reckless stunts. The game is highly accessible as it requires no props and can be played entirely through vocal and emotional shifts if mobility is a concern.
