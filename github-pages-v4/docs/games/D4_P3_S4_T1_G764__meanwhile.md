# Meanwhile

![Meanwhile — infographic](img/D4_P3_S4_T1_G764__meanwhile.jpg){ .game-hero }

> Weave a tapestry of parallel scenes using rapid, thematic narrative edits.

## Overview
An ensemble-driven editing game where players use the verbal cue 'Meanwhile...' to cut between parallel storylines, locations, or perspectives. By listening closely to the active scene, off-stage players dynamically edit and launch new, interconnected scenes inspired by the current action.

## What It Trains
- **Domain:** D4 — The Ensemble
- **Principle(s):** Group Mind; Serve the Piece; Serve the Story
- **Skill(s):** Pacing & Rhythm; Thematic Synthesis; Format Literacy; Narrative Architecture
- **Technique(s):** Edits (Sweep, Tag-Out, Sound/Light); Weave the threads; Longform vs. shortform mechanics
- **Focus:** mixed

**Objective:** To develop ensemble pacing, rhythmic editing, and thematic synthesis. Players learn to 'serve the piece' by recognizing when a scene has peaked and executing clean, narrative-driven transitions that expand the story world.

## Setup
An open performance space with a clear stage area. The ensemble stands on the wings (sides of the stage) in a 'backline' or 'sideline' formation, ready to enter. No props or materials are required.

## How to Play
1. Begin with two players stepping onto the stage to initiate a grounded, relationship-focused scene based on a simple location suggestion.
2. The remaining players stand on the sidelines, actively listening to the scene's details, characters, and themes without pre-planning their own moves.
3. At any point, an off-stage player identifies a narrative thread, off-camera character, or parallel location mentioned or implied in the active scene.
4. The off-stage player steps forward and calls out 'Meanwhile...' followed by a brief description of the new focus (e.g., 'Meanwhile, back at the principal's office...').
5. Upon hearing the edit, the active on-stage players immediately freeze and exit the stage, clearing the space for the new scene.
6. The player who called the edit, along with one or two partners, immediately enters the stage to initiate the new scene, matching the energy of the transition.
7. Repeat this cycle, allowing scenes to jump back and forth between established storylines or branch out into entirely new, related directions.
8. The facilitator can initially call the 'Meanwhile' edits to establish the rhythm before handing full control over to the ensemble.

## Facilitation Notes
- Side-coach players to edit on a high point or a moment of tension rather than letting a scene peter out or waiting for a logical conclusion.
- Pitfall: Players calling 'Meanwhile' to introduce completely unrelated ideas. Fix: Remind the ensemble that edits must be directly inspired by a detail, character, or relationship established in the active scene.
- Encourage physical commitment: when entering after an edit, players should step in with clear physical offers and immediate relationship dynamics.
- Watch for pacing: if edits are happening every ten seconds, the scenes cannot develop; if they take three minutes, the momentum stalls. Aim for a rhythmic sweet spot of 45-90 seconds per scene.

## Variations
- Micro-Locations: Restrict all 'Meanwhile' edits to occur within a single, larger structure (e.g., different rooms of a department store or different tables at a wedding reception) to practice environmental exploration.
- Character-Centric: Instead of changing locations, the 'Meanwhile' edit must follow a specific off-stage character who was just mentioned in the dialogue.
- Audience-Driven: In a performance setting, the host freezes the scene and asks the audience to supply the 'Meanwhile' prompt based on what they have seen so far.

## Debrief
- How did it feel to trust your ensemble mates to edit you out, and how did you know when the right moment to edit had arrived?
- What strategies did you use to ensure your 'Meanwhile' edit felt like a natural expansion of the world rather than a jarring interruption?
- How does this style of editing help build a cohesive, multi-layered narrative without a pre-planned script?

## Safety & Inclusion
Ensure the physical transition space is clear of obstacles so players can exit and enter safely at a brisk pace. If players have mobility considerations, the verbal 'Meanwhile' cue can be accompanied by a designated physical gesture or a slower, staged transition rather than a rapid run-on.

## Why It Works
This game works because it gamifies the edit, removing the anxiety of 'when to cut' by providing a clear verbal mechanism. It trains the group mind to listen for narrative opportunities and teaches players that editing is an act of support—saving a scene at its peak and expanding the shared universe.
