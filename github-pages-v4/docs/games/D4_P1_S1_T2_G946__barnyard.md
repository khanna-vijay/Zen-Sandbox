# Sound Herd

![Sound Herd — infographic](img/D4_P1_S1_T2_G946__barnyard.jpg){ .game-hero }

> Find your secret sound family using only your ears and vocal cues in a chaotic space.

## Overview
A high-energy, sensory-focused warm-up where players are secretly assigned animal identities and must locate their matching group members. By relying entirely on vocalizations and active listening amidst a wall of noise, players develop deep acoustic focus and group cohesion.

## What It Trains
- **Domain:** D4 — The Ensemble
- **Principle(s):** Group Mind; Commit 100%
- **Skill(s):** Peripheral Awareness; Vocal Craft; Active Listening
- **Technique(s):** Thread-tracking drills; Vocal characterization
- **Focus:** connection

**Objective:** To cultivate peripheral auditory awareness, vocal commitment, and group mind by tracking a specific audio thread within a chaotic, multi-layered soundscape.

## Setup
An open, obstacle-free room. The facilitator secretly assigns each player an animal identity (e.g., cow, sheep, chicken, frog) ensuring that there are matching cohorts of 3 to 5 players per animal. Players then disperse randomly throughout the space.

## How to Play
1. The facilitator secretly assigns an animal identity to every player, ensuring that each animal is shared by a small group of 3 to 5 people.
2. Instruct all players to disperse throughout the room, mix up thoroughly, and stand with their eyes closed (or soft-focused on the floor) to emphasize auditory tracking.
3. On the facilitator's signal, players begin continuously making their assigned animal's signature sound, using no human words or physical gestures.
4. Players must move slowly and safely through the space, listening closely to isolate their matching animal sounds from the surrounding noise.
5. When two players of the same animal type find each other, they link hands or elbows and continue searching for the rest of their cohort.
6. Once a cohort believes they have gathered all members of their specific animal group, they must sit down together in a circle.
7. The game concludes when all groups have successfully united and sat down, or when the first complete group achieves unity.

## Facilitation Notes
- Side-coaching cue: 'Project your sound clearly, but keep your ears open to track your partners through the noise!'
- Pitfall: Players moving too fast with eyes closed. Fix: Emphasize slow, deliberate 'bumper-car' physical movement with hands up at chest level for safety.
- Side-coaching cue: 'Commit fully to the vocalization—don't hold back, let the sound carry!'
- Pitfall: Uneven group sizes causing confusion. Fix: Clearly state beforehand how many of each animal exist, or let them discover it organically as a pure listening challenge.

## Variations
- Silent Gestures: Perform the game in complete silence using only physical, non-verbal movements and characteristic animal postures to find cohorts.
- Emotional Tones: Instead of animals, assign players specific emotional states (e.g., ecstatic, terrified, deeply bored) expressed only through abstract vocal sighs or gibberish.
- Rhythmic Pulses: Assign players distinct rhythmic claps or vocal beats to find their musical ensemble.

## Debrief
- How did you filter out the competing noise to focus on your specific group's sound?
- What did it feel like when you finally connected with a member of your cohort amidst the chaos?
- How does this level of active listening translate to tracking multiple narrative threads in an improv scene?

## Safety & Inclusion
If players are uncomfortable closing their eyes, they may keep them open with a soft downward gaze. Ensure the space is completely clear of tripping hazards. Encourage players to keep their hands up at chest level, palms out, to gently buffer any physical contact.

## Why It Works
This game forces players to move past visual dominance and rely entirely on auditory tracking. By isolating a single 'thread' of sound within a chaotic environment, players practice the exact cognitive skill needed to track subtle narrative offers in complex group scenes. It builds immediate ensemble trust through shared vulnerability and high-energy vocal commitment.
