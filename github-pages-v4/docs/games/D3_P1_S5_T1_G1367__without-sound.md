# Silent C.R.O.W.

![Silent C.R.O.W. — infographic](img/D3_P1_S5_T1_G1367__without-sound.jpg){ .game-hero }

> Build a rich, physical world and deep relationships without uttering a single sound.

## Overview
Two players perform a fully realized scene without using any spoken words, vocalizations, or sound effects. By relying entirely on physical action, facial expressions, and precise object work, players must establish their Characters, Relationships, Objectives, and Where (C.R.O.W.).

## What It Trains
- **Domain:** D3 — The Scene
- **Principle(s):** Commit 100%; Make Your Partner a Genius; Show, Don't Tell
- **Skill(s):** Physicality & Space Work; Silence & Stillness; Active Listening; Single-Partner Empathy & Mirroring; World-Building
- **Technique(s):** Object work; Do nothing exercises; Mirror exercise; C.R.O.W. (Character, Relationship, Objective, Where)
- **Focus:** skill_drill

**Objective:** To develop physical world-building, precise object work, and non-verbal communication, forcing players to 'show, don't tell' their C.R.O.W. elements.

## Setup
A clear performance space with moderate room to move. No props or chairs are required, though two chairs can be placed off-stage if needed.

## How to Play
1. Pair up two players and have them stand in the performance space.
2. Provide a simple, physical suggestion of a location or activity to kickstart the scene.
3. Instruct the players that once the scene begins, they cannot make any vocal sounds, words, or intentional sound effects.
4. Player A initiates with a clear, slow physical action (object work) that establishes the 'Where' and their 'Character' or 'Objective.'
5. Player B enters, observing Player A's physical choices, and immediately mirrors or complements the environment, establishing their 'Relationship' to Player A.
6. Both players must interact with the imaginary environment and each other, using eye contact, physical touch, and body language to discover their shared objective.
7. The scene continues for two to three minutes, focusing on slow, deliberate physical storytelling rather than rushing to mime complex plots.
8. The facilitator calls 'scene' once a clear narrative arc or emotional shift has been physically expressed.

## Facilitation Notes
- Coaching cue: 'Slow down your movements. Let us see the weight, size, and texture of the objects you are holding.'
- Coaching cue: 'Look at your partner's face. What is their expression telling you about your relationship right now?'
- Pitfall: Players trying to 'mouth' words or use sign language/charades. Fix: Remind them to live in the reality of the scene physically, not to translate English into silent gestures.
- Pitfall: Rushing through actions. Fix: Side-coach them to feel the physical resistance of their environment, like the heavy pull of a drawer or the heat of a stove.

## Variations
- Soundtrack Only: Play instrumental music in the background to influence the emotional tone of the silent scene.
- The Verbal Reveal: Run the scene silently for two minutes, then on a facilitator's cue, players transition into spoken dialogue, maintaining the exact physical reality they built.
- Tag-In Silence: Allow audience members to tag into the silent scene to change the relationship or objective physically.

## Debrief
- How did removing your voice change how closely you had to watch your partner?
- What specific physical choices made the 'Where' (environment) feel real to you?
- How did you discover your relationship and objective without speaking?

## Safety & Inclusion
Ensure the physical space is clear of tripping hazards. Since players cannot use verbal cues to establish physical boundaries, establish a non-verbal 'pause' or 'stop' signal (like a raised hand) before the exercise begins to ensure physical comfort and safety during close contact.

## Why It Works
Stripping away verbal dialogue forces improvisers to abandon intellectualizing and instead rely on physical empathy and spatial awareness. By physically committing to the environment, players naturally embody their characters and relationships, proving that the strongest foundation of a scene is often non-verbal.
