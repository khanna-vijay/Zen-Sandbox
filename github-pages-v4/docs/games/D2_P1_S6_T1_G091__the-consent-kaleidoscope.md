# The Consent Prism

![The Consent Prism — infographic](img/D2_P1_S6_T1_G091__the-consent-kaleidoscope.jpg){ .game-hero }

> Pause, negotiate, and replay scene moments to master real-time boundary navigation and partner support.

## Overview
A structured scene-work exercise where players use intentional pauses to step out of character, negotiate physical or thematic boundaries, and immediately replay the moment with clear consent. It transforms moments of uncertainty or discomfort into collaborative, real-time adjustments, ensuring safety and creative alignment.

## What It Trains
- **Domain:** D2 — The Partner
- **Principle(s):** Consent & Boundaries; Yes, And; Vulnerability; Truth Over Pandering
- **Skill(s):** Boundary Navigation; Active Listening; Offer Reception; Support Work
- **Technique(s):** Check-ins; Cut calls; Negotiating physical contact
- **Focus:** connection

**Objective:** To develop active boundary navigation, real-time check-ins, and the ability to prioritize personal and partner safety over narrative momentum (Truth Over Pandering).

## Setup
An open, comfortable playing space. Designate a specific physical area slightly off to the side as the 'Reflection Zone' where players step when a pause is called. No props are required.

## How to Play
1. Two players step into the performance space to begin a standard, character-driven scene based on a simple suggestion.
2. At any point during the scene—especially when physical contact is initiated, sensitive themes emerge, or a player feels internal hesitation—any participant (active player, offstage support player, or facilitator) can call out 'Reflect!'
3. Upon hearing the call, players immediately freeze, step out of character, and walk together into the designated 'Reflection Zone' to transition from actors to collaborative partners.
4. The facilitator guides a brief, two-minute micro-debrief focusing on three elements: what just happened, the intent behind the offer, and how the offer landed with the receiver.
5. The players explicitly negotiate the next step, stating clear boundaries (e.g., 'I am comfortable with a hand on my shoulder, but not a hug' or 'Let's steer away from family conflict').
6. Once a mutual agreement is reached, the players step back into the performance space, rewind the action to just before the 'Reflect' call, and replay the moment using the newly established boundaries.
7. At any point, any player can call 'Cut!' to immediately and permanently end the scene if a boundary is crossed or if they feel unsafe, bypassing the negotiation phase entirely.

## Facilitation Notes
- Establish a strong, low-stakes environment before starting, emphasizing that calling a reflection is a win for the team, not a failure of the scene.
- Keep the micro-debriefs strictly under two minutes to maintain energy; focus on objective observations ('I noticed a hand reach out') rather than judgment ('Why did you grab them?').
- Watch for non-verbal cues of discomfort (e.g., tensing up, stepping back, forced laughter) and use them as teaching moments to call a reflection from the sidelines.
- Ensure players do not use the negotiation phase to plan the narrative; the focus must remain solely on physical safety, emotional comfort, and boundary alignment.

## Variations
- Silent Check-In: Players use a designated non-verbal gesture (like a double-tap on the shoulder) to check in mid-scene without calling a full verbal pause.
- Support-Led Reflections: Only the offstage support players are allowed to call 'Reflect', training the ensemble's active listening and protective instincts for their teammates.

## Debrief
- How did it feel to step out of character mid-scene to discuss boundaries, and did it get easier with practice?
- In what moments did you feel tempted to 'pander' or push through discomfort for the sake of the joke, and how did the pause help?
- How can we carry this heightened awareness of partner comfort into fast-paced, unstructured scenes where we cannot pause?

## Safety & Inclusion
This game is highly safety-sensitive. Establish a clear group agreement beforehand. Ensure players know they have absolute autonomy over their bodies and comfort levels. The 'Cut!' command must be treated as absolute and non-negotiable, requiring no explanation or justification from the player who called it.

## Why It Works
By decoupling the player's personal boundaries from their character's actions, this game removes the pressure to always say 'yes' to physical or emotional escalation. Replaying the moment immediately after negotiating boundaries builds muscle memory for active consent, proving that setting limits actually enhances creative freedom and trust.
