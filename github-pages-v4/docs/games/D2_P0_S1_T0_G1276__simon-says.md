# Simon Says

![Simon Says — infographic](img/D2_P0_S1_T0_G1276__simon-says.jpg){ .game-hero }

> Sharpen active listening and bypass autopilot by responding only to exact, verified verbal cues.

## Overview
A high-energy, non-elimination focus game where players execute physical actions only when preceded by a specific verbal cue. Instead of sitting out upon making a mistake, players celebrate the slip-up, reset, and immediately rejoin the action. This fast-paced drill trains participants to override physical autopilot, ignore misleading visual cues, and listen to the literal words spoken.

## What It Trains
- **Domain:** D2 — The Partner
- **Principle(s):** Commit 100%
- **Skill(s):** Active Listening; Unfiltered Spontaneity; Peripheral Awareness
- **Focus:** skill_drill

**Objective:** To develop acute auditory active listening and impulse control, training players to respond to the actual verbal offers of their partners rather than anticipating or reacting to visual assumptions.

## Setup
Players stand in a circle or spread out comfortably across the playing space, all facing the facilitator who begins as the Commander. No props are required.

## How to Play
1. The facilitator designates themselves as the Commander and explains that they will issue rapid-fire physical commands.
2. Players must only execute a command if it is preceded by the exact phrase 'Simon says.' If the command lacks this prefix, players must remain completely frozen.
3. To eliminate the exclusionary nature of traditional play, there is no elimination. If a player makes a mistake, they must celebrate the mistake by throwing their hands in the air, shouting 'Woo!', taking one spin, and immediately rejoining.
4. The group works cooperatively to build a 'Focus Streak'—counting how many correct commands they can execute consecutively as a collective unit before anyone triggers a reset.
5. The Commander actively tries to trick the players by performing the physical actions themselves while omitting the verbal prefix, testing whether players are truly listening or just copying visual cues.
6. The Commander can also use verbal traps, such as asking casual questions like 'Is everyone ready?' to catch players nodding or responding physically without the cue.
7. After a few minutes, the role of Commander is passed to a participant, allowing different players to practice vocal clarity, rhythm, and reading the room's physical energy.

## Facilitation Notes
- Side-coach the connection to scene work: 'Listen to the exact words spoken, not just the physical vibe. In scenes, we react to what is actually said, not what we assume is coming.'
- Encourage joyful failure: Ensure the 'Woo!' reset is high-energy and celebratory. This removes the fear of messing up and keeps the energy high.
- Vary the tempo: Start at a moderate pace to establish the pattern, then rapidly accelerate the commands to force players out of their analytical minds and into pure spontaneous reaction.
- Watch for visual copying: Remind players to keep their eyes on the Commander but rely entirely on their auditory processing. If the Commander touches their head but says 'Simon says touch your knees,' players must touch their knees.
- Online Adaptation: In a virtual setting, players can play in gallery view. Instead of full-body movements, use close-up gestures like framing the face or blinking, or use the camera on/off feature as the primary movement.

## Variations
- Narrative Simon: The Commander tells a rapid-fire story. Whenever they say 'Simon says [Action]', players must do it. If they describe an action in the story without the prefix, players must remain still. This directly bridges literal listening to narrative tracking.
- Emotional Shift: Add emotional directives to the commands, such as 'Simon says jump joyfully' or 'Simon says look around suspiciously', training players to layer emotional commitment onto physical offers.
- The Echo Chamber: When 'Simon says' is used, the players must loudly repeat the command back to the leader before executing it, reinforcing auditory processing and vocal agreement.

## Debrief
- How did it feel to focus on the literal words spoken rather than just copying the physical movements you saw?
- In what ways does reacting to visual cues or anticipating a command mirror how we sometimes jump ahead of our scene partners instead of truly listening to their dialogue?
- How did removing the elimination mechanic and celebrating mistakes change your willingness to commit fully to each movement?

## Safety & Inclusion
Ensure all physical commands are highly adaptable. Encourage players to modify any movement to fit their physical comfort and mobility levels, such as blinking instead of jumping, or gesturing with a hand instead of a full-body movement. Maintain a supportive, low-stakes environment where mistakes are treated as gifts.

## Why It Works
By removing elimination, the game transforms from a stressful test of compliance into a cooperative focus drill. It targets the gap between hearing and listening; players must filter out misleading visual stimuli and focus entirely on the auditory cue. This directly builds the neural pathways needed in scene work to hear a partner's exact words rather than reacting to our own assumptions of what they mean.
