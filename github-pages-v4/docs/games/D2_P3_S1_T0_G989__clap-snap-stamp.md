# Snap, Clap, Stamp

![Snap, Clap, Stamp — infographic](img/D2_P3_S1_T0_G989__clap-snap-stamp.jpg){ .game-hero }

> A high-energy coordination game of mutual support, rhythm, and joyful failure.

## Overview
Players work in pairs to establish a simple numerical count, gradually replacing each number with a physical action. As the cognitive load increases, partners must rely on intense eye contact and shared rhythm to keep the sequence alive, celebrating mistakes together when the pattern breaks.

## What It Trains
- **Domain:** D2 — The Partner
- **Principle(s):** Fail Joyfully; Make Your Partner a Genius; Group Mind
- **Skill(s):** Unfiltered Spontaneity; Self-Recovery; Active Listening; Pacing & Rhythm
- **Technique(s):** Reframe-the-flub reps; Timing exercises
- **Focus:** connection

**Objective:** To build deep partner connection, active listening, and physical synchronization while training players to recover instantly and joyfully from mistakes.

## Setup
Players stand in pairs facing each other throughout the room. No props or special materials are required; just enough space for pairs to move their arms and stamp their feet safely.

## How to Play
1. Divide the group into pairs, standing face-to-face with comfortable eye contact.
2. Instruct pairs to begin a continuous, alternating count from one to three (Player A says 'one', Player B says 'two', Player A says 'three', Player B says 'one', and so on).
3. Once pairs establish a steady, rapid rhythm, instruct them to replace the number 'one' with a finger snap, while still vocalizing 'two' and 'three' in sequence.
4. After a minute of practice, introduce the next level: replace the number 'two' with a single hand clap, keeping the snap for 'one' and the spoken 'three'.
5. Introduce the final level: replace the number 'three' with a firm stamp of one foot, resulting in a completely non-verbal sequence of snap, clap, stamp.
6. If either partner hesitates, makes a mistake, or performs the wrong action, both partners must instantly celebrate the error (e.g., with a quick bow or high-five) and immediately restart the sequence from the beginning.

## Facilitation Notes
- Side-coach players to maintain soft eye contact rather than staring at their partner's hands or feet; this builds intuitive connection.
- Encourage a high tempo. Going too slowly actually makes the cognitive processing harder and lowers the energy.
- Watch for frustration. Remind players to 'fail joyfully' by laughing off mistakes and restarting immediately without analyzing who messed up.
- If a player cannot snap their fingers, suggest they make a clicking sound with their tongue or tap their chest instead.

## Variations
- Group Circle: Run the game in a single large circle, passing the sequence clockwise around the room.
- Custom Actions: Allow pairs to invent their own three physical movements or sounds to replace the numbers once they master the basic version.
- The Glitch: On a facilitator's signal, pairs must reverse the order of the actions (stamp, clap, snap) without stopping.

## Debrief
- How did your focus shift as we transitioned from spoken numbers to physical actions?
- What did it feel like when your partner made a mistake, and how did your reaction affect their recovery?
- How does maintaining eye contact help you anticipate your partner's timing?

## Safety & Inclusion
Ensure participants with physical limitations or joint pain can substitute any action (like stamping or snapping) with a comfortable alternative, such as a nod, a shoulder shrug, or a vocal sound.

## Why It Works
By stripping away verbal communication and increasing cognitive demand, the game forces players to look outside themselves and focus entirely on their partner's physical cues. The rapid pace prevents overthinking, while the rule of celebrating mistakes removes the fear of failure, fostering a supportive 'make your partner look good' mindset.
