# Pop-Up Book

![Pop-Up Book — infographic](img/D4_P1_S2_T3_G808__pop-up-book.jpg){ .game-hero }

> Bring a physical storybook to life through synchronized narration, sudden poses, and mechanical animations.

## Overview
One player acts as the narrator of a whimsical children's book, while the remaining players lie flat on the floor as the pages. When the narrator physically turns a page, the floor players instantly pop up into frozen, three-dimensional scenic elements or characters. The narrator then manipulates these elements by pulling imaginary tabs, turning dials, or sliding levers, prompting the players to animate in response.

## What It Trains
- **Domain:** D4 — The Ensemble
- **Principle(s):** Group Mind; Serve the Piece; Commit 100%; Show, Don't Tell
- **Skill(s):** Support Work; Physicality & Space Work; Narrative Architecture; World-Building
- **Technique(s):** Playing architecture/objects; Object work; Story Spine
- **Focus:** mixed

**Objective:** To develop physical support work, group mind, and spatial awareness by transforming the body into scenic architecture and responsive mechanical objects.

## Setup
A large, open playing space free of obstacles. One player is designated as the Narrator and stands to the side. The remaining players (3 to 6) lie completely flat on the floor in the center of the stage, representing the closed pages of the book.

## How to Play
1. The Narrator asks the group for the title of a never-before-written children's book to inspire the story.
2. The Narrator begins the story with a brief spoken introduction, setting the scene while the floor players remain completely still and flat.
3. The Narrator mimics turning a giant, heavy page of a book, announcing what the new page reveals (e.g., 'And there, in the middle of the deep dark forest, stood the ancient candy cottage!').
4. As the page turns, the floor players must instantly pop up from the ground into a frozen, physical tableau representing the described scene, using their bodies to build the architecture, scenery, or characters.
5. The Narrator describes and physically interacts with interactive elements on the page, such as pulling a tab, sliding a lever, or turning a wheel (e.g., 'When you pull this tab, the witch's chimney starts to smoke!').
6. The players instantly animate their bodies to match the Narrator's mechanical cue, moving only in the specific, repetitive way dictated by the tab or dial, returning to a freeze when the Narrator releases it.
7. The Narrator continues the story, turning the page again; the players must immediately drop back down to the floor, completely flat, waiting for the next page to be turned.
8. Repeat this cycle for 3 to 4 pages, building a simple narrative arc with a clear beginning, middle, and end, before closing the book to finish the game.

## Facilitation Notes
- Encourage the Narrator to be highly specific with their physical descriptions and mechanical cues, giving the players clear actions to perform.
- Side-coach the floor players to work as an ensemble to build a complete picture rather than everyone trying to be the main character; some must play the background architecture.
- Pitfall: Players moving too fluidly like actors in a standard scene. Fix: Remind them they are cardboard cutouts and mechanical parts; their movements should be rigid, repetitive, and directly tied to the narrator's physical tabs and levers.
- Ensure the transition back to the floor is fast and silent, emphasizing the contrast between the flat page and the active pop-up.

## Variations
- Sound Effects: The players must make mechanical, repetitive sound effects that match their physical animations when a tab is pulled.
- Multi-Tab Pages: The narrator can pull multiple tabs at once, requiring different players to animate independently or in a chain reaction.
- The Pop-Up Dialogue: The narrator can touch a speech bubble on the page, allowing a specific popped-up character to speak one line of dialogue before freezing again.

## Debrief
- How did it feel to build a scene physically before knowing what your character or object would do?
- As the narrator, how did the physical shapes of the players inspire the direction of your story?
- What strategies did you use to ensure the stage looked like a cohesive page rather than a chaotic group of individuals?

## Safety & Inclusion
Since this game requires players to rapidly drop to the floor and pop back up, offer modifications for players with mobility or joint concerns. A player can sit in a chair and fold over to represent a closed page, or stand and simply cover their face or turn away, opening up when the page turns.

## Why It Works
This game relies on the 'Show, Don't Tell' principle by forcing players to communicate narrative elements purely through physical architecture. By stripping away verbal dialogue for the majority of the players, it heightens their reliance on Group Mind and physical support work, requiring them to instantly read the stage's negative space and fill it to create a balanced, cohesive visual composition.
