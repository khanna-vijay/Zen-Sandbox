# The Invocation

![The Invocation — infographic](img/D4_P1_S3_T1_G1136__invocation.jpg){ .game-hero }

> Elevate a mundane object into a mythic deity to inspire rich, thematic ensemble scenes.

## Overview
The Invocation is a ritualistic long-form opening where the ensemble collectively deconstructs a simple suggestion. By progressing through stages of literal description, personification, and poetic abstraction, players build a shared mythos. This high-energy, vocal-focused exercise establishes a deep group mind and generates complex characters and themes for subsequent scenes.

## What It Trains
- **Domain:** D4 — The Ensemble
- **Principle(s):** Group Mind; Serve the Piece; The First Thought Is a Gift; Make Your Partner a Genius
- **Skill(s):** Suggestion Deconstruction (A-to-C); Thematic Synthesis; Unfiltered Spontaneity; Vocal Craft; Active Gifting; World-Building
- **Technique(s):** A-to-C drills; Harold; Gibberish; Endowment-gifting drills; C.R.O.W. (Character, Relationship, Objective, Where)
- **Focus:** mixed

**Objective:** Develops advanced A-to-C suggestion deconstruction, active listening, and thematic synthesis, helping players move past literal interpretations into emotional and metaphorical play.

## Setup
Players stand in a tight semi-circle facing the playing space. No props or materials are required. The facilitator or audience provides a single, concrete, mundane object as a suggestion.

## How to Play
1. Step 1: Obtain a simple, concrete suggestion of an inanimate object, such as a stapler, a toaster, or a key.
2. Step 2: Begin Phase One (It Is) where players take turns making literal, physical observations about the object, always starting their sentences with "It is..." (e.g., "It is metal," "It is cold").
3. Step 3: Transition to Phase Two (You Are) once the physical attributes are established. Address the object directly, endowing it with human traits, emotions, and relationships, starting each sentence with "You are..." (e.g., "You are a keeper of secrets," "You are a silent witness to late-night work").
4. Step 4: Elevate to Phase Three (Thou Art or I Am) by shifting into poetic, mythic, or abstract language. Speak to the object as a deity or speak from its perspective, using grand metaphors (e.g., "Thou art the binder of broken worlds," "I am the pressure that holds the chaos together").
5. Step 5: Build the vocal energy and physical presence of the group during Phase Three, allowing voices to overlap, repeat key phrases, or create a rhythmic, chanting soundscape.
6. Step 6: Conclude the invocation at a high point of collective energy, then immediately transition into the first scene, with players stepping forward to embody the characters, dynamics, or themes discovered during the ritual.

## Facilitation Notes
- Side-coach the transitions: Call out "Phase Two: You Are" or "Phase Three: Thou Art" if the group is hesitant to shift linguistic gears.
- Watch out for players rushing through the phases. Ensure the group fully explores the literal attributes before jumping to the abstract, as a strong foundation makes the poetry richer.
- Encourage vocal variety and rhythm. If the energy feels flat, coach players to play with volume, pitch, whispering, or overlapping their lines.
- Remind players to listen to every contribution. The themes of the final scenes should directly inherit the emotional truths established in the later phases of the invocation.

## Variations
- Physicalized Invocation: Players add abstract, repetitive physical movements or tableaus that evolve alongside the linguistic phases.
- The Dark Invocation: Frame the object as a forbidden, sinister, or occult force to explore darker, high-stakes dramatic themes.
- Solo Invocation: A single player performs all three phases rapidly as a solo warm-up to stretch their own associative thinking.

## Debrief
- How did moving systematically from "It is" to "You are" change your emotional connection to a simple object?
- What themes emerged in the poetic phase that you wouldn't have thought of from the initial suggestion?
- How did the shared rhythm of the chant help the ensemble step into the first scene with a unified tone?

## Safety & Inclusion
Ensure players are mindful of vocal safety, especially during high-energy chanting or whispering. Encourage physical adaptations, such as sitting or leaning, for players who find standing in a semi-circle for extended periods challenging.

## Why It Works
By forcing players to follow a strict linguistic progression, the game bypasses the logical, joke-making brain. It systematically guides the ensemble from a literal starting point (A) to an associative middle (B) to a highly abstract, thematic destination (C), creating a rich, shared vocabulary that feeds directly into organic scene work.
