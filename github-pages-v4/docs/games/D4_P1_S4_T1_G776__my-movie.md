# My Movie, My Movie

![My Movie, My Movie — infographic](img/D4_P1_S4_T1_G776__my-movie.jpg){ .game-hero }

> A rapid-fire, high-energy movie generator driven by instant pitches, quick scenes, and rhythmic group edits.

## Overview
Players stand in a backline, ready to pitch fictional films based on two audience-provided letters. Guided by the facilitator's physical cues, players instantly generate titles, pitch hilarious synopses, and jump into rapid-fire scenes. The entire ensemble maintains a high-tempo rhythm, using a unified vocal edit to reset the stage and keep the momentum surging.

## What It Trains
- **Domain:** D4 — The Ensemble
- **Principle(s):** Group Mind; The First Thought Is a Gift; Start in the Middle
- **Skill(s):** Pacing & Rhythm; Unfiltered Spontaneity; Support Work; World-Building
- **Technique(s):** Edits (Sweep, Tag-Out, Sound/Light); First Thought drills; Walk-ons; C.R.O.W. (Character, Relationship, Objective, Where)
- **Focus:** comedy_game

**Objective:** Develops rapid group mind, sharp pacing, and seamless ensemble edits. Players practice immediate commitment to their first thoughts, instant scene support, and collective rhythmic transitions.

## Setup
An in-person stage or open playing space with a clear backline area. The ensemble stands in a horizontal line facing the audience. The facilitator acts as the director/host standing downstage or off to the side. No props are required.

## How to Play
1. The ensemble stands in a straight line across the back of the stage, facing the audience.
2. The facilitator asks the audience for two random letters of the alphabet to serve as the initials for all movie titles.
3. The facilitator points directly to any player in the line, who must instantly shout out a movie title using the two letters as the initials.
4. If the facilitator points to another player, that player immediately offers a different movie title using the same initials.
5. When the facilitator holds their hands apart in a stretching gesture, the currently selected player must rapidly pitch a brief, high-concept synopsis of their movie.
6. At any point during the synopsis, the facilitator calls out 'Let's see it!' to initiate a scene.
7. The pitching player and any supporting players immediately step forward to perform a high-energy, start-in-the-middle scene representing a key moment from that movie.
8. To end the scene, any player on stage or in the backline initiates the group edit by chanting 'My movie, my movie, my movie!' while stepping forward. The entire ensemble joins the chant, sweeps the stage, and resets to the backline to start the cycle again.

## Facilitation Notes
- Keep the pacing relentless. Do not let players stall or overthink their titles; encourage them to trust their first instinct, even if it makes no sense.
- Side-coach players in the backline to actively listen and immediately jump in to support the scenes. The moment 'Let's see it!' is called, support players should already be moving to establish the environment.
- Ensure the 'My movie' edit is loud, physical, and collective. It should feel like a rhythmic punctuation mark that instantly clears the energy of the previous scene.
- Pitfall: Players pitching long, convoluted synopses. Fix: Cut them off early with 'Let's see it!' to force them to discover the movie through action rather than explanation.

## Variations
- Genre Shift: The facilitator can shout out a genre right before pointing to a player, forcing the title and synopsis to match that style.
- Director's Cut: During the scene, the facilitator can call out 'Director's Cut!' and give a quick adjustment to instantly shift the scene's dynamic.
- Tag-Out Takeover: Allow players in the backline to tag-out characters within the movie scene to explore different parts of the same film before the final group edit.

## Debrief
- How did the rapid tempo of the edits affect your ability to overthink or second-guess your choices?
- What made the transition from the verbal pitch to the physical scene successful and immediate?
- How did the collective 'My movie' edit help maintain the high energy and group mind of the ensemble?

## Safety & Inclusion
Ensure the physical sweep or edit chant is energetic but respects personal space; players should avoid physical collisions during rapid transitions. Encourage players to use clear verbal and physical offers so that everyone, regardless of physical mobility, can safely and actively participate in the fast-paced scenes.

## Why It Works
This game works because it bypasses the analytical brain through speed and physical cues. By using a structured, rhythmic edit, the ensemble shares the responsibility of pacing, ensuring that no scene overstays its welcome. It builds group mind by requiring absolute focus on the facilitator's cues and immediate, unhesitating support from the backline.
