# The Relay Edit

![The Relay Edit — infographic](img/D4_P3_S4_T1_G1280__slacker.jpg){ .game-hero }

> Pass the narrative baton by following departing characters into entirely new scenes.

## Overview
The Relay Edit is a fluid long-form structure where scenes transition seamlessly without traditional sweeps or blackouts. Instead, transitions occur when a character exits an active scene, and the performance immediately follows that character into a brand-new environment with new partners. This creates a continuous, unbroken chain of interconnected vignettes driven entirely by character movement.

## What It Trains
- **Domain:** D4 — The Ensemble
- **Principle(s):** Serve the Piece; Serve the Story
- **Skill(s):** Pacing & Rhythm; Format Literacy; Narrative Architecture
- **Technique(s):** Edits (Sweep, Tag-Out, Sound/Light); Longform vs. shortform mechanics
- **Focus:** mixed

**Objective:** To develop advanced pacing, organic editing, and narrative architecture by using physical exits as the sole mechanism for scene transitions.

## Setup
An open performance space with a clear stage area and an off-stage area where players can observe. No props or chairs are required.

## How to Play
1. Begin with the entire ensemble standing off-stage, leaving the main performance space clear.
2. Two players enter the stage to initiate a grounded, two-person scene.
3. Establish the relationship, environment, and platform of the initial scene clearly, allowing it to develop naturally.
4. To initiate a transition, one of the active players must make a clear, motivated physical exit from the current scene.
5. As that player exits their current location, they do not return to the off-stage group; instead, they immediately move to a new area of the stage.
6. One or more off-stage players must instantly recognize this exit and step forward to meet the transitioning player in their new location, establishing a completely new scene.
7. The player who remained in the original scene must quietly slip off-stage as soon as their partner exits, clearing the space for the new focus.
8. The transitioning player must carry over their established character, but they are now in a new setting, at a different time, or interacting with different people.
9. Repeat this cycle of exits and entrances, allowing the narrative focus to shift seamlessly from character to character, creating an unbroken chain of stories.

## Facilitation Notes
- Coaching Cue: 'Follow the momentum!' Remind players off-stage to watch the active scene closely so they can jump in the moment a player begins their exit.
- Pitfall: The exiting player tries to 'wipe' the stage or do a sweep edit. Fix: Emphasize that the exiting player must remain on stage in their character, simply moving to a new imaginary location.
- Coaching Cue: 'Make the exit motivated.' The transition works best when the character has a strong, character-driven reason to leave the current scene.
- Pitfall: The remaining player lingers on stage, creating confusion about where the focus is. Fix: Once your partner leaves, immediately and silently exit to the wings.
- Coaching Cue: 'Vary the pace.' Not every scene needs to be the same length. Some transitions can happen rapidly, while others can breathe and develop.

## Variations
- The Return Loop: After several transitions, a previously exited character re-enters the chain, eventually bringing the sequence back to the very first scene or character to close the loop.
- The Tag-Team Relay: Instead of the exiting player carrying over, the player who stays is the one who gets visited by a new character entering their space, shifting the scene's context while keeping the location constant.
- Emotional Carryover: The transitioning character must carry the exact emotional state or physical posture from the end of the previous scene directly into the start of the next, regardless of how much time has passed.

## Debrief
- How did relying solely on character exits change the rhythm and speed of our edits compared to traditional sweep edits?
- What challenges did you face when trying to instantly establish a new reality for a character who just walked out of a different scene?
- How did this structure help us 'serve the piece' as an ensemble rather than focusing only on our individual scenes?

## Safety & Inclusion
Ensure the stage pathway is clear of physical obstacles, as players will be transitioning rapidly across the space. Encourage players to use clear verbal cues if physical exits are restricted by mobility, allowing them to 'exit' by turning their back or announcing their departure.

## Why It Works
This game works because it replaces the jarring nature of external edits with organic, character-driven transitions. By forcing players to focus on the physical movement of their ensemble partners, it builds a heightened group mind and teaches players to treat transitions as active, narrative-building moments rather than empty space.
