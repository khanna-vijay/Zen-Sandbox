# Leave for a Reason

![Leave for a Reason — infographic](img/D4_P1_S1_T0_G1153__leave-for-a-reason.jpg){ .game-hero }

> Exit the space together in complete silence, driven by a single, unspoken shared realization.

## Overview
Players move silently through the space, gradually tuning into each other's physical cues. Without speaking, the group must collectively discover a shared, imaginary reason to exit the room, executing a synchronized departure where everyone understands why they are leaving. It is a powerful exercise in non-verbal agreement, physical storytelling, and ensemble connection.

## What It Trains
- **Domain:** D4 — The Ensemble
- **Principle(s):** Group Mind; Follow the Follower
- **Skill(s):** Peripheral Awareness; Pacing & Rhythm; Silence & Stillness
- **Technique(s):** Timing exercises; Hold-the-beat reps
- **Focus:** connection

**Objective:** To develop deep peripheral awareness, group mind, and the ability to follow the follower by reading subtle physical offers and committing to a collective narrative without verbal communication.

## Setup
A clear, open room with a defined exit such as a door or a designated boundary line. No props are needed. Players stand scattered throughout the space.

## How to Play
1. Begin with all players milling about the room at a moderate, neutral pace, maintaining silence and soft-focus eye contact.
2. Instruct players to remain highly sensitive to the physical movements, postures, and focus of everyone else in the room.
3. At some point, one player initiates a subtle physical shift or reaction to an imaginary stimulus, such as looking up in fear, sniffing the air, or reacting to a sudden drop in temperature.
4. Rather than ignoring or overriding this offer, other players must immediately adopt, support, and amplify this physical reality, letting it ripple through the entire group.
5. As the shared imaginary circumstance becomes clear to everyone through physical mimicry and shared focus, the group must collectively decide—without speaking—to move toward the exit.
6. The entire group must exit the space at the exact same moment, fully committed to the same physicalized reason for leaving.
7. Once outside the space, the facilitator asks the group to state the reason they left to verify if the group mind achieved a singular, unified narrative.

## Facilitation Notes
- Coaching cue: Don't force an idea. Wait for a physical offer to emerge, then instantly agree with it.
- Coaching cue: Use your peripheral vision. If someone starts moving faster, feel the tempo of the room shift.
- Pitfall: Players might rush to exit before the reason is clear or shared. Fix: Remind them that the exit must be a consequence of the shared physical reality, not just a race to the door.
- Pitfall: Multiple competing ideas clash. Fix: Coach them to follow the follower—if you see two ideas, immediately drop yours and commit to the one that has more traction.

## Variations
- The Return: After exiting, the group must silently agree on a reason to return to the space, entering with a new shared physical state.
- Varying Tempos: Challenge the group to execute the departure in extreme slow motion or at a frantic, high-energy pace, maintaining perfect synchronization.
- The Single Instigator: Assign one secret leader who initiates the reason, while everyone else must detect and follow the shift without knowing who started it.

## Debrief
- How did it feel to transition from individual movement to a unified group action without speaking?
- What specific physical cues helped you understand the reason before you reached the exit?
- How did you handle moments where multiple physical offers were happening at the same time?

## Safety & Inclusion
Ensure the exit pathway is completely clear of tripping hazards. Remind players to be mindful of physical boundaries and varying mobility levels when moving quickly or changing levels to ensure everyone can safely participate in the exit.

## Why It Works
This game strips away verbal negotiation, forcing players to rely entirely on physical listening and peripheral awareness. By practicing follow the follower, players learn to surrender individual agendas in favor of supporting the emerging group consensus, which builds the foundational trust required for high-level ensemble work.
