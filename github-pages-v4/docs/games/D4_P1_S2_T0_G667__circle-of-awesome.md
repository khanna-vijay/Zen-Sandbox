# The Memory Cauldron

![The Memory Cauldron — infographic](img/D4_P1_S2_T0_G667__circle-of-awesome.jpg){ .game-hero }

> A high-energy closing ritual where players toss shared successes into a collective cauldron of appreciation.

## Overview
This is a collaborative closing exercise where participants stand in a circle and call out specific, positive moments they observed during the session. As each memory is shared, the entire group enthusiastically validates it by physically throwing it into an imaginary central cauldron. The game culminates in a unified, high-energy group celebration that cements ensemble bonding.

## What It Trains
- **Domain:** D4 — The Ensemble
- **Principle(s):** Group Mind; Make Your Partner a Genius
- **Skill(s):** Support Work; Active Gifting
- **Focus:** connection

**Objective:** To build ensemble cohesion, reinforce the principle of making your partner look good, and practice active gifting by celebrating others' creative choices.

## Setup
Players stand in a circle facing inward. No props or special materials are required; just a clear floor space.

## How to Play
1. Form a standing circle with all participants, leaving an open space in the center representing an imaginary cauldron.
2. Explain that the goal is to collectively harvest and celebrate the best moments, offers, and discoveries of the session.
3. Any player can speak up at any time in a popcorn style to call out a specific moment they appreciated, focusing on another player's contribution.
4. Immediately after a memory is shared, the entire group must shout a unified word of agreement, such as 'Yes!', while physically miming the action of grabbing that memory and tossing it into the center cauldron.
5. Continue this popcorn-style sharing, allowing multiple players to contribute their favorite moments and keeping the energy high with rapid-fire physical tosses.
6. Once the sharing naturally winds down, the facilitator guides the group to step forward and mime stirring the giant cauldron together.
7. On a count of three, the entire group leaps into the air, throwing their hands up, and shouts a final, unifying word to close the session.

## Facilitation Notes
- Coaching cue: 'Keep the focus on specific, concrete moments—the smaller and more detailed, the better!'
- Pitfall: Players might focus on their own performance. Fix: Gently remind them that this is an exercise in active gifting and making others look good; encourage them to highlight a peer's choice.
- Coaching cue: 'Match the physical energy of the toss! Make every single memory feel like a massive victory.'
- Pitfall: Long pauses between shares. Fix: The facilitator can jump in with a memory to kickstart the momentum, or transition to the stirring phase if the energy begins to dip.

## Variations
- Structured Rotation: Instead of popcorn style, pass the focus sequentially around the circle so every player has a dedicated moment to share and be celebrated.
- Self-Appreciation: Have players share a moment where they took a risk or felt proud of their own choice, helping build self-confidence.
- Neighborly Gift: Each player shares one specific thing they appreciated about the person standing directly to their left.

## Debrief
- How does it feel to have your specific choices remembered and celebrated by the entire group?
- Why is it valuable for an ensemble to actively look for and remember each other's successes?
- How does celebrating others' work make it easier to take creative risks in future scenes?

## Safety & Inclusion
Ensure that physical movements like tossing, stirring, and jumping are adaptable for all physical abilities; players can participate with small gestures or vocalizations if jumping is not accessible.

## Why It Works
It operationalizes the 'make your partner a genius' mindset by turning observation into a physical gift. By celebrating specific choices, it reinforces positive play patterns and builds a strong, supportive group mind where players feel safe to fail because they know their successes will be amplified.
