# Agreement Anchors

![Agreement Anchors — infographic](img/D3_P2_S5_T1_G629__agreement-scenes.jpg){ .game-hero }

> Establish a rock-solid base reality by aligning instantly on a single shared element of the scene.

## Overview
Two players step into the space with the singular goal of establishing and agreeing on one specific element of their base reality—such as location, relationship, or emotional atmosphere—within the first few lines. By prioritizing immediate alignment over conflict or cleverness, players build a stable foundation for the scene to unfold naturally. It teaches players how to receive and validate physical and verbal offers to create a cohesive world.

## What It Trains
- **Domain:** D3 — The Scene
- **Principle(s):** Yes, And; Base Reality First
- **Skill(s):** Active Listening; Offer Reception; World-Building
- **Technique(s):** Endowment-acceptance; C.R.O.W. (Character, Relationship, Objective, Where)
- **Focus:** skill_drill

**Objective:** To develop the skill of establishing a clear Base Reality (C.R.O.W.) through immediate agreement and physical/verbal offer reception, preventing early-scene confusion or negotiation.

## Setup
A clear performance space at the front of the room. The rest of the group sits as an active audience. No props or materials are needed. The facilitator prepares a list of 'Agreement Categories' (e.g., Location, Relationship, Activity, Emotion, Atmosphere).

## How to Play
1. The facilitator calls up two players to the performance space and assigns them a specific 'Agreement Category' (e.g., 'Location' or 'Atmosphere').
2. Player A enters the space and initiates with a clear physical action, posture, or line of dialogue that establishes the assigned category.
3. Player B immediately enters, observes Player A's offer, and mirrors, supports, or directly agrees with that established element through their own physical choices and dialogue.
4. Both players must explicitly confirm and lean into this shared reality within the first three exchanges, ensuring there is zero doubt about the chosen category.
5. Once the agreement is firmly established (the 'anchor' is set), the players continue the scene for one to two minutes, letting the narrative grow naturally from this stable foundation.
6. The facilitator calls 'scene' once the base reality is fully realized and stable, then rotates in a new pair with a different category.

## Facilitation Notes
- Side-coaching cue: 'Match their physical energy!' or 'Say yes to the space they have built!'
- Common Pitfall: Players trying to introduce conflict too early. Fix: Remind them that agreement is the engine of this drill; conflict can wait until the base reality is solid.
- Common Pitfall: Verbalizing the agreement too explicitly or dryly (e.g., 'We are at the beach, Bob.'). Fix: Encourage physicalizing the environment or relationship first, then using natural dialogue to confirm it.
- Side-coaching cue: 'Show us where you are before you tell us.'

## Variations
- Blind Agreement: Players do not know which category they are agreeing on beforehand; Player A initiates a category physically, and Player B must identify and match it instantly without verbal negotiation.
- Double Anchor: Players must agree on two categories simultaneously (e.g., Location AND Emotion) within the first three lines.
- Silent Agreement: The first thirty seconds of the scene must be entirely silent, establishing the agreement (like relationship or activity) purely through physical mirroring and object work before any dialogue begins.

## Debrief
- How did it feel to start a scene where you knew your partner was 100% aligned with your initial choice?
- What physical cues did you rely on to understand what your partner was establishing?
- Why does establishing a clear 'where' or 'who' early on make the rest of the scene feel easier and less pressured?

## Safety & Inclusion
Ensure physical actions (like mirroring or close proximity) respect personal boundaries. Encourage players to establish non-contact physical agreements (e.g., sitting side-by-side, matching posture) unless explicit consent is established.

## Why It Works
By narrowing the players' focus to a single, shared point of agreement, it removes the cognitive load of inventing a plot. When both players commit to the same Base Reality (C.R.O.W.) immediately, they eliminate the 'negotiation phase' of a scene, allowing them to discover the scene's comedy or drama organically from a place of mutual trust.
