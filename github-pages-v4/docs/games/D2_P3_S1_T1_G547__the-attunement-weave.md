# The Attunement Weave

![The Attunement Weave — infographic](img/D2_P3_S1_T1_G547__the-attunement-weave.jpg){ .game-hero }

> Weave active listening, emotional echoing, and status shifts into a seamless, deeply connected dialogue.

## Overview
A two-player exercise where partners build a scene by strictly dividing every conversational turn into two parts: an Echo and an Advance. Players first validate and reflect their partner's last offer before gifting new information and shifting their status. This structured exchange prevents players from planning ahead, forcing them to stay entirely present and responsive.

## What It Trains
- **Domain:** D2 — The Partner
- **Principle(s):** Yes, And; Make Your Partner a Genius; Assume Competence
- **Skill(s):** Active Listening; Status Modulation; Single-Partner Empathy & Mirroring; Offer Reception; Active Gifting
- **Technique(s):** Meisner Repetition; Last Word Response; Status Seesaw; High/low-status walks; Mirror exercise; Emotional-echo drills; Yes, And… sentence games; Endowment-acceptance; Endowment-gifting drills; Give them the answer
- **Focus:** connection

**Objective:** To develop deep active listening, emotional mirroring, and dynamic status modulation, training players to prioritize their partner's offers and make them look brilliant before advancing the scene.

## Setup
Players stand in pairs facing each other with comfortable eye contact. No pre-determined characters or settings are established. The facilitator provides a simple, open-ended relationship prompt to kick off the dialogue.

## How to Play
1. Establish pairs facing each other. Player A initiates the scene with a simple, grounded statement or physical action.
2. Player B must begin their response with 'The Echo,' which is a direct, empathetic reflection of Player A's verbal or physical offer.
3. Immediately following the Echo, Player B delivers 'The Advance,' which is a proactive gift of new information that builds directly on the echoed material.
4. During the Advance, Player B must make a conscious choice to modulate their status relative to Player A, adjusting their posture, vocal tone, or eye contact.
5. Player A then takes their turn, starting with an Echo of Player B's Advance, followed by their own Advance with a new status choice.
6. Continue this alternating pattern, ensuring every single turn contains both a clear, validating Echo and a progressive, status-conscious Advance.
7. The facilitator calls 'Freeze' after 3 to 4 minutes of play to let pairs reset, reflect, and switch starting roles.

## Facilitation Notes
- Coaching Cue: 'Hear the echo before you think of your next line. Let their words land in your body first.'
- Pitfall: Players often rush the Echo to get to their planned Advance. Fix: Have them pause for one full breath after their partner speaks before delivering the Echo.
- Coaching Cue: 'Play with the status seesaw. If your partner lowered their status, how does that affect yours?'
- Pitfall: The dialogue feels mechanical or robotic. Fix: Encourage players to focus on emotional mirroring (matching the partner's sigh, smile, or tension) rather than just repeating words.

## Variations
- The Silent Echo: The Echo phase must be entirely non-verbal (mirroring physical posture, breath, or facial expression) before the verbal Advance.
- Status Lock: One player is assigned to always lower their status during the Advance, while the other must always raise theirs, exploring how this affects the emotional connection.
- Meisner Repetition Start: Begin the Echo phase by repeating the partner's exact last three words before translating it into an emotional observation.

## Debrief
- How did it feel to have your partner explicitly echo your offer before they added anything new?
- Did you find it difficult to balance active listening with planning your status shift?
- How did modulating your status change the emotional stakes of the relationship?

## Safety & Inclusion
Ensure players establish comfortable physical boundaries before starting, especially when mirroring body language or making intense eye contact. Players may opt out of direct eye contact if it causes anxiety, focusing instead on vocal tone and physical gestures.

## Why It Works
By forcing a two-part structure of reception (Echo) and contribution (Advance), the game short-circuits the improviser's habit of planning ahead. The Echo acts as an embodied 'Yes,' validating the partner's competence and making them the focus of the scene, while the status modulation adds immediate dramatic tension and relational depth.
