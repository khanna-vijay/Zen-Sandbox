# Three-Part Tableau

![Three-Part Tableau — infographic](img/D4_P1_S2_T3_G802__piece-of-cheese.jpg){ .game-hero }

> Build instant, three-part physical pictures by offering the most obvious, supportive connection possible.

## Overview
Players work in rapid succession to build a three-person physical tableau based on simple, thematic associations. One player steps into the space as an object, two others quickly join to complete the scene, and the cycle resets by carrying one element forward. It is a fast-paced, high-energy exercise in physicalizing objects and immediate, unthinking support.

## What It Trains
- **Domain:** D4 — The Ensemble
- **Principle(s):** Group Mind; Yes, And; The First Thought Is a Gift
- **Skill(s):** Support Work; Offer Reception; Unfiltered Spontaneity
- **Technique(s):** Playing architecture/objects; First Thought drills
- **Focus:** connection

**Objective:** To develop group mind and physical support work by making immediate, obvious offers that build on a teammate's physical and verbal premise without overthinking.

## Setup
Players stand in a large, open circle with a clear performance space in the center. No props or materials are required.

## How to Play
1. Begin with all players standing in a circle, leaving the center space clear.
2. One player spontaneously steps into the center of the circle, strikes a distinct physical pose, and declares what they are (e.g., 'I am a tree!').
3. A second player must immediately rush in, strike a complementary pose, and declare a related object that connects to the first (e.g., 'I am a branch!').
4. A third player quickly joins, posing and declaring a third related object that completes the thematic picture (e.g., 'I am a squirrel!').
5. Once all three players are frozen in their tableau, the first player who entered selects one of the other two players to remain in the space (e.g., 'I will keep the squirrel').
6. The first player and the unselected player return to the outer circle, leaving only the chosen player in the center.
7. The remaining player maintains their pose, repeats their identity ('I am a squirrel'), and the process repeats as two new players rush in to build a brand-new tableau around them.

## Facilitation Notes
- Encourage speed over cleverness: Remind players that the first, most obvious association is always the best choice.
- Focus on physical levels: Coach players to use high, medium, and low physical levels to make the tableau visually interesting and dynamic.
- Pitfall: Players hesitating at the edge of the circle. Fix: Side-coach with 'Run in! Don't think, just move your feet!'
- Pitfall: Trying to be funny or original instead of obvious. Fix: Remind the group that a boring, obvious choice (like a cup to go with a teapot) is highly supportive and satisfying.

## Variations
- Abstract Concepts: Instead of physical objects, players must initiate with emotions, abstract concepts, or environments (e.g., 'I am sadness', 'I am rain').
- Soundscapes: Players must add a repetitive sound or vocal effect that matches their object, creating a noisy, kinetic machine.
- Silent Tableau: Build the entire three-part picture in complete silence, relying purely on physical shapes and eye contact to establish the relationship.

## Debrief
- How did it feel to jump in without a fully formed plan?
- What made a teammate's offer easy to support physically and conceptually?
- How does choosing the 'obvious' option actually make the scene more cohesive for the group?

## Safety & Inclusion
Ensure the physical space is clear of tripping hazards. Players should be mindful of personal boundaries and physical comfort when posing close to others; physical contact is not required to show connection.

## Why It Works
This game bypasses the analytical brain by demanding rapid physical commitment. By forcing players to immediately support an established offer, it builds trust and demonstrates that cohesive group mind emerges from simple, obvious additions rather than complex, individual invention.
