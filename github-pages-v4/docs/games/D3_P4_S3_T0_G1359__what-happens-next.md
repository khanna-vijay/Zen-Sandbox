# What Happens Next

![What Happens Next — infographic](img/D3_P4_S3_T0_G1359__what-happens-next.jpg){ .game-hero }

> Collaboratively direct a solo performer's physical journey, building a cohesive story one step at a time.

## Overview
One performer takes the stage as a physical avatar, executing actions dictated entirely by the surrounding group. After completing each action, the performer asks 'What happens next?', prompting the group to supply the next logical step in the narrative. The challenge lies in the group's ability to listen to one another and construct a satisfying, coherent story arc rather than a series of disconnected gag setups.

## What It Trains
- **Domain:** D3 — The Scene
- **Principle(s):** Serve the Story; Group Mind; Yes, And
- **Skill(s):** Narrative Architecture; Support Work; Thematic Synthesis; Offer Reception
- **Technique(s):** Endowment-acceptance
- **Focus:** narrative

**Objective:** To develop narrative architecture and collaborative story-building. Players learn to serve the story by offering logical, progressive steps that build on previous actions, practicing active listening and thematic synthesis.

## Setup
Arrange the group in a semi-circle facing a designated performance space. No props or materials are required. One player volunteers to start in the center as the active performer, while the remaining players act as the collective narrative engine from the audience area.

## How to Play
1. Select one player to stand in the center of the playing space; this player will act as the physical executor of the story.
2. The seated players (the narrators) initiate the scene by calling out a simple, mundane starting action for the performer (e.g., 'You are standing at a bus stop holding a wet umbrella').
3. The performer immediately executes this action physically, using clear object work and pantomime, without speaking.
4. Once the performer has fully committed to and completed the physical action, they look to the group and ask, 'What happens next?'
5. Any player from the seated group calls out the next single action. This action should logically follow the previous one, advancing the plot or deepening the character's situation.
6. The performer executes this new action precisely as described, adding physical detail but not inventing new narrative directions on their own.
7. The cycle repeats: the performer completes the action, asks 'What happens next?', and the group provides the next beat, working together to guide the story through a beginning, middle, and clear resolution.
8. Once a satisfying conclusion is reached, the group calls 'Scene,' and a new performer takes the center to start a new story.

## Facilitation Notes
- Side-coach the performer to remain a 'blank slate' or physical vessel. They should not try to solve the story's problems or add unprompted narrative elements; their job is pure physical execution.
- Watch out for narrators shouting over each other. If multiple suggestions are made, the performer should instantly accept the first clear one they hear, or the facilitator can gently designate which one to follow.
- Encourage the narrators to focus on logical progression rather than random, absurd twists. If a narrator suggests something completely unrelated, remind them to 'serve the story' and build on what already exists.
- Remind players to use physical space and object work. The performer should take their time to make the actions look real, which gives the narrators more time to think of the next beat.

## Variations
- Emotional Undercurrent: The group must specify not only the action but also the emotion the performer feels while doing it (e.g., 'You open the letter, but you are terrified of what is inside').
- Genre-Bound: Challenge the group to tell the story within a specific genre (e.g., Film Noir, Fairy Tale, Sci-Fi), forcing them to synthesize thematic tropes into their narrative suggestions.
- The Silent Performer: The performer is completely mute throughout, using a physical gesture (like an open-handed shrug) instead of the verbal cue 'What happens next?' to prompt the group.

## Debrief
- How did it feel to have absolute control over the character's actions versus having no control over where the story went?
- What made a suggestion feel 'right' or satisfying versus jarring or disruptive to the narrative flow?
- How did we balance keeping the story grounded with moving the plot forward?

## Safety & Inclusion
Ensure the physical actions suggested are safe and accessible for the performer in the center. Performers should feel empowered to modify any physical suggestion to fit their comfort level and physical abilities without breaking the flow of the game.

## Why It Works
This game separates the physical execution of a scene from the narrative planning, allowing the performer to focus entirely on physical commitment and object work. Meanwhile, the group is forced to practice collective narrative architecture. Because no single person owns the story, players must practice 'Yes, And' on a structural level, accepting the previous narrator's contribution and building the next logical step to serve the overall story arc.
