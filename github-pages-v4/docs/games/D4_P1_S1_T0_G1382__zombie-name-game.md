# Zombie Name Chase

![Zombie Name Chase — infographic](img/D4_P1_S1_T0_G1382__zombie-name-game.jpg){ .game-hero }

> Deflect a slow-moving zombie by calling out a classmate's name before you get tagged.

## Overview
A high-energy circle warm-up where players must maintain intense focus and peripheral awareness to avoid a slow-moving zombie. By calling out a peer's name, a targeted player deflects the threat, forcing the zombie to instantly pivot toward the newly named target. It builds rapid-fire recall, group cohesion, and spontaneous reaction under pressure.

## What It Trains
- **Domain:** D4 — The Ensemble
- **Principle(s):** Group Mind; The First Thought Is a Gift
- **Skill(s):** Peripheral Awareness; Unfiltered Spontaneity; Active Listening
- **Technique(s):** First Thought drills
- **Focus:** connection

**Objective:** To develop peripheral awareness, active listening, and unfiltered spontaneity by forcing players to quickly scan the room, recall names under pressure, and stay highly attuned to the physical movement of the group.

## Setup
Have all players stand in a wide, clear circle with plenty of space in the center. Ensure there are no tripping hazards. No props are required. Ensure everyone knows each other's names before starting.

## How to Play
1. Gather all players in a large circle, facing inward, and establish that everyone must remain in their spot unless they are the active zombie.
2. Designate one player to start in the center as the Zombie. This player must move at a slow, steady, dramatic pace with arms outstretched toward a target in the circle.
3. The Zombie chooses an initial target in the circle and begins walking directly toward them.
4. To save themselves, the targeted player must make eye contact with another player in the circle and loudly call out that player's name before the Zombie touches them.
5. As soon as a name is called, the Zombie must immediately pivot and begin walking toward the newly named player.
6. If the Zombie successfully tags their target before that target can say a name, the tagged player becomes the new Zombie, and the old Zombie takes their place in the circle.
7. The new Zombie must immediately begin targeting someone else from the center of the circle to keep the momentum going.

## Facilitation Notes
- Coaching cue: Keep the zombie pace slow and steady! If the zombie runs, the game becomes chaotic and unsafe; the challenge should be mental, not athletic.
- Pitfall: Players freeze and can't think of a name. Fix: Encourage them to blurt out the first name they see, even if they make a mistake. Remind them that the first thought is a gift.
- Coaching cue: Eyes open, ears open! Players not currently targeted must stay highly alert, as they could be named at any second and must react instantly.
- Pitfall: Players only name their close friends or the same three people. Fix: Introduce a rule that you cannot name the person who just named you, or challenge the group to name people across the circle.

## Variations
- Category Swap: Instead of names, use a pre-determined category like fruits, countries, or animals. Players must shout a unique item from that category to deflect the zombie.
- Double Zombie: Introduce a second zombie to the center, requiring players to track two threats simultaneously and doubling the need for peripheral awareness.
- Emotional Zombie: The zombie moves with a specific emotion, and the targeted player must adopt that emotion when shouting the next name.

## Debrief
- How did it feel when you heard your name called? What did you have to do to prepare for that moment?
- How did peripheral awareness help you anticipate where the threat was moving?
- What happened when you hesitated or tried to find the perfect name to call? How does first thought thinking help us here?

## Safety & Inclusion
Ensure the tag is a gentle tap on the shoulder or arm. For players with mobility challenges, the zombie's speed can be adjusted to a very slow crawl, or the zombie can use a pointing gesture from a safe distance instead of physical contact.

## Why It Works
This game works because it pairs physical tension with cognitive recall. The slow, relentless approach of the zombie creates a playful pressure cooker that bypasses the analytical brain, forcing players to rely on unfiltered spontaneity. It demands that the entire circle stay connected through active listening and sightlines, embodying the concept of Group Mind.
