# Spotlight Story Pass

![Spotlight Story Pass — infographic](img/D2_P2_S4_T1_G201__zoom-story-spotlight-shuffle.jpg){ .game-hero }

> Build a collaborative virtual story using physical reactions, rapid spotlighting, and silent visual handoffs.

## Overview
A dynamic, virtual-first storytelling game where players collaboratively construct a narrative one sentence at a time. By utilizing the video platform's spotlight feature, physical reactions, and exaggerated pointing, players bypass remote latency to build a seamless, high-energy story.

## What It Trains
- **Domain:** D2 — The Partner
- **Principle(s):** Yes, And; Serve the Story; Group Mind
- **Skill(s):** Physicality & Space Work; Active Listening; Offer Reception; Narrative Architecture; Peripheral Awareness
- **Technique(s):** Yes, And… sentence games; Story Spine; Thread-tracking drills
- **Focus:** narrative

**Objective:** To develop rapid offer reception, active listening, and physical embodiment in a virtual space, reinforcing the 'Yes, And' principle by requiring players to physically react to an offer before building upon it.

## Setup
Conducted on a video conferencing platform with a spotlight feature. All participants must be in grid view with their cameras on. The facilitator acts as the host with spotlighting privileges. No physical props are required, but players should ensure their upper bodies are visible in their camera frames.

## How to Play
1. The facilitator establishes a narrative prompt or starting premise in the chat, such as an antique object holding a strange secret.
2. The facilitator spotlights the first storyteller, who delivers a single opening sentence to launch the narrative based on the prompt.
3. Immediately after speaking, the active player must make an exaggerated physical pointing gesture toward another player's video box in the grid view to pass the narrative baton.
4. The facilitator quickly switches the platform's spotlight to the player who was pointed to.
5. Before speaking, the newly spotlighted player must perform a silent, exaggerated physical reaction, such as a gasp, a shudder, or a look of shock, that reflects their character's emotional response to the previous sentence.
6. The spotlighted player then delivers exactly one sentence that accepts the previous line and advances the story.
7. This player then physically points to a new storyteller, and the cycle repeats, keeping the narrative moving briskly for approximately five to eight minutes.
8. The facilitator calls 'And scene!' to bring the story to a satisfying or hilariously abrupt conclusion.

## Facilitation Notes
- As the facilitator, practice quick-key spotlighting beforehand to minimize technical lag and keep the energy high.
- If a player forgets to react physically before speaking, gently side-coach with 'Show us your reaction first!' to reinforce physical embodiment.
- Encourage players to point clearly and hold the gesture for a second so the facilitator and the group can easily identify the next target.
- If the story becomes too disjointed, the facilitator can briefly interject with a guiding narrative prompt to steer the group back toward a cohesive arc.

## Variations
- Emotional Shift: The facilitator drops a new emotion in the chat mid-game, and the next spotlighted player must immediately adopt that emotional state in their physical reaction and sentence.
- Object Work Integration: Require each storyteller to mime holding or interacting with a physical object mentioned in the previous sentence during their turn.
- Sound Effect Start: Instead of a physical reaction, the spotlighted player must start their turn with a vocal sound effect that matches the previous line before delivering their sentence.

## Debrief
- How did having to physically react before speaking change how you listened to the previous line?
- What challenges did we face with virtual turn-taking, and how did the physical pointing help resolve them?
- How did we balance keeping the story logical versus letting it become wonderfully chaotic?

## Safety & Inclusion
Ensure players who may have physical mobility limitations can use alternative clear visual cues, such as a distinct head nod, a verbal name drop, or a virtual hand-raise reaction, to pass the turn.

## Why It Works
This game supercharges 'Yes, And' by forcing physical agreement before verbal addition. The mandatory physical reaction acts as a cognitive buffer against virtual audio lag, giving the player's brain time to process the offer and respond organically. Using the spotlight feature focuses the group's collective attention, eliminating the awkward 'who speaks next' overlap common in virtual spaces.
