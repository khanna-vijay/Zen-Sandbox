# Scene Blueprint

![Scene Blueprint — infographic](img/D3_P2_S5_T1_G364__scene-blueprint.jpg){ .game-hero }

> Establish a rich, unspoken physical and relational foundation in thirty seconds before launching your scene.

## Overview
A high-focus, two-phase training drill that challenges pairs to construct a robust base reality without explicit exposition. In the first thirty seconds, players use physical environment work and coded dialogue to imply their relationship, location, and activity. Once established, they immediately transition into a fully realized scene where they must justify and expand upon this blueprint.

## What It Trains
- **Domain:** D3 — The Scene
- **Principle(s):** Show, Don't Tell; Base Reality First; Yes, And
- **Skill(s):** World-Building; Justification; Game Identification; Active Listening; Offer Reception; Physicality & Space Work
- **Technique(s):** C.R.O.W. (Character, Relationship, Objective, Where); If this is true, what else is true?; Object work
- **Focus:** skill_drill

**Objective:** To master the rapid, collaborative establishment of C.R.O.W. (Character, Relationship, Objective, Where) using physical space work and implicit dialogue, ensuring a solid base reality before narrative progression begins.

## Setup
An open playing space with no physical props. The facilitator needs a stopwatch or timer. The rest of the group sits as an active audience, ready to observe and decode the physical and verbal offers.

## How to Play
1. Invite two players to the stage and obtain a simple, non-locational suggestion from the audience to inspire their initial physical action.
2. Announce 'Blueprint!' and start a strict 30-second timer to begin the Blueprint Phase.
3. Instruct the players to immediately begin establishing their environment, relationship, and objective using physical space work and dialogue, strictly avoiding any direct naming of their location, roles, or tasks.
4. Require players to alternate between physical offers, such as handling imaginary objects, and verbal offers that hint at their interpersonal dynamic.
5. If the players successfully establish a clear Who, What, and Where before the timer runs out, the facilitator calls 'Clear!' to signal an immediate transition.
6. If the timer reaches 30 seconds first, the facilitator calls 'Scene!' to transition the players instantly into the next phase without any pause or reset.
7. During the Scene Phase, players must continue the scene for one to two minutes, fully committing to, justifying, and exploring the logical consequences of the established reality.
8. End the scene by calling 'Cut!' once the players have successfully explored a single, justified narrative beat built on their initial blueprint.

## Facilitation Notes
- Watch out for 'verbal blueprinting' where players bypass the rule by using overly descriptive dialogue. Side-coach: 'Show us the weight of the tool, don't name it!'
- Ensure players do not freeze physically once the Scene Phase begins. Remind them that the environment they built in the first 30 seconds still exists and must be maintained throughout the scene.
- If players struggle to establish a relationship, prompt them with: 'How do you feel about this person? Let your physical proximity or tone of voice reveal your history.'
- Pitfall: Players sometimes treat the Blueprint Phase as a separate game and 'reset' their characters when the Scene Phase starts. Remind them that the transition is seamless; the scene is a direct continuation of the blueprint.

## Variations
- Silent Blueprint: The first 30 seconds must be entirely silent, relying solely on physical space work and facial expressions to establish the Where and the What before dialogue is permitted in the Scene Phase.
- Blind Blueprint: The off-stage player must enter the space and immediately adapt to the physical environment and activity initiated by the first player, discovering the 'Where' and 'What' entirely through physical mimicry.
- Speed Blueprint: Reduce the Blueprint Phase to 15 seconds, forcing players to make bold, high-stakes physical and relational choices instantly.

## Debrief
- To the audience: What were the specific physical clues that told you exactly where the characters were before they even spoke?
- To the players: How did having a pre-established physical and relational blueprint change your confidence when entering the main scene?
- How did avoiding direct exposition force you to listen and observe your partner more closely?

## Safety & Inclusion
Ensure that physical space work accommodates all mobility levels; players can establish environments and objects that match their physical comfort, and partners must mirror and respect those physical boundaries.

## Why It Works
By separating scene initiation into a high-constraint setup phase, players are forced to bypass lazy expository dialogue and instead rely on active space work and relational subtext. This builds a deep, shared commitment to the physical reality, making the subsequent scene feel grounded, highly detailed, and easy to justify.
