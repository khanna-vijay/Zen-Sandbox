# Sound and Motion Mirror

![Sound and Motion Mirror — infographic](img/D2_P2_S3_T1_G1251__remote-control.jpg){ .game-hero }

> Translate sound into movement and movement into sound to build deep, non-verbal partner connection.

## Overview
A dynamic partner exercise where one player acts as a live sound effects generator and the other physically embodies those sounds in real-time. It builds an intuitive, highly responsive connection where players must listen and react with absolute commitment.

## What It Trains
- **Domain:** D2 — The Partner
- **Principle(s):** Commit 100%; Yes, And; Make Your Partner a Genius
- **Skill(s):** Physicality & Space Work; Vocal Craft; Active Listening; Single-Partner Empathy & Mirroring
- **Technique(s):** Gibberish; Mirror exercise
- **Focus:** connection

**Objective:** To develop deep physical empathy, active listening, and immediate physical 'yes-and' commitment by translating auditory cues into physical actions.

## Setup
Players pair up and find a comfortable space in the room where they can move freely without bumping into other pairs. No props or special staging required.

## How to Play
1. Divide players into pairs and designate one as Player A (the Sound Maker) and one as Player B (the Mover).
2. Player A and Player B stand facing each other, maintaining soft eye contact to establish a connection.
3. Player A begins making vocal sounds, mouth noises, or body percussion (claps, snaps, stomps) without moving their own body significantly.
4. Player B immediately translates every sound made by Player A into physical movement, acting as if the sound is physically moving their limbs, torso, or entire body.
5. Player A must vary the pitch, volume, rhythm, and texture of their sounds, while Player B commits fully to mirroring those qualities physically (e.g., a sharp click causes a sharp jerk; a low hum causes a slow, heavy slouch).
6. After a few minutes, the facilitator calls out 'Switch!' and the players swap roles so Player B makes the sounds and Player A moves.

## Facilitation Notes
- Coaching cue: 'Don't just wait for the sound to finish—let the sound physically pull or push your body the instant it starts.'
- Coaching cue: 'Sound makers, play with contrast! Give your partner long, sustained drones, sudden sharp pops, and rhythmic patterns.'
- Pitfall: Players might overthink the 'meaning' of the sound (e.g., hearing a whoosh and pretending to sweep). Fix: Remind them to react abstractly to the texture, pitch, and energy of the sound rather than literal interpretations.
- Pitfall: Sound makers might make sounds too fast for the mover to process. Fix: Coach the sound maker to watch their partner and leave space for the movement to fully land before introducing the next sound.

## Variations
- Reverse Control: The mover initiates the physical action, and the sound maker must instantly provide the exact sound effect that matches the speed, weight, and trajectory of the movement.
- Organic Loop: No designated leader. Both players move and make sound simultaneously, passing the lead back and forth organically so it becomes a seamless, co-created dance.
- Emotional Overlay: Add an emotional prompt (e.g., 'joyful,' 'suspicious,' 'exhausted') that both players must filter their sounds and movements through.

## Debrief
- How did it feel to let someone else's voice control your physical body?
- Sound makers, how did watching your partner's physical response change the sounds you chose to make next?
- What did this exercise teach us about 'yes-and' when we aren't using spoken dialogue?

## Safety & Inclusion
Ensure players are mindful of their physical boundaries and the space around them. Encourage movers to adapt the movements to their own physical comfort and mobility levels, and remind sound makers to adjust their volume to be inclusive of sensitive ears.

## Why It Works
By separating the vocal and physical elements of performance and distributing them across two people, this game forces players to step out of their analytical minds. It demands absolute presence, turning 'yes-and' into a physical reflex where every sound is accepted as an undeniable physical truth.
