# The Catalyst Collider

![The Catalyst Collider — infographic](img/D3_P4_S3_T2_G132__the-catalyst-collider.jpg){ .game-hero }

> Weave sudden, disruptive narrative tilts into the structural foundation of an evolving, cohesive story.

## Overview
The Catalyst Collider is an advanced narrative game where a scene's trajectory is repeatedly disrupted by external, highly disparate prompts. Players must move beyond merely acknowledging these inputs, treating them instead as pivotal, reality-shifting events that retroactively reframe and deepen the entire story. This process forces players to synthesize unexpected elements into a unified, satisfying narrative arc.

## What It Trains
- **Domain:** D3 — The Scene
- **Principle(s):** Serve the Story; Base Reality First; Yes, And; Fail Joyfully
- **Skill(s):** Narrative Architecture; Justification; Game Identification; Active Listening; Offer Reception; Stakes / The 'Want'
- **Technique(s):** Platform/Tilt; If this is true, what else is true?; Justify the absurd; C.R.O.W. (Character, Relationship, Objective, Where); Endowment-acceptance
- **Focus:** narrative

**Objective:** To master the Platform and Tilt technique by training players to use sudden, disruptive inputs as structural opportunities to re-evaluate, justify, and synthesize a cohesive story.

## Setup
Three active players stand in the performance space while the remaining players act as the Narrative Guild off-stage. For in-person play, the facilitator or off-stage players hold physical Catalyst Cards and random objects. For digital play, a shared digital document, private chat messages, or a virtual random generator is prepared to distribute prompts instantly.

## How to Play
1. Obtain a mundane, everyday suggestion from the audience or off-stage players to establish a solid, grounded base reality.
2. Begin the scene with two active players focusing heavily on character relationships, physical environment, and clear individual objectives.
3. Off-stage players actively track the scene's narrative arc, writing down potential disruptive catalysts on cards or typing them into a digital chat queue.
4. After ninety seconds of established base reality, the facilitator or an off-stage player delivers the first Catalyst (a sudden, disruptive truth or physical object) to one active player.
5. The receiving player must immediately accept and integrate this catalyst, treating it as an absolute, pre-existing truth of their reality.
6. All players on stage immediately apply the 'If this is true, what else is true?' principle, adjusting their characters' relationships and objectives to accommodate this new truth.
7. A third active player enters the scene as a supporting character, carrying a second, even more disparate catalyst that must bridge the original reality and the first disruption.
8. The players collaborate to find a logical or thematic connection between all elements, driving the story to a satisfying, unified resolution that justifies every disruption.

## Facilitation Notes
- Pacing the Tilt: Ensure the players have fully established their base reality, environment, and relationships before introducing the first catalyst.
- Justification over Acknowledgment: Side-coach players to explain the disruption's presence and meaning rather than just naming it and moving on.
- Active Off-Stage Roles: Keep non-active players highly engaged by tasking them with generating the catalysts based on what the scene is missing (e.g., if the scene is too quiet, generate a loud catalyst).
- Digital Prompt Delivery: In online settings, send catalysts via private chat to individual players to preserve the element of surprise without interrupting the audio flow.

## Variations
- The Digital Collider: Played entirely over video conference, where off-stage players use private chat to send catalysts, and active players use household objects within their camera frame as physical props.
- The Soundscape Catalyst: Off-stage players act as an audio chorus, introducing environmental sounds (e.g., sirens, rain, ticking clocks) that serve as the disruptive catalysts.
- The Genre Shift: One of the catalysts forces the entire scene to instantly adopt a specific theatrical or cinematic style, requiring players to justify their current situation through that stylistic lens.

## Debrief
- How did the sudden tilts force you to listen more closely to your partner's immediate justification?
- What was the difference between simply acknowledging a disruption and actually weaving it into the narrative architecture?
- For off-stage players, how did tracking the story's logic help you select the perfect catalyst to escalate the stakes?

## Safety & Inclusion
Establish a non-verbal 'skip' signal (or a specific digital chat character like 'X') if a catalyst accidentally touches on a personal boundary. Ensure catalyst prompts focus on absurd, environmental, or stylistic disruptions rather than sensitive personal themes. For online play, ensure all text-based prompts are read aloud or accessible via screen readers if needed.

## Why It Works
This game operationalizes the Platform and Tilt technique in an escalating, structured format. By forcing players to constantly ask 'If this is true, what else is true?', it prevents scenes from stalling. The external disruptions act as a narrative engine, demanding immediate justification and active listening, which ultimately teaches players that any choice can be woven into a satisfying story if they commit to serving the narrative over their individual plans.
