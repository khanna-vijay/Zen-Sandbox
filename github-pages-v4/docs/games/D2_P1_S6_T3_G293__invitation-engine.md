# Invitation Engine

![Invitation Engine — infographic](img/D2_P1_S6_T3_G293__invitation-engine.jpg){ .game-hero }

> Integrate explicit physical consent directly into the narrative flow of your scenes.

## Overview
A structured scene-work drill where players must verbally articulate their physical desires and intentions before making any physical contact. By turning physical touch into an explicit verbal offer, players practice active boundary negotiation, making consent a collaborative, dramatic driver of the scene rather than an afterthought. The experience shifts the focus from impulsive physical action to mindful, shared physical storytelling.

## What It Trains
- **Domain:** D2 — The Partner
- **Principle(s):** Consent & Boundaries; Yes, And; Make Your Partner a Genius; Truth Over Pandering
- **Skill(s):** Boundary Navigation; Offer Reception; Active Listening
- **Technique(s):** Negotiating physical contact; Cut calls; Check-ins
- **Focus:** skill_drill

**Objective:** To develop advanced boundary navigation and physical negotiation skills, training players to proactively frame physical contact as a collaborative offer while honoring personal comfort and the principle of Truth Over Pandering.

## Setup
An open, comfortable playing space. Players stand in a circle or semi-circle, with two players stepping into the center to perform a scene. No props or special materials are required.

## How to Play
1. Two players step into the playing space to initiate a standard scene, establishing a clear relationship and environment without any immediate physical contact.
2. When a player feels an impulse to initiate physical contact or enter their partner's close personal space, they must pause their physical movement and verbally state their character's internal desire and intended physical action.
3. The initiating player must frame this verbalization as a 'Generative Invitation' that explicitly invites a response, such as: 'I feel so proud of you right now that I want to step forward and give you a firm hug. How does that feel to you?'
4. The receiving player must immediately respond with one of three choices: an enthusiastic 'Yes', a negotiated 'Yes, and...', or a clear 'No' (either in-character or out-of-character).
5. If the response is an enthusiastic 'Yes', both players execute the physical action together at a mutually agreed pace, ensuring active, shared engagement.
6. If the response is a negotiated 'Yes, and...', the receiver proposes a modified physical boundary (e.g., 'I'm not a big hugger, but I would love to shake your hand warmly'), and both players execute the modified action.
7. If the response is an in-character 'No' (e.g., 'I am still too angry to let you hug me'), the players accept this boundary as a narrative truth and continue the scene without physical contact.
8. If at any point a player feels genuine out-of-character discomfort with an invitation or action, they say 'Cut!' to immediately pause the scene, allowing the facilitator to check in and reset safely.
9. The scene continues with players seeking opportunities to weave these verbal invitations and negotiations naturally into their dialogue, rotating players out after two to three minutes.

## Facilitation Notes
- Side-coach players to avoid passive permission-seeking; the invitation should feel like a rich, active character offer that reveals their internal emotional state.
- Actively celebrate 'No' and negotiated responses. Remind the group that setting a boundary is a gift to the scene that provides clear, dramatic platforming, not a block.
- Watch out for 'polite pandering' where players accept physical contact they are uncomfortable with just to keep the scene moving. Intervene and remind them of 'Truth Over Pandering'.
- Ensure players do not pre-emptively start the physical movement before the verbal invitation is fully answered; the verbal agreement must precede the physical action.
- If a player calls 'Cut!', immediately pause the group, validate the call without demanding a public explanation, and offer a brief, supportive check-in before moving to the next pair.

## Variations
- Non-Verbal Cueing: Play the same game but replace the verbal invitation with a highly exaggerated, slow-motion physical telegraph (e.g., slowly extending an open hand), requiring the partner to match, modify, or reject the physical offer before contact is made.
- High-Stakes Status: Assign high and low status roles where the low-status character must negotiate physical boundaries with a high-status character, exploring how power dynamics affect consent.

## Debrief
- How did verbalizing your physical intentions before acting them out change the emotional depth of your character's relationship?
- What did it feel like to negotiate or decline a physical offer? Did you feel empowered to prioritize your actual comfort over the scene's momentum?
- How did a negotiated 'Yes, and...' or an in-character 'No' help drive the narrative forward in an unexpected way?
- How can we carry this practice of explicit physical negotiation into our regular, fast-paced improv scenes without losing spontaneity?

## Safety & Inclusion
This game is highly safety-sensitive. Establish a firm, zero-judgment policy for using the 'Cut!' safety word before play begins. Ensure all players understand that their real-world physical boundaries always supersede any character choice or theatrical impulse. If a player is uncomfortable with any physical contact, they are fully encouraged to negotiate a completely non-contact alternative (such as maintaining eye contact or standing closer) without needing to justify their boundary to the room.

## Why It Works
By slowing down physical impulses and forcing them through a verbal filter, this game deconstructs the assumption that physical contact is a default requirement of dramatic intimacy. It builds muscle memory for active boundary navigation, proving that negotiating consent is not a narrative roadblock but a powerful tool for character development and relationship building. It directly reinforces 'Truth Over Pandering' by rewarding players for honoring their genuine boundaries.
