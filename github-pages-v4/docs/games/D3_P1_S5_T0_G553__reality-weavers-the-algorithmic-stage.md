# Reality Weavers

![Reality Weavers — infographic](img/D3_P1_S5_T0_G553__reality-weavers-the-algorithmic-stage.jpg){ .game-hero }

> Co-create and instantly adapt to a shifting, player-controlled digital environment in real time.

## Overview
A high-energy virtual improv game where players collaboratively build and alter their physical scene using a shared digital whiteboard. Instead of just describing their surroundings, off-stage players drag visual assets onto a shared canvas, forcing the active actors to immediately 'Yes, And' the new visual reality. This transforms the virtual stage from a static backdrop into an active, unpredictable partner in the scene.

## What It Trains
- **Domain:** D3 — The Scene
- **Principle(s):** Show, Don't Tell; Yes, And; Group Mind
- **Skill(s):** World-Building; Offer Reception; Active Gifting; Support Work
- **Technique(s):** Endowment-acceptance; Endowment-gifting drills
- **Focus:** mixed

**Objective:** To develop advanced world-building and 'Show, Don't Tell' skills by integrating immediate, player-driven visual changes into the narrative, fostering deep offer reception, active gifting, and group mind.

## Setup
Players join a video call and open a shared, collaborative digital whiteboard in a split-screen or secondary window. The whiteboard is divided into two zones: a central 'Stage Frame' (which is screen-shared by the facilitator) and a surrounding 'Backstage Palette' pre-loaded with simple, high-impact visual assets (icons, landscapes, weather effects, and objects). Two players are designated as the active actors, while the remaining players act as the environmental weavers.

## How to Play
1. The facilitator shares their screen showing only the 'Stage Frame' of the collaborative whiteboard, which starts completely blank.
2. Two active actors begin a scene, establishing their characters, relationship, and an initial broad setting through dialogue and physical choices.
3. The remaining players (the weavers) keep the collaborative whiteboard open on their own screens, positioned next to their video feeds.
4. At any point, any weaver can select a visual asset from the 'Backstage Palette' and drag it directly into the 'Stage Frame' to make an environmental offer.
5. Weavers can also resize, rotate, or layer assets to change the scale, weather, or atmosphere of the scene.
6. The active actors must immediately and without hesitation 'Yes, And' this new visual element, reacting to it physically and verbally as if it just materialized in their physical space.
7. Actors must justify the sudden appearance of the object or environmental shift within the context of their scene, rather than breaking character to acknowledge the technology.
8. As the scene progresses, weavers continue to add, move, or delete assets, dynamically shifting the environment and driving the narrative forward.

## Facilitation Notes
- Coaching Cue: 'React physically before you explain!' Encourage actors to gasp, duck, or reach out to the new visual asset before they verbally label it.
- To prevent visual clutter and decision paralysis, limit the 'Backstage Palette' to 15-20 highly distinct, iconic images (e.g., a key, a storm cloud, a giant eye, a door).
- Ensure weavers do not overcrowd the stage; coach them to wait for the actors to fully justify and play with one visual offer before introducing another.
- If an actor misses a visual addition due to focusing on their partner, the other actor or a weaver should subtly draw attention to it in-character (e.g., 'Watch out for that sudden downpour!').

## Variations
- The Blind Build: The active actors close their eyes while the weavers place 2-3 assets in the Stage Frame. On 'eyes open,' the actors must instantly justify the bizarre environment they find themselves in.
- Atmospheric Shift: Weavers are restricted to using solid color blocks or semi-transparent overlays to change the lighting, mood, or weather of the scene, forcing actors to adapt their emotional states.
- The Prop Master: One specific weaver is designated as the 'invisible hand' who can only drop small, hand-held objects directly into the actors' virtual hands, requiring immediate physical endowment.

## Debrief
- How did having a tangible, immediate visual change alter your physical and emotional choices compared to purely verbal world-building?
- What strategies did you use to seamlessly integrate an unexpected visual asset without breaking the narrative flow?
- How did sharing control of the physical stage affect the power dynamics and collaborative spirit of the ensemble?

## Safety & Inclusion
Ensure all visual assets in the library are pre-screened to be inclusive, accessible, and free of triggering imagery. For players with visual impairments, a designated 'audio describer' among the weavers should instantly and vividly describe any visual changes as they occur (e.g., 'A massive storm cloud has appeared above you') so all players can fully participate.

## Why It Works
By externalizing the imagined space into a shared, mutable digital artifact, this game forces players to move beyond verbal exposition and practice true 'Show, Don't Tell.' The physical act of manipulating the stage creates a high-stakes feedback loop where visual offers must be instantly justified, sharpening the ensemble's active gifting, support work, and collective group mind.
