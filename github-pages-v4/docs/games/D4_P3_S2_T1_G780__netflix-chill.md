# Streaming Queue

![Streaming Queue — infographic](img/D4_P3_S2_T1_G780__netflix-chill.jpg){ .game-hero }

> Two viewers control the remote while an ensemble physically manifests their chaotic television screen.

## Overview
Two players sit on a couch as television viewers, while the rest of the ensemble stands ready to instantly perform whatever show, movie, or commercial is selected. The viewers use an imaginary remote control to pause, mute, fast-forward, or change channels, forcing the performing ensemble to adapt their physical and verbal choices on the fly. It is a high-energy game of rapid support work, sharp edits, and playful ensemble responsiveness.

## What It Trains
- **Domain:** D4 — The Ensemble
- **Principle(s):** Serve the Piece; Make Your Partner a Genius; Show, Don't Tell; The Audience Is the Final Scene Partner
- **Skill(s):** Support Work; Pacing & Rhythm; Active Gifting; World-Building; Room Reading
- **Technique(s):** Walk-ons; Edits (Sweep, Tag-Out, Sound/Light); Endowment-gifting drills; C.R.O.W. (Character, Relationship, Objective, Where)
- **Focus:** comedy_game

**Objective:** To develop rapid support work and seamless walk-ons by training the ensemble to instantly commit to physical and narrative offers initiated by the viewers' commentary.

## Setup
Place two chairs center-stage to represent a couch. The remaining players (the ensemble) stand in the wings or along the back wall, ready to enter the 'screen' area directly in front of the couch. Obtain a suggestion from the audience for a genre of a show or a snack the viewers are eating to kick off the scene.

## How to Play
1. Two players sit on the chairs as the 'viewers' holding an imaginary remote control, while the rest of the players form the 'ensemble' offstage.
2. The host gets an initial suggestion from the audience (e.g., a bizarre movie title or a specific snack) to inspire the first program.
3. The viewers begin the scene by pantomiming eating snacks, chatting, and turning on the television to the suggested program.
4. As soon as a program is mentioned or selected, two or more ensemble members must immediately run into the space in front of the couch and physically perform that show.
5. The viewers actively comment on what they are watching, offering specific details (e.g., 'I love how this main character always whispers' or 'Look at that giant monster in the background!').
6. The performing ensemble must instantly incorporate the viewers' commentary into their physical actions and dialogue, treating every comment as an absolute truth.
7. The viewers can use the imaginary remote at any time to pause the action, mute the sound (requiring the actors to mouth words silently), fast-forward (requiring high-speed physical comedy), or change the channel entirely.
8. When the channel is changed, the current actors must quickly exit, and a new group from the ensemble must immediately jump in to establish the next show based on the viewers' new selection.

## Facilitation Notes
- Coaching Cue: 'Serve the screen!' Remind the ensemble that their job is to make the viewers' descriptions look accurate, not to push their own independent storylines.
- Pitfall: The viewers talk too much without letting the screen perform. Fix: Encourage the viewers to pause the TV or watch silently for a few seconds to let the physical comedy breathe.
- Coaching Cue: 'Vary the remote controls!' Encourage the viewers to use different functions like slow-motion, language dubbing, or rewind to challenge the ensemble's physicality.
- Pitfall: Ensemble members hesitate to enter the screen. Fix: Remind offstage players that any entrance is a good entrance; jump in with a strong physical posture and let the viewers label who you are.

## Variations
- The Heartbroken Soloist: A single viewer has just gone through a dramatic breakup, causing every channel they tune into to hilariously mirror their personal relationship trauma.
- Foreign Dub: The ensemble performs a foreign-language film, and the viewers use the remote's 'audio settings' to translate or dub the voices live.

## Debrief
- How did it feel to have your scene partner (the viewer) dictate your character's traits and actions from off-camera?
- What strategies did the ensemble use to make transitions between channels seamless and high-energy?
- How does 'serving the piece' differ when you are the controller versus when you are the content being controlled?

## Safety & Inclusion
Ensure the performance space in front of the couch is completely clear of tripping hazards, as ensemble members will be entering and exiting rapidly. Encourage players to be mindful of physical boundaries during high-speed fast-forward or rewind sequences.

## Why It Works
This game functions as a perfect game engine because it separates the narrative control (the viewers) from the physical execution (the ensemble). By forcing the ensemble to immediately physicalize the viewers' verbal offers, it bypasses overthinking and reinforces the core principle of making your partner a genius through instant, active support.
