# Ground Control

![Ground Control — infographic](img/D3_P1_S6_T1_G1102__ground-control.jpg){ .game-hero }

> Puppeteer your partner's movements with flashlights, forcing them to justify every absurd physical choice.

## Overview
In this dynamic four-player scene, two actors perform a dialogue-driven scene while two off-stage controllers dictate their physical movements using flashlights. The actors cannot move unless guided by their controller's light, requiring them to constantly justify their sudden, unexpected physical shifts within the reality of the scene.

## What It Trains
- **Domain:** D3 — The Scene
- **Principle(s):** Show, Don't Tell; Yes, And; Make Your Partner a Genius
- **Skill(s):** Justification; Offer Reception; Active Gifting; Physicality & Space Work
- **Technique(s):** Justify the absurd; Endowment-acceptance; Endowment-gifting drills
- **Focus:** mixed

**Objective:** To master physical justification and show-don't-tell by instantly finding logical, character-driven reasons for arbitrary physical movements imposed by an external force.

## Setup
Four players: two designated as Actors and two as Controllers. The two Controllers stand at the back of the audience space or downstage corners, each holding a flashlight fitted with a high-visibility orange cone. The two Actors stand center stage. The performance space should have dimmable lighting so the flashlight beams are clearly visible.

## How to Play
1. Assign each Actor a dedicated Controller who will guide their physical movements throughout the scene.
2. Establish the movement rules: Actors must remain completely frozen unless their Controller's flashlight beam is shining on them or actively moving.
3. Define the directional vocabulary: Moving the flashlight beam left or right pulls the Actor in that direction; raising the beam forces them to reach, climb, or jump; lowering the beam forces them to duck, crawl, or sit.
4. Get a simple, non-action-heavy suggestion from the audience to start a standard, relationship-based scene.
5. Begin the scene with the Actors initiating dialogue. Simultaneously, the Controllers begin manipulating their Actors' physical positions using the flashlight beams.
6. The Actors must immediately comply with any physical movement dictated by the light, integrating the movement seamlessly into their character's actions.
7. The Actors must verbally or physically justify why they are moving (e.g., if pulled suddenly to the floor, they might justify it by searching for a lost contact lens or ducking from an imaginary bee).
8. The scene continues for 3 to 5 minutes, balancing dialogue, physical comedy, and narrative progression before editing.

## Facilitation Notes
- Coaching Cue: 'Don't just walk—justify!' Remind actors to find a narrative reason for the movement rather than just mindlessly drifting to follow the light.
- Pitfall: Controllers moving the lights too quickly or constantly. Fix: Encourage controllers to hold a position once an actor reaches it, allowing the actor to play in that physical space before moving them again.
- Coaching Cue: 'Let the movement inspire the dialogue.' If an actor is forced to crawl, that physical state should immediately affect their vocal tone and emotional state.
- Pitfall: Actors ignoring the light or anticipating movements. Fix: Remind actors to maintain a physical freeze until the light actively pulls them.

## Variations
- Emotional Control: Instead of physical movement, the flashlight's position or intensity dictates the actor's emotional volume or intensity level.
- Audience Controllers: Invite two audience members to act as the controllers, giving them simple instructions on how to move the flashlights to challenge the experienced improvisers.
- Sound Effects Integration: Combine this with a third player providing live sound effects that must also be justified alongside the physical movements.

## Debrief
- How did having your physical movement controlled externally change the choices you made in your dialogue?
- What was the most surprising justification you had to make, and how did it deepen the scene's reality?
- Controllers, how did you balance challenging your actor with supporting their narrative needs?

## Safety & Inclusion
Ensure the playing space is clear of tripping hazards, cords, or sharp furniture, as actors will be moving with their focus split between their partner and the flashlight beams. If dimming the lights, ensure there is still enough ambient light for safe movement.

## Why It Works
By separating physical movement from verbal intent, this game bypasses the analytical brain. Actors cannot plan their physical blocking; they must react physically first and justify intellectually second. This perfectly embodies the 'Show, Don't Tell' principle, as the physical posture dictates the emotional and narrative reality of the scene.
