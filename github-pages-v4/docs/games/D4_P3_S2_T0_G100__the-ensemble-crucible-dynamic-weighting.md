# Dynamic Weighting

![Dynamic Weighting — infographic](img/D4_P3_S2_T0_G100__the-ensemble-crucible-dynamic-weighting.jpg){ .game-hero }

> Balance the stage by consciously dividing the labor of driving, sustaining, and disrupting a scene.

## Overview
Dynamic Weighting is an ensemble-building exercise where players are assigned specific contribution profiles—the Mover, the Sustainer, or the Catalyst—to shape their improvisational choices. By restricting or focusing how each player supports the scene, the group learns to consciously balance narrative momentum, emotional grounding, and strategic shifts. The resulting performance reveals how different types of support work together to build a cohesive, self-regulating piece.

## What It Trains
- **Domain:** D4 — The Ensemble
- **Principle(s):** Group Mind; Serve the Piece; Yes, And; Make Your Partner a Genius
- **Skill(s):** Peripheral Awareness; Support Work; Suggestion Deconstruction (A-to-C); Pacing & Rhythm; Thematic Synthesis; Active Listening; Offer Reception; Active Gifting
- **Technique(s):** Timing exercises; A-to-C drills; Callbacks & Mapping; Last Word Response; Yes, And… sentence games; Endowment-gifting drills
- **Focus:** skill_drill

**Objective:** To develop advanced ensemble support work and peripheral awareness by training players to consciously modulate their contribution style, ensuring they serve the piece rather than relying on default habits.

## Setup
An open playing space for 3 to 5 active players, with the remaining group observing. No props or materials are required. The facilitator prepares to assign roles either openly or secretly before the scene begins.

## How to Play
1. Select three to five players to step onto the stage and obtain a simple, open-ended suggestion from the audience to establish a basic scene premise.
2. Assign each player one of three distinct Dynamic Weights, either openly to the group or whispered secretly to individual players to challenge the observers.
3. Define the first role, 'The Mover' (Heavy Weight), whose job is to drive the narrative forward, initiate bold actions, make strong emotional choices, and provide the scene's primary engine of momentum.
4. Define the second role, 'The Sustainer' (Medium Weight), whose job is to flesh out existing details, deepen relationships, ground the environment, and enthusiastically 'yes-and' whatever the Mover initiates.
5. Define the third role, 'The Catalyst' (Light Weight), who must remain mostly silent or passive, observing closely to deliver only one or two highly strategic, game-changing interventions that reframe the entire scene.
6. Begin the scene, instructing players to let their assigned weight filter their natural improvisational impulses rather than acting like rigid robots.
7. Run the scene for approximately three to five minutes, allowing the unique chemistry of these combined weights to find a natural rhythm and resolution.
8. Conclude the scene and immediately transition into a structured reflection, focusing on how the distribution of roles affected the overall group mind.

## Facilitation Notes
- Coaching Cue: If the scene stagnates, side-coach the Mover: 'Mover, find the next gear!' or 'Sustainer, paint the picture of what is already there!'
- Pitfall: The Catalyst speaks too often, diluting their impact. Fix: Remind the Catalyst to wait for the absolute peak of tension or a moment of silence before dropping their strategic offer.
- Pitfall: The Mover steamrolls without listening. Fix: Remind the Mover that driving the scene still requires building on the Sustainer's grounding details—driving is not ignoring.
- Coaching Cue: For the Sustainer: 'Sustainer, make the big choice of your partner look like a stroke of genius by giving it a physical home.'
- Facilitator Tip: If playing with 4 or 5 players, double up on Sustainers to create a rich, grounded environment, or double up on Movers to practice high-stakes negotiation.

## Variations
- Mid-Scene Shift: The facilitator calls out 'Switch!' during the scene, prompting players to instantly pass their role to the person on their left, forcing rapid cognitive adaptation.
- Organic Discovery: Run a standard scene with no pre-assigned roles. Pause the scene halfway through and ask the players to identify who has naturally assumed which weight, then resume with those roles consciously locked in.
- Secret Weights: Assign the roles in secret. Have the observing players guess who held which dynamic weight based purely on their stage behavior and contribution patterns.

## Debrief
- How did it feel to play a weight that is opposite to your natural improvisational default?
- For the observers, when did the Catalyst's intervention feel most satisfying, and what made that timing work?
- How did having clearly defined contribution roles make it easier to 'serve the piece' as a collective unit?
- In what ways did the Sustainer's grounding work make the Mover's bold choices actually land with the audience?

## Safety & Inclusion
Ensure players understand that 'The Mover' driving the scene does not mean crossing physical boundaries or forcing high-intensity physical contact without consent. Encourage players to use verbal and spatial cues to drive momentum safely.

## Why It Works
This game works because it deconstructs the abstract concept of 'support' into actionable, specialized behaviors. By separating the labor of initiation, preservation, and disruption, players escape the trap of everyone trying to do everything at once. It builds a highly responsive group mind where players actively look for what the scene is missing and fill that specific gap.
