# The Trigger Protocol

![The Trigger Protocol — infographic](img/D2_P1_S6_T2_G552__boundary-weave-the-trigger-protocol.jpg){ .game-hero }

> Practice setting firm limits and executing decisive cut calls using pre-selected boundary cards.

## Overview
This structured skill drill trains improvisers to navigate physical and emotional boundaries in real time. By combining proactive, private boundary selection with active in-scene check-ins and immediate cut calls, players build the muscle memory needed to prioritize personal safety over narrative momentum. The experience shifts the focus from pleasing the audience to establishing absolute mutual trust.

## What It Trains
- **Domain:** D2 — The Partner
- **Principle(s):** Consent & Boundaries; Truth Over Pandering
- **Skill(s):** Boundary Navigation; Active Listening
- **Technique(s):** Check-ins; Cut calls; Negotiating physical contact
- **Focus:** skill_drill

**Objective:** To develop competent boundary navigation and active listening skills, specifically training players to execute clear, non-negotiable cut calls when personal limits are approached, and to collaboratively adjust scenes when minor discomfort arises.

## Setup
Prepare a deck of Boundary Cards (index cards listing common improvisational triggers, such as sudden loud shouting, unsolicited physical contact to the face, mock restraint, or intense staring). Clear a moderate performance space. Players work in groups of 3 to 5, with one person acting as the dedicated facilitator and the others rotating as active players and safety spotters.

## How to Play
1. Before the scene begins, each active player privately selects two Boundary Cards from the deck representing specific triggers they want to practice defending, keeping these cards hidden from their partners.
2. The two active players step into the space out of character and conduct a brief, explicit verbal check-in to establish general physical and emotional baselines.
3. The facilitator provides a scene prompt designed to naturally invite physical proximity or emotional intensity.
4. Players begin improvising, establishing their characters and relationship while remaining highly attuned to their partner's physical and verbal cues.
5. If a player initiates an action that approaches a general, non-card boundary, either player can pause the action to ask, 'Check-in: are you comfortable with this?'
6. If an in-scene check-in results in a boundary being set, the players rewind the action by a few seconds and collaboratively improvise an alternative, consensual path forward.
7. If a player's secretly selected trigger card is approached or breached, that player must immediately and loudly call 'BOUNDARY CUT!' to halt the scene instantly.
8. Upon calling a Boundary Cut, the player steps out of character, reveals their private card to their partner and the facilitator, and briefly explains how the trigger was approached.
9. At any point, any player or the facilitator can call a traditional Time Out or Cut for any reason, ensuring absolute safety throughout the drill.

## Facilitation Notes
- Actively celebrate players who call 'BOUNDARY CUT!' to reinforce that stopping the scene is a successful demonstration of boundary navigation, not a failure of the improvisation.
- As the facilitator, do not steer the scene's narrative toward the players' private triggers; let the interactions unfold naturally so the boundary crossings occur organically.
- Ensure that when 'BOUNDARY CUT!' is called, the scene stops immediately without allowing players to power through or finish their lines.
- Coach players on the difference: Check-ins are for collaborative negotiation and continuing the scene, while Boundary Cuts are for immediate, non-negotiable halts to protect personal safety.
- Keep a close eye on the room's energy and pause the exercise entirely to offer support if a player seems genuinely distressed after a cut.

## Variations
- Have an inactive player act as a Spotter who can call a check-in or cut from the sidelines if they observe a boundary being approached that the active players seem to miss.
- Instead of private cards, players openly agree on three specific boundaries before the scene starts, practicing navigating known, visible limits in real time.

## Debrief
- How did it feel to privately select your boundary cards, and did that anticipation change how you approached the scene physically or emotionally?
- What was the internal experience of calling 'BOUNDARY CUT!' versus initiating a collaborative 'Boundary Check-in'?
- As the partner, how did you process hearing a 'Boundary Cut' called, and how did you manage the instinct to keep the scene going?
- How does practicing these explicit, structured cuts help you feel safer when performing open, unscripted scenes?

## Safety & Inclusion
This game is highly safety-sensitive. Participation must be entirely voluntary, and players should be allowed to write their own custom boundary cards if the pre-made deck does not reflect their personal limits. Ensure a supportive, non-judgmental environment where no player is pressured to explain the personal history behind their chosen boundaries.

## Why It Works
By gamifying the cut call, this drill removes the social pressure of stopping a scene. It directly supports the principle of Truth Over Pandering by rewarding players for prioritizing their physical and emotional safety over narrative completion. The dual-track system of check-ins (for negotiation) and cuts (for hard limits) builds a sophisticated vocabulary for real-time consent.
