# Boundary Co-Pilot

![Boundary Co-Pilot — infographic](img/D2_P1_S6_T3_G279__consent-co-pilot.jpg){ .game-hero }

> Practice navigating physical contact, setting clear boundaries, and negotiating alternatives in real-time scenes.

## Overview
A structured partner exercise where players practice checking in with themselves and their partners before and during physical contact. One player receives a secret prompt to initiate a physical action, while the other practices honoring their personal comfort limits by pausing the scene to negotiate or calling a complete stop. This drill builds muscle memory for prioritizing personal safety and clear communication over scenic momentum.

## What It Trains
- **Domain:** D2 — The Partner
- **Principle(s):** Consent & Boundaries; Truth Over Pandering
- **Skill(s):** Boundary Navigation; Active Listening; Offer Reception
- **Technique(s):** Check-ins; Cut calls; Negotiating physical contact
- **Focus:** skill_drill

**Objective:** To develop practical skills in negotiating physical contact, setting and respecting personal boundaries, and practicing active listening and offer reception under pressure, prioritizing personal truth over scenic obligation.

## Setup
Players form pairs. The facilitator prepares two sets of cards: 'Scene Starters' (e.g., two estranged siblings meeting, neighbors over a fence) and 'Physical Action Prompts' (PAPs) which detail specific physical offers (e.g., a warm hug, a hand on the shoulder, a high-five, standing very close). The playing space should be clear of obstacles to allow safe movement.

## How to Play
1. Divide the group into pairs and designate one player as the Initiator and the other as the Responder for the first round.
2. Provide the pair with a simple Scene Starter to establish their characters and relationship, and have them begin playing the scene verbally.
3. After about 30 seconds of play, the facilitator discreetly shows the Initiator a 'Physical Action Prompt' card (e.g., 'Offer a firm handshake that turns into a hug') without letting the Responder see it.
4. The Initiator must immediately perform an internal check-in: if they do not feel personally comfortable executing this action, they must ignore the card and continue the scene verbally.
5. If the Initiator is comfortable, they may either ask for consent explicitly in character (e.g., 'Can I give you a hug?') or begin to initiate the physical action slowly and transparently.
6. The Responder assesses their own comfort level in real-time. If comfortable, they accept the physical contact and continue the scene.
7. If the Responder feels any discomfort, they must honor their boundary by calling 'Pause!' to freeze the action, then clearly state their boundary without apologizing (e.g., 'I don't want to be hugged right now').
8. Upon hearing 'Pause!', the Initiator must immediately stop, acknowledge the boundary, and offer a non-contact alternative that preserves the scene's emotional intent (e.g., 'Understood. How about a warm wave instead?').
9. If the Responder is still uncomfortable, or if the boundary cannot be easily negotiated, either player can call 'Cut!' to immediately and safely end the scene with zero penalty or explanation required.

## Facilitation Notes
- Side-coaching cue: Remind Initiators to telegraph their physical movements slowly so Responders have time to process and react before contact is made.
- Pitfall: Players apologizing when setting boundaries (e.g., 'I'm sorry, but...'). Fix: Coach them to state the boundary as a neutral, clear fact: 'I prefer not to be touched on the shoulder.'
- Celebrate 'Pause' and 'Cut' calls enthusiastically. Frame them as successful demonstrations of boundary navigation rather than scene failures.
- Ensure players step out of character (de-role) by shaking off the scene or stating their real names before starting the debrief.

## Variations
- Graduated Prompts: Start with low-stakes physical prompts (e.g., a high-five or wave) and gradually introduce closer contact prompts as trust builds.
- Verbal-Only Check-ins: Restrict the Initiator to only initiating physical contact after an explicit, in-character verbal request, practicing the language of proactive consent.

## Debrief
- For the Responders: What did it feel like to voice a boundary in the moment? Did you feel any pressure to 'pander' to the scene, and how did you overcome it?
- For the Initiators: How did it feel to have your physical offer paused or redirected? How did you adapt your creative choices to respect your partner's boundary?
- For the observers: How did witnessing clear boundary negotiation affect your sense of safety and trust in the ensemble?

## Safety & Inclusion
This exercise is highly safety-sensitive. Participation must be entirely voluntary. Before starting, establish that 'Cut' is an absolute safety valve that anyone can use at any time for any reason, with no questions asked. Ensure physical prompts are tailored to the group's pre-established comfort levels, and never force physical contact.

## Why It Works
By gamifying the negotiation of physical contact, this drill removes the social stigma of saying 'no' in a creative setting. It replaces the traditional, unchecked 'Yes, And' with a nuanced understanding that personal consent always overrides scenic agreement. The structured pause-and-negotiate loop builds the muscle memory needed to handle physical boundaries gracefully and collaboratively.
