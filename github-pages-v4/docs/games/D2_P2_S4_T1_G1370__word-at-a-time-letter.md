# Word-at-a-Time Letter

![Word-at-a-Time Letter — infographic](img/D2_P2_S4_T1_G1370__word-at-a-time-letter.jpg){ .game-hero }

> Co-write a single letter one word at a time, treating every pen stroke as an offer.

## Overview
Two players sit together to compose a single written letter, alternating word-by-word on a single sheet of paper. Because the medium is physical, players must accept and build upon not just the words chosen, but the handwriting, spacing, punctuation, and visual layout. The result is a highly focused exercise in deep listening, radical acceptance, and shared narrative control.

## What It Trains
- **Domain:** D2 — The Partner
- **Principle(s):** Yes, And; Make Your Partner a Genius; Serve the Story
- **Skill(s):** Active Listening; Offer Reception; Narrative Architecture
- **Technique(s):** Yes, And… sentence games; Story Spine
- **Focus:** skill_drill

**Objective:** To develop acute offer reception and 'Yes, And' mechanics by forcing players to slow down, accept their partner's exact linguistic and physical contributions, and collaboratively build a coherent narrative structure.

## Setup
Pairs sit side-by-side at a table or on the floor. Each pair is given one sheet of blank paper and a single writing utensil to share. No prior planning or genre selection is needed before starting.

## How to Play
1. Player A writes the very first word of the letter anywhere on the page, then hands the pen to Player B.
2. Player B writes the second word, continuing the sentence or establishing a new line, then hands the pen back.
3. Players continue alternating word-by-word, allowing the tone, format, and recipient of the letter to emerge organically without verbal discussion.
4. Every mark on the page—including punctuation, line breaks, margins, and capitalization—counts as a turn or an offer that must be accepted.
5. Players must write legibly and avoid planning ahead; they must react only to the word that was just written.
6. The letter is complete when one player writes a final closing word (such as 'Sincerely' or a signature) and both players agree the narrative has reached a natural conclusion.

## Facilitation Notes
- Side-coaching cue: 'Look at the physical space. If your partner writes in tiny letters in the corner, match that energy or justify why the letter is written that way.'
- Pitfall: Players trying to force a pre-planned joke or genre (e.g., trying to make it a ransom note when the partner wrote 'Dear Grandmother'). Fix: Remind them to serve the last word written, not their internal script.
- Side-coaching cue: 'Punctuation counts! A period, comma, or question mark can be a player's entire turn to help guide the sentence structure.'
- Pitfall: Writing multiple words or compound words to steer the story. Fix: Enforce the strict one-word-at-a-time rule to maintain equal partnership.

## Variations
- Silent Delivery: The entire exercise is conducted in absolute silence, with no verbal communication before, during, or after writing until the facilitator calls time.
- The Dual-Color Pen: Give each player a different colored pen. This visually highlights the alternating contributions and reveals how the narrative control shifts back and forth.
- The Postcard: Limit the physical space to a small index card, forcing players to be highly concise and deliberate with their narrative architecture.

## Debrief
- How did the physical layout of the words (spacing, size, handwriting) influence the tone of the letter?
- When did you have to let go of a great idea because your partner's word took the letter in a completely different direction?
- How did writing the letter compare to spoken word-at-a-time games in terms of pacing and pressure?

## Safety & Inclusion
Ensure writing utensils are comfortable for all participants. If physical writing is a barrier, players can use a shared digital document on a tablet or laptop, alternating typing single words, or one player can act as a neutral scribe for both.

## Why It Works
By slowing down the 'Yes, And' process to the speed of physical writing, players cannot rely on fast-talking or steamrolling. They are forced to look at the literal, concrete offer on the page. This builds deep offer reception because every single word is preserved in ink, making it impossible to ignore or overwrite a partner's contribution. It highlights how narrative architecture is built incrementally, one small agreement at a time.
