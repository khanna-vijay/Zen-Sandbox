# Predatory Bunny

![Predatory Bunny — infographic](img/D4_P1_S1_T2_G1142__killer-bunny.jpg){ .game-hero }

> A high-stakes rhythm game of sudden betrayal, rapid defense, and hyper-focused group awareness.

## Overview
Players stand in a circle passing a fast-paced, rhythmic chant using hand gestures and vocal cues. At any moment, the player holding the central focus can break the rhythm to attack one of their neighbors, unless a vigilant bystander spots the threat and shouts a warning in time. It is a chaotic, high-energy warm-up that demands absolute focus on the entire group dynamic.

## What It Trains
- **Domain:** D4 — The Ensemble
- **Principle(s):** Group Mind; Commit 100%
- **Skill(s):** Peripheral Awareness; Pacing & Rhythm; Unfiltered Spontaneity
- **Technique(s):** Thread-tracking drills; Timing exercises
- **Focus:** connection

**Objective:** To develop peripheral awareness, group mind, and split-second reaction times by tracking multiple moving parts of a rhythmic system simultaneously.

## Setup
Have all players stand in a shoulder-to-shoulder circle with clear sightlines to everyone else. Ensure there is enough space for players to lunge safely without colliding.

## How to Play
1. Establish the base rhythm: The active player (the Bunny) taps their fingers together like a mouth, chanting 'Bunny, bunny, bunny, bunny' while looking at and gesturing to another player to pass the turn.
2. Define the flanking support: The players immediately to the left and right of the active Bunny must support them by waving their outer hand as an 'ear' and chanting 'Toka, toka, toka, toka' in sync with the Bunny.
3. Pass the energy: The active Bunny passes the focus to a new player in the circle by pointing their 'bunny mouth' hands at them. That new player instantly becomes the Bunny, and their neighbors become the new 'ears.'
4. Introduce the threat: At any point, instead of passing the energy, the active Bunny can raise both hands high above their head, signaling an impending attack.
5. Execute the attack: From this raised position, the Bunny can lunge left or right to 'eat' one of their flanking ears by tapping them on the shoulder. If they touch the neighbor before anyone intervenes, that neighbor is out.
6. Intervene to save: Any player in the circle (who is not the active Bunny or the immediate ears) can save the targeted neighbor by shouting 'Freeze!' the moment the Bunny's hands go up.
7. Determine the outcome: If a bystander shouts 'Freeze!' before the Bunny completes the lunge, the Bunny is out instead. If the Bunny completes the lunge before anyone shouts, the targeted ear is out.
8. Resume play: The player who was targeted (if saved) or the player who saved them restarts the base rhythm to keep the game flowing.

## Facilitation Notes
- Coaching cue: 'Keep your eyes wide! Don't just look at the person with the bunny; watch the whole circle.'
- If players get too caught up in the rhythm and forget to attack, prompt a 'predator phase' where attacks must happen within three passes.
- Coaching cue: 'Commit 100% to the chant. The louder and more rhythmic the base game is, the more shocking the sudden attack will be.'
- If there is a dispute about whether the lunge or the shout happened first, the facilitator acts as the referee. If it is too close to call, rule it a tie and keep both players in to maintain momentum.

## Variations
- Silent Predator: Play the entire game in complete silence, using only physical gestures and eye contact, with the saving shout replaced by a double-clap from any bystander.
- Double Bunny: Introduce two active bunnies passing energy simultaneously, doubling the cognitive load and the potential for sudden attacks.
- No Elimination: Instead of being knocked out of the game, players who are 'eaten' or 'caught' must perform a quick physical penalty (like a spin or a jump) and then immediately rejoin the circle to keep the energy high.

## Debrief
- How did your focus shift when you realized you had to watch the entire circle, not just the person passing the rhythm?
- What did it feel like to step in and save someone else? How does that relate to supporting your scene partners on stage?
- How did maintaining a tight, collective rhythm make the sudden disruptions easier or harder to spot?

## Safety & Inclusion
Ensure players maintain a safe physical distance so that lunges do not result in actual physical collisions. Remind players that 'eating' an ear is represented by a dramatic gesture or a light tap on the shoulder, not aggressive physical contact.

## Why It Works
This game forces players out of their internal monologues and into a state of hyper-vigilant group mind. By requiring players to track the active center, the flanking support roles, and the entire circle's defensive potential all at once, it builds deep peripheral awareness and teaches players to commit fully to both support and sudden action.
