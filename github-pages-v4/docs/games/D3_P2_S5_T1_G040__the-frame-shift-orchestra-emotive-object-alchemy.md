# Virtual Frame Architects

![Virtual Frame Architects — infographic](img/D3_P2_S5_T1_G040__the-frame-shift-orchestra-emotive-object-alchemy.jpg){ .game-hero }

> Collaboratively construct immersive digital worlds and characters using virtual backgrounds, props, and dynamic spotlighting.

## Overview
A virtual-native improv game designed to transform the limitations of video conferencing into a collaborative playground. Players sequentially layer virtual backgrounds, physical props, and profile names to establish a rich, shared environment and cast of characters before launching into a fast-paced, spotlight-driven scene. The result is a highly visual, deeply connected long-form foundation that trains players to treat their digital frame as an active improv partner.

## What It Trains
- **Domain:** D3 — The Scene
- **Principle(s):** Base Reality First; Yes, And; Group Mind
- **Skill(s):** World-Building; Active Listening; Offer Reception; Active Gifting; Peripheral Awareness
- **Technique(s):** C.R.O.W. (Character, Relationship, Objective, Where); Yes, And… sentence games; Endowment-gifting drills; Edits (Sweep, Tag-Out, Sound/Light)
- **Focus:** mixed

**Objective:** To master the C.R.O.W. (Character, Relationship, Objective, Where) framework in a virtual space, establishing a robust Base Reality First through collaborative environmental world-building and active visual gifting.

## Setup
Conducted on a video conferencing platform with renaming, virtual backgrounds, and spotlighting enabled. Participants should have two to three everyday desk objects (like pens, mugs, or books) nearby and a small selection of pre-saved virtual backgrounds. The facilitator acts as the technical director, managing the spotlight feature.

## How to Play
1. Phase 1 (Establishing the Where): The facilitator selects the first Architect to name a location (e.g., 'a forgotten museum basement') and set a corresponding virtual background.
2. Phase 2 (Layering the Environment): The first Architect calls on another player, who must 'Yes, And' the location by changing their own background to a complementary setting (e.g., 'the dusty display case inside the basement') and briefly describing their addition. Repeat this with two more players to build a multi-perspective environment.
3. Phase 3 (Defining the Who): The facilitator prompts a player to initiate character creation. This player renames their digital profile to their character's name, adopts a distinct physical posture, holds up a desk prop, and introduces themselves (e.g., 'Dr. Vance, the obsessive curator').
4. Phase 4 (Connecting the Cast): The first character calls on another player, who renames themselves and introduces a character with a clear relationship to the first (e.g., 'Barnaby, the stolen mummy Dr. Vance is trying to hide'). This continues until all players have established a character, a prop, and a physical profile.
5. Phase 5 (Launching the Scene): The facilitator spotlights two players to begin the scene. They immediately initiate a dialogue rooted in their established characters, relationships, and the shared environment.
6. Phase 6 (The Perspective Shift): At any point, one of the active spotlighted players calls out another player's character name to swap them in. The facilitator immediately spotlights the new player, replacing the caller.
7. Phase 7 (Visual or Verbal Offer Reception): The newly spotlighted player must instantly 'Yes, And' the scene either visually (by displaying a prop or adjusting their background to match the current action) or verbally (by delivering a line that builds directly on the last spoken dialogue).
8. Phase 8 (Active Audience Gifting): Players not currently spotlighted must keep their cameras on and actively listen. They can offer non-verbal support by sending single-emoji reactions or single-word chat suggestions to feed the active scene.
9. Phase 9 (Resolution): The facilitator allows the scene to cycle through multiple character combinations, ensuring everyone is spotlighted at least once, before calling 'Scene!' at a high point of narrative resolution.

## Facilitation Notes
- Coaching Cue: 'Play the frame, not just the voice! Use your physical space, your props, and your background as active offers.'
- Pitfall & Fix: Players get stuck trying to find the 'perfect' background, causing dead air. Fix: Remind them that any background can work if they justify it verbally. If they cannot change it quickly, they can use their physical camera angle or a prop to represent the change.
- Coaching Cue: 'Establish the Base Reality first. Before you introduce conflict, make sure we know who you are to each other and where you are standing.'
- Pitfall & Fix: The facilitator lags on spotlighting, disrupting the flow. Fix: The facilitator should keep their mouse hovered over the participant list, ready to spotlight as soon as a name is called. Practice a quick one-minute tech run-through before starting.

## Variations
- The Split-Screen Split: Instead of replacing one player, the facilitator occasionally spotlights three players at once, forcing a three-way relationship dynamic.
- Prop Alchemy: Every time a new player is spotlighted, they must take whatever prop the departing player was holding and 're-gift' it as a completely different object in their own frame.
- Background Roulette: The facilitator randomly changes the background of a spotlighted player mid-sentence, forcing them to immediately justify why their environment suddenly shifted.

## Debrief
- How did having a pre-established visual environment (the 'Where') change how you made your initial character choices?
- What did it feel like to 'Yes, And' using physical props and backgrounds instead of just spoken dialogue?
- How did the spotlight transitions help or hinder your active listening and peripheral awareness?
- How did we maintain a cohesive Group Mind even when we were physically isolated in our own rooms?

## Safety & Inclusion
Ensure all participants are comfortable with having their cameras on. If a participant has technical limitations or privacy concerns regarding their home environment, allow them to use standard solid-color virtual backgrounds or participate purely via audio/chat as a 'Narrator' or 'Tech Director' assistant. Ensure props used are safe, non-hazardous household items.

## Why It Works
This game succeeds because it systematically addresses the primary challenges of virtual improv—latency, lack of physical presence, and audio overlap—by turning them into structured mechanics. By utilizing the spotlight feature, it establishes clear focus, while the sequential building of backgrounds and characters ensures that a robust Base Reality (C.R.O.W.) is locked in before any action begins. It forces players to look at their screen as a physical stage, developing deep peripheral awareness and multi-modal offer reception.
