# Dubbed Dialogue

![Dubbed Dialogue — infographic](img/D2_P3_S4_T2_G1037__dubbed-movie.jpg){ .game-hero }

> Physical actors and voice actors collaborate to perform a seamlessly synchronized, highly collaborative scene.

## Overview
Four players split into two teams: two physical actors who perform silent, expressive actions on stage, and two voice actors who sit downstage providing all spoken dialogue. The magic lies in the perfect synchronization and mutual support, making it look like a poorly dubbed foreign film where every physical choice and spoken word is instantly accepted and justified.

## What It Trains
- **Domain:** D2 — The Partner
- **Principle(s):** Yes, And; Make Your Partner a Genius; Show, Don't Tell; Group Mind
- **Skill(s):** Active Listening; Offer Reception; Active Gifting; World-Building; Support Work
- **Technique(s):** Meisner Repetition; Endowment-acceptance; Endowment-gifting drills; C.R.O.W. (Character, Relationship, Objective, Where)
- **Focus:** comedy_game

**Objective:** To develop deep offer reception and endowment-acceptance. Players learn to treat every physical movement or spoken word from their partner as an absolute gift, instantly justifying and building upon it to make their partner look brilliant.

## Setup
An open performance space. Two chairs are placed downstage left or right, facing the stage but angled slightly toward the audience, for the voice actors. The remaining performance area is clear for the physical actors. No props are needed.

## How to Play
1. Divide the players into two pairs: the Physical Actors (who will perform on stage) and the Voice Actors (who will sit downstage).
2. Assign each Voice Actor to a specific Physical Actor; Voice Actor A will speak only for Physical Actor A, and Voice Actor B only for Physical Actor B.
3. Get a simple suggestion from the group (e.g., a location or an unusual relationship) to initiate the scene.
4. The Physical Actors begin the scene silently, using expressive physical movements, object work, and mouth movements without making any sound.
5. The Voice Actors immediately provide the spoken dialogue, matching the timing of the physical actors' mouth movements as closely as possible.
6. Both pairs must actively listen and watch: the physical actors must instantly adapt their body language to match the emotional tone and facts established by the voices, while the voice actors must narrate and justify the physical actions occurring on stage.
7. If a physical actor picks up an imaginary object, their voice actor must instantly name what it is; if a voice actor declares their character is limping, the physical actor must immediately start limping.
8. Continue the scene for 3 to 5 minutes, focusing on building a cohesive narrative through mutual endowment and rapid acceptance.

## Facilitation Notes
- Side-coach the voice actors to avoid over-talking. Give the physical actors room to breathe, move, and initiate physical offers.
- Pitfall: Physical actors waiting for the voices to tell them what to do, resulting in static scenes. Fix: Remind physical actors to initiate bold physical choices and object work to feed the voice actors.
- Pitfall: Voice actors ignoring the physical reality on stage. Fix: Side-coach with 'Voice actors, look at what they are holding! What is that?' to force endowment-acceptance.
- Encourage 'justification' over correction. If a voice actor says something that contradicts a physical movement, the physical actor should find a creative way to make both true.

## Variations
- Foreign Language Dub: The physical actors speak in a gibberish language, and the voice actors translate their words into English with a slight delay.
- Genre Dub: Perform the scene in a specific cinematic style, such as a 1940s film noir, a martial arts epic, or a melodramatic soap opera.
- Switch-Off: On a facilitator's clap, the physical actors become the voice actors and vice versa, maintaining the same characters and storyline.

## Debrief
- How did it feel to have your physical choices instantly named and defined by someone else?
- What strategies did you use to make your partner look like a genius when they threw you an unexpected curveball?
- How does relinquishing control over your own voice or body actually free you up to be more creative?

## Safety & Inclusion
Ensure the physical actors are mindful of spatial awareness, especially since they are focusing on matching external voices. Voice actors should sit comfortably with a clear, unobstructed view of the stage to avoid neck strain.

## Why It Works
This game works because it completely decouples physical action from verbal intent, forcing players into a state of radical interdependence. By stripping away a player's control over either their voice or their body, they must rely entirely on their partner's offers. This builds the core improv muscle of endowment-acceptance, where every unexpected input is treated as a perfect, deliberate choice.
