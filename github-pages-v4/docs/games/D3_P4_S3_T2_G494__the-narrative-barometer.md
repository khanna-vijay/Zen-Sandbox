# The Narrative Barometer

![The Narrative Barometer — infographic](img/D3_P4_S3_T2_G494__the-narrative-barometer.jpg){ .game-hero }

> Steer your story's architecture in real time by reading and responding to audience engagement signals.

## Overview
This exercise turns audience attention into a live, physicalized feedback loop for improvisers mid-scene. A side-coaching facilitator monitors the room's energy and uses silent hand signals to guide the players' narrative choices. Players must instantly adapt by either deepening their current trajectory or executing a dramatic narrative pivot to recapture focus.

## What It Trains
- **Domain:** D3 — The Scene
- **Principle(s):** Serve the Story; The Audience Is the Final Scene Partner; Yes, And
- **Skill(s):** Narrative Architecture; Stakes / The 'Want'; World-Building; Justification; Room Reading; Audience-Energy Management
- **Technique(s):** Platform/Tilt; C.R.O.W. (Character, Relationship, Objective, Where); If this is true, what else is true?; Justify the absurd; Energy-calibration
- **Focus:** narrative

**Objective:** To develop advanced narrative architecture skills, specifically mastering the Platform and Tilt technique. Players learn to read the room, dynamically adjust stakes, and seamlessly justify sudden plot shifts to keep the audience deeply invested.

## Setup
Set up a performance space with two to three active players on stage and the remaining participants acting as the audience. The facilitator stands or sits at the edge of the stage, positioned where the active players can see them in their peripheral vision but the audience's focus remains on the scene. No props are required.

## How to Play
1. Establish the Signals: The facilitator introduces three silent hand signals to the players: a high hand (high audience engagement), a low hand (low or fading engagement), and a flat hand (neutral or stable engagement).
2. Initiate the Scene: Two or three players step up and begin a scene based on a simple suggestion, establishing a clear platform including who, what, where, and their initial relationships.
3. Monitor the Room: The facilitator closely observes the audience's physical and vocal reactions, such as laughter, leaning forward, shifting in seats, or silence, for the first 30 to 45 seconds of the scene.
4. Deliver the First Signal: Once a clear energy trend is identified, the facilitator discreetly flashes a signal to the players without interrupting the dialogue.
5. Respond to High Engagement: If the signal is high, the players must immediately deepen or escalate the current narrative thread by revealing a secret, raising the emotional stakes, or leaning into a vulnerability.
6. Respond to Low Engagement: If the signal is low, the players must immediately disrupt or simplify the scene by introducing a sudden physical threat, clearly restating their objective, or shifting the physical location.
7. Respond to Neutral Engagement: If the signal is neutral, players maintain their current trajectory, continuing to build their platform steadily without forcing a major shift.
8. Justify the Shift: Regardless of the signal, players must instantly justify the narrative change using the principle of if true, what else is true, making the shift feel like an organic, logical progression of the story.
9. Continue the Loop: The facilitator continues to monitor the room and feed signals every 30 to 60 seconds, guiding the scene through multiple narrative peaks and valleys until calling Scene.

## Facilitation Notes
- Keep signals highly discreet: The facilitator should position themselves so players can catch the hand signals in their peripheral vision, preventing the audience from focusing on the facilitator instead of the scene.
- Avoid anticipation: Coach players not to wait passively for a signal. They must actively build a strong, committed scene, treating the signals as sudden environmental shifts rather than instructions to pause.
- Focus on justification: If a player introduces a sudden disruption due to a low-energy signal, the other players must immediately accept and justify it rather than blocking or questioning the reality.
- Accurate room reading: As the facilitator, base your signals on genuine audience cues like fidgeting, whispering, or dead silence versus rapt attention and laughter, rather than your personal taste in the scene's direction.

## Variations
- Audience-Led Barometer: Instead of a facilitator, designate a small sub-section of the audience to hold up colored cards (green for high, red for low) to directly signal their engagement levels to the players.
- The Emotional Barometer: Shift the focus from narrative plot points to emotional intensity. High signals demand deeper emotional vulnerability, while low signals demand a sudden, extreme shift in emotional state.
- Silent Barometer: Run the entire scene in gibberish or complete silence, forcing players to rely entirely on physical stakes, body language, and spatial relationships to shift the audience's engagement.

## Debrief
- How did it feel to have your narrative choices dictated by external audience energy rather than your internal plan?
- What specific strategies did you find most effective for recapturing the audience's attention during a low engagement signal?
- How did justifying a sudden, forced narrative shift actually help expand the world of the scene?
- In what ways did this exercise change how you listen to the room while performing?

## Safety & Inclusion
Ensure players feel supported when making big, sudden narrative shifts. Remind the group that disrupting a scene should never involve crossing physical boundaries or introducing sensitive or triggering content without prior consent. Encourage physical safety during sudden high-energy actions.

## Why It Works
This game works because it externalizes the invisible feedback loop between the stage and the house. By forcing players to treat audience energy as a concrete narrative offer, it breaks the habit of playwriting and trains improvisers to use the Platform and Tilt technique dynamically. It teaches that a flagging scene is not a failure, but an invitation to tilt the narrative and re-engage the room.
