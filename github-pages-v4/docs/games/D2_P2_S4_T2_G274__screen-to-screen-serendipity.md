# Grid Gift Relay

![Grid Gift Relay — infographic](img/D2_P2_S4_T2_G274__screen-to-screen-serendipity.jpg){ .game-hero }

> Co-create and pass an invisible, evolving object across your virtual video grid.

## Overview
Grid Gift Relay is a virtual-first improv game where players collaboratively shape, endow, and pass an imaginary object through their screens. By dynamically changing their display names to match the object's current state, players create a highly visible, shared physical reality. The experience is playful, focused, and highly collaborative, turning the limitations of virtual space into a creative playground.

## What It Trains
- **Domain:** D2 — The Partner
- **Principle(s):** Yes, And; Make Your Partner a Genius; Show, Don't Tell; Group Mind
- **Skill(s):** Physicality & Space Work; Active Listening; Offer Reception; Active Gifting; World-Building; Peripheral Awareness
- **Technique(s):** Object work; Endowment-acceptance; Endowment-gifting drills; Endowment chains
- **Focus:** skill_drill

**Objective:** To develop offer reception and endowment-acceptance by forcing players to physically handle, verbally expand upon, and digitally document an imaginary object passed between video feeds.

## Setup
Ensure all participants are on a virtual video platform with 'Grid View' enabled and the permission to rename themselves turned on. Players should start with their microphones muted and their cameras on, displaying their real names.

## How to Play
1. The facilitator starts by miming the retrieval of an imaginary object from off-screen, shaping it physically, and describing its sensory details like weight, temperature, and texture.
2. The facilitator then renames their screen display name to reflect the object, using a format like '[[The Whispering Teacup]] - Facilitator Name', and demonstrates interacting with it.
3. The facilitator passes the object by miming a push toward the edge of their camera frame and calling out a specific player's name.
4. The facilitator immediately changes their display name back to their real name and mutes their microphone.
5. The designated receiver immediately unmutes, renames themselves to reflect the object (e.g., '[[The Whispering Teacup]] - Player Name'), and mimes catching it from the direction it was thrown.
6. The receiver must 'Yes, And' the object by physically handling it according to its established properties, then adding one new sensory detail or magical function.
7. The receiver then passes the object to a new player by calling their name, miming the pass, reverting their own display name, and muting.
8. Play continues in this relay fashion until every participant has received, endowed, and passed the object at least once.

## Facilitation Notes
- Side-coaching cue: 'Show us the weight! If it is heavy, let your shoulders drop. If it is hot, do not hold it too long!'
- Pitfall: Players forget to rename themselves, stalling the visual tracking. Fix: Have the facilitator gently prompt them in the chat or verbally: 'Rename and claim!'
- Side-coaching cue: 'Build on what came before! If it was a whispering teacup, it cannot suddenly be a silent bowling ball—but it can become a whispering teacup that now leaks stardust.'
- Pitfall: Audio lag or talking over each other. Fix: Enforce strict mute etiquette where only the active holder of the object is unmuted.

## Variations
- The Metamorphosis: Instead of keeping the same object and adding details, each pass must transform the object into something entirely new inspired by the previous player's final physical gesture.
- The Emotional Relic: The object carries an emotional charge (such as extreme joy, fear, or suspicion) that the holder must instantly adopt and express while handling it.

## Debrief
- How did changing your display name affect your sense of ownership over the imaginary object?
- What physical cues from your partners helped you believe the object was real before it reached your screen?
- How did you balance honoring the existing details of the object with adding your own creative twist?

## Safety & Inclusion
For participants with limited physical mobility or those unable to use cameras, allow them to describe the object's properties and pass it using purely verbal cues or chat-based descriptions, with the group validating their choices with equal enthusiasm.

## Why It Works
This game works because it pairs physical mime (object work) with a concrete digital anchor (the display name). By forcing players to rename themselves, it creates a literal, visual contract of 'Yes, And'—they must accept the exact name and state of the object handed to them before they can pass it on, reinforcing active listening and endowment.
