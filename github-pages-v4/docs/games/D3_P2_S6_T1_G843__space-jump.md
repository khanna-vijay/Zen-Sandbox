# Space Jump

![Space Jump — infographic](img/D3_P2_S6_T1_G843__space-jump.jpg){ .game-hero }

> A physical chain-reaction game of freezing, justifying absurd postures, and reversing back to the start.

## Overview
Players build a series of nested scenes, adding one player at a time based on frozen physical postures. Once the stage is full, the game reverses, peeling players away one by one to resolve each previous scene in backward order. It is a high-energy, physical exercise that demands sharp focus and rapid narrative justification.

## What It Trains
- **Domain:** D3 — The Scene
- **Principle(s):** Base Reality First; Commit 100%; Group Mind
- **Skill(s):** Justification; Physicality & Space Work; Support Work; Pacing & Rhythm
- **Technique(s):** Justify the absurd; Object work; Tap-ins; C.R.O.W. (Character, Relationship, Objective, Where)
- **Focus:** mixed

**Objective:** Develops rapid physical justification, establishes a strong base reality instantly, and trains players to support their scene partners by making sense of unexpected physical shapes.

## Setup
Players stand in a line or semi-circle at the back of the playing space (the backline). The stage area should be clear of obstacles. No props or chairs are required.

## How to Play
1. One player steps into the performance space and begins a solo, highly physical scene, establishing a clear activity and environment.
2. After 15 to 30 seconds, the facilitator calls out 'Jump!' and the active player must instantly freeze in their exact physical posture.
3. A second player from the backline enters the space, assumes a physical position that interacts with the frozen player, and initiates a brand-new, two-person scene with a completely different context, justifying both of their physical shapes.
4. Once the two-person scene is established, the facilitator calls 'Jump!' again, and both players freeze instantly in their current physical positions.
5. A third player enters, assumes a position relative to the frozen players, and starts a three-person scene with a new reality, justifying all three physical postures.
6. This pattern of freezing and adding players continues sequentially until all players (typically 5 to 8) are active in a single, large-group scene.
7. Once the final, full-group scene is established, the facilitator calls 'Jump!' and instructs the last player who entered to exit the stage.
8. The remaining players must instantly resume the previous scene (with one fewer player) exactly where they left off, maintaining their physical positions and narrative context.
9. The facilitator continues to call 'Jump!' to peel away players one by one, reversing through each scene in backward order until only the original solo player remains to finish their initial scene.

## Facilitation Notes
- Side-coach players to freeze instantly on the 'Jump!' cue without adjusting their posture to make it 'easier' or more comfortable; the comedy and challenge come from justifying awkward, accidental shapes.
- If an entering player struggles to justify the shapes, side-coach them to: 'Touch the frozen player or use their hand as an object to force a physical connection.'
- Remind players on the backline to pay close attention to the physical states and narrative contexts of the earlier scenes, as they will need to recall them perfectly during the reverse sequence.
- Watch out for players rushing the scenes. Each layer needs enough time to establish a clear base reality (who, what, where) before the next 'Jump!' is called.

## Variations
- Emotional Jump: Instead of just physical shapes, the incoming player must justify the emotional states of the frozen players, escalating the emotional stakes of the new scene.
- Genre Jump: Each new layer of the jump must be played in a completely different theatrical or cinematic genre (e.g., Western, Sci-Fi, Shakespearean, Soap Opera).
- Blind Jump: The players on the backline must turn their backs to the stage and only turn around to see the frozen shapes when 'Jump!' is called, forcing pure, unfiltered spontaneity.

## Debrief
- How did physicalizing your character first help you discover the base reality of the new scene?
- What strategies did you use to remember the context and physical state of your previous scenes during the reverse sequence?
- How does justifying an absurd physical posture lead to more interesting scene choices than starting with a blank slate?

## Safety & Inclusion
Ensure players are mindful of physical boundaries when entering the space and interacting with frozen players. Avoid high-risk physical freezes (like balancing on one foot or holding heavy weight) that cannot be sustained safely. Players should adapt their freezes to their own physical comfort and accessibility needs.

## Why It Works
This game forces players to bypass intellectual planning by using physical shapes as the primary inspiration. By justifying an arbitrary, frozen posture, players practice 'Base Reality First' and 'Justifying the Absurd,' turning physical accidents into deliberate, meaningful narrative choices.
