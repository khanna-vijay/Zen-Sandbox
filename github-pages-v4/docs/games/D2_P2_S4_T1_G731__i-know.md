# I Know

![I Know — infographic](img/D2_P2_S4_T1_G731__i-know.jpg){ .game-hero }

> Build instant agreement and shared history by starting every single line with 'I know.'

## Overview
A two-player scene-work drill where every line of dialogue must begin with the phrase 'I know.' This constraint forces players to immediately validate their partner's contributions as established facts while instantly adding new details to the shared reality. It creates a highly collaborative environment where both players share the burden of creation equally.

## What It Trains
- **Domain:** D2 — The Partner
- **Principle(s):** Yes, And; Base Reality First
- **Skill(s):** Offer Reception; Active Gifting; World-Building
- **Technique(s):** Yes, And… sentence games; Endowment-gifting drills; C.R.O.W. (Character, Relationship, Objective, Where)
- **Focus:** skill_drill

**Objective:** To master offer reception and active gifting by eliminating denial, establishing a rich base reality, and sharing the responsibility of world-building.

## Setup
Two players stand facing each other in the performance space. No props or special staging are required. The rest of the group observes as the audience.

## How to Play
1. Player A initiates the scene with a statement of fact or an observation, starting with the phrase 'I know...'
2. Player B must immediately accept this premise as absolute truth by responding with a line that also begins with 'I know...'
3. Each subsequent line spoken by either player must begin with the exact phrase 'I know...'
4. Players must use their 'I know' statements to build upon the existing information, adding specific details about their relationship, environment, or shared history.
5. Avoid asking questions; every line must be a declarative statement of shared knowledge.
6. Continue the scene for one to two minutes, allowing a rich, collaborative story to unfold through mutual agreement.

## Facilitation Notes
- Side-coach players to avoid 'Yes, but' energy. If Player A says 'I know we are lost,' Player B shouldn't say 'I know, but we have a map.' Instead, say 'I know, and the wolves are getting closer.'
- Watch out for players repeating the exact same information. The 'And' part of 'Yes, And' requires adding new details, not just agreeing with the old ones.
- Encourage emotional connection. Knowing something about your partner or your situation should evoke an emotional reaction (fear, joy, guilt, excitement).
- If a player forgets to start with 'I know,' gently call out 'I know!' to prompt them to restart their line with the constraint.

## Variations
- Physical 'I Know': Players must perform a physical action or object work that matches the shared knowledge established in their lines.
- Emotional 'I Know': Each player is assigned a specific emotion, and every 'I know' statement must justify or escalate that emotional state.

## Debrief
- How did starting every line with 'I know' change how you listened to your partner?
- Did you feel more or less pressure to be clever when the rule forced you to agree instantly?
- How did this constraint help build a shared history or relationship between the characters?

## Why It Works
By forcing players to claim they already know what their partner is introducing, the game bypasses the analytical brain's urge to negotiate, deny, or question. It establishes an instant, high-status alignment where both characters are on the same page, rapidly constructing a deep base reality.
