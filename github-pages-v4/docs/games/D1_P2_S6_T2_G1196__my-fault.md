# My Fault!

![My Fault! — infographic](img/D1_P2_S6_T2_G1196__my-fault.jpg){ .game-hero }

> Celebrate mistakes instantly by diving to the floor and claiming full responsibility for every drop.

## Overview
A high-energy, physical warm-up where players walk around a space tossing water-weighted bottles to one another upon making eye contact. When a bottle inevitably drops, both players immediately drop to the floor and joyfully shout 'My fault!', transforming a moment of failure into a shared celebration.

## What It Trains
- **Domain:** D1 — The Self
- **Principle(s):** Fail Joyfully; Make Your Partner a Genius; Group Mind
- **Skill(s):** Self-Recovery; Active Listening; Peripheral Awareness
- **Technique(s):** Reframe-the-flub reps
- **Focus:** connection

**Objective:** To build resilience against the fear of making mistakes, practice rapid self-recovery, and foster a supportive group dynamic where failure is met with enthusiasm rather than shame.

## Setup
Clear a moderate-sized room of obstacles. Prepare two sturdy plastic bottles, each filled with about an inch of water to give them a satisfying weight for tossing. Have players stand scattered throughout the space.

## How to Play
1. Instruct all players to begin walking around the room at a moderate pace, maintaining awareness of the entire group.
2. Introduce two water-weighted plastic bottles to two different players in the space.
3. To pass a bottle, a player holding it must make direct eye contact with another player; once eye contact is established, they toss the bottle to that person.
4. If a catch is successful, the new holder seeks eye contact with someone else and tosses it, keeping the bottles in constant motion.
5. If a bottle is dropped, both the thrower and the intended receiver must immediately drop to the floor (kneeling, sitting, or lying down) and enthusiastically shout, 'My fault!'
6. After celebrating the drop, both players stand back up, one retrieves the bottle, and play immediately resumes without hesitation.
7. Encourage players to actively seek out challenging, long-distance throws to increase the likelihood of drops and practice joyful failure.

## Facilitation Notes
- Side-coaching cue: 'Seek out the drop! Don't play it safe—throw across the room!'
- Side-coaching cue: 'Celebrate the mistake! Make "My fault!" the loudest, happiest sound in the room.'
- Pitfall: Players might hesitate or apologize sincerely when dropping. Fix: Remind them that drops are the goal of the game; reframe the drop as a gift to the group.
- Pitfall: Throwing without eye contact, leading to unsafe blind tosses. Fix: Pause and remind players that eye contact is the 'consent' to throw.

## Variations
- Hot Potato: The bottles are 'red hot' and must be thrown within one second of catching them, increasing the speed and frequency of drops.
- Heavy/Light: The facilitator calls out physical properties for the bottles (e.g., 'They weigh 50 pounds!' or 'They are light as feathers!'), requiring players to adjust their physical commitment and object work while tossing.
- Group Fault: When a bottle drops, the entire room (not just the thrower and receiver) must drop to the floor and shout 'My fault!' in unison, reinforcing collective support.

## Debrief
- How did it feel to physically throw yourself into celebrating a mistake instead of tensing up?
- How did the energy in the room shift when we started actively cheering for the drops?
- How can we bring this attitude of 'My fault!' (joyful ownership of mistakes) into our scenic improv work?

## Safety & Inclusion
Ensure players who have mobility issues or difficulty getting down to the floor are offered alternative physical celebrations (e.g., throwing hands in the air, taking a dramatic bow, or striking a superhero pose while shouting 'My fault!'). Ensure the floor is dry and free of slipping hazards from any potential bottle leaks.

## Why It Works
By pairing a physical 'mistake' (dropping a bottle) with an immediate, high-energy, and physically low-status response (dropping to the floor and shouting 'My fault!'), the game re-wires the brain's threat response to failure. It gamifies the flub, turning a moment of potential embarrassment into a high-status, shared comedic release that builds trust and group mind.
