# Play With That

![Play With That — infographic](img/D3_P0_S1_T1_G803__play-with.jpg){ .game-hero }

> An active side-coaching drill where off-stage players highlight and escalate the scene's funniest patterns.

## Overview
In this dynamic scene-work exercise, two active players perform a scene while two off-stage observers act as 'game spotters.' Whenever an observer notices a compelling choice, emotional reaction, or quirky behavior, they call out 'Play with [that element],' prompting the actors to immediately lean into and escalate that specific pattern.

## What It Trains
- **Domain:** D3 — The Scene
- **Principle(s):** Yes, And; Make Your Partner a Genius
- **Skill(s):** Game Identification; Heightening & Exploration; Active Listening; Offer Reception; Support Work
- **Technique(s):** Finding & Playing the Game; The 'ladder' (escalating beats); Endowment-acceptance
- **Focus:** skill_drill

**Objective:** To develop rapid game identification, active listening, and heightening skills by outsourcing the recognition of comedic patterns to external observers, lowering the cognitive load on the active performers.

## Setup
A clear performance space for two players. The remaining players sit or stand as an active audience, with two designated as the active 'Spotters' standing at the edge of the stage.

## How to Play
1. Designate two players to step into the performance space to initiate a standard, relationship-focused scene based on a simple suggestion.
2. Designate two other players to stand at the edge of the stage as 'Spotters,' while the rest of the group observes.
3. The active players begin their scene, focusing on establishing a grounded relationship and environment.
4. The Spotters watch closely for any unusual behavior, emotional spikes, repetitive physical habits, or interesting verbal choices made by the actors.
5. When a Spotter identifies a fun element, they call out loudly and clearly: 'Play with [the element]!' (e.g., 'Play with the passive-aggressive sigh!' or 'Play with the obsession over the clean spoon!').
6. Upon hearing the prompt, the active players must immediately accept the direction, repeating, magnifying, or justifying that specific element in their next interactions.
7. The Spotters continue to call out new 'Play with' prompts as the scene progresses, helping the actors layer and escalate the comedic game.
8. The facilitator edits the scene after two to three minutes, then rotates roles so everyone gets a turn as both an active player and a Spotter.

## Facilitation Notes
- Coaching Cue: 'Spotters, look for organic reactions, not just jokes. What did the character do accidentally that felt true or funny?'
- Pitfall: Spotters calling out completely new ideas that aren't in the scene. Fix: Remind Spotters that their job is to highlight existing offers, not inject external plot points.
- Coaching Cue: 'Actors, don't drop the reality of the scene when a prompt is called. Justify why your character is doing that behavior even more intensely.'
- Pitfall: Actors treating the prompt like a sudden, disconnected command, breaking character. Fix: Encourage them to integrate the prompt smoothly into their character's emotional state.

## Variations
- Self-Spotting: The active players themselves call out 'Play with...' on their own or their partner's offers, taking ownership of game identification mid-scene.
- Emotional Escalation: Spotters can only call out emotional states or reactions (e.g., 'Play with that underlying jealousy' or 'Play with that sudden burst of joy').
- Pass the Baton: When a Spotter calls 'Play with...', they tag in and take over the role of the player who was performing that behavior, continuing the game.

## Debrief
- How did it feel to have external observers point out the 'game' for you? Did it make heightening easier or harder?
- What did you notice about the difference between a random choice and a choice that was highlighted and 'played with'?
- How can we apply this 'spotting' mindset when we are actually in a scene without external callers?

## Safety & Inclusion
Ensure that Spotters call out character behaviors, physical choices, or dialogue patterns, rather than making comments on the actor's actual body, identity, or personal attributes. Keep the focus strictly on the fictional character's choices.

## Why It Works
By separating the act of identifying a game from the act of performing it, this exercise reduces the cognitive overload on improvisers. It trains the 'observer mind' of the off-stage players to recognize patterns, while teaching on-stage players to instantly trust, accept, and expand upon the comedic potential of their own accidental or minor choices.
