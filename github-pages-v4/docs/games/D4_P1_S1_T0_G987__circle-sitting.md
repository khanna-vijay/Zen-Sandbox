# The Infinite Chair

![The Infinite Chair — infographic](img/D4_P1_S1_T0_G987__circle-sitting.jpg){ .game-hero }

> Support the group by trusting the person behind you and sitting on air.

## Overview
A highly physical, cooperative trust exercise where the entire group forms a self-supporting circle by sitting on each other's laps. It requires absolute synchronization, physical commitment, and spatial awareness to prevent the structure from collapsing. The result is a literal, physical manifestation of mutual support.

## What It Trains
- **Domain:** D4 — The Ensemble
- **Principle(s):** Group Mind; Commit 100%
- **Skill(s):** Peripheral Awareness; Support Work; Physicality & Space Work
- **Focus:** connection

**Objective:** To build deep physical trust, develop peripheral awareness, and experience the literal manifestation of mutual support and Group Mind.

## Setup
An open, flat floor space with no obstacles. Players stand in a tight, shoulder-to-shoulder circle facing inward.

## How to Play
1. Instruct all players to stand in a perfect circle, shoulder-to-shoulder, facing the center.
2. Have everyone make a quarter-turn to their right, so that each player is facing the back of the person in front of them.
3. Ask the group to take a step inward to close the gaps, ensuring that each person is standing extremely close to the person in front of them, almost touching chest-to-back.
4. Instruct players to place their hands lightly on the hips or shoulders of the person in front of them for stability.
5. On a synchronized count of three, have everyone slowly bend their knees and sit down onto the thighs of the person behind them, keeping their own spine straight.
6. Once the weight is distributed and the circle is stable, challenge the group to release their hands and hold the self-supporting structure using only core strength and balance.

## Facilitation Notes
- Ensure the circle is perfectly round and tight before sitting; any gaps or flat edges will cause the structure to collapse.
- Side-coach players to keep their spines upright rather than leaning forward, which throws off the center of gravity.
- If the circle collapses, laugh it off, reset immediately, and emphasize that success requires 100% commitment from every single participant.
- Watch for physical discomfort or height disparities; taller players may need to adjust their stance slightly to accommodate shorter players behind them.

## Variations
- The Walking Circle: Once stable, challenge the group to take coordinated steps forward (e.g., starting with the left foot) to make the entire sitting circle rotate.
- Wave Release: Have players raise their hands in a wave pattern around the circle while remaining seated.
- Silent Sync: Attempt the entire setup and sit without any verbal cues or counting, relying purely on breath and physical cues.

## Debrief
- How did it feel to trust that the person behind you would support your weight?
- What happened to the structure if even one person hesitated or didn't fully commit?
- How does this physical experience of mutual support translate to supporting your scene partners on stage?

## Safety & Inclusion
This game involves close physical contact and weight-bearing. Always offer an opt-out for players with knee, back, or hip issues, or those uncomfortable with close physical proximity. An alternative role for opting-out players is to act as 'spotters' or 'architects' who help align the circle and guide the descent.

## Why It Works
This exercise physically instantiates the core improv concept of 'having your partner's back.' Because the circle is interconnected, no single person bears more weight than anyone else; the system only works when everyone commits fully and simultaneously, fostering an immediate, visceral sense of Group Mind.
