# Audience Pulse

![Audience Pulse — infographic](img/D5_P1_S1_T1_G300__audience-pulse-scene.jpg){ .game-hero }

> Calibrate your scene's energy in real-time by treating digital audience reactions as active physical offers.

## Overview
In this virtual improv game, two to three active players perform a scene while a muted audience provides real-time emotional feedback using their video platform's reaction emojis. The performers must constantly monitor these fleeting visual cues, treating them as immediate, collective offers that dictate their characters' emotional states, relationships, and narrative choices.

## What It Trains
- **Domain:** D5 — The Audience
- **Principle(s):** The Audience Is the Final Scene Partner; Yes, And
- **Skill(s):** Room Reading; Offer Reception; Justification
- **Technique(s):** Energy-calibration; Endowment-acceptance; Justify the absurd
- **Focus:** mixed

**Objective:** To develop advanced room-reading and energy-calibration skills by treating the virtual audience as an active, silent scene partner whose real-time reactions must be instantly justified and integrated.

## Setup
A virtual meeting room with at least 5 participants (ideally 10-30). The facilitator establishes a clear legend mapping specific platform emojis to distinct emotional or narrative meanings. Active players must set their platform view to grid view so they can easily see both their scene partners and the audience's reaction pop-ups. All non-performing audience members must mute their microphones.

## How to Play
1. The facilitator assigns specific emotional meanings to the platform's standard reaction emojis (e.g., Thumbs Up means agreement/approval, Heart means deep affection/warmth, Laugh means comedic absurdity, Surprised means a shocking revelation, and Thumbs Down means disapproval/rejection).
2. The facilitator selects two to three active players to perform a scene and provides a simple, open-ended relationship or scenario prompt.
3. The active players begin their scene in grid view, establishing their characters, environment, and initial objectives.
4. The audience members, remaining muted, are instructed to actively and continuously use the platform's reaction emojis to express their real-time emotional responses to the scene's dialogue and action.
5. The active players must treat any cluster of audience reactions as a direct, physical offer from a silent scene partner, immediately adjusting their character's behavior, emotional intensity, or narrative direction to match.
6. If a player notices a wave of a specific reaction, they must verbally justify it within the reality of the scene (e.g., reacting to a wave of 'Hearts' by saying, 'I can feel the warmth between us right now').
7. The facilitator acts as a conductor, occasionally calling out missed reaction trends or prompting the audience to share their feelings if the reaction feed slows down.
8. After two to three minutes of dynamic, calibrated play, the facilitator calls 'Scene!' and rotates in a new set of active players with a new prompt.

## Facilitation Notes
- Coaching Cue: Remind players that emoji reactions on virtual platforms disappear quickly (usually within 10 seconds). They must keep their eyes scanning the entire grid, not just their scene partner.
- Pitfall: Players ignoring the reactions because they are too focused on their pre-planned narrative. Fix: Have the facilitator gently side-coach by saying, 'Look at the screen, what is the room telling you right now?'
- Coaching Cue: Encourage the audience to react honestly and frequently. Their active participation is what drives the scene's energy calibration.
- Pitfall: Conflicting reactions (e.g., half Hearts, half Thumbs Down). Fix: Teach players to justify the conflict itself (e.g., 'I know some of you hate this plan, but I see the love in your eyes!').

## Variations
- The Emotional Hijack: When a specific reaction dominates the screen (e.g., more than 5 of the same emoji), the active players must immediately shift their entire character's emotional state to match that emoji's assigned meaning, regardless of what they were doing.
- The Silent Director: One audience member is designated as the 'Director' and uses the 'Raise Hand' feature to signal a major narrative pivot, which the active players must immediately justify and execute.

## Debrief
- How did it feel to treat silent, digital icons as active, physical offers in your scene?
- What strategies did you use to balance watching your scene partner while reading the audience's reaction pulse?
- How did calibrating your energy to the audience's real-time feedback change the trajectory of your scene compared to a standard virtual scene?

## Safety & Inclusion
Ensure all participants are comfortable with the assigned emoji meanings. If any participant has visual processing challenges that make tracking rapid emoji pop-ups difficult, the facilitator can verbally narrate the dominant audience reactions in real-time to keep the game accessible.

## Why It Works
This game works because it translates the subtle, physical feedback of a live theater audience (laughter, gasps, shifts in posture) into a structured, digital equivalent. By forcing players to constantly scan the screen and justify the audience's reactions, it builds the muscle of room reading and energy calibration, ensuring the audience is treated as the final, vital scene partner even in a virtual space.
