# The Consent Canvas

![The Consent Canvas — infographic](img/D2_P1_S6_T1_G548__the-consent-canvas.jpg){ .game-hero }

> Navigate hidden boundaries and prioritize player safety over scene momentum through active, real-time check-ins.

## Overview
A structured skill-building exercise where players navigate scenes while secretly holding specific personal or physical boundaries. It trains improvisers to actively listen, read subtle cues, and prioritize mutual comfort over comedic or narrative payoff. The experience shifts the focus from pleasing the crowd to building deep, real-time trust with a partner.

## What It Trains
- **Domain:** D2 — The Partner
- **Principle(s):** Consent & Boundaries; Truth Over Pandering
- **Skill(s):** Boundary Navigation; Active Listening; Offer Reception
- **Technique(s):** Check-ins; Cut calls; Negotiating physical contact
- **Focus:** skill_drill

**Objective:** To develop proactive boundary navigation, active listening, and offer reception by practicing real-time check-ins and empowering players to halt scenes when comfort is compromised.

## Setup
Prepare a deck of 'Boundary Cards' (each listing a specific physical, emotional, or spatial boundary, e.g., 'no direct eye contact for over 5 seconds' or 'requires verbal permission before physical touch') and a deck of 'Scenario Prompts' (everyday tasks requiring close cooperation). Arrange a moderate playing space for 2-3 active players, with the remaining players observing.

## How to Play
1. Divide the group so 2 to 3 players are active on stage, while the remaining players act as active observers.
2. Have each active player secretly draw one Boundary Card, which they must strictly honor as their character's and their own personal comfort zone throughout the scene.
3. Provide the active players with a cooperative Scenario Prompt and a clear, shared objective (e.g., assembling a delicate model or packing fragile heirlooms).
4. Instruct players to begin improvising the scene, working toward their shared objective while constantly monitoring their own comfort and their partner's non-verbal cues.
5. Require players to initiate a check-in (verbal, non-verbal, or a brief out-of-character pause) whenever they feel they are approaching a boundary or before initiating physical contact.
6. If a check-in reveals discomfort, the initiating player must immediately adjust their action, finding an alternative, consensual way to pursue the scene's objective.
7. Empower any player to call 'Cut!' immediately if a boundary is crossed or if they feel genuine discomfort; this is celebrated as a successful enforcement of safety.
8. Rotate players after each scene, ensuring everyone experiences both active boundary navigation and active observation.

## Facilitation Notes
- Frame 'Cut' as a Win: Explicitly instruct the group that calling 'Cut!' is a successful demonstration of boundary enforcement, not a failure of the scene.
- Monitor the Cards: Ensure the Boundary Cards contain manageable, non-triggering constraints (e.g., spatial distance, touch limits) appropriate for the group's experience level.
- Side-Coach Check-Ins: If you notice a player hesitating or pushing past a subtle boundary, gently pause the action and ask, 'Let's do a quick player-to-player check-in here.'
- Encourage Creative Alternatives: Remind players that respecting a boundary doesn't end the scene's narrative; it invites creative problem-solving to achieve the objective differently.
- Observe the Observers: Task the off-stage players with watching for the exact moment a check-in occurs and how the partner receives and adapts to the offer.

## Variations
- Open Boundaries: Play the same structure but with open, pre-stated boundaries, focusing purely on the mechanics of physical negotiation without the element of surprise.
- The Silent Canvas: Run the scene entirely in gibberish or silence, forcing players to rely solely on non-verbal check-ins and physical cues to navigate boundaries.
- Graduated Intensity: Start with very simple, low-stakes physical boundaries (e.g., 'no touching shoulders') and progress to emotional or narrative boundaries in subsequent rounds.

## Debrief
- How did it feel to prioritize your secret boundary over the natural momentum or comedic payoff of the scene?
- What types of check-ins (verbal, non-verbal, in-character, out-of-character) felt most natural and effective for you?
- How did your partner's boundary force you to become more creative or adaptive in pursuing your shared objective?
- What did this exercise reveal about the difference between 'yes-and' agreement and active, ongoing consent?

## Safety & Inclusion
This exercise is highly safety-sensitive. Always offer a 'pass' option on any drawn Boundary Card, allowing players to swap cards immediately with no questions asked. Establish a clear, universal 'Cut' or 'Time Out' signal before starting, and ensure the facilitator is prepared to step in and support players if real-world triggers are activated.

## Why It Works
By introducing secret boundaries, the game disrupts the automatic 'yes-and' impulse that can sometimes lead players to ignore their own comfort. It gamifies boundary navigation, making the act of checking in and adjusting a core creative mechanic rather than an interruption, thereby building muscle memory for safe, high-trust collaboration.
