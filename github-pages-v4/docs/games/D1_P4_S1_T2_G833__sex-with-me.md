# Sex With Me

![Sex With Me — infographic](img/D1_P4_S1_T2_G833__sex-with-me.jpg){ .game-hero }

> Deliver rapid-fire, suggestive comparisons using everyday objects to practice unfiltered, high-energy spontaneity.

## Overview
A high-energy, line-up style comedy game where players receive a suggestion of a mundane object and immediately step forward to complete the comparison. It is a fast-paced exercise in trusting your very first instinct, embracing the absurd, and playing directly to the audience's laughter.

## What It Trains
- **Domain:** D1 — The Self
- **Principle(s):** The First Thought Is a Gift; Fail Joyfully; The Audience Is the Final Scene Partner
- **Skill(s):** Unfiltered Spontaneity; Stage Presence & Clarity
- **Technique(s):** First Thought drills; Make the choice readable
- **Focus:** comedy_game

**Objective:** To develop unfiltered spontaneity and trust in one's first thoughts by forcing rapid-fire associations under pressure, while building stage presence and comedic timing.

## Setup
Players stand in a horizontal line facing the audience. The facilitator acts as the host, standing to one side to solicit suggestions from the audience. No props or special staging are required.

## How to Play
1. The host asks the audience for a suggestion of a common, everyday household object.
2. Once the suggestion is given, players must immediately step forward one by one to deliver a comparison starting with the formula: 'Sex with me is like [suggested object] because...' followed by their punchline.
3. Players should step forward based on impulse rather than waiting in a strict linear order, keeping the rhythm fast and unpredictable.
4. Punchlines can range from a single word to a short, detailed explanation, but must be delivered with confidence and direct eye contact with the audience.
5. After a player delivers their line, they step back into the line, allowing the next player to step forward with a new angle on the same object.
6. Once the comedic potential of an object is exhausted, or after a particularly strong punchline, the host calls for a brand-new object suggestion from the audience and the process repeats.

## Facilitation Notes
- Side-coach players to step forward before they have a fully formed joke, as the physical act of stepping forward forces the brain to commit.
- If players are overthinking and stalling, encourage them to say the setup phrase slowly to buy their subconscious a split second to find the punchline.
- Remind players to fail joyfully; if a comparison makes no sense, delivering it with absolute confidence often makes it even funnier to the audience.
- Keep the energy high by keeping the host's transitions tight, cutting to a new suggestion immediately after a massive laugh to leave the audience wanting more.

## Variations
- Secret Personas: Before starting, each player secretly chooses a character archetype or specific attitude and delivers all their comparisons through that lens.
- Abstract Concepts: Instead of physical objects, solicit abstract concepts or professional occupations to elevate the complexity of the comparisons.
- The Reverse Comparison: Flip the prompt to 'A [object] is like sex with me because...' to force a different grammatical and conceptual setup.

## Debrief
- How did it feel to step forward before you knew what you were going to say?
- What happened when a line didn't make logical sense but was delivered with absolute confidence?
- How does trusting your very first thought prevent the 'writer' brain from blocking your creativity?

## Safety & Inclusion
While the game uses a suggestive prompt, players should be reminded that they can interpret 'sex' in highly abstract, absurd, or non-explicit ways. Establish a boundary before playing that allows participants to opt out or adjust the prompt to a non-sexual variation (such as 'Going to school with me' or 'Cooking with me') if anyone in the group feels uncomfortable with the default theme.

## Why It Works
By combining a rigid linguistic template with a completely random suggestion, the analytical mind is bypassed. The pressure of the audience and the speed of the lineup force players to rely entirely on their subconscious associations, training the principle that the first thought is a gift.
