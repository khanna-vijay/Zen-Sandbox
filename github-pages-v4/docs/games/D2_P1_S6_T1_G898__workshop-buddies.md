# Peer Check-In Allies

![Peer Check-In Allies — infographic](img/D2_P1_S6_T1_G898__workshop-buddies.jpg){ .game-hero }

> Establish safe, consensual peer connections to anchor support and reflection throughout a workshop.

## Overview
A structured pairing exercise that bookends a workshop or rehearsal. Players form small support units, negotiate physical and emotional boundaries, create a custom consensual greeting, and act as a safe harbor for reflection.

## What It Trains
- **Domain:** D2 — The Partner
- **Principle(s):** Consent & Boundaries; Make Your Partner a Genius; Group Mind
- **Skill(s):** Boundary Navigation; Single-Partner Empathy & Mirroring; Active Gifting; Support Work
- **Technique(s):** Check-ins; Endowment-gifting drills
- **Focus:** connection

**Objective:** To build psychological safety, practice explicit boundary negotiation, and establish a peer-to-peer support system for active gifting and reflection.

## Setup
In-person, minimal space. Players stand or sit in pairs or trios. No props needed.

## How to Play
1. Divide the group into pairs or trios (allies) at the very beginning of the session.
2. Instruct partners to introduce themselves and explicitly check in about physical boundaries (e.g., asking what level of touch they are comfortable with).
3. Have partners co-create a brief, consensual anchor greeting (such as a handshake, fist bump, synchronized gesture, or verbal call-and-response) that respects everyone's stated boundaries.
4. Give partners two minutes to share their personal goals, hopes, or any anxieties they have about the upcoming session.
5. Run the main workshop or rehearsal as planned, encouraging players to keep an eye out for their allies' successes and moments of bravery.
6. At the end of the session, reconvene the allies to perform their anchor greeting and share one specific, positive observation about each other's work.

## Facilitation Notes
- Model a non-contact greeting first (like a synchronized bow or double-wink) to show that physical touch is entirely optional.
- Remind players to ask, not assume, when designing their greeting: 'A boundary check-in is a gift of safety.'
- If you notice a group skipping the boundary discussion, pause them and ask them to explicitly state their comfort levels before proceeding.
- Encourage players to focus their end-of-session feedback on effort, support, and specific choices rather than general talent.

## Variations
- Mid-Session Check-In: Pause a high-energy or high-vulnerability workshop halfway through to let allies reconnect, perform their greeting, and do a quick emotional temperature check.
- Silent Support: Allies are tasked with offering a specific, non-disruptive visual cue of support (like a thumbs-up or a specific nod) from across the room during exercises.

## Debrief
- How did it feel to explicitly negotiate your physical boundaries before creating your greeting?
- Did having a designated ally change how supported you felt during challenging exercises?
- How does checking in with a peer differ from receiving feedback from a facilitator?

## Safety & Inclusion
This exercise is highly safety-sensitive. Emphasize that 'no touch' is a completely valid boundary. Facilitators must explicitly state that a wave, a bow, or a verbal catchphrase are excellent, high-energy alternatives to physical handshakes.

## Why It Works
By starting with an explicit boundary check-in, players practice the mechanical skill of asking for consent and stating limits in a low-stakes, playful context. This builds a foundation of trust, making them more willing to take creative risks during the main session because they know they have a dedicated, safe peer anchor.
