# The Event Planners

![The Event Planners — infographic](img/D2_P2_S4_T1_G795__party-planning-picnic-basket.jpg){ .game-hero }

> Experience the stark contrast between blocking and building by planning an imaginary celebration.

## Overview
A classic two-phase partner exercise where players attempt to plan an event together. In the first phase, every suggestion is shut down; in the second phase, every suggestion is enthusiastically accepted and expanded upon.

## What It Trains
- **Domain:** D2 — The Partner
- **Principle(s):** Yes, And; Make Your Partner a Genius
- **Skill(s):** Active Listening; Offer Reception; Unfiltered Spontaneity
- **Technique(s):** Yes, And… sentence games; First Thought drills
- **Focus:** skill_drill

**Objective:** To physically and emotionally experience the difference between blocking and 'Yes, And-ing,' highlighting how offer reception affects collaboration, momentum, and partner trust.

## Setup
Players stand or sit in pairs facing each other. No props or special staging required. The facilitator sets a clear, low-stakes event context like a backyard party or a space expedition launch.

## How to Play
1. Divide the group into pairs and designate Player A and Player B in each pair.
2. Announce the objective: the pairs are going to plan a massive, extravagant party or event together.
3. Start Round 1 (The Block): Player A makes a suggestion for the party, and Player B must immediately reject the idea, starting their sentence with 'No' or 'No, because...', then offering an alternative.
4. Have Player A respond to Player B's alternative with another rejection, continuing this cycle of blocking for 90 seconds.
5. Pause the group to briefly observe the energy, physical posture, and emotional state of the room after being repeatedly rejected.
6. Start Round 2 (The Yes, And): Reset the planning process. This time, Player A makes an offer, and Player B must reply starting with the exact words, 'Yes, and...' followed by an addition that builds on the original idea.
7. Have Player A accept Player B's addition with another 'Yes, and...' building the idea even further, continuing this collaborative cycle for 90 seconds.
8. Bring the entire group back together for a structured debrief on the physical and psychological differences between the two rounds.

## Facilitation Notes
- Side-coach during Round 1 to have players notice their physical posture; blocking often leads to crossed arms, closed body language, and dropping eye contact.
- Watch out for 'Yes, but...' in Round 2, which is a disguised block that halts momentum. Gently remind players to use 'Yes, and...' to keep the forward motion.
- Encourage players to make their partner look like a genius by treating even the wildest ideas in Round 2 as brilliant contributions.
- If players get stuck in Round 2, remind them that they do not need to be clever; they just need to accept the last thing said and add one small, obvious detail.

## Variations
- The 'Yes, but...' Round: Insert a middle round where players must say 'Yes, but...' to experience the exhausting nature of passive-aggressive agreement.
- The Escalation: Each 'Yes, and' must raise the stakes or scale of the event, pushing the limits of imagination from a simple picnic to an intergalactic festival.

## Debrief
- How did your body language and physical tension change between the 'No' round and the 'Yes, And' round?
- Which round felt like it required more intellectual effort or 'work,' and why?
- How did the 'Yes, And' rule affect your sense of responsibility for keeping the conversation going?

## Safety & Inclusion
This game is highly accessible and low-physicality. Ensure partners are mindful of vocal volume if the room gets loud during the high-energy 'Yes, And' round. If a participant has processing differences, allow them slightly more time to formulate their 'And' without pressure.

## Why It Works
It provides an immediate, visceral contrast. 'No' creates a cognitive dead-end, forcing the brain to constantly restart from scratch. 'Yes, And' offloads the creative burden, allowing partners to build on top of existing momentum, demonstrating that collaboration is easier and more fun than solo creation.
