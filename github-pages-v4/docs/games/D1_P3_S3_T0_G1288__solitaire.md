# Blind Runway

![Blind Runway — infographic](img/D1_P3_S3_T0_G1288__solitaire.jpg){ .game-hero }

> Run blind down a human corridor, trusting your ensemble to guide and catch you safely.

## Overview
A high-trust physical exercise where a single player runs with closed eyes down a designated path. The remaining players form a protective corridor, gently guiding the runner's trajectory and safely catching them at the finish line. It creates a powerful, visceral experience of vulnerability and collective support.

## What It Trains
- **Domain:** D1 — The Self
- **Principle(s):** Vulnerability; Group Mind
- **Skill(s):** Physicality & Space Work; Support Work
- **Focus:** connection

**Objective:** To cultivate deep physical vulnerability, surrender control, and build absolute trust in the ensemble's physical support and spatial coordination.

## Setup
A large, open room free of obstacles. The group divides: one player is the runner, while the rest of the players form two parallel lines (the "walls" of the runway) leading to a dense, supportive "catching wall" at the far end.

## How to Play
1. Designate one player as the runner at one end of the room, and arrange the remaining players to form a long, straight corridor (the runway) leading away from them.
2. Position at least three or four strong players at the very end of the runway to act as the primary "catching wall," standing shoulder-to-shoulder with knees slightly bent and hands up.
3. Instruct the corridor players along the sides to stand facing inward, ready to use gentle, flat-handed nudges to keep the runner centered if they drift.
4. The runner closes their eyes, takes a deep breath, and establishes physical readiness with arms slightly forward and a relaxed posture.
5. When the runner feels ready, they begin to jog or run down the center of the runway with their eyes completely closed, maintaining a steady forward momentum.
6. As the runner moves, side players silently and gently guide them back to the center if they veer off course, using minimal, supportive physical contact.
7. When the runner reaches the end of the runway, the catching wall absorbs their momentum safely, bringing them to a gentle, complete stop.
8. Rotate roles so that multiple players have the opportunity to experience being the runner, ensuring everyone participates in both guiding and catching.

## Facilitation Notes
- Coaching Cue: 'Trust your feet and the space. Let go of the urge to peek or slow down prematurely.'
- Coaching Cue: 'Catchers, be active and springy. Absorb the runner's weight with your legs, don't just stand stiffly.'
- Pitfall: Runners slowing down to a crawl out of fear. Fix: Encourage them to start with a fast walk, then progress to a jog once they feel the safety of the side guides.
- Pitfall: Side guides being too rough or pushing the runner. Fix: Remind guides to use soft, flat palms to redirect, not push or shove.

## Variations
- The Whisper Guide: Instead of physical touch, the side walls use soft vocal cues (like whispering the runner's name or 'here') to guide them down the center.
- The Windup: The runner spins around three times before starting to challenge their internal compass even further.
- The Curved Path: The corridor forms a gentle S-curve, requiring active, continuous physical redirection from the guides.

## Debrief
- How did it feel to lose your visual orientation while moving at speed? At what point did you feel the urge to open your eyes?
- For the guides and catchers, what did it require of your focus to ensure the runner felt completely safe?
- How does the physical vulnerability experienced here translate to taking creative risks on stage?

## Safety & Inclusion
This exercise requires physical contact and high trust. Ensure all participants consent to being touched on the shoulders, arms, and torso for safety redirection. If a participant has mobility or visual impairments, adjust the speed to a comfortable walk, or allow them to keep their eyes open while focusing on the sensation of being supported by the group.

## Why It Works
By removing sight, the runner must override their survival instincts and rely entirely on the physical presence of their ensemble. This immediate physical vulnerability fosters a deep, non-verbal connection and builds 'group mind,' as the entire room must coordinate silently to protect and support a single player.
