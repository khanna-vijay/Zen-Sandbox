# Threshold and Response

![Threshold and Response — infographic](img/D2_P1_S6_T2_G507__threshold-response.jpg){ .game-hero }

> Practice setting boundaries and calling 'Cut' to prioritize emotional truth over polite compliance.

## Overview
A structured scene-work drill where players navigate high-stakes scenarios designed to test emotional and physical comfort zones. Players actively practice using proactive check-ins and immediate 'Cut' calls to halt scenes the moment a boundary is approached or emotional truth is compromised. The exercise normalizes boundary setting as a creative success rather than a narrative failure.

## What It Trains
- **Domain:** D2 — The Partner
- **Principle(s):** Consent & Boundaries; Truth Over Pandering
- **Skill(s):** Boundary Navigation; Active Listening
- **Technique(s):** Check-ins; Cut calls; Negotiating physical contact
- **Focus:** skill_drill

**Objective:** To develop practical boundary navigation skills, specifically training players to recognize internal discomfort, execute clear 'Cut' calls that instantly override scene agreements, and prioritize genuine emotional truth over people-pleasing or pandering.

## Setup
An intimate, trust-focused space with 3 to 6 players. The facilitator prepares a list of 'Threshold Prompts'—scenarios that naturally invite tension, physical proximity, or emotional intensity without being genuinely traumatic. Players sit in a semi-circle facing the performance space.

## How to Play
1. The facilitator introduces the exercise, emphasizing that calling 'Cut' is a successful demonstration of boundary navigation and self-advocacy, not a failure.
2. Two players step into the performance space while the remaining players act as active observers.
3. The facilitator assigns a 'Threshold Prompt'—a scenario designed to gently test boundaries, such as a pushy salesperson invading personal space or an overbearing mentor dismissing a student's feelings.
4. Players begin the scene, focusing on maintaining high awareness of their own physical and emotional comfort levels.
5. If a player intends to initiate physical contact or step significantly closer, they must briefly pause the scene reality to verbally check in with their partner (e.g., asking 'Is it okay if I put my hand on your shoulder?').
6. At any point, if a player feels their personal boundary is approached, or if they feel the scene is losing its emotional truth and turning into forced compliance (pandering), they must clearly call 'Cut!'.
7. The moment 'Cut!' is called, both players must immediately freeze, drop the scene's reality, and step out of character with zero resistance or questioning.
8. The facilitator immediately pauses the workshop to lead a brief, supportive, and non-judgmental debrief, starting with the player who called the halt.

## Facilitation Notes
- Frame the 'Cut' call as a tool of empowerment: Remind players that stopping a scene is a highly skilled improv choice that protects both the player and the integrity of the art.
- Carefully curate prompts: Ensure scenarios are challenging enough to require boundary navigation (e.g., a pushy family member) but avoid highly sensitive, traumatic, or graphic themes.
- Watch for non-verbal hesitation: If you notice a player stiffening, pulling back, or looking distressed but not calling 'Cut', gently pause the scene yourself to model the check-in process.
- Prevent defensive reactions: Ensure the partner of the player who called 'Cut' understands that the halt is not a personal attack or a punishment, but a vital piece of communication.
- Address the 'Yes-And' trap: Explicitly teach that 'Yes-And' means accepting the reality of the offer, not consenting to physical discomfort or emotional inauthenticity.

## Variations
- Observer Cuts: Allow observing players to call 'Cut!' if they notice a boundary being crossed or a player showing unaddressed discomfort, practicing collective care.
- The Silent Signal: Introduce a non-verbal physical gesture (like tapping one's own chest) as an alternative to the verbal 'Cut' call for players who find vocalizing boundaries difficult in the moment.

## Debrief
- To the player who called 'Cut': What internal signal (physical or emotional) let you know it was time to halt the scene?
- To the partner: How did it feel to receive the 'Cut' call, and how did you transition out of the scene's fiction?
- To the group: What subtle, non-verbal cues did you observe leading up to the halt that could have been addressed with an earlier check-in?
- To all: How does knowing you have an absolute 'Cut' option change your willingness to take creative risks in a scene?

## Safety & Inclusion
This game is highly safety-sensitive. Establish a firm, collective agreement before playing that any 'Cut' call is absolute and requires no justification. Ensure players know they can step out of the exercise entirely at any time without explanation. Physical contact must be explicitly negotiated verbally before it occurs.

## Why It Works
By formalizing the 'Cut' call as an absolute rule that overrides all prior scene agreements, the game dismantles the social pressure to politely endure discomfort for the sake of a performance. It builds muscle memory for boundary setting, transforming consent from an abstract concept into an active, embodied theatrical tool.
