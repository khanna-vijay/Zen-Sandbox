# Magnify the Action

![Magnify the Action — infographic](img/D1_P1_S3_T2_G1164__make-more-interesting.jpg){ .game-hero }

> Slow down time and amplify your physical choices through deep, committed exploration.

## Overview
In this physical skill drill, players are challenged to unpack a single, mundane physical action rather than rushing to the next narrative beat. When prompted by a facilitator's cue, the player must find new depth, struggle, or detail within their current physical task without advancing the plot.

## What It Trains
- **Domain:** D1 — The Self
- **Principle(s):** Commit 100%; Show, Don't Tell
- **Skill(s):** Physicality & Space Work; Heightening & Exploration
- **Technique(s):** Object work; The 'ladder' (escalating beats)
- **Focus:** skill_drill

**Objective:** Develops physical commitment, precise object work, and the ability to heighten a moment through physical expression rather than relying on verbal exposition.

## Setup
A clear playing space where players can move freely. The group stands in a circle or semi-circle, with one player stepping into the center. The facilitator needs to be ready to provide a sharp audio cue, such as a single clap.

## How to Play
1. One player steps into the center of the space and begins performing a simple, everyday physical task, such as pouring a cup of tea or unlocking a door.
2. The player must execute this task with high-fidelity object work, establishing the weight, size, and location of the imaginary items.
3. At any point during the action, the facilitator gives a sharp audio cue, such as a single clap.
4. Upon hearing the cue, the player must immediately make their current physical action more detailed, complex, or dramatic.
5. The player is strictly forbidden from advancing the narrative; they must stay frozen in the timeline of that exact micro-action (e.g., if unlocking a door, they cannot open it, but must instead struggle with a stuck key or a heavy lock).
6. The player continues to heighten this specific physical struggle, committing 100% to the physical reality of the moment.
7. The facilitator can clap a second time to allow the player to finally complete the action, or call 'scene' to rotate to a new player.

## Facilitation Notes
- Coaching cue: 'Don't move forward in time! Stay in the struggle of this exact second.'
- Common pitfall: Players often default to verbalizing their struggle. Remind them to 'Show, Don't Tell' and express the difficulty through physical resistance and facial expression alone.
- Coaching cue: 'Find the comedy or drama in the micro-movement. How does your entire body react to this tiny obstacle?'
- If a player accidentally advances the plot after the clap, freeze them physically, ask them to rewind five seconds to the exact moment of the cue, and try again slower.

## Variations
- Two-Player Expansion: Two players perform a scene together. When the facilitator claps, both must magnify their current physical relationship or shared object work without speaking.
- The Intensity Dial: Instead of a binary clap, the facilitator calls out numbers from 1 to 10 to gradually turn up the physical complexity and emotional commitment of the current action.

## Debrief
- How did it feel to be forced to stay in a single physical moment instead of rushing to what happens next?
- What did you discover about your object work when you were forced to slow down and magnify it?
- How does committing 100% to a physical struggle make a scene more engaging for an audience?

## Safety & Inclusion
Ensure players choose physical actions suited to their individual mobility levels. Emphasize that magnifying an action does not require high-impact physical strain, but rather precision, facial expression, and creative tension.

## Why It Works
This game forces the brain out of verbal plot-generation mode and drops the player directly into their physical body. By restricting narrative progress, it builds tolerance for silence and teaches players that physical commitment is inherently compelling to watch.
