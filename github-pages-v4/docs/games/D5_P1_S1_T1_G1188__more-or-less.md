# More or Less

![More or Less — infographic](img/D5_P1_S1_T1_G1188__more-or-less.jpg){ .game-hero }

> Calibrate scene length and narrative momentum by letting the audience vote to continue or cut.

## Overview
A dynamic showcase format where individual players take turns directing and casting short scenes. At critical narrative junctures, the director pauses the action to ask the audience 'More or Less?', using the crowd's vocal response to either drive the story forward or immediately transition to a new piece.

## What It Trains
- **Domain:** D5 — The Audience
- **Principle(s):** Serve the Story; Serve the Piece; The Audience Is the Final Scene Partner
- **Skill(s):** Narrative Architecture; Pacing & Rhythm; Format Literacy; Room Reading; Audience-Energy Management
- **Technique(s):** Edits (Sweep, Tag-Out, Sound/Light); Longform vs. shortform mechanics; Energy-calibration; Breaking the 4th Wall / Direct Address
- **Focus:** mixed

**Objective:** To develop room-reading skills and energy calibration, teaching players to sense audience engagement, manage narrative pacing, and treat the audience's energy as an active guide for scene duration.

## Setup
An performance space with a clear stage area and an audience area. Players sit or stand on the wings/sidelines, ready to be cast. No props or special materials are required.

## How to Play
1. Designate one player to step forward as the first Director, who will pitch a basic scene premise or ask the audience for a simple suggestion.
2. The Director casts other players from the sidelines to perform the scene, setting up the initial stage picture and starting the action.
3. The cast performs the scene while the Director stands at the edge of the stage, actively observing both the stage action and the audience's reactions.
4. After approximately one to two minutes—ideally at a cliffhanger, a high-energy beat, or a moment of narrative tension—the Director freezes the scene.
5. The Director addresses the audience directly and asks: 'More or Less?'
6. The audience responds by shouting either 'More!' or 'Less!' based on their interest in seeing where the story goes.
7. The Director must read the room's volume and enthusiasm: if 'More' wins, the Director unfreezes the scene and lets it continue for another beat before asking again later.
8. If 'Less' wins (or if the energy is lukewarm), the Director immediately ends the scene, thanks the cast, and steps back to the sidelines.
9. A new player steps forward to become the next Director, initiating a completely new scene with a different cast and premise.

## Facilitation Notes
- Encourage Directors to ask 'More or Less' at moments of high tension or curiosity, rather than when a scene has already dragged or completely died.
- Side-coach Directors to listen to the quality of the shout, not just the volume. A polite 'More' might actually mean 'Less', while a passionate 'More' means they are genuinely hooked.
- If a scene gets a 'More', the actors must immediately re-enter the fiction with high energy; they shouldn't stall or restart the beat.
- Pitfall: Directors taking 'Less' as a personal failure. Remind the group that 'Less' is a valuable tool for tight pacing and keeps the show's overall energy high.

## Variations
- The Director's Twist: If the audience votes 'More', the Director must introduce a new constraint, genre, or character complication to heighten the stakes.
- Silent Voting: Instead of shouting, the audience votes using thumbs up or thumbs down, forcing the Director to visually scan the room and read physical body language.
- Speed Run: Shorten the initial scene time to 30 seconds, forcing rapid-fire decisions and high-energy transitions.

## Debrief
- How did it feel to have the audience directly control the lifespan of your scene?
- What physical or vocal cues did you notice from the audience before you asked 'More or Less' that tipped you off to their engagement?
- As a Director, how did you balance your own narrative curiosity with the room's actual energy?
- How does knowing the scene can be cut at any moment change how quickly you establish stakes and platform?

## Safety & Inclusion
Ensure the audience understands that voting 'Less' is a playful mechanic of the game's format, not a personal rejection of the performers. Keep the tone lighthearted and celebratory of both quick cuts and long runs.

## Why It Works
By giving the audience direct veto power, this game forces players to step out of their internal narrative loops and actively listen to the room. It demystifies the relationship between performer and viewer, turning the audience into an active collaborator that co-signs the pacing and structure of the show.
