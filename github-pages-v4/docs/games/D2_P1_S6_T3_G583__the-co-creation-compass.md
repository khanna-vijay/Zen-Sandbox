# The Boundary Compass

![The Boundary Compass — infographic](img/D2_P1_S6_T3_G583__the-co-creation-compass.jpg){ .game-hero }

> Navigate physical boundaries and integrate 'no' as a creative gift to build trust and safety.

## Overview
An active scene-work drill where players practice explicit physical consent and boundary negotiation in real-time. By utilizing a structured system of verbal and non-verbal check-ins, players learn to treat a partner's boundary not as a roadblock, but as a rich, narrative-driving offer. An external observer helps guide the process, ensuring a safe, supportive environment for experimentation.

## What It Trains
- **Domain:** D2 — The Partner
- **Principle(s):** Consent & Boundaries; Truth Over Pandering
- **Skill(s):** Active Listening; Offer Reception; Boundary Navigation; Justification
- **Technique(s):** Check-ins; Cut calls; Negotiating physical contact
- **Focus:** skill_drill

**Objective:** To develop the ability to proactively negotiate physical contact, read subtle non-verbal cues, and gracefully integrate boundaries into the scene's narrative without breaking flow or sacrificing character truth.

## Setup
Players stand in a circle. Designate two active players to step into the center to perform a scene, and one player to act as the 'Navigator' (an active observer standing just outside the playing space). The rest of the group acts as the supportive audience. No props or special materials are required.

## How to Play
1. The two active players step into the center and initiate a grounded, relationship-focused scene based on a simple suggestion.
2. When a player wishes to close physical distance or make a non-contact physical gesture (like leaning in or reaching out without touching), they must offer a clear, slow physical cue.
3. The receiving partner must immediately respond to this proximity offer with a clear non-verbal signal: an open posture or nod to accept, or a step back or closed posture to decline.
4. Before making any actual physical contact (such as a handshake, hug, or touch on the arm), the initiating player must ask an explicit, in-character verbal question (e.g., 'May I hold your hand?').
5. The receiving player must respond verbally with a clear 'Yes,' 'No,' or a modified boundary (e.g., 'You can hold my shoulder, but not my hand'). Silence or hesitation is always treated as a 'No'.
6. If a boundary or 'No' is established, the initiating player must immediately accept and justify this choice in-character, treating the boundary as a true, narrative-enriching offer.
7. The designated Navigator observes the scene closely and can call 'Pause!' at any point to check in on a boundary exchange, asking the players how the negotiation felt before resuming.
8. The Navigator can call 'Cut!' to end the scene if a boundary is crossed without consent, or when the scene reaches a natural, satisfying conclusion.
9. After each scene, roles rotate so that every player has a turn as an active improviser and as the Navigator.

## Facilitation Notes
- Emphasize that 'No' is a gift: Remind players that a boundary is a highly specific character choice that reveals relationship dynamics, not a rejection of the scene.
- Differentiate player vs. character: Make it clear that player safety and comfort always override character desires. If a player feels uncomfortable, they must say 'No' regardless of what their character might want.
- Empower the Navigator: Ensure the Navigator knows their role is supportive, not punitive. Calling 'Pause' is a tool for collective learning, not a call-out for making mistakes.
- Pitfall: Rushing the asks. Fix: Encourage players to slow down their physical movements and make their verbal asks deliberate and distinct, allowing the partner ample time to process and respond.

## Variations
- Silent Navigation: Run the entire scene without any verbal dialogue, forcing players to rely entirely on physical cues, eye contact, and body language to negotiate proximity and contact.
- The Boundary Scorecard: The Navigator keeps track of how many successful, graceful boundary integrations occur, celebrating creative pivots during the debrief.

## Debrief
- How did it feel to explicitly ask for permission before making physical contact? Did it hinder or help your focus?
- When you received a 'No,' how did you creatively justify it in the moment to keep the scene grounded in truth?
- As the Navigator, what subtle non-verbal cues did you notice that the active players might have missed?
- How does establishing clear physical boundaries actually increase your creative freedom in a scene?

## Safety & Inclusion
This game is highly safety-sensitive. Establish a firm rule before playing that any player can step out of a scene or call 'Cut' for themselves at any time without explanation. Ensure all players understand that a 'No' must be respected instantly and without question, both in and out of character. For players with mobility or sensory sensitivities, adapt the physical cues to match their comfort and physical capabilities.

## Why It Works
By gamifying the negotiation of physical space, this exercise removes the social anxiety of setting boundaries. It reframes a 'No' from a narrative dead-end into a generative offer, aligning with the principle of Truth Over Pandering. The presence of the Navigator provides a structured safety net, allowing players to take creative risks while maintaining absolute physical agency.
