# Scenic Shifts

![Scenic Shifts — infographic](img/D2_P2_S4_T1_G187__the-zoomscape-saga.jpg){ .game-hero }

> Weave an epic collaborative narrative by dynamically changing your virtual backgrounds to Yes-And your partner.

## Overview
Scenic Shifts is a virtual-native narrative game where players use their video backgrounds as active scenic offers. By changing their digital backdrops in real-time, players visually and verbally accept and expand upon the story, transforming a standard video call into a fluid, multi-layered theatrical space.

## What It Trains
- **Domain:** D2 — The Partner
- **Principle(s):** Yes, And; Make Your Partner a Genius; Serve the Story; Group Mind
- **Skill(s):** Active Listening; Offer Reception; Active Gifting; World-Building; Peripheral Awareness
- **Technique(s):** Meisner Repetition; Yes, And… sentence games; Endowment-acceptance; Endowment-gifting drills; C.R.O.W. (Character, Relationship, Objective, Where)
- **Focus:** narrative

**Objective:** To develop advanced Yes-And skills by integrating visual and verbal offers simultaneously, sharpening active listening, and building cohesive narrative worlds under digital constraints.

## Setup
Four to eight players on a video conferencing platform. Each player must have virtual backgrounds enabled and a pre-prepared folder of 10 to 15 diverse images (ranging from mundane offices to fantastical landscapes). Everyone must be in Gallery View. The facilitator establishes a speaking order in the chat.

## How to Play
1. The facilitator posts a sequential speaking order in the chat. All players rename themselves to include their real name and a blank space for their character name, such as 'Alex | Pending'.
2. The first player selects an initial virtual background, renames themselves to fit the scene (e.g., 'Alex | Desert Nomad'), and delivers a 20-second monologue establishing the setting, their character, and a clear narrative desire or cliffhanger.
3. The next player in the chat order must immediately change their virtual background to visually Yes-And the first player's offer (e.g., if Player 1 is in a desert, Player 2 might choose an oasis, a sandstorm, or a futuristic dome).
4. Player 2 renames themselves to reflect their new character (e.g., 'Sam | Oasis Guardian') and delivers a 20-second monologue that verbally integrates both Player 1's story and their own new visual background.
5. Each subsequent player continues this chain, changing their background to react to the previous scene, renaming their character, and delivering a short monologue that bridges the visual and verbal elements.
6. The facilitator monitors the flow, ensuring players take a deliberate 2-second pause after changing their background to let the visual offer land before they begin speaking.
7. The story continues until all players have contributed, culminating in a final player who must bring the narrative to a satisfying resolution using their final background choice.

## Facilitation Notes
- Coaching Cue: 'Let the picture speak first!' Remind players to change their background, pause for two seconds to let the group absorb the visual, and then start speaking.
- Pitfall: Players choosing random, wacky backgrounds that derail the narrative. Fix: Side-coach them to justify the bizarre visual. If they show a giant rubber duck, it must be a colossal monument or a terrifying beast in their story.
- Coaching Cue: 'Look at the whole gallery!' Encourage players to observe how their background fits into the collective grid, treating the entire screen as a shared stage.
- Pitfall: Audio overlap due to lag. Fix: Strictly enforce the chat-based turn order and use the raise hand feature to signal transitions.

## Variations
- The Spotlight Shift: The facilitator dynamically spotlights two players at a time, forcing them to do a rapid-fire two-person scene where they must change backgrounds mid-dialogue whenever the facilitator calls 'Shift!'
- Genre Roulette: The facilitator types a new genre in the chat mid-story, forcing the next player to adapt their background and tone instantly.

## Debrief
- How did having a visual constraint change how you listened to and built upon your partner's verbal offers?
- What strategies did you use to justify a background that didn't perfectly match the story's trajectory?
- How did the physical layout of the gallery view affect your sense of shared space and group mind?

## Safety & Inclusion
Ensure all pre-prepared backgrounds are appropriate for the group's shared boundaries. If a player has technical limitations that prevent virtual backgrounds, allow them to use physical props, hand-drawn signs, or descriptive verbal scenic painting instead to keep the game fully accessible.

## Why It Works
It forces players to practice multi-modal Yes-And. Instead of just agreeing with words, they must visually adapt, which activates different creative pathways. The physical act of changing the background acts as a commitment to the partner's reality, while the renaming mechanic grounds character relationships instantly, bypassing the typical virtual lag and lack of physical cues.
