# Walkabout Greetings

![Walkabout Greetings — infographic](img/D1_P4_S3_T1_G766__meet-greet-walkabout.jpg){ .game-hero }

> Transform your posture, status, and relationships instantly with every person you pass in the room.

## Overview
Players move dynamically through a shared space, instantly adopting new physicalities, statuses, and relationship dynamics prompted by the facilitator. By greeting fellow players under these shifting constraints, participants bypass overthinking and discover characters through immediate physical action.

## What It Trains
- **Domain:** D1 — The Self
- **Principle(s):** The First Thought Is a Gift; Make Your Partner a Genius
- **Skill(s):** Physicality & Space Work; Unfiltered Spontaneity; Status Modulation; Active Listening
- **Technique(s):** Character Walks/Centers; First Thought drills; High/low-status walks
- **Focus:** connection

**Objective:** To develop physical characterization, status modulation, and unfiltered spontaneity by committing instantly to physical centers and relationship prompts without pre-planning.

## Setup
An open room cleared of obstacles. All players stand scattered throughout the space, ready to move.

## How to Play
1. Instruct all players to begin walking around the room at a moderate pace, filling the empty spaces and avoiding walking in a simple circle.
2. Explain that you will call out a specific relationship, social status, or character archetype.
3. Upon hearing the prompt, players must instantly adjust their physical center (e.g., leading with the chest, nose, or knees) and walk as that character.
4. When players make eye contact with someone nearby, they must briefly greet them in character using both physical gestures and a short verbal exchange.
5. Encourage players to accept whatever characterization their partner presents, instantly adapting their own reaction to make their partner look brilliant.
6. After a few moments of interaction, call out 'Keep moving' to prompt players to return to a neutral walk before the next prompt.
7. Introduce a new prompt with a different physical or status dynamic, repeating the cycle for several rounds.

## Facilitation Notes
- Side-coach players to lead with different body parts (e.g., 'Lead with your chin for high status, or your shoulders for low status') to ground the character in physicality.
- If players hesitate or overthink, remind them: 'Your first physical instinct is the right one. Don't plan, just move.'
- Pitfall: Players staying in tight clusters or talking to the same person. Fix: Encourage them to constantly seek out new partners and fill the entire room.
- Remind players to keep greetings brief (10-15 seconds) so they can interact with multiple people per prompt.

## Variations
- Asymmetrical Roles: Assign half the room one role (e.g., royalty) and the other half a contrasting role (e.g., peasants) to explore stark status differences.
- Silent Walkabout: Run the entire exercise in complete silence, relying purely on body language, eye contact, and physical gestures to communicate the relationship.
- Emotional Centers: Instead of archetypes, prompt players with core emotions (e.g., overwhelming joy, deep suspicion, extreme urgency) to drive their movement and greetings.

## Debrief
- How did changing your physical center or posture automatically change the way you spoke and felt?
- What happened when you committed to your very first physical impulse instead of planning a character?
- How did your partner's physical status instantly inform how you treated them?

## Safety & Inclusion
Remind players to maintain physical boundaries and seek consent before initiating any physical contact (like handshakes or high-fives). Offer low-impact movement options for players with mobility constraints, emphasizing that status and character can be expressed entirely through facial expressions and upper-body posture.

## Why It Works
By focusing on immediate physical adjustments (character centers) and rapid-fire interactions, players bypass the analytical mind. This embodies the principle that 'the first thought is a gift' because physical action precedes intellectual justification, leading to organic, high-commitment character choices.
