# The Pressure Cooker

![The Pressure Cooker — infographic](img/D3_P4_S7_T1_G612__the-pressure-cooker.jpg){ .game-hero }

> Systematically turn up the heat on a simple scene to master narrative escalation.

## Overview
A structured scene-work exercise where players deliberately escalate the stakes of a simple scenario in waves. Guided by facilitator prompts, players use logical justification to transform minor inconveniences into high-stakes dramatic or comedic climaxes.

## What It Trains
- **Domain:** D3 — The Scene
- **Principle(s):** Serve the Story; Yes, And
- **Skill(s):** Raising the Stakes; Stakes / The 'Want'; Narrative Architecture; Justification; Active Listening
- **Technique(s):** Stakes-escalation reps; If this is true, what else is true?; Make it matter more beats
- **Focus:** narrative

**Objective:** To develop the ability to identify, raise, and justify narrative stakes and character motivations using the 'if true, what else is true' principle, driving scenes to a satisfying climax.

## Setup
An open performance space. Two to three players step forward to perform, while the remaining players observe as active audience members. No props or special materials are required.

## How to Play
1. Get a simple, low-stakes relationship and setting suggestion from the group, such as two coworkers organizing a supply closet.
2. The active players begin a grounded, realistic scene, establishing their characters, environment, and a minor, low-stakes conflict.
3. After 30 to 45 seconds of established reality, the facilitator calls out 'Pressure UP!'
4. Upon hearing the cue, one player must immediately introduce a new piece of information or action that raises the stakes, building logically on what was already established.
5. The players must fully accept and justify this new reality, playing out the heightened tension for another 30 to 45 seconds.
6. The facilitator calls 'Pressure UP!' two to three more times, with players escalating the stakes with each round based on the principle of 'if this is true, what else must be true?'
7. Once the tension reaches its peak, the facilitator calls 'Boiling Point!'
8. The players immediately collaborate to bring the highly escalated conflict to a dramatic climax and rapid, logical resolution, ending the scene.

## Facilitation Notes
- Coaching Cue: 'If this is true, what else is true? Don't invent a random new problem; make the existing problem matter ten times more.'
- Pitfall: Players introduce completely disconnected absurdities (e.g., suddenly introducing aliens). Fix: Remind players to escalate through logical consequence and personal relationships rather than random external events.
- Coaching Cue: 'Listen to the emotional impact of the escalation. How does this change what your character wants right now?'
- Pitfall: The scene stalls because players ignore the 'Pressure UP!' cue or just repeat the same stakes. Fix: Side-coach immediately with 'What is the immediate, worse consequence of that?' to force a leap.

## Variations
- Silent Pressure: Instead of the facilitator calling 'Pressure UP!', players must read the room and organically escalate the stakes themselves when they feel the scene plateauing.
- The Stakes Pass: Each 'Pressure UP!' cue requires a specific player to take the lead on the escalation, ensuring all players practice initiating the stakes-raise.
- Emotional Pressure: Instead of escalating external plot stakes, players must escalate their internal emotional states and interpersonal dynamics.

## Debrief
- How did building on 'if true, what else is true' make the extreme escalations feel earned and believable?
- What was the difference between introducing a random new problem versus deepening the existing conflict?
- How did the 'Boiling Point' climax feel to execute once the stakes were fully established?

## Safety & Inclusion
While this game is not highly physical, escalating stakes can sometimes lead players toward dark or sensitive themes. Establish a clear boundary before playing that escalations should remain safe, collaborative, and respectful of all players' comfort levels, avoiding real-world trauma or sensitive personal topics unless explicitly agreed upon.

## Why It Works
By externalizing the pacing of a scene through structured prompts, players learn the mechanical rhythm of narrative tension. It forces them to move past the comfort zone of polite conversation and practice the 'Yes, And' of consequence—not just agreeing to a reality, but actively magnifying its importance.
