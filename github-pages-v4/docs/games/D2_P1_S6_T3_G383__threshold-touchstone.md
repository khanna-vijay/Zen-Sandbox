# Threshold and Touchstone

![Threshold and Touchstone — infographic](img/D2_P1_S6_T3_G383__threshold-touchstone.jpg){ .game-hero }

> Navigate physical intimacy and personal boundaries in real-time through structured, high-stakes emotional scenes.

## Overview
This exercise provides a structured framework for players to practice initiating, receiving, and negotiating physical contact during emotionally charged scenes. Operating in a dedicated physical space, players learn to balance narrative momentum with personal comfort, ensuring that physical touch is always a mutual, active choice. The core mechanic introduces a pause-and-reset tool that transforms boundary management into a collaborative, creative dialogue.

## What It Trains
- **Domain:** D2 — The Partner
- **Principle(s):** Consent & Boundaries; Truth Over Pandering; Vulnerability
- **Skill(s):** Boundary Navigation; Active Listening; Offer Reception
- **Technique(s):** Negotiating physical contact; Check-ins; Cut calls
- **Focus:** skill_drill

**Objective:** To develop practical boundary navigation and physical negotiation skills, training players to prioritize authentic personal comfort ('truth over pandering') and practice clear verbal and non-verbal consent during high-stakes scene work.

## Setup
An open, safe playing space where two active players can stand 5 to 6 feet apart. The remaining 1 to 4 players sit nearby as active observers. No props or materials are required. The facilitator prepares a list of high-stakes, emotionally intimate scene prompts that suggest a deep connection but do not mandate physical contact.

## How to Play
1. Two players stand facing each other, approximately six feet apart, establishing the 'Threshold' zone, while the remaining players observe.
2. The facilitator provides an emotionally resonant, high-stakes scenario (such as two estranged family members meeting after years apart) that naturally invites a desire for closeness.
3. Players begin the scene verbally, establishing their relationship and building the emotional stakes without immediate physical contact.
4. To initiate contact, a player must slowly close the physical distance and offer a clear, non-verbal invitation (such as open arms or an outstretched hand), optionally paired with a simple, open-ended verbal check-in like 'May I hold your hand?'
5. The receiving player must respond based on their genuine, real-time comfort level, choosing to accept the offer, decline it clearly, or negotiate a modified physical interaction (such as 'I don't want a hug, but I'd love to hold your hand').
6. If any player, observer, or the facilitator senses discomfort, a missed check-in, or an unnegotiated physical advance, they must immediately call out 'Boundary Check!'
7. Upon hearing 'Boundary Check!', the scene instantly freezes, any physical contact is broken, and players step back to their original neutral distance.
8. The facilitator guides a brief, non-judgmental check-in, asking what was felt and what is needed, allowing players to practice 'truth over pandering' by stating their authentic boundaries.
9. Based on the check-in, the players either resume the scene from the freeze point with the newly negotiated boundaries in place, or reset the scene entirely.

## Facilitation Notes
- Prioritize psychological safety by explicitly stating before the exercise that declining touch or calling a boundary check is a successful, highly valued demonstration of the skill.
- Model the 'Boundary Check!' early in the session. Do not wait for an extreme boundary crossing; call it for a subtle, unnegotiated step forward to show that pausing is a normal, constructive tool.
- Watch out for 'pandering'—where a player accepts physical contact they are uncomfortable with just to please the audience or keep the scene moving. Side-coach them to prioritize their real-world comfort.
- Encourage specific, nuanced negotiation language. Instead of a simple 'yes' or 'no', coach players to suggest alternatives like 'You can put a hand on my shoulder, but please don't hug me.'
- Remind players that a scene can achieve profound emotional depth and connection without any physical contact whatsoever.

## Variations
- Silent Threshold: Run the entire scene in complete silence, forcing players to rely entirely on micro-expressions, body language, and physical proximity to negotiate boundaries.
- Blind Initiation: One player keeps their eyes closed while the other approaches. The approaching player must use clear, gentle verbal check-ins before making any physical contact, training auditory consent cues.

## Debrief
- How did it feel to explicitly negotiate physical contact while trying to maintain the emotional reality of the scene?
- Did you experience an urge to 'pander' or accept touch you didn't fully want for the sake of the story? How did you handle that impulse?
- For observers, what subtle non-verbal cues did you notice that signaled comfort or discomfort before a verbal check-in occurred?
- How did calling or experiencing a 'Boundary Check!' affect your sense of safety and trust with your scene partner?

## Safety & Inclusion
This exercise is highly physical and safety-sensitive. Establish a clear, universal opt-out signal before starting. Ensure players know they have absolute autonomy over their bodies, and that any boundary set is final and non-negotiable. For players with mobility or sensory differences, adapt the physical distance and types of touch allowed to ensure full comfort and accessibility.

## Why It Works
By separating physical initiation into distinct phases (Threshold, Invitation, Touchstone), the game slows down the rapid decision-making process of improv. This friction allows players to consciously evaluate their comfort levels rather than relying on automatic, polite compliance. The 'Boundary Check!' mechanic destigmatizes pausing a scene, teaching players that boundary negotiation is not a narrative failure, but a collaborative tool that deepens trust and artistic vulnerability.
