# I Need A...

![I Need A... — infographic](img/D1_P4_S1_T2_G734__i-need-a.jpg){ .game-hero }

> A rapid-fire passing game where your last need becomes the next player's first inspiration.

## Overview
Players stand in a circle and cross the space to deliver a spontaneous list of three 'needs.' The recipient must immediately use the final need of that list as the starting point for their own three-part list, passing the energy to a new player. It is a fast-paced, high-energy warm-up designed to bypass the inner editor and celebrate immediate, unfiltered impulses.

## What It Trains
- **Domain:** D1 — The Self
- **Principle(s):** The First Thought Is a Gift; Fail Joyfully; Yes, And
- **Skill(s):** Unfiltered Spontaneity; Active Listening; Offer Reception
- **Technique(s):** First Thought drills; Last Word Response
- **Focus:** skill_drill

**Objective:** To develop unfiltered spontaneity and rapid offer reception by forcing players to accept and build upon the very first thought that enters their mind without hesitation or self-censorship.

## Setup
Have all players stand in a circle with clear space in the center to walk across. No props or materials are required.

## How to Play
1. Form a standing circle with all participants.
2. Player A initiates the game by walking across the circle toward another player (Player B).
3. While walking, Player A must state three things they 'need' in the format: 'I need [X], I need [Y], and I need [Z].' These should be the absolute first things that pop into their head.
4. Player A arrives at Player B's spot, and Player B immediately begins walking toward a new player (Player C).
5. As Player B walks, they must start their list of three needs using Player A's third need ([Z]) as their first need: 'I need [Z], I need [A], and I need [B].'
6. This pattern continues with each receiving player immediately converting the previous player's final item into their own first item as they cross the circle.
7. Keep the physical movement and verbal delivery continuous, aiming for zero lag time between one person finishing and the next person starting their walk.

## Facilitation Notes
- Side-coaching cue: 'Walk before you think! Let your feet start moving before your mouth knows what it is going to say.'
- Side-coaching cue: 'Don't search for the perfect or funny third item. The boring, obvious, or weird choice is exactly what we want.'
- Pitfall: Players standing frozen at the edge of the circle trying to plan their three items before they walk. Fix: Encourage them to physically step out into the circle the instant their name/item is delivered; the physical movement helps unlock vocal spontaneity.
- Pitfall: Hesitation or apologizing when a 'weird' or nonsensical word comes out. Fix: Celebrate the weirdness! Remind the group to 'fail joyfully' and keep the momentum going without pausing to correct themselves.

## Variations
- Multi-Pass: Introduce a second or third active traveler into the circle simultaneously once the group has mastered the single-thread rhythm, requiring high spatial awareness.
- Emotional Needs: Instead of physical objects or random concepts, players must express emotional or abstract needs (e.g., 'I need validation, I need a quiet moment, I need chaos').
- Speed Run: Challenge the group to complete a set number of passes within a strict time limit, forcing them to prioritize speed over logical sense.

## Debrief
- How did it feel to have your first word chosen for you by the previous player?
- What happened to your brain when you tried to plan your three items in advance versus when you just let them fly?
- How does physical movement (walking across the circle) affect your ability to speak without filtering?

## Safety & Inclusion
Ensure the walking path is clear of obstacles. For players with mobility challenges, the physical crossing can be replaced with a strong, direct physical gesture (like a point or a throw) or players can play seated, passing the focus dynamically with eye contact.

## Why It Works
By linking physical movement with rapid-fire verbal listing, the game disrupts the cognitive 'editor' that usually filters our thoughts for social acceptability or cleverness. Using the last player's final item as the next player's first item enforces deep active listening and immediate 'yes-and' acceptance, proving that the first thought is always a usable gift.
