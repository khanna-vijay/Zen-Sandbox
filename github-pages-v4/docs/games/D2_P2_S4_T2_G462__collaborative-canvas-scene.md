# Collaborative Canvas

![Collaborative Canvas — infographic](img/D2_P2_S4_T2_G462__collaborative-canvas-scene.jpg){ .game-hero }

> Weave verbal storytelling and real-time digital sketching into a single, shared physical reality.

## Overview
In this virtual-native game, two actors improvise a scene while a third player acts as a live visual illustrator on a shared digital whiteboard. As the illustrator sketches props, environments, and abstract feelings, the actors must instantly treat these drawings as physical, tangible elements of their environment. The result is a highly collaborative, multi-sensory narrative where words and drawings continuously shape one another.

## What It Trains
- **Domain:** D2 — The Partner
- **Principle(s):** Yes, And; Make Your Partner a Genius; Show, Don't Tell; Base Reality First; Group Mind
- **Skill(s):** Active Listening; Offer Reception; Active Gifting; World-Building; Support Work
- **Technique(s):** Endowment-acceptance; Endowment-gifting drills; C.R.O.W. (Character, Relationship, Objective, Where); Playing architecture/objects
- **Focus:** mixed

**Objective:** To master the art of endowment-acceptance by treating visual, non-verbal inputs as immediate physical realities, sharpening active listening and support work.

## Setup
Conducted via a video conferencing platform with screen-sharing and annotation tools enabled for all participants. The facilitator ensures all players know how to access the drawing, stamp, and text tools. The active illustrator shares a blank digital whiteboard, while all other players set their view to side-by-side mode so they can see both the whiteboard and the actors' video feeds clearly.

## How to Play
1. Assign roles for the round: two active Scene Players, one Visual Illustrator, and the remaining participants as active observers.
2. The facilitator provides a simple relationship or location prompt to kick off the scene (e.g., 'two scientists opening a mysterious vault').
3. The Visual Illustrator immediately shares their screen with a blank digital whiteboard and prepares their annotation tools.
4. The Scene Players begin improvising their scene verbally, establishing their characters and the base reality.
5. The Visual Illustrator listens closely and begins sketching elements mentioned by the players, or introducing new visual offers (like a sudden storm cloud, a strange key, or a physical barrier) using simple shapes, stamps, or lines.
6. The Scene Players must immediately 'Yes, And' the illustrator's drawings by physically and verbally interacting with them as if they are real, three-dimensional objects in their space.
7. If the illustrator draws an abstract or ambiguous shape, the Scene Players must instantly endow it with meaning (e.g., declaring a squiggly line to be a dangerous laser beam or a snake) and react accordingly.
8. After 2 to 3 minutes of play, the facilitator calls 'freeze' and rotates roles so new players can experience both the acting and illustrating dynamics.

## Facilitation Notes
- Coaching Cue: 'Don't just name it, use it!' Remind actors to physically interact with the drawings—mime leaning on a drawn table, shivering under a drawn cloud, or picking up a drawn tool.
- Pitfall: The illustrator tries to create a masterpiece, slowing down the scene. Fix: Coach the illustrator to use simple stick figures, basic geometric shapes, and stamps. Speed and clarity of the offer trump artistic beauty.
- Coaching Cue: 'Endow the abstract!' If the drawing is unclear, the first actor to speak gets to define what it is. Embrace the ambiguity as a gift.
- Pitfall: Actors ignore the drawings and focus only on their verbal dialogue. Fix: Side-coach the actors to pause their verbal track and let the visual drawing dictate their next physical action.

## Variations
- The Vanishing Canvas: The illustrator uses a 'vanishing pen' tool (if available) so drawings disappear after a few seconds, forcing players to react quickly before the object fades from memory.
- The Silent Illustrator: The illustrator is not allowed to draw anything the actors say; they can only introduce completely new, unprompted visual obstacles or gifts that the actors must justify.
- The Disembodied Hand: The illustrator is treated as an in-universe character—a giant, invisible deity or wizard drawing things into existence that the characters can actually see happening in real-time.

## Debrief
- How did it feel to have your physical environment constantly altered by an external force?
- What strategies did you use to quickly endow ambiguous drawings with meaning without stalling the scene?
- As the illustrator, how did you balance supporting the actors' words versus leading them with your own visual offers?

## Safety & Inclusion
Ensure that players with limited fine motor skills or those using trackpads are encouraged to use simple stamp tools, text boxes, or pre-made shapes rather than freehand drawing. Alternatively, they can play as Scene Players or verbally dictate visual changes as a 'Scenic Narrator' if drawing tools are inaccessible.

## Why It Works
This game forces players to move beyond purely verbal 'Yes, And' by introducing a visual medium that cannot be ignored. By requiring actors to physically adapt to and endow the illustrator's drawings, it builds deep support work and reinforces the principle of making your partner look like a genius. It breaks players out of 'talking heads' syndrome by demanding immediate physical justification of their digital environment.
