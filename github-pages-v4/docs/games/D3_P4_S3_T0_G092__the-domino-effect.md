# The Domino Effect

![The Domino Effect — infographic](img/D3_P4_S3_T0_G092__the-domino-effect.jpg){ .game-hero }

> Build a tight, escalating narrative arc through a structured chain of cause and logical consequence.

## Overview
A structured, turn-based narrative game designed for virtual play where two active players build a scene's story arc. One player relentlessly pursues a core character desire, while the other introduces escalating, logical consequences to every action taken. The result is a highly disciplined exploration of cause-and-effect storytelling that culminates in a facilitator-prompted climax and resolution.

## What It Trains
- **Domain:** D3 — The Scene
- **Principle(s):** Serve the Story; Yes, And; Make Your Partner a Genius
- **Skill(s):** Narrative Architecture; Stakes / The 'Want'; Justification; Raising the Stakes; Active Listening; Offer Reception
- **Technique(s):** If this is true, what else is true?; Stakes-escalation reps; C.R.O.W. (Character, Relationship, Objective, Where)
- **Focus:** narrative

**Objective:** To develop narrative architecture and the ability to raise stakes logically using the 'if true, what else is true' principle, ensuring every story beat is a direct consequence of the last.

## Setup
Conducted in a virtual meeting space. All players keep their cameras off except for the two active players and the facilitator. No physical props or materials are required.

## How to Play
1. The facilitator selects two active players (Player A and Player B) to turn on their cameras, while the rest of the group remains off-camera as the active audience.
2. The facilitator establishes a simple platform (Who and Where) and asks Player A to declare a clear, actionable 'Strong Want' (e.g., 'I want to get my neighbor to return my lawnmower').
3. Player B initiates the scene by introducing an immediate obstacle that directly blocks Player A's want (e.g., 'I painted it gold and it is now the centerpiece of my garden').
4. Player A must react to this obstacle, taking an action or making an emotional statement that continues to pursue their original want.
5. Player B immediately delivers a logical, escalating consequence of Player A's reaction, raising the stakes and complicating the situation without resolving it (e.g., 'Since you yelled, I'm calling the neighborhood association about your unkept lawn').
6. The players continue to alternate: Player A reacts to the new reality while holding onto their want, and Player B introduces a new, escalating consequence based on 'if this is true, what else is true?'
7. The facilitator monitors the tension; when the stakes reach a boiling point, the facilitator calls out 'Climax!' prompting the players to immediately confront the peak of their conflict.
8. After the climax peaks, the facilitator calls out 'Resolution!' prompting the players to play out the final, logical fallout of their actions to close the scene.

## Facilitation Notes
- Side-coaching cue: 'If this is true, what else is true?' Use this to keep consequences grounded in the established reality rather than introducing random, wacky elements.
- Pitfall: Players trying to resolve the conflict too early. Fix: Remind Player B that their job is to complicate, not solve, and remind Player A to remain stubborn in their pursuit of the want.
- Virtual Tip: Ensure players wait for the other to finish speaking completely before responding to prevent audio clipping, treating the turn-based structure as a deliberate pause-and-play mechanic.
- Facilitator Timing: Do not let the escalation go on indefinitely. Call 'Climax!' when the emotional stakes are at their highest and a major confrontation is inevitable.

## Variations
- Multi-Scene Chain: Run this across three short scenes. The consequence of Scene 1 becomes the starting platform and 'want' for Scene 2, played by two different players.
- Status Swap: Player A starts with high status and Player B with low status. With each escalating consequence, the status dynamic must visibly shift or invert.

## Debrief
- How did having a strict turn-based structure change how you listened to your partner's offers?
- What is the difference between a random complication and a logical consequence?
- How did holding onto a strong, unchanging 'want' help drive the narrative forward?

## Safety & Inclusion
Since this game relies on escalating tension and conflict, players should establish boundaries during the setup. If the escalation begins to touch on sensitive personal themes, players can use a pre-agreed virtual stop signal (like holding a hand to the camera) to reset the stakes.

## Why It Works
By separating the narrative into distinct turns of 'action/reaction' and 'consequence,' the game strips away the panic of inventing plot. It forces players to practice the core improv principle of 'Yes, And' through strict cause-and-effect, proving that compelling stories are built by looking backward at what was just established and heightening its logical implications.
