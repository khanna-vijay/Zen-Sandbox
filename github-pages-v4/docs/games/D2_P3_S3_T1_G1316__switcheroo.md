# Role Swap

![Role Swap — infographic](img/D2_P3_S3_T1_G1316__switcheroo.jpg){ .game-hero }

> Players instantly trade characters mid-scene, matching their partner's physical, emotional, and narrative choices.

## Overview
In this dynamic scene-continuation game, a small group of players initiates a grounded scene. At the facilitator's signal, players instantly freeze and rotate roles, stepping directly into the physical space, posture, and mindset of their partner's character. The scene then continues seamlessly, demanding high-fidelity observation and deep empathy.

## What It Trains
- **Domain:** D2 — The Partner
- **Principle(s):** Make Your Partner a Genius; Yes, And; Base Reality First; Group Mind
- **Skill(s):** Active Listening; Single-Partner Empathy & Mirroring; World-Building; Peripheral Awareness
- **Technique(s):** Mirror exercise; C.R.O.W. (Character, Relationship, Objective, Where); Thread-tracking drills
- **Focus:** mixed

**Objective:** Develops active listening, physical mirroring, and single-partner empathy by forcing players to deeply observe and instantly adopt their partner's physical, vocal, and emotional choices.

## Setup
Three to five players stand in the performance space. Establish a clear rotation order (e.g., Player A becomes Player B, B becomes C, C becomes A) before beginning. No props or chairs are required.

## How to Play
1. Begin a standard, character-driven scene with three players based on a simple location or relationship suggestion.
2. Instruct players to establish clear, distinct physicalities, vocal patterns, and emotional viewpoints for their characters.
3. At a random interval, the facilitator calls out 'Swap!' or claps to pause the action.
4. Upon the signal, players must instantly freeze in place.
5. Players then physically move to the spatial position of the character they are inheriting, adopting that character's exact posture and facial expression.
6. Resume the scene immediately; players must now speak and act as their new character, continuing the exact conversation and physical tasks in progress.
7. The facilitator calls 'Swap!' multiple times throughout the scene, ensuring every player has the opportunity to inhabit each character.

## Facilitation Notes
- Side-coach players to focus on physical mirroring first: 'Adopt their spine and facial expression before you speak.'
- Watch out for players dropping the established character choices to play their own version; remind them to honor their partner's exact emotional state.
- If players get confused about who they are playing, pause briefly, have them point to their target character, and then resume.
- Encourage players to 'yes-and' the narrative history of the character they inherit, maintaining their relationships and secrets.

## Variations
- Emotional Escalation: Each time a swap occurs, the incoming player must increase the character's core emotion by ten percent.
- Object Work Hand-off: Players must precisely take over any mimed objects or environmental elements the previous actor was using.
- Blind Swap: Instead of a predetermined rotation, the facilitator calls out specific name pairings to swap roles on the fly.

## Debrief
- What physical or vocal details did you find easiest to mirror, and which were the hardest?
- How did it feel to see another player step into the character you created? What did they highlight that you didn't realize you were doing?
- How does this game help us practice making our partners look like geniuses?

## Safety & Inclusion
Ensure players are mindful of physical boundaries and mobility levels; if a player has physical limitations, partners should mirror their specific range of motion and comfort level rather than attempting high-impact physical replication.

## Why It Works
By physically and vocally stepping into a partner's exact posture and perspective, players bypass intellectual planning and tap into intuitive empathy. This direct mirroring builds a shared group mind and reinforces a cohesive base reality, proving that character is an externalized physical and emotional state.
