# Typewriter

![Typewriter — infographic](img/D3_P4_S3_T1_G878__typewriter.jpg){ .game-hero }

> Co-create a live-action story where a writer's narration and actors' physical choices instantly collide.

## Overview
One player acts as a novelist typing a story on an imaginary typewriter, narrating the plot, setting, and character details aloud. The remaining players stand in the performance space, instantly physicalizing the narration, speaking dialogue, and making bold physical offers that the writer must then justify and incorporate into the unfolding tale.

## What It Trains
- **Domain:** D3 — The Scene
- **Principle(s):** Yes, And; Make Your Partner a Genius; Serve the Story; Group Mind
- **Skill(s):** Offer Reception; Active Gifting; Narrative Architecture; World-Building; Justification; Support Work
- **Technique(s):** Endowment-acceptance; Endowment-gifting drills; Story Spine; Platform/Tilt; C.R.O.W. (Character, Relationship, Objective, Where); Justify the absurd
- **Focus:** narrative

**Objective:** To develop narrative architecture, active listening, and justification skills by balancing narrative control between a narrator and performers using the Story Spine framework.

## Setup
One player (the Narrator) sits or stands at a side stage position with an imaginary typewriter or keyboard. The remaining players (the Actors) stand in the main performance area ready to enter. Obtain a suggestion from the audience for a book title or an unusual object to inspire the story.

## How to Play
1. The Narrator begins the game by miming typing and speaking the opening lines of the story, establishing the protagonist, setting, and initial status quo.
2. As soon as the Narrator introduces a character or an action, the Actors immediately step into the scene to embody those characters and physicalize the description.
3. The Narrator pauses typing to let the Actors play out the scene, allowing them to speak dialogue, establish relationships, and make physical offers.
4. At any point, the Narrator can resume typing to advance the plot, transition to a new location, introduce a twist, or narrate a character's internal thoughts.
5. Actors must instantly accept and physicalize whatever the Narrator writes, treating the narration as absolute truth.
6. The Narrator must actively watch the Actors and use their typing to justify any unexpected physical choices or dialogue the Actors introduce.
7. The story should follow a clear narrative arc (using the Story Spine: Once upon a time, every day, until one day, because of that, until finally) leading to a satisfying climax and resolution.
8. The game concludes when the Narrator types the final sentence of the story and says, 'The End.'

## Facilitation Notes
- Coaching Cue: 'Narrator, don't write over the actors. Give them space to speak, discover, and play before you type the next beat.'
- Coaching Cue: 'Actors, make bold physical choices! Give the writer interesting, unexpected actions that they have to justify.'
- Pitfall: The Narrator micro-manages every single movement of the actors, leaving them no room to make original offers. Fix: Remind the Narrator that typing is an editing tool; use it to transition scenes or introduce obstacles, not to dictate hand gestures.
- Pitfall: The Actors ignore the Narrator's established reality or contradict the genre. Fix: Side-coach the actors to 'Yes, And' the narration immediately with their bodies and voices.

## Variations
- Genre Study: The audience suggests a specific literary genre (e.g., Gothic Horror, Hardboiled Detective, Sci-Fi Epic) that both the writer and actors must strictly emulate.
- The Editor's Cut: A separate player acts as an Editor who can shout 'Rewrite!' at any point, forcing the Narrator to change their last typed sentence and the actors to instantly pivot their action.
- Word Count Constraint: The Narrator is only allowed to type short, punchy sentences of five words or fewer, forcing rapid-fire handoffs between narration and acting.

## Debrief
- How did it feel to share control of the narrative between the writer and the performers?
- What strategies did the Narrator use to successfully justify the unexpected physical choices made by the actors?
- How did the Story Spine structure help keep the narrative on track despite having multiple creators?

## Safety & Inclusion
Ensure the performance space is clear of physical hazards, as actors may need to react quickly to sudden narrative changes (e.g., 'Suddenly, the floor turned to hot lava'). Encourage actors to establish physical boundaries before the scene starts, especially regarding physical contact dictated by the narrator.

## Why It Works
This game functions as a perfect narrative engine because it forces a high level of mutual support. The narrator cannot succeed without watching the actors, and the actors cannot succeed without listening to the narrator. This mutual dependence builds group mind and teaches players how to serve the story over their individual comedic agendas.
