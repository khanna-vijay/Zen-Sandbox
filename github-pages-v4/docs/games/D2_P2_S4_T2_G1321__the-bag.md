# The Character Bag

![The Character Bag — infographic](img/D2_P2_S4_T2_G1321__the-bag.jpg){ .game-hero }

> Discover a rich character by unpacking imaginary items and answering curious questions.

## Overview
One player sits in the hot seat and pulls random, imaginary items from a mimed bag, naming each one instantly. The remaining players act as curious interviewers, asking questions about the items to help the main player discover and embody a unique character. Through collaborative endowment and physical adjustments, a fully realized persona emerges from these random objects.

## What It Trains
- **Domain:** D2 — The Partner
- **Principle(s):** Yes, And; The First Thought Is a Gift; Base Reality First
- **Skill(s):** Offer Reception; Unfiltered Spontaneity; Physicality & Space Work; Active Gifting; World-Building
- **Technique(s):** Endowment-acceptance; First Thought drills; Character Walks/Centers; Endowment-gifting drills; C.R.O.W. (Character, Relationship, Objective, Where)
- **Focus:** skill_drill

**Objective:** To develop active offer reception and endowment-acceptance, allowing external questions and random physical objects to organically shape a character's voice, posture, and point of view.

## Setup
Place one chair at the front of the space for the main player. The other 2 to 5 players sit in a semi-circle facing the chair. No physical props are needed; the bag and all its contents are entirely imaginary.

## How to Play
1. One player volunteers to sit in the hot seat with an imaginary, large bag placed on the floor or in their lap.
2. The hot-seat player mimes reaching into the bag, pulling out a single object, holding it clearly in space, and naming it immediately without overthinking.
3. The surrounding players begin asking open-ended questions about the object, such as its origin, its emotional significance, or how the player acquired it.
4. The hot-seat player answers these questions honestly, treating every question as a gift and accepting any implied details (endowments) about their life or status.
5. As the hot-seat player answers, they should let the details influence their physical posture, vocal tone, and emotional attitude.
6. After a minute of questioning, the facilitator or the player prompts the extraction of a second, completely unrelated object from the bag.
7. The interviewers ask new questions, connecting the new object to the established details or exploring new facets of the character.
8. The round concludes once the hot-seat player has fully stepped into the physical and vocal life of a distinct, cohesive character.

## Facilitation Notes
- Encourage interviewers to ask curious, open-ended questions rather than highly leading or accusatory ones (e.g., 'Why do you have a golden key?' instead of 'Did you steal that key from your boss?').
- Remind the hot-seat player to let the physical weight and texture of the mimed objects influence their body language immediately.
- If the hot-seat player gets stuck trying to be clever, coach them to say the very first mundane object that pops into their head—the magic comes from the justification, not the object itself.
- Watch for physical shifts: prompt the player to lean into any accidental vocal changes or physical tics that emerge during the Q&A.

## Variations
- Emotional Baggage: The bag contains emotional states or secrets instead of physical objects, which the player must reveal and justify.
- The Shared Satchel: Two players sit in hot seats, sharing a single bag, and must discover their relationship and shared history through the items they pull out.
- Occupational Bag: The interviewers specify a broad setting (e.g., 'a spaceship' or 'a Victorian manor') and the items pulled must build a character specific to that world.

## Debrief
- How did answering questions about random objects make it easier to find a character's voice and posture?
- For the interviewers, how did you balance supporting the player versus challenging them with your questions?
- What was the turning point where the character felt 'real' rather than just a list of facts?

## Safety & Inclusion
Ensure players feel safe to decline answering any questions that touch upon personal real-world trauma. Interviewers should keep the tone of curiosity playful and supportive rather than interrogative or hostile.

## Why It Works
This game works because it bypasses the analytical mind's desire to plan a character. By forcing the player to accept random physical offers (the objects) and external narrative offers (the questions), it builds a character from the outside-in. The principle of 'Yes, And' is applied directly to character creation, proving that any random combination of details can be justified into a coherent human story.
