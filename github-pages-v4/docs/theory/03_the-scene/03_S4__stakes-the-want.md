# 🧠 Stakes / The 'Want'

> *WHY we care — distinct from CROW's 'O' setup move and from 'Raising the Stakes' technique*

![Square infographic explaining the improv skill “Stakes / The 'Want'”](../../assets/infographics/D3.S4.jpg){ .infographic }

## 🧠 What it is

At its core, the paired skill of **Stakes** (what a character stands to lose) and the **"Want"** (what a character is actively trying to achieve) is the real-time calibration of consequence. As an improviser, you are constantly making a crucial judgment: *Why does this specific moment matter to this specific person?* 

It is the active decision to move beyond merely existing in a space or performing an activity, and instead inject a driving emotional motivation. You are deciding how deeply your character is invested in the outcome of the scene. This skill transforms a polite, meandering conversation into a compelling interaction because it gives the scene an engine—the audience leans in the exact moment they realize something of value is on the line.

This judgment is genuinely difficult because it requires vulnerability in a highly unpredictable environment. When improvisers feel lost or panicked, their natural defense mechanism is often apathy—playing characters who are cool, detached, or unaffected by the chaos around them. It feels safer not to care. Furthermore, the cognitive load of establishing the basic facts of a scene (the *who, what, and where*) often leaves the character's internal drive as an afterthought. Deciding to genuinely care about a fabricated, often absurd situation requires you to drop your armor, commit emotionally to a reality you just invented, and risk your character "losing" in front of an audience.

!!! abstract "The Core Judgment"
    You are not just deciding *what* your character is doing (the basic setup), nor are you just making things crazier (the technique of "raising the stakes"). You are deciding **how much it hurts to fail** and **how badly you need to win**, right here, right now.

## 👀 The litmus test

The true test of whether an improviser has internalized Stakes and the "Want" is simple: **If a director yells "Freeze!" halfway through a scene, can the actor instantly articulate what their character stands to lose?** More importantly, does the *audience* already feel it?

It is tempting to treat stakes as a mere technique—a line of dialogue you inject to make a scene feel important (e.g., "If I don't defuse this bomb, the city explodes!"). But establishing stakes is fundamentally a **Skill**. It is the real-time, contextual judgment of *why* the current moment matters to the people in it, and calibrating your reactions accordingly. A technique is a mechanical tool you deploy; a skill is the brain deciding *which* tool to use, *when*, and *how much*.

Under the live pressure of a scene, an improviser must constantly weigh their character's desires against their partner's offers. If your partner suddenly reveals they are breaking up with you, the skill of Stakes dictates that your character's "Want" must immediately react to this new threat. You don't just keep playing the activity of sweeping the floor; the broom becomes a battleground for your dignity, your relationship, or your fear of being alone. 

!!! abstract "The Litmus Test"
    **Does the "Want" survive contact with the unexpected?**
    An improviser lacking this skill will drop their objective the moment a new distraction or joke appears. An improviser wielding this skill lets their underlying desire filter every reaction, line, and physical movement, no matter what curveballs are thrown.

!!! example "In a scene: Technique vs. Skill"
    *   **Technique (The mechanical move):** "I really want to win this spelling bee, Dad." The improviser states a want, but then spends the next three minutes making unrelated, detached jokes about the microphone.
    *   **Skill (The contextual judgment):** The improviser never explicitly says they want to win. Instead, they grip their numbered placard so tightly their knuckles turn white, they refuse to break eye contact with the judge, and they flinch every time their dad sighs in the audience. The *want* drives the behavior under pressure.

## ⚙️ How it works in a scene

The skill of managing Stakes and the "Want" operates as a continuous, subconscious loop during a scene. It is the cognitive engine that turns a polite conversation into compelling theater. You aren't just reacting to what your partner says; you are evaluating every piece of new information against what your character desperately needs.

Here is the moment-to-moment cognitive loop in action:

**1. Perceive: Locate the emotional core**  
As the scene begins, you scan for the **Want**. This might be a concrete objective established early on (e.g., "I want to buy this car"), but the skill lies in perceiving the *emotional* want beneath it. You listen to your partner's tone and your own spontaneous reactions to ask: *Why do I care about this right now?* (e.g., "I want this car to prove to my dad I'm successful.")

**2. Decide: Weigh the consequence**  
Once the Want is identified, your brain calculates the **Stakes**. What happens if I fail? If the answer is "nothing," the scene will feel flat. You must decide to care. You mentally attach a cost to failure—loss of status, a broken heart, public embarrassment, or a missed opportunity. This internal decision dictates the emotional weight of your next move.

**3. Act: Deploy a tactic**  
You filter your next line or physical action through the pressure of those stakes. You don't just talk about the car; you deploy tactics to get it. You might flatter the salesman, threaten to leave, or beg. If a tactic fails, the stakes demand that you try a new one, rather than giving up or dropping the subject.

!!! example "In a scene"
    **Without Stakes:** Two roommates argue about doing the dishes. They trade logical points about whose turn it is. It is a conflict, but it lacks a pulse.  
    **With Stakes:** Roommate A wants the dishes done (the Want) because they are terrified Roommate B is going to move out (the Stakes). Now, A isn't just arguing about soap; A is pleading for the survival of the friendship. Every wipe of the sponge is loaded with desperation.

!!! tip "On stage"
    **Anchor your Want in your partner.** It is much easier to maintain stakes when what you need can *only* be given by the person standing across from you (e.g., their approval, their forgiveness, their submission). If your Want is entirely external, your partner becomes an obstacle rather than a participant.

## 📈 The maturity arc

Mastering stakes is the journey from merely *doing things* on stage to *caring deeply* about what happens next. As an improviser develops this skill, the "Want" moves from a mechanical requirement to an invisible, driving emotional force.

| Stage | The "Want" / Stakes | Observable Behavior in a Scene |
|---|---|---|
| **1 Novice** | Plays activities with no reason to care | Two improvisers fold laundry or argue about a parking space, but neither character is emotionally affected by the outcome. If they lose the argument, they just shrug. It is action without consequence. |
| **2 Adv. Beginner** | States a "Want" when reminded | The improviser explicitly announces their goal ("I really want to win this baking contest!"), but their physical and emotional choices don't reflect that urgency. The want is a line of dialogue, not a lived reality. |
| **3 Competent** | Establishes what's at risk for the character | The improviser actively defines the consequence of failure ("If I don't win this baking contest, the bank takes the family bakery"). The scene now has a clear, logical reason for the characters to keep fighting. |
| **4 Proficient** | Stakes are felt, not stated; they fuel the scene | The improviser stops talking about the stakes and starts *embodying* them. They don't need to say they are desperate; their frantic energy, hyper-focus, and the aggressive way they whisk the eggs show the audience exactly how much they care. |
| **5 Master** | Makes the audience genuinely care about absurd people | The improviser fully commits to the emotional reality of the scene, no matter how ridiculous the premise. Through sheer, grounded emotional investment, the audience feels actual, breathless tension over whether a sentient toaster will find true love. |

!!! abstract "The Turning Point"
    The biggest leap in this progression happens between **Competent** and **Proficient**. 
    
    A Competent improviser treats stakes as a piece of *information* to be communicated to the audience (the "what"). A Proficient improviser treats stakes as an *emotional filter* that colors every word, movement, and reaction in the scene (the "how"). You know you are leveling up when you stop explaining why you care, and simply start caring.

## 🧰 The techniques that train it

Because Stakes and the "Want" live in the brain as a contextual judgment—knowing *why* a character cares and *how much* they care—they must be trained through targeted repetition. You cannot simply tell an improviser to "care more." Instead, we use specific techniques to build the reflex of attaching consequence and deep desire to otherwise mundane scene work.

Here are the primary techniques used to train this skill:

### 1. "What do they stand to lose?" Reps
This technique trains the diagnostic reflex. In early development, improvisers often confuse *activity* (what I am doing) with *stakes* (why it matters). By running rapid-fire scene starts focused entirely on identifying the risk, improvisers learn to spot the absence of consequence.

*   **How it works:** Two improvisers initiate a scene. After three lines, the coach pauses the action and asks the players (or the backline), *"What does Character A stand to lose right now?"* If the answer is "nothing," the players must immediately add a line of dialogue that introduces a consequence, then restart.
*   **How it builds the skill:** It forces the brain to constantly scan for vulnerability. It teaches the improviser that a scene about folding laundry is dead on arrival, but a scene about folding laundry *because your hyper-critical mother-in-law is pulling into the driveway* has immediate, playable stakes.

!!! tip "In rehearsal: The 'Or Else' drill"
    A great way to drill this is to force players to append an unspoken "or else..." to their internal monologue. 
    
    *   "I need to fix this tire... *or else we miss the wedding.*"
    *   "I want you to look at me... *or else I'll know this marriage is over.*"
    
    Practicing this explicitly eventually makes the reflex implicit.

### 2. Objective-Laddering
A character's **objective** (their "Want") rarely exists on a single level. Objective-laddering is the technique of scaling a character's desire up or down by asking *"Why?"* to connect a superficial, physical want to a deep, emotional need. 

*   **How it works:** You take a stated, surface-level want and "ladder up" to the core human desire driving it. 
*   **How it builds the skill:** It trains the improviser's judgment of *how much* stakes to apply. It teaches them that they don't need to invent a bomb under the table to make a scene matter; they just need to connect the current situation to the character's ego, heart, or identity.

!!! example "Laddering in action"
    Notice how the stakes deepen as the objective climbs the ladder from tactical to emotional:
    
    1. **Surface Want:** "I want this specific parking spot." *(Low stakes, easily resolved)*
    2. **Tactical Want:** "I want to be on time for this meeting." *(Higher stakes, introduces a deadline)*
    3. **Relational Want:** "I want my boss to finally notice my dedication." *(High stakes, introduces interpersonal risk)*
    4. **Core Emotional Need:** "I want to feel like I am enough." *(Maximum stakes, fuels the entire character arc)*

By practicing these techniques, the improviser's brain learns to automatically anchor their characters to a meaningful "Want" the moment they step on stage, ensuring the audience always has a reason to lean in.

## 🔀 Dialing it up vs. down

Stakes are not a volume knob that you simply crank to maximum and snap off. While a scene without a "Want" is lifeless, a scene with stakes that are too high, too fast, becomes paralyzed. Mastering this skill means knowing exactly how much pressure to apply to the characters.

**When to dial it UP (Lean in)**

*   **The scene is polite but meandering:** If characters are casually discussing the weather or agreeing on everything, inject a Want. Make one character desperately need validation, a favor, or the last donut. 
*   **The characters are "too cool":** Apathy is the enemy of improv. If players are detached or sarcastic, dialing up the stakes forces them to care, making them vulnerable and instantly more interesting to watch.
*   **The scene is stuck in "talking heads" mode:** When players are philosophizing rather than doing, a strong, immediate objective forces action and physicalizes the scene.

!!! tip "On stage"
    If you feel lost in the middle of a scene and don't know what to do next, ask yourself: *"What do I want from my partner right now?"* Pick something small and pursue it relentlessly. The scene will immediately find its footing.

**When to dial it DOWN (Exercise restraint)**

*   **The stakes are apocalyptic:** If the world is ending, or someone has a gun to their head, the scene often freezes. There is nowhere left to escalate. Pull back to *relational* stakes (e.g., "I know the meteor is hitting in ten minutes, but are you still mad I forgot your birthday?").
*   **The scene has devolved into a screaming match:** Volume and anger are often mistaken for high stakes. True stakes are about *loss*, not just conflict. If you are yelling, try dialing down the volume and dialing up the internal vulnerability.
*   **The comedic Game needs room to breathe:** In a heavily Game-driven scene, massive emotional stakes can sometimes distract from the central absurdity. 

!!! warning "Watch out: The 'Gun in the Scene' Problem"
    Introducing a literal weapon or a life-or-death threat in the first thirty seconds is a classic novice mistake. It creates massive *external* stakes but usually destroys the *internal* stakes. The scene becomes entirely about the gun, erasing the relationship and the nuance.

### Balancing with Neighboring Skills

Your judgment of Stakes must work in harmony with the other skills in the Scene domain:

*   **Balancing with Game Identification:** In a Game-driven scene, the stakes often live in the contrast. The "voice of reason" cares deeply about the rules of reality, while the absurd character cares deeply about their bizarre logic. If the external stakes get too high (e.g., a real medical emergency), the audience stops laughing at the Game and starts worrying about the plot. You must keep the stakes grounded enough that the absurdity remains funny.
*   **Balancing with Narrative Architecture:** In a Story-driven scene, stakes are the fuel for the narrative engine. They must escalate *proportionally* with the story arc. You cannot start at a 10; you must build the platform, establish a baseline Want, and then raise the stakes as the narrative progresses toward the climax.

## 🤝 How it serves the domain

The domain of **The Scene** is about architecting compelling moments in real time, seamlessly utilizing both the narrative and game engines. Mastering Stakes and the "Want" provides the structural integrity for that architecture. Without stakes, a scene is just two people talking in a room; with stakes, it becomes a compelling event that the audience leans into.

Good judgment of stakes serves the domain's ultimate goal by acting as the universal fuel for both improv engines:

*   **Fueling the Narrative Engine:** In a story-driven scene, the "Want" creates a vector for change. When the **Tilt** (the disruption of the normal routine) occurs, the stakes dictate how the character reacts. If a character has nothing to lose or gain, the story stalls because they can simply walk away. Stakes force the character to stay, struggle, and ultimately change.
*   **Grounding the Game Engine:** In a game-driven scene, stakes provide the necessary reality to anchor the absurdity. The comedic engine revs highest when characters care *deeply* about trivial, bizarre, or illogical things. The stakes justify the unusual behavior, preventing the scene from devolving into random, weightless chaos. 

!!! abstract "The ultimate filter"
    Understanding your character's stakes removes the burden of invention. When you know exactly what your character wants and what they stand to lose, you instantly know how to react to *any* offer your partner makes. You no longer have to think, "What is a funny response?" You only have to ask, "How does this affect my goal?"

By mastering this skill, improvisers learn to read what the scene needs. If a scene feels aimless, a skilled improviser will inject a clear "Want" to give it direction. If a game feels too silly or untethered, they will dial up the emotional stakes to ground it. 

!!! example "In a scene"
    Two coworkers are arguing over who gets to use the copy machine first.  
    **Without stakes:** It's a bickering match that goes in circles.  
    **With stakes:** One needs to copy a presentation that will save their job; the other needs to copy a lost dog flyer before the pound closes. Suddenly, the scene has architecture, urgency, and a reason for the audience to care.

## ⚠️ Common failure modes & how to grow

When improvisers struggle with Stakes and the "Want," it usually stems from a desire to protect themselves. Caring about something on stage requires vulnerability; it is much safer to be detached, to joke around, or to invent a chaotic distraction. 

Here are the most common ways this skill breaks down, followed by a roadmap for pushing through them.

### Common Failure Modes

!!! warning "Watch out: The Apathy Trap (Playing 'Too Cool')"
    This is the most common defense mechanism in improv. A player adopts a detached, sarcastic, or unfazed persona. If a monster attacks, they shrug. If their spouse leaves them, they say, "Whatever." 
    **Why it fails:** If the character doesn't care about what is happening, the audience has no reason to care either. Apathy kills momentum because it removes the possibility of consequence.

!!! warning "Watch out: The Artificial Emergency"
    Sensing that a scene lacks energy, a panicked player will suddenly introduce a bomb, a heart attack, or a hostage situation out of nowhere. 
    **Why it fails:** While similar to the "Gun in the Scene" problem, this specifically happens as a *rescue maneuver* when players feel lost. It forces external life-or-death stakes to compensate for a lack of internal emotional stakes, derailing the existing relationship and turning the scene into a frantic action movie where the characters' actual "wants" are abandoned.

!!! warning "Watch out: The Hollow Argument"
    Two characters yell at each other over a transactional detail—like the price of a toaster or whose turn it is to take out the trash. The volume is high, but the stakes are low.
    **Why it fails:** Bickering is a substitute for vulnerability. The players are fighting over the *object* rather than the *relationship*. The scene only moves forward when the players realize the argument isn't about the trash; it's about a deeper want, like the desire to feel respected.

!!! example "In a scene: Fixing the Hollow Argument"
    **Broken:** "I told you to take out the trash! You're so lazy!" "Well, you didn't wash the dishes!" *(This will loop endlessly).*  
    **Fixed:** "When you don't take out the trash, it makes me feel like you don't value my time. I just want you to see how hard I'm working for us." *(The 'want' is validation; the stakes are the health of the relationship).*

### How to Grow at Every Stage

Progressing in this skill means moving from a place where stakes are absent, to where they are mechanically stated, to where they are deeply and invisibly felt.

| Stage | Current Behavior | Your Next Step to Grow |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **1 Novice** | Plays activities with no reason to care. | **Pick one thing to care about.** If you are folding laundry, decide you *need* these shirts perfectly creased. Give your mundane action an emotional weight. |
| **2 Adv. Beginner** | States a "Want" when reminded. | **Make it relational.** Instead of just wanting a promotion, want the promotion *so your scene partner will finally respect you*. Tie your desire directly to the person standing in front of you. |
| **3 Competent** | Establishes what's at risk for the character. | **Let the stakes alter your behavior.** If you stand to lose something important, let that fear or desire physically change how you speak, move, and react in the moment. |
| **4 Proficient** | Stakes are felt, not stated; they fuel the scene. | **Layer the absurd with the profound.** Practice taking a completely ridiculous premise (e.g., you are a sentient jar of mayonnaise) and applying deeply grounded, human stakes to it (e.g., the fear of outliving your expiration date). |
| **5 Master** | Makes the audience genuinely care about absurd people. | **Play with the invisible.** Trust the silence. Allow the audience to feel the weight of what your character wants without ever having to point at it or explain it. |

!!! tip "On stage: The 'I Care' Check-in"
    If you ever feel emotionally disconnected in the middle of a scene, ask yourself internally: *"What does my character care about right now?"* If the answer is "nothing," immediately make a choice to care deeply about the very last thing your partner said or did.

## 📚 References & Further Reading

### Foundational sources
*   **Michael Shurtleff, *Audition* (1978)** — Though written for scripted acting, Shurtleff’s "12 Guideposts" are the bedrock of modern improv scene work. Guidepost 2 ("Conflict: What are you fighting for?") defines the "Want," while Guidepost 8 ("Importance") explicitly demands that actors raise the stakes to make the scene matter, warning that without importance, a scene devolves into boring, everyday reality.
*   **Constantin Stanislavski, *An Actor Prepares* (1936)** — The origin of the "Objective" (what a character wants in a scene) and the "Super-objective" (what they want in life). Because the cognitive load of improv is so high, improvisers adapt this into the real-time "Want" to ensure their characters have a driving motivation when the plot becomes chaotic.
*   **Keith Johnstone, *Impro: Improvisation and the Theatre* (1979)** — Johnstone’s extensive work on "Status" provides the physical and social mechanics for how characters fight for what they want. Status transactions are the observable behaviors of characters negotiating stakes and what they stand to lose in a relationship.
*   **Viola Spolin, *Improvisation for the Theater* (1963)** — Spolin’s concept of the "Point of Concentration" acts as the mechanical precursor to the "Want." By giving players a specific, immediate problem to solve on stage, she inherently creates stakes for the exercise, forcing the actor to care about the outcome.

### Practitioner guides & manuals
*   **Mick Napier, *Improvise: Scene from the Inside Out* (2004)** — Napier famously challenges the passive "wait and see" approach to improv. He argues that improvisers must "take care of themselves first" by immediately making a bold choice, establishing a want, and doing something they care about to leverage the "law of inertia" in a scene.
*   **TJ Jagodowski, David Pasquesi, and Pam Victor, *Improvisation at the Speed of Life: The TJ and Dave Book* (2015)** — A masterclass in emotional stakes. TJ and Dave argue that improv is about human nature, not just "the game," emphasizing that scenes thrive when characters genuinely care about each other and the relationship itself is what is on the line.
*   **Will Hines, *How to Be the Greatest Improviser on Earth* (2016)** — Hines introduces the "Know, Care, Say" framework. The "Care" pillar specifically addresses the necessity of emotional stakes, urging improvisers to drop their armor and find a reason to be genuinely, emotionally invested in the scene's fabricated reality.
*   **Matt Besser, Ian Roberts, and Matt Walsh, *The Upright Citizens Brigade Comedy Improvisation Manual* (2013)** — While heavily focused on comedic patterns, the UCB manual details how to establish a character's "deal" or perspective. This perspective serves as the engine for their wants, and the UCB maxim "If this is true, what else is true?" is the primary tool for logically heightening the stakes.

### Lineage & teachers
*   **The Annoyance Theatre (Chicago)** — Founded by Mick Napier, this theater's training center is the primary lineage for teaching improvisers to prioritize strong, selfish character choices and immediate emotional wants over polite, meandering agreement.
*   **Sanford Meisner (The Neighborhood Playhouse)** — Meisner's definition of acting as "living truthfully under imaginary circumstances" is the guiding philosophy for treating improvised stakes as real, demanding that actors react genuinely to fabricated consequences rather than playing them for detached laughs.

### Research & theory
*   **Philip Zarrilli, *Psychophysical Acting: An Intercultural Approach after Stanislavski* (2009)** — Explores the cognitive and physical connection between an actor's intention (the want) and their physiological response, explaining why stakes must be felt in the body (e.g., white knuckles, tense posture) rather than just spoken as a line of dialogue.
*   **R. Keith Sawyer, *Improvised Dialogues: Emergence and Creativity in Conversation* (2003)** — A psychological and linguistic analysis of how unscripted conversations develop, highlighting how participants continuously negotiate goals and status (stakes) in real time through collaborative emergence.

### Talks, videos & courses
*   **Jimmy Carrane, *Improv Nerd* (Podcast, 2011–Present)** — Carrane frequently interviews master improvisers about their process, consistently returning to the themes of vulnerability, emotional investment, and the courage required to play scenes with genuine stakes rather than bailing out for a cheap joke.

### Communities & adjacent reading
*   **Uta Hagen, *Respect for Acting* (1973)** — Hagen’s concept of "Obstacles" pairs perfectly with the "Want." She teaches that a character's desire is only as compelling as the forces (or stakes) standing in their way, a vital lesson for improvisers building conflict.
*   **David Mamet, *True and False: Heresy and Common Sense for the Actor* (1997)** — A provocative take on acting that strips away psychological overthinking in favor of raw, immediate objectives—highly applicable to the fast-paced, action-oriented nature of improv where the "Want" must survive contact with the unexpected.

### Where it comes from
The concept of the "Want" (or "Objective") is foundational to modern Western acting, popularized by Konstantin Stanislavski in the early 20th century and later refined by American practitioners like Uta Hagen. In the realm of improv, Viola Spolin adapted this into the "Point of Concentration" (POC)—a specific problem or goal the players must solve, which forces them out of their heads and into active pursuit. Later, long-form pioneers like Del Close and Mick Napier emphasized bringing these genuine theatrical stakes into unscripted comedy, pushing improvisers to care deeply about the fabricated reality rather than just making detached jokes.

### A telling example
**The "Voice of Reason" with actual stakes**  
Improv teacher and author Will Hines often illustrates how stakes transform a scene by looking at the "Voice of Reason" (the straight man). If a character is told they must present a pitch to the heads of a major company, a low-stakes improviser might just shrug and say, "I don't care, I'm going to sing an Oasis song." It is a wacky choice, but it lacks a pulse. 

However, when the improviser injects genuine stakes, the comedy and the drama instantly heighten: *"I will not sing 'Wonderwall'—we are presenting to the heads of Google! This could make our whole careers! My big brother is on the board and I'll be humiliated!"* The scene transforms from a detached, goofy premise into a desperate, high-stakes battle for professional survival. 

**Taking care of yourself first**  
In *Improvise: Scene from the Inside Out*, Mick Napier challenges the traditional improv dogma that you must constantly defer to your partner. He argues that the most supportive thing an improviser can do is to "selfishly" make a strong choice and take care of their own character's deal first. By immediately establishing what *they* want and how much they care, they give their partner a concrete, high-stakes reality to react to, rather than two improvisers politely waiting for the other to decide why the scene matters.
