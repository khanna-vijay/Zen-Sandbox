# 🧠 Raising the Stakes

> *The judgment of which technique to use, when, and how much.*

![Square infographic explaining the improv skill “Raising the Stakes”](../../assets/infographics/D3.S7.jpg){ .infographic }

## 🧠 What it is

At its core, **Raising the Stakes** is the real-time judgment of *how much a situation matters* to the characters, and deciding exactly when to turn that dial up. Because this skill serves the **Narrative** engine of improv, it is fundamentally about intensifying *consequences*. 

In the moment, the improviser is constantly evaluating the scene's emotional weight: *What does my character want? What happens if they don't get it? How can I make the current situation cost them more?* It is the deliberate choice to move beyond casual banter by anchoring the scene in a deeply felt need. You are deciding to make the characters care profoundly about the outcome, transforming a disposable interaction into a compelling event.

!!! abstract "The Core Distinction"
    While heightening a **Game** is about escalating a comedic *pattern*, raising the stakes in a **Narrative** is about escalating the *consequences*. It is the vital shift from asking "How can this get weirder?" to asking "How can this matter more?"

This judgment is genuinely difficult because our survival instincts on stage often lead us astray. When a scene feels flat, improvisers frequently panic and inject artificial, external stakes—pulling a mimed weapon, announcing a ticking bomb, or suddenly declaring a divorce. But true stakes are internal and relational. It is terrifyingly vulnerable to stand on stage and allow your character to genuinely care about something as mundane as a neighborhood bake sale or a borrowed sweater. It requires the discipline to stop inventing crazy new plot points and instead invest deeply in the reality you have already built. You must resist the urge to play for a cheap laugh, trusting instead that the audience will lean in when they see a character with something real to lose.

## 👀 The litmus test

The true test of whether an improviser has mastered this skill lies in their ability to read a scene that is coasting and deliberately inject consequence. 

Because this is a **Skill**—the contextual judgment of the brain—it is not simply a matter of executing a rote action. Anyone can deploy a blunt technique to create artificial urgency. But the *skill* of raising the stakes is the live, under-pressure choice of knowing exactly **which** emotional or situational lever to pull, **when** the narrative needs it, and **how much** pressure to apply without shattering the reality of the scene.

To determine if an improviser is actively using this skill, apply this litmus test: **If the scene ended right now, would it cost the characters anything?** 

You can spot this skill in action by looking for three real-time choices:

*   **Deepening, not widening:** The improviser chooses to make the *current* situation matter more, rather than inventing a brand new, unrelated problem to solve.
*   **Emotional investment:** They shift the focus from the physical activity (what they are doing) to the **cost of failure** (what happens if they don't succeed).
*   **Consequence over pattern:** They recognize they are operating in the Narrative engine. They aren't just doing the same joke faster or louder; they are intensifying the actual impact the events have on the characters' lives.

!!! example "In a scene: The Parking Spot"
    Two characters are arguing over a parking space. 
    
    *   **No Stakes:** "I was here first." / "No, I was." *(A flat argument).*
    *   **Technique without Skill (Panic):** "Give me the spot or I'll blow up the car!" *(Artificial, unearned escalation).*
    *   **The Skill in Action:** "Please. If I don't get this spot, I'll miss the start of my daughter's recital, and my wife promised she'd finally leave me." 
    
    The improviser felt the scene stalling, diagnosed the lack of consequence, and made a surgical choice to raise the emotional stakes. The parking spot is no longer just a spot; it is the linchpin of a marriage.

Ultimately, the litmus test asks: did the improviser recognize the exact moment the audience stopped caring, and make a deliberate choice to make the outcome matter?

## ⚙️ How it works in a scene

Raising the stakes is not about getting louder or talking faster. It is a real-time cognitive loop where you measure the emotional temperature of a scene and deliberately inject consequence. 

Because your goal is to drive change and meaning, here is the moment-to-moment loop in a performer’s brain:

**1. Perceive: Scanning for apathy**
While in the scene, you are constantly monitoring the emotional weight of the interaction. You are listening for casualness, brushed-off offers, or "transactional" dialogue (e.g., two people just ordering coffee). The trigger to raise the stakes is the realization: *“We are talking about things, but neither of us is affected by them.”* 

**2. Decide: Locating the vulnerability**
Once you feel the scene plateauing, you do not invent a random, unrelated catastrophe (like an alien invasion). Instead, you look at the mundane reality already established and ask yourself: 
* *Why is this the most important day of my character's life?*
* *What happens if I don't get what I want right now?*
* *What does this object or action mean to our relationship?*

**3. Act: Injecting consequence**
You make a move that locks your character into caring. You can raise the stakes across three different vectors:
* **Internal stakes:** You choose to have a massive emotional reaction to a small offer. 
* **Relational stakes:** You make the current topic a referendum on your relationship with your scene partner.
* **External stakes:** You establish a severe, tangible penalty for failure.

!!! example "In a scene: The lost keys"
    **The Offer:** "I can't find the keys to the Honda."
    
    **Low Stakes (Apathy):** "Oh well, let's just take the bus." *(The scene deflates; there is no narrative engine.)*
    
    **External Stakes:** "If we aren't at the courthouse in ten minutes, the judge is giving custody to your mother." *(Tangible consequence.)*
    
    **Relational Stakes:** "You didn't lose them. You hid them because you're terrified of finally moving in with me." *(The keys now represent the relationship.)*
    
    **Internal Stakes:** *(Dropping to your knees, voice cracking)* "I am a failure. I can't even keep track of a piece of metal, how am I supposed to raise a child?" *(Massive emotional consequence.)*

!!! tip "On stage: The 'Make it Matter' filter"
    When you feel lost in a scene, stop trying to invent plot. Instead, look at whatever your partner is holding, doing, or saying, and silently tell yourself: **"This is the only thing in the world that matters to me right now."** Let your next line of dialogue be driven entirely by that manufactured desperation.

## 📈 The maturity arc

The ability to raise the stakes evolves from a mechanical, often forgotten step into an invisible, emotional engine that drives the scene. Early on, improvisers struggle to care about what they are doing; eventually, they possess the skill to make the audience care deeply about the impossible.

Here is how the judgment and application of stakes mature over time:

| Stage | Stakes / The "Want" | Observable Behavior on Stage |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **1 Novice** | Plays activities with no reason to care | Scenes are just two people doing things (folding laundry, driving) while making casual conversation. If a meteor strikes, they react with mild annoyance. Nothing matters. |
| **2 Adv. Beginner** | States a "Want" when reminded | The improviser remembers they need a goal and announces it bluntly: *"I really want to win this spelling bee."* However, their emotional state rarely matches the declaration, and they easily drop the want if distracted. |
| **3 Competent** | Establishes what's at risk for the character | The improviser connects the desire to a clear consequence. *"If I don't win this spelling bee, my dad will sell my dog."* The stakes are logical, clearly communicated, and give the scene a definitive direction. |
| **4 Proficient** | Stakes are felt, not stated; they fuel the scene | The actor's body language, breathing, and reactions communicate the risk. They don't need to announce their desperation; the audience sees it in how tightly they grip the microphone and how they flinch when the judge rings the bell. |
| **5 Master** | Makes the audience genuinely care about absurd people | The improviser grounds ridiculous premises in universal human longing. They can play a sentient toaster desperate for a promotion, and because the stakes are played with absolute emotional truth, the audience will literally gasp when the toaster gets unplugged. |

!!! abstract "The Great Leap: From Head to Heart"
    The hardest transition in this arc is moving from **Competent** (Stage 3) to **Proficient** (Stage 4). 
    
    Competent improvisers *invent* stakes using their intellect—they add a ticking clock, a bomb, or a threat of divorce to force the scene to matter. Proficient improvisers *embody* stakes using their empathy—they allow the character to be genuinely, physically affected by whatever small thing is happening right now, trusting that emotion is what actually makes the audience lean in.

## 🧰 The techniques that train it

To build the judgment of when and how much to raise the stakes, improvisers must practice isolating the variable of consequence. The following techniques train the improviser to stop inventing random plot twists and start deepening the reality of what is already there.

### 🧗 Stakes-Escalation Reps
This technique involves rapid-fire drills where players take a base scenario and incrementally dial up what happens if the character fails. 

*   **How it builds judgment:** It teaches the brain the difference between a logical escalation and a random, shark-jumping leap. By practicing the "ladder of risk," improvisers learn to calibrate *how much* pressure to apply to a scene at any given moment. They learn to spot the next natural consequence rather than reaching for an artificial one.

    !!! example "In a scene: The Escalation Ladder"
        Notice how the stakes rise logically without breaking the base reality:
        
        *   **Base:** "I need to bake this cake perfectly." *(Low stakes: personal pride)*
        *   **Step 1:** "I need to bake this cake perfectly for the bake sale." *(Social stakes: public judgment)*
        *   **Step 2:** "I need to bake this cake perfectly because my rival, Brenda, is at the bake sale." *(Interpersonal stakes: rivalry/status)*
        *   **Step 3:** "I need to bake this cake perfectly because if Brenda wins again, I'm quitting the PTA and moving out of this town." *(Existential stakes: identity and belonging)*

### 💖 "Make It Matter More" Beats
Often used as a side-coaching tool or a dedicated scene constraint, this technique forces players to deepen their emotional connection to the *existing* elements of the scene rather than adding new external threats.

*   **How it builds judgment:** It trains the improviser to look inward for stakes rather than outward. It cures the common novice habit of introducing a sudden terminal illness to force drama. By practicing this, the brain learns that the highest stakes are often deeply personal, not globally catastrophic.

    !!! tip "On stage: Internal over External"
        If you feel a scene losing its momentum, your instinct might be to invent a new problem (e.g., *"Oh no, the kitchen is on fire!"*). Instead, use a **Make it matter more beat** to double down on the current problem. 
        
        If your scene partner just dropped a coffee mug, don't start a fire. Tell them *why* that specific mug mattered: *"That was the mug we bought on our honeymoon, right before we stopped talking to each other."* You have instantly raised the stakes without changing the physical reality of the scene.

    !!! abstract "Key idea: The Judgment of Depth"
        Practicing these two techniques shifts an improviser's mindset from **"What happens next?"** to **"What does this cost me?"** Once that judgment is honed, you can step into any scene, identify what the characters care about, and surgically apply pressure to it.

## 🔀 Dialing it up vs. down

Raising the stakes acts as a narrative accelerator. It forces characters into action, deepens emotional investment, and makes the audience lean in. But because it applies intense pressure to a scene, it requires precise calibration. Knowing when to squeeze and when to release is the hallmark of a mature improviser.

**When to dial it UP**
More pressure is required when a scene feels safe, stagnant, or trivial. 
*   **To cure "bickering":** If characters are arguing over who left the milk out, the scene will quickly stall. Dialing up the stakes means attaching a deeper consequence to the triviality: *"You leaving the milk out proves you aren't ready to raise a child."*
*   **To force a climax:** When a narrative arc is plateauing, increasing the cost of failure forces the protagonist to make a difficult, scene-ending decision.
*   **To fix apathy:** If the improvisers don't genuinely care about the outcome of the scene, the audience won't either. Dialing up the emotional "want" bridges that gap.

**When to exercise RESTRAINT**
Stakes are heavy. Applying them at the wrong time or in the wrong engine can crush a scene.
*   **During the Platform phase:** Early in a scene, you are establishing the baseline reality (the **Platform** — the who, what, and where). If you introduce life-or-death consequences in the first ten seconds, you destroy the reality before it can even form. You cannot threaten a relationship that hasn't been built yet.
*   **In a Game-driven scene:** The Narrative engine runs on *consequences*; the Game engine runs on *pattern*. If you inject heavy, grounded emotional stakes into a purely comedic game scene, you force the audience to feel empathy when they should be enjoying the absurdity. 
*   **When it borders on melodrama:** Unearned stakes feel manipulative. If you haven't built the emotional groundwork, suddenly screaming, crying, or introducing a terminal illness feels like a desperate grab for attention rather than a natural evolution.

!!! warning "Watch out: The 'Improv Gun'"
    The most infamous example of dialing up the stakes poorly is suddenly pulling a mime gun on your scene partner. It is an artificial, unearned spike in consequences that usually means the improviser panicked because they didn't know what their character actually cared about. True stakes are emotional, not just mortal.

### Balancing the Engines: Stakes vs. Escalation

It is easy to confuse *Raising the Stakes* (a Narrative skill) with *Escalating the Game* (a Game skill). They are neighboring concepts, but they serve entirely different masters.

| Feature | Raising the Stakes (Narrative) | Escalating the Game (Game) |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **The Goal** | To increase the **consequences** of the situation. | To heighten the **pattern** of the unusual behavior. |
| **The Effect** | The characters feel more pressure; the audience feels more invested. | The behavior becomes more absurd; the audience laughs harder. |
| **The Focus** | What happens *next*? (The future is at risk). | What else is *like this*? (The pattern is expanding). |

!!! example "In a scene: The Bad Haircut"
    **The Setup:** A customer is unhappy with their haircut.
    
    **Dialing up the Stakes (Narrative):** The customer reveals this haircut was for their wedding in an hour, and their fiancé already has doubts. The consequence of the bad haircut is now immense.
    
    **Escalating the Game (Game):** The barber confidently defends the terrible haircut, then proceeds to offer an even worse shave using a weed-whacker. The pattern of "delusional confidence in terrible service" is heightened, but the emotional consequences don't matter.

## 🤝 How it serves the domain

The domain of **The Scene** demands that improvisers architect compelling moments in real time, fluidly choosing between the two primary engines of improv: Game and Narrative. Mastering the skill of raising the stakes is what makes the Narrative engine actually run.

If a scene feels aimless, flat, or like a mere conversation, it is almost always because the stakes are too low. By skillfully raising the stakes, you inject gravity into the scene, transforming a casual interaction into a pivotal moment in the characters' lives.

!!! abstract "Pattern vs. Consequence"
    A crucial distinction in architecting scenes:
    *   **Escalating Game:** Making the unusual behavior more absurd, frequent, or extreme (intensifying the *pattern*).
    *   **Raising Stakes:** Making the outcome of the situation matter more to the characters (intensifying the *consequences*).

Good judgment in raising stakes serves the domain's ultimate goal in three vital ways:

*   **It commands audience investment:** Audiences do not care about *what* characters are doing; they care about *why it matters* to them. When you raise the stakes, you give the audience a reason to lean forward and emotionally invest in the outcome.
*   **It forces character choices:** A compelling scene requires action. High stakes back characters into corners where they must make difficult, revealing decisions. 
*   **It drives inevitable change:** The hallmark of a great narrative scene is that the characters are fundamentally different at the end than they were at the beginning. Stakes provide the necessary pressure to forge that change.

!!! tip "On stage"
    When you feel a scene losing momentum, don't just add new information, introduce a new character, or invent a crazy plot twist. Instead, look at what is already true between the characters and ask: *"How can I make this matter more to my partner right now?"*

## ⚠️ Common failure modes & how to grow

When improvisers struggle to raise the stakes, they usually substitute artificial intensity for genuine consequence. Because true stakes require vulnerability—admitting that a character cares deeply about something—players often hide behind defense mechanisms that derail the narrative.

Here are the most common traps, followed by how to train your way out of them.

### Common Failure Modes

*   **The External Threat (The "Bomb"):** When a scene feels flat, a panicked improviser will often invent a sudden, extreme external danger—a bomb, a sudden bear attack, or a collapsing roof. This destroys the existing reality instead of deepening it. True stakes come from *within* the relationship, not from the sky.
*   **Volume Over Value:** Confusing intensity of volume with intensity of consequence. Yelling at your scene partner doesn't mean the stakes are high; it usually just means you've run out of ways to express *why* the situation matters. Anger is a reaction, not a stake.
*   **Teflon Characters:** Playing a character to whom nothing sticks. If your character shrugs off a breakup, a firing, or a ghost, the audience will shrug too. If the outcome doesn't matter to the character, it is impossible for it to matter to the audience.
*   **Jumping to a 10:** Escalating the consequences too quickly before the audience understands the baseline reality. If you threaten divorce over a misplaced pen in line one, there is nowhere left to go. Stakes need a foundation to push against.

!!! warning "The 'Gun in the Scene' Trap"
    Introducing a literal weapon is the ultimate false stake. It forces the scene to become about survival, instantly erasing the nuanced, relatable relationship dynamics you were building. Unless you are explicitly playing a genre piece (like a noir or an action movie), keep the weapons holstered and make the *emotional* consequences lethal instead.

### How to Grow: The Next Step for Every Stage

To advance your ability to raise the stakes, identify where you currently sit on the maturity arc and focus *only* on the next immediate milestone.

| If you are here... | Your next growth step is... |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Stage 1: Novice**<br>*(Plays activities with no reason to care)* | **State a "Want".** Stop just doing things on stage. If you are folding laundry, decide *why* this laundry matters. Practice explicitly stating your desire: *"I want this shirt perfect for my date."* |
| **Stage 2: Adv. Beginner**<br>*(States a "Want" when reminded)* | **Establish the risk.** Move from just wanting something to defining the consequence of failure. What happens if you don't get it? *"If this shirt isn't perfect, she'll think I'm a slob like my father."* |
| **Stage 3: Competent**<br>*(Establishes what's at risk for the character)* | **Internalize the stakes.** Stop talking about the stakes and start *feeling* them. Let the risk fuel your physical and emotional reactions. The audience should see the desperation in your posture, not just hear it in your dialogue. |
| **Stage 4: Proficient**<br>*(Stakes are felt, not stated; they fuel the scene)* | **Ground the absurd.** Apply profound, felt stakes to utterly ridiculous situations. Your goal is to make the audience genuinely hold their breath over whether a talking dog gets a promotion at the bank. |

!!! tip "On stage: The 'Make it Matter More' check"
    If you feel a scene losing energy, ask yourself internally: *"What is the worst thing that could happen to my character right now, based on what is already true?"* Then, make a choice that brings that worst-case scenario one step closer to reality.

## 📚 References & Further Reading

### Foundational sources
*   **Keith Johnstone, *Impro for Storytellers* (Faber and Faber, 1999)** — Expands on his earlier work to focus heavily on narrative progression, keeping the audience invested, and why improvisers must avoid "canceling" or lowering the stakes of a scene. Johnstone emphasizes that a scene only matters if the characters are genuinely altered by the events.
*   **Viola Spolin, *Improvisation for the Theater* (Northwestern University Press, 1963)** — While she uses the terminology of "Focus" and "Point of Concentration," her foundational exercises train the improviser to care deeply about the immediate problem. This intense focus on solving a specific, shared problem is the root of internal stakes.

### Practitioner guides & manuals
*   **T.J. Jagodowski, David Pasquesi, and Pam Victor, *Improvisation at the Speed of Life: The TJ and Dave Book* (Solo Roma, Inc., 2015)** — The definitive guide to relational stakes. The authors argue that the relationship *is* the scene, and that improvisers must play the reality of the moment with genuine emotional investment rather than inventing external plot points.
*   **Mick Napier, *Improvise: Scene from the Inside Out* (Heinemann Drama, 2004)** — Challenges rote improv rules by emphasizing the need to make a strong initial choice, take care of yourself, and commit to the internal stakes of your character. Napier diagnoses "wimping" as a failure to care about the scene's reality.
*   **Will Hines, *How to Be the Greatest Improviser on Earth* (Pretty Great Publishing, 2016)** — Features specific chapters on playing real, grounding the scene, and letting things matter. Hines provides a clear bridge between comedic premise and narrative consequence, teaching improvisers how to care deeply about the mundane.
*   **Patti Stiles, *Improvise Freely* (Big Toast Entertainment, 2021)** — A masterclass from the Johnstone lineage that focuses on narrative skill, emotional connection, and breaking free from rigid rules to find genuine stakes. Stiles encourages improvisers to embrace vulnerability and consequence.
*   **Rob Norman, *Improvising Now: A Practical Guide to Modern Improv* (CreateSpace, 2014)** — Offers practical tools for discovering character dynamics and heightening ideas without resorting to artificial panic or external plot drops.
*   **Charna Halpern, Del Close, and Kim "Howard" Johnson, *Truth in Comedy: The Manual of Improvisation* (Meriwether Publishing, 1994)** — The foundational text for long-form improv that insists the humor must come from the reality of the characters' relationships and their genuine reactions, rather than forced jokes or artificial stakes.

### Lineage & teachers
*   **The Annoyance Theatre (Chicago)** — Founded by Mick Napier, this theater pioneered an approach that prioritizes the individual performer's emotional commitment and internal stakes over strict adherence to structural "rules."
*   **The Loose Moose Theatre Company (Calgary)** — The birthplace of Theatresports (created by Keith Johnstone), representing a tradition that heavily emphasizes narrative stakes, status, and audience investment.
*   **iO Theater (Chicago)** — While famous for the Harold and the "Game of the Scene," its core philosophy of "truth in comedy" (championed by Del Close and Charna Halpern) demands that performers play the reality of the scene, making the stakes matter.

### Research & theory
*   **Constantin Stanislavski, *An Actor Prepares* (Theatre Arts Books, 1936)** — The foundational acting text on "The Magic If" and "Given Circumstances," which provides the psychological framework for why a character cares about an outcome.
*   **Uta Hagen, *Respect for Acting* (Macmillan, 1973)** — Her concepts of "Objectives" (what the character wants) and "Obstacles" (what stands in the way) are the exact mechanisms by which improvisers raise stakes.
*   **Robert McKee, *Story: Substance, Structure, Style and the Principles of Screenwriting* (HarperCollins, 1997)** — Essential reading for understanding narrative stakes; defines how the gap between expectation and result creates the "cost" of failure that drives a story.
*   **Jeanne Leep, *Theatrical Improvisation: Short Form, Long Form, and Sketch-Based Improv* (Palgrave Macmillan, 2008)** — Provides an academic overview of how different improv formats rely on varying degrees of narrative stakes and character investment to succeed.

### Talks, videos & courses
*   **Will Hines, *World's Greatest Improv School (WGIS)* courses** — His online and in-person classes frequently focus on "playing real" and grounding the scene before escalating, directly addressing the "Make it Matter" filter.
*   **TJ & Dave (T.J. Jagodowski and David Pasquesi), Live Performances** — Observing their unscripted shows provides a masterclass in establishing high relational stakes without relying on external plot devices.

### Communities & adjacent reading
*   **Michael Chekhov, *To the Actor: On the Technique of Acting* (Harper & Brothers, 1953)** — His work on the "Psychological Gesture" helps improvisers physically embody the internal stakes and desires of their characters.
*   **Lajos Egri, *The Art of Dramatic Writing* (Simon & Schuster, 1946)** — Explores how a clear premise and deeply orchestrated characters naturally generate escalating stakes and inevitable conflict.

### Where it comes from
The concept of "stakes" in modern acting and improv traces heavily back to casting director Michael Shurtleff's seminal 1978 book *Audition*. Shurtleff established twelve "Guideposts" for actors, explicitly demanding they ask themselves, "What are you fighting for?" and urging them to find the maximum emotional importance in any given moment. In the improv world, Keith Johnstone expanded on this in *Impro for Storytellers* (1999) under the banner of "Consequences," warning that without genuine narrative stakes, improv devolves into "mindless entertainment" where characters refuse to be altered by each other.

### A telling example
**The Laundry Shift**
An illustrative scenario often used in improv classes to demonstrate stakes involves two improvisers folding laundry. 

*   **Without stakes:** They chat casually about the weather or the fabric softener. The scene is a polite, disposable interaction. 
*   **Artificial stakes (Panic):** One improviser suddenly pulls a mimed gun from the laundry basket and demands a million dollars. The reality of the scene shatters for a cheap, unearned escalation.
*   **True stakes:** One improviser holds up a shirt and says, "You're packing the blue sweater. You promised you wouldn't go back to him." The laundry is no longer just laundry; it is the physical manifestation of a relationship ending. The improviser has deliberately injected consequence into the mundane, making the scene matter.
