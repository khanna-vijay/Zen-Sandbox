# 🧠 Suggestion Deconstruction (A-to-C)

> *The judgment of which technique to use, when, and how much.*

![Square infographic explaining the improv skill “Suggestion Deconstruction (A-to-C)”](../../assets/infographics/D4.S3.jpg){ .infographic }

## 🧠 What it is

**Suggestion Deconstruction** (often called **A-to-C thinking**) is the cognitive skill of mining a single audience suggestion for its richest, most playable premise. When an audience yells out a word, the improviser must instantly make a series of rapid-fire judgments. They aren't just brainstorming; they are deciding *which* associative leap will best serve the scene. Do we play the literal object? The immediate, cliché association? Or do we push further to the underlying themes, emotions, or philosophies connected to the word? 

This skill is the real-time filter that discards the obvious to select an angle providing a strong, sustainable launchpad for the work ahead.

!!! abstract "The A-to-C Framework"
    - **A (The Suggestion):** The literal word given by the audience (e.g., "Bicycle").
    - **B (The Obvious):** The immediate, literal association (e.g., "Riding a bike," "The Tour de France").
    - **C (The Inspired):** The thematic, emotional, or lateral leap that provides a rich premise (e.g., "Learning to balance," "Never forgetting a skill," "Being a third wheel").

This is genuinely difficult because it fights the brain's natural instinct to seek the path of least resistance under pressure. When the lights go up and adrenaline spikes, the easiest lifeline is to simply pantomime the suggestion. It takes immense discipline to pause, let the obvious "B" ideas pass by, and trust your mind to find a deeper "C" connection. 

Furthermore, this judgment requires a delicate balancing act. If you play the suggestion too literally, the scene feels like a cheap parlor trick. If you jump all the way to a disconnected "Z" (an association so abstract only you understand it), the audience feels ignored and cheated. Mastery lies in finding the sweet spot: a premise that is surprising and inventive, yet makes perfect, satisfying sense to the audience the moment it is revealed.

## 👀 The litmus test

Word association is a mechanical exercise; **Suggestion Deconstruction** is a high-stakes editorial choice. The technique itself is simply brainstorming, but the *skill* is having the discipline to filter out dead-end ideas in real time, selecting the one premise that will best serve the ensemble.

!!! abstract "The Litmus Test"
    When the audience provides a suggestion, does the improviser immediately initiate a literal, first-thought scene, or do they take a breath, process the word, and initiate based on a rich, lateral, or thematic leap?

Under the heat of the stage lights, the pressure to "just start talking" is immense. Novices succumb by grabbing the literal suggestion. The true test of this skill is the improviser's ability to stand in the silence of the opening seconds, rapidly generate a web of associations, and confidently choose the non-obvious premise that offers the most emotional or comedic potential.

!!! example "In a scene: The suggestion is 'Dentist'"
    *   **The "A" (Literal):** Two improvisers sit in chairs; one mimes holding a drill and says, "Open wide." (A predictable, transactional dead-end).
    *   **The "B" (Associated):** A scene about kids eating too much candy on Halloween. (Better, but still highly expected).
    *   **The "C" (Deconstructed):** A scene about a couple where one person is desperately trying to "extract" the truth from the other, or a scene about a boss who takes sadistic pleasure in delivering painful news. 

The skill lies entirely in the **judgment of selection**. You are not just proving you know what the word means; you are deciding *how* the team will use it to build a sustainable, expansive playground.

## ⚙️ How it works in a scene

The cognitive loop of Suggestion Deconstruction happens in the breathless seconds between the host shouting "Thank you!" and the first improviser stepping downstage. It is a rapid-fire mental filter that transforms a static noun into a dynamic, playable scene premise. 

The moment-to-moment process looks like this:

1. **Receive the Anchor (A):** You hear the literal suggestion from the audience. Your brain registers the word and its basic definition.
2. **Acknowledge the Bridge (B):** Your brain instantly offers the most obvious, immediate association. If the suggestion is "Bicycle," the bridge is "getting a flat tire." *You notice this, and you deliberately step past it.*
3. **Discover the Destination (C):** You make a lateral jump from the bridge. You abstract the idea by asking: *What is the underlying theme? What is the emotional core? What is a completely different context that shares this dynamic?* From the Tour de France, you might jump to "intense, unnatural competitiveness" or "wearing inappropriate athletic gear to a casual event."
4. **Select and Initiate:** You evaluate your "C" options for playability. You aren't looking for the cleverest wordplay; you are looking for the idea that offers the strongest relationship, emotion, or philosophy. You step out and initiate based on that choice.

!!! example "In a scene: Deconstructing 'Library'"
    **A (The Suggestion):** "Library"  
    **B (The Obvious):** Shushing librarians, overdue book fines, reading.  
    **C (The Lateral Jumps):**  
    - *Thematic:* Hoarding knowledge, absolute silence as a form of control.  
    - *Relational:* Two people forced to whisper during a massive, relationship-ending argument.  
    - *Tangential:* The Dewey Decimal System → A person who obsessively categorizes their friends.  
    
    **The Initiation:** An improviser steps out, furiously sorting a stack of polaroids into labeled folders, and whispers intensely: "I'm sorry, Brenda, but you're a 300—Social Sciences. I only date 100s—Philosophy and Psychology."

The true mark of this skill in action is **premise generation**. A novice uses A-to-C to find a new noun to talk about; a skilled improviser uses A-to-C to find a new *behavior* to play. 

!!! tip "On stage: Mine the 'Why' and 'How'"
    If your brain is stuck on the obvious association, ask yourself *why* that thing exists, or *how* it feels. If the suggestion is "Rollercoaster" and your brain is stuck on "going fast and screaming," ask: *Why do people ride them?* Answer: To experience manufactured danger. *Boom.* Your "C" is a scene about a bored suburban couple hiring a burglar to break into their house just to feel alive.

## 📈 The maturity arc

The ability to deconstruct a suggestion is a muscle. It evolves from a literal, panic-driven grab at the first thought to a sophisticated, thematic extraction that fuels an entire ensemble. As an improviser matures, they stop treating the suggestion as a mandatory prop and begin treating it as a philosophical launchpad.

| Stage | Framework Definition | Observable Behavior |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **1. Novice** | Plays the first, most obvious association | Panics and plays the suggestion literally (**A-to-A**). If the word is "Doctor," they immediately put on an imaginary stethoscope. The suggestion traps them rather than inspiring them. |
| **2. Adv. Beginner** | Generates several associations in a brainstorm | Can list multiple related ideas off-stage, but struggles to filter them quickly under the lights. Often settles for a slightly removed, but still highly related idea (**A-to-B**). |
| **3. Competent** | Selects the non-obvious ("C") premise | Consistently bypasses the literal. Hears "Doctor" and initiates a scene about someone with a God complex, or a mother disappointed her son chose art over medicine. The leap is clear, but the scene is no longer about the word itself. |
| **4. Proficient** | Mines a suggestion for its richest, most playable angle | Looks for the *dynamic* or *theme* rather than just a noun. They extract relationship dynamics, emotional states, or philosophical questions from a single word, setting up their scene partner with a rich, immediate point of view. |
| **5. Master** | Turns any word into a premise the whole team can run | Instantly extracts a unifying theme that the entire ensemble can build a piece around. They don't just find a clever idea for themselves; they find the angle that provides the most fertile ground for the whole cast to play in. |

!!! example "The Arc in Action: The Suggestion is 'Mirror'"
    *   **Novice:** "Look at me in this mirror, I'm brushing my teeth." (Literal)
    *   **Adv. Beginner:** "Welcome to the funhouse, look at these crazy mirrors!" (A-to-B: Mirror $\rightarrow$ Funhouse)
    *   **Competent:** "I just can't date someone who is exactly like me." (A-to-C: Mirror $\rightarrow$ Reflection $\rightarrow$ Dating your clone)
    *   **Proficient:** Initiates a grounded scene about a parent realizing they are passing their worst flaws down to their child. (Mining the richest emotional angle of "reflection")
    *   **Master:** The ensemble wordlessly steps out and creates a cohesive opening exploring the theme of vanity, self-deception, and the gap between how we see ourselves versus how the world sees us. The word "mirror" is never spoken.

!!! tip "On stage"
    If you find yourself stuck at the Novice or Advanced Beginner stage, force yourself to take a physical breath before stepping out. That single second of silence is often all your brain needs to discard the "A" idea and find the "C" idea.

## 🧰 The techniques that train it

To build the reflex of finding the non-obvious, playable angle, improvisers rely on specific training techniques. These exercises isolate the brain's association pathways, allowing the ensemble to practice lateral thinking without the pressure of immediately acting in a scene. 

* **A-to-C Drills**  
  In this rapid-fire word association exercise, a player hears a word (A), silently thinks of an immediate association (B), and speaks the next association (C). 
  * *How it builds judgment:* It forces the brain to bypass the literal, first-level response. By practicing this, improvisers learn to trust their lateral leaps, ensuring the resulting scene is *inspired* by the suggestion rather than just a literal depiction of it.

* **Premise Brainstorm Rounds**  
  The team takes a single suggestion and rapidly pitches distinct, fully-formed scene **premises** (the foundational *Who, What, and Where*) without actually initiating them. 
  * *How it builds judgment:* It bridges the gap between a raw word and a playable situation. It teaches improvisers to quickly evaluate which angles actually have legs, training the filter for "playability."

* **"What's interesting about this?" Mining**  
  An analytical exercise where the team takes a mundane suggestion and asks questions to uncover its underlying themes, mechanics, or associated human behaviors (e.g., *What kind of person uses this? What is the opposite of this? What is the emotional weight of this?*).
  * *How it builds judgment:* It moves the ensemble from playing the *noun* to playing the *concept*. It trains the improviser's eye to find the emotional core or philosophical weight of a suggestion.

!!! example "In practice: Processing 'Pineapple'"
    Here is how the same suggestion is processed through the lens of each training technique to yield entirely different scene inspirations:
    
    * **A-to-C Drill:** "Pineapple" (A) $\rightarrow$ *Spiky* (B) $\rightarrow$ **"Porcupine"** (C). *(The scene is now about a pet porcupine that keeps popping the family's inflatable furniture.)*
    * **Premise Brainstorm:** "Pineapple." $\rightarrow$ **"Two rival fruit stand owners trying to sabotage each other's displays."** *(The scene uses the literal object but immediately applies a strong relational dynamic.)*
    * **Mining:** "Pineapple... it's tough and prickly on the outside, but sweet on the inside." $\rightarrow$ **"Let's play a scene about a tough, intimidating biker gang that secretly runs a charity bake sale."** *(The scene abandons the fruit entirely to play its thematic metaphor.)*

!!! tip "On stage"
    You don't need to use all three techniques for every suggestion. The goal of practicing them off-stage is to build a diverse toolkit. When you step on stage, your brain will automatically grab the tool that best fits the word you were just given.

## 🔀 Dialing it up vs. down

Suggestion deconstruction is a filter between the audience’s prompt and your team’s performance. Like any filter, you must actively choose how thick to make it. You do not *always* need to push a suggestion to its third or fourth degree of separation. The skill lies in reading the room, the format, and the prompt itself to determine how much deconstruction is required.

### When to dial it UP (Deep Deconstruction)
Crank the dial toward "C" (or even "D" and "E") when the suggestion is thin, overly common, or designed to trap you. Deep deconstruction protects the ensemble from playing out tired tropes.

*   **The cliché suggestion:** When you hear "Dentist," "Pirate," or "Tinder," a literal scene will almost certainly be something the audience has seen a dozen times. Pushing to "C" (Dentist $\rightarrow$ pulling teeth $\rightarrow$ extracting a painful confession) gives you a fresh, playable premise.
*   **The crude or "troll" suggestion:** If a rowdy crowd yells something inappropriate, deep deconstruction allows you to accept the suggestion gracefully without rolling in the mud. You extract the *theme* (e.g., betrayal, anatomy, taboo) and play a smart scene about that theme.
*   **Thematic long-form:** In formats like the Harold, the opening is designed to generate a wide thematic canopy. Dialing up the deconstruction here provides a rich vocabulary of ideas for the rest of the piece.

!!! tip "Dialing UP: Finding the 'Why'"
    If the suggestion is a physical object (e.g., "A toaster"), don't just associate to other kitchen appliances. Dial up the deconstruction by asking *what the object represents*. A toaster pops up unexpectedly; it burns things if left unattended; it only has one job. Those are rich, playable human behaviors.

### When to dial it DOWN (Restraint)
Hold back and play closer to "A" or "B" when the audience hands you a genuine, highly specific gift, or when the show's momentum demands immediate action.

*   **The highly specific prompt:** If the suggestion is "My grandmother's rusted locket" or "A bitter divorce," do not deconstruct it into oblivion. The audience has already given you a rich emotional core. Honor it by playing it relatively straight.
*   **High-energy short-form:** In fast-paced games or montages, the audience wants to see the immediate, visceral connection to their word. Over-intellectualizing slows the show down.
*   **When the team is stuck in their heads:** If the ensemble is standing in a circle during an opening, furrowing their brows and silently agonizing over the "perfect" connection, dial it down. Grab the simplest "B" association and start moving.

!!! warning "The 'A-to-Z' Disconnect"
    If you deconstruct a suggestion so far that the audience can no longer trace your logic, you break the implicit promise of improv. If the suggestion is "Bicycle" and your first scene is about the geopolitical ramifications of the 1990s space race, the audience will assume you ignored them to perform a pre-planned scene. 

### Balancing neighboring skills
Suggestion Deconstruction sits in delicate balance with **Pacing & Rhythm**. Over-deconstructing leads to "analysis paralysis," where the team spends so much time mining the suggestion that the energy of the show dies before the first scene begins. Conversely, under-deconstructing forces the team to rely heavily on **Support Work** to save thin, literal scenes that have nowhere to go. The master improviser uses just enough deconstruction to give the scene a strong spine, then immediately surrenders to the rhythm of the show.

## 🤝 How it serves the domain

The domain of **The Ensemble** demands that improvisers surrender their individual egos to perceive, support, generate, and weave a unified piece without pre-planning. Suggestion Deconstruction is the primary engine for the "generate" and "weave" portions of this mandate. 

When an ensemble relies on literal interpretations, players often end up competing for the exact same obvious joke, or they accidentally trample each other trying to initiate the one scene the suggestion clearly dictates. By collectively pushing to "C," the team transforms a single word into a sprawling, shared playground.

Here is how mastering this skill directly serves the ensemble:

*   **It creates distinct threads for weaving:** To weave a long-form piece without pre-planning, a team needs scenes that are distinct in character and setting, yet thematically related. If the suggestion is "Bicycle," one scene might be about a literal bike race, but another explores the emotional "training wheels" coming off a marriage, and a third involves the historical invention of the wheel. Because they all stem from the same deconstruction, the ensemble can weave them together effortlessly later.
*   **It demands active listening over solo invention:** True A-to-C deconstruction in a group setting requires you to listen to your teammate's "B" association to find your "C." You cannot pre-plan your contribution; you must surrender to the chain of thought the group is building in the moment.
*   **It prevents stage-hogging:** When a suggestion is mined for its richest, most varied angles, there is enough material for everyone. No single player needs to dominate the show, because the deconstruction has provided a unique entry point for every cast member.

!!! abstract "Thematic Unity over Literal Agreement"
    In a strong ensemble, unity doesn't mean everyone plays the exact same idea. It means everyone plays different facets of the same theme. Suggestion deconstruction is the tool that shatters a single suggestion into enough unique facets for the whole cast to shine.

## ⚠️ Common failure modes & how to grow

When improvisers struggle with suggestion deconstruction, they usually fall into one of two extremes: clinging too tightly to the literal word, or abandoning it so completely that the audience feels cheated. Because this skill happens entirely in the brain before a scene begins, failures often look like hesitation, confusion, or scenes that feel instantly cliché.

Here are the most common traps and how to recognize them:

!!! warning "Watch out: The Literal 'A' (The Prop Trap)"
    This happens when a player takes the suggestion and simply places it in the middle of the stage as a physical object. If the suggestion is "Bicycle," the scene is about two people riding a bicycle. The scene has nowhere to go once the object is established, forcing the players to invent a plot from scratch while pedaling imaginary bikes.

!!! warning "Watch out: The Cliché 'B' (The First-Thought Trap)"
    The player makes an association, but it is the exact same association the audience made. If the suggestion is "Pirate," the scene is about a parrot, a peg leg, and buried treasure. It lacks a unique point of view, leading to a scene that feels like a tired sketch.

!!! warning "Watch out: The Disconnected 'Z' (The Six Degrees Trap)"
    In an effort to be clever, a player chain-links associations so far away from the original suggestion that the audience loses the thread. *Bicycle $\rightarrow$ Wheels $\rightarrow$ Cars $\rightarrow$ Traffic $\rightarrow$ Road Rage $\rightarrow$ A scene about a bad marriage.* While the player knows how they got there, to the audience (and often their scene partner), it just looks like they ignored the suggestion entirely.

!!! warning "Watch out: Analysis Paralysis"
    The player stands on the backline, furiously trying to calculate the perfect, most brilliant "C" premise. By the time they figure it out, the scene has already started, the moment has passed, or they enter with such a rigid, pre-planned idea that they bulldoze their scene partner's initiation.

### How to Grow: Next Steps for Every Stage

To advance your deconstruction skills, you must shift your focus from *finding clever words* to *discovering playable themes*. Here is the next step to take based on where you currently are:

**If you are a Novice...**
*(You tend to play the first, most obvious association or use the suggestion as a prop.)*
*   **Your next step:** Practice volume over quality. When you hear a suggestion, force yourself to silently list three different associations before you step out. Break the habit of acting on your very first impulse. 

**If you are an Advanced Beginner...**
*(You can generate several associations in a brainstorm, but often still pick the safest one.)*
*   **Your next step:** Start throwing away your first two ideas. Consciously bypass the "B" (the obvious) and force yourself to initiate with the "C" (the non-obvious). If the suggestion is "Doctor," throw away *hospitals* and *stethoscopes*, and initiate a scene about *someone who always has to be right*.

**If you are Competent...**
*(You successfully select non-obvious "C" premises, but sometimes they are too intellectual or hard to play.)*
*   **Your next step:** Shift from mining for *nouns* to mining for *dynamics*. Ask yourself: "What is the emotion of this suggestion?" or "What kind of relationship does this word imply?" A "C" premise is only good if it gives you a strong behavioral filter to play.

**If you are Proficient...**
*(You mine suggestions for rich, playable angles, but mostly use them for your own initiations.)*
*   **Your next step:** Start listening to how your *teammates* are deconstructing the suggestion. Your goal is no longer just to find your own brilliant premise, but to recognize the thematic engine your partner has discovered and support it instantly. 

!!! tip "On stage: The Master's approach"
    A Master improviser doesn't just find a clever premise; they find a premise that the entire ensemble can use. They turn a single word into a thematic playground, initiating scenes that establish a clear philosophical game, making it effortless for the rest of the team to recognize the pattern and join in.

## 📚 References & Further Reading

### Foundational sources
*   **Matt Besser, Ian Roberts, and Matt Walsh, *The Upright Citizens Brigade Comedy Improvisation Manual* (Comedy Council of Nicea LLC, 2013)** — This is the definitive text that coined and codified the specific "A-to-C" terminology. The manual dedicates significant space to explaining how to use an audience suggestion to inspire a comedic premise, explicitly warning against "A-to-A" (literal) and "A-to-B" (obvious) choices in favor of lateral, thematic leaps.
*   **Charna Halpern, Del Close, and Kim "Howard" Johnson, *Truth in Comedy: The Manual of Improvisation* (Meriwether Publishing, 1994)** — While it predates the "A-to-C" phrasing, this book introduces the "Pattern Game" (a word association opening) and the core philosophy of elevating a suggestion from the commonplace to the extraordinary. It establishes the foundational idea that improvisers should use the suggestion to find underlying themes rather than just pantomiming the word.

### Practitioner guides & manuals
*   **Mick Napier, *Improvise: Scene from the Inside Out* (Heinemann Drama, 2004)** — Napier offers practical exercises for generating scenes *from* a suggestion rather than being trapped by it. He emphasizes the importance of doing something active with the word instead of just letting the word dictate the entire reality of the scene, which is crucial for moving past the literal "A."
*   **Will Hines, *How to Be the Greatest Improviser on Earth* (2016)** — A highly practical guide from a veteran UCB teacher that breaks down the mechanics of premise generation. Hines discusses how to make clear, playable choices from an opening or suggestion, and how to trust your brain's associative leaps under the pressure of a live show.

### Lineage & teachers
*   **The Upright Citizens Brigade (UCB)** — The theater and training center that formalized the "A-to-C" framework. Their curriculum for premise-based long-form improv relies heavily on this specific cognitive skill, teaching students to extract a playable game or theme from a single word before stepping on stage.
*   **Del Close & iO Theater (formerly ImprovOlympic)** — The Chicago lineage from which thematic extraction originated. Through the development of the Harold and its opening pattern games, Close taught improvisers to be "pattern makers rather than storytellers," laying the groundwork for deconstructing a suggestion into a web of associations.

### Research & theory
*   **Paul T. Sowden, Lucie Clements, Chrishelle Redlich, and Carine Lewis, *Improvisation facilitates divergent thinking and creativity* (Psychology of Aesthetics, Creativity, and the Arts, 2015)** — Psychological research demonstrating that even brief improvisation exercises significantly enhance divergent thinking. This supports the mechanism behind A-to-C thinking: training the brain to generate non-obvious, flexible associations rather than settling for the first thought.
*   **Yoed N. Kenett, et al., *Investigating the structure of semantic networks in low and high creative persons* (Frontiers in Human Neuroscience, 2014)** — Network science research showing that highly creative individuals possess more flexible semantic memory networks. This provides a cognitive explanation for why some improvisers can easily make the lateral "A-to-C" leaps, as their brains are structurally primed to connect distant concepts more efficiently than literal "A-to-B" thinkers.
*   **Roger E. Beaty, Mathias Benedek, Paul J. Silvia, and Daniel L. Schacter, *Creative Cognition and Brain Network Dynamics* (Trends in Cognitive Sciences, 2016)** — Neuroscientific studies highlighting how the brain's default network (responsible for spontaneous thought) and executive control network (responsible for filtering and selection) cooperate during creative tasks. This perfectly mirrors the A-to-C process: generating a wide web of associations and then applying editorial judgment to select the most playable premise.

### Communities & adjacent reading
*   **Edward de Bono, *Lateral Thinking: Creativity Step by Step* (Harper & Row, 1970)** — A foundational text on indirect and creative problem-solving. De Bono's concept of lateral thinking perfectly mirrors the cognitive leap required to move from a literal suggestion to a thematic premise, offering exercises to break out of predictable, linear thought patterns.
*   **Keith Johnstone, *Impro: Improvisation and the Theatre* (Faber and Faber, 1979)** — While Johnstone's tradition focuses less on premise-generation and more on narrative and status, his writings on the imagination and how the adult brain censors "obvious" or "crazy" ideas provide vital context for why improvisers freeze up and grab the literal suggestion out of fear.

## 💬 Quotes & Anecdotes

!!! quote "— Matt Besser, Ian Roberts, and Matt Walsh, *The Upright Citizens Brigade Comedy Improvisation Manual* (2013)"
    When you go from A to B, you often end up just listing synonyms for the suggestion or a subset of elements that belong in the category represented by the suggestion…. Making these A to B moves is natural. The way you can learn to make less obvious moves is to manually go through the process of 'going A to C.' You could also think of this process as 'going to your next thought.'

!!! quote "— Charna Halpern, *Art By Committee* (2006)"
    Your job is to take that suggestion and discover a theme for the piece.

!!! quote "— Mick Napier, *Interview with People & Chairs* (2015)"
    If the suggestion is 'bowling alley,' then someone's gonna put their hand in the air in order to hold a ball... Or if someone says 'graveyard,' they're gonna grab a shovel to dig, and it's just these associations that we make. So those become trying after a while, and difficult to pay attention to because it's the same constructs.

!!! quote "— Del Close (as recounted by students)"
    Throw out the first thought, and the third thought is often the best.

### Where it comes from
The specific terminology of "A-to-C thinking" was codified by the Upright Citizens Brigade (UCB) in their teachings and their 2013 *Comedy Improvisation Manual*. However, the underlying philosophy of treating the suggestion as an inspiration rather than a literal mandate traces back to Del Close and Charna Halpern's development of the Harold in Chicago. Close famously urged improvisers to "throw out the first thought" and look for the "third thought," which he believed yielded poetry rather than a cheap, sitcom-level joke.

### A telling example
The classic way to practice A-to-C thinking is through a word association chain that forces the brain past the obvious. 

If the audience suggestion is **"Sand" (A)**:
*   The immediate, literal association **(B)** might be "Rock" or "Beach."
*   If you force yourself to go to the next thought **(C)**, "Rock" might lead you to "The Rolling Stones" or "Heavy Metal," while "Beach" might lead you to "Sunburn" or "Sharks."

By skipping the "B" and initiating a scene about an aging rock band or a paranoid lifeguard, the improviser honors the suggestion's lineage without making the audience watch a literal scene about sitting in the sand. 

Similarly, if the suggestion is **"Bicycle" (A)**, the obvious association **(B)** is "Riding a bike." The lateral leap **(C)** might be "Learning to balance" or "Never forgetting a skill." Initiating a scene about a father who refuses to let go of his adult son's shoulders during a major life event captures the *feeling* of teaching someone to ride a bike, without ever miming handlebars.
