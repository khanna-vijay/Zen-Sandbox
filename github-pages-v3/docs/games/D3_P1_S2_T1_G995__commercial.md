# The Infomercial

![The Infomercial — infographic](img/D3_P1_S2_T1_G995__commercial.jpg){ .game-hero }

> Sell an absurd product by escalating its ridiculous benefits through high-energy physical demonstration.

## Overview
The Infomercial is a high-energy, performance-oriented game where a small group of players pitches a fictional or mundane product directly to the audience. Players must physically demonstrate the product's increasingly absurd benefits, escalating the stakes with each new claim. It is a fast-paced exercise in comedic timing, physical commitment, and rapid heightening.

## What It Trains
- **Domain:** D3 — The Scene
- **Principle(s):** Show, Don't Tell; The Audience Is the Final Scene Partner
- **Skill(s):** Heightening & Exploration; World-Building; Audience-Energy Management; Stage Presence & Clarity
- **Technique(s):** The 'ladder' (escalating beats); C.R.O.W. (Character, Relationship, Objective, Where); Breaking the 4th Wall / Direct Address; Cheating out
- **Focus:** comedy_game

**Objective:** To master the 'ladder' technique of escalation (heightening) and practice physicalizing claims ('Show, Don't Tell') under the high-energy pressure of direct audience address.

## Setup
Three to four players stand on stage facing the audience. The remaining participants act as the live studio audience. No props are used; all product demonstrations must be entirely mimed.

## How to Play
1. Ask the audience for a suggestion of a mundane household object or a completely fictional product name.
2. The players immediately step forward as enthusiastic, high-energy infomercial hosts, addressing the audience directly.
3. Host 1 introduces the product and demonstrates its basic, everyday function with exaggerated enthusiasm.
4. Host 2 introduces a common, relatable problem that the product solves, physically miming the struggle with high physical comedy.
5. Host 3 steps in to introduce the first 'escalated' feature of the product, showing how it solves an increasingly ridiculous or unexpected problem.
6. The players take turns climbing the 'ladder' of escalation, with each new claim becoming more absurd, grand, or existential than the last.
7. While one player pitches a feature, the other players must instantly physicalize and mime the demonstration, showing the product's hilarious effects or side effects.
8. The commercial culminates in a final, mind-blowing claim and a high-energy, synchronized slogan or jingle to close the pitch.

## Facilitation Notes
- Coaching cue: 'Don't just tell us it's strong—show us it lifting a car!' Push players to physicalize every single claim immediately.
- Pitfall: Players get stuck in 'talking heads' mode, listing features without showing them. Fix: Pause the pitch and instruct a player to step forward and mime the feature in action.
- Coaching cue: 'Climb the ladder!' Remind players that each step must build on the last. If the first feature is 'cleans dishes,' the second shouldn't be 'cleans forks'—it should be 'cleans your dirty conscience.'
- Encourage players to feed off the audience's energy, pausing for laughs and treating the crowd as their vital scene partner.

## Variations
- The Behind-the-Scenes: Play the commercial, then cut to the stressful corporate marketing meeting where they invented the product, then cut back to the live taping.
- Radio Spot: Turn off the stage lights and have players perform the commercial purely through vocal energy, sound effects, and spoken-word heightening.
- The Blooper Reel: The facilitator calls out 'Glitch!' or 'Blooper!' during the pitch, forcing the players to replay a moment where the product catastrophically malfunctions while maintaining their happy host personas.

## Debrief
- How did physicalizing the product's effects help you discover the next, more absurd feature?
- What did it feel like to 'climb the ladder' of escalation? How did you pace your claims so you didn't peak too early?
- How did direct audience address change your energy level compared to a standard character-driven scene?

## Safety & Inclusion
Ensure the performance space is clear of obstacles for physical demonstrations. Players should be mindful of their physical boundaries and can substitute high-energy verbal descriptions or smaller gestures if high-impact physical movement is not accessible.

## Why It Works
The infomercial format naturally demands a 'ladder' structure of escalation, moving from basic utility to life-changing miracle. By forcing players to physically demonstrate these claims, it instantly enforces 'Show, Don't Tell,' turning abstract ideas into concrete, hilarious physical comedy that feeds off audience energy.
