# Shared Lens

![Shared Lens — infographic](img/D3_P2_S5_T1_G721__group-scene-point-of-view.jpg){ .game-hero }

> Align a whole group's emotional perspective to instantly build a cohesive, high-agreement base reality.

## Overview
In this exercise, a small group of players collaborates to perform a scene where every character shares the exact same emotional point of view or attitude toward their situation. By mirroring and amplifying this collective perspective, players bypass interpersonal conflict to establish a rich, unified base reality. The humor and depth of the scene emerge from how the group collectively processes their environment through this single emotional filter.

**Objective:** To develop strong world-building and C.R.O.W. skills by establishing a clear, shared emotional foundation (Base Reality First) through group agreement and physical mirroring.

## Setup
A semi-circle of 4 to 6 players on stage, with the remaining participants acting as active observers. No props or chairs are required, though the space should be clear for physical movement.

## How to Play
1. Gather a group of 4 to 6 players on stage in a line.
2. Obtain a mundane location suggestion (e.g., a laundromat, a dentist's waiting room) and a specific emotional attitude or point of view (e.g., deeply suspicious, overwhelmingly joyful, profoundly bored).
3. The first player enters the performance space, establishing the physical environment (Where) and immediately embodying the chosen emotional attitude through their posture, movement, and initial line of dialogue.
4. The remaining players enter the scene one by one, immediately adopting the exact same emotional point of view and matching the established physical reality.
5. As players interact, they must 'yes-and' the shared emotion, finding different ways to express the same attitude toward the environment, the objects, and each other.
6. Focus on building the C.R.O.W. elements: clarify who they are to each other (Relationship), what they are doing (Objective), and where they are (Where), all filtered through their shared emotional lens.
7. The scene continues until a natural peak of collective emotional expression is reached, at which point the facilitator calls 'scene.'

## Facilitation Notes
- Side-coaching cue: 'Match the physical tension of the first player. If they are slouching in boredom, let that physical choice infect your body too.'
- Pitfall: Players trying to create conflict by introducing an opposing point of view too early. Fix: Remind them that the game's engine relies on total agreement; the fun comes from how they all feel the same way, not from arguing.
- Side-coaching cue: 'Find specific details in the room to react to. How does a suspicious person look at a washing machine?'
- Pitfall: The scene becoming a series of disconnected monologues. Fix: Encourage players to mirror and support each other's physical actions and dialogue, building a collective rhythm.

## Variations
- Organic Discovery: Start the scene without a predetermined emotion. The first player initiates with a physicalized attitude, and subsequent players must discover and mirror it organically.
- The Split Room: Divide the group into two factions (e.g., 3 vs 3) where each faction shares a unified but opposing point of view about the same event (e.g., half are terrified of jumping out of a plane, half are ecstatic).
- Emotional Shift: Start with one shared point of view, and have a single external trigger (like a phone call or a weather change) instantly shift the entire group to a new, shared point of view.

## Debrief
- How did sharing a single emotional point of view make establishing the 'Where' and 'Relationship' easier?
- What did you notice about the physical comedy or tension that arose from everyone agreeing completely?
- How did you use peripheral awareness to ensure you were matching the group's emotional level?

## Safety & Inclusion
Ensure that the chosen emotional points of view do not mock or caricature real-world trauma or mental health conditions. Keep the focus on situational attitudes (e.g., 'suspicious of the technology,' 'awed by the mundane') rather than clinical states.

## Why It Works
By removing the negotiation of 'how do we feel about each other/this place,' players can focus entirely on world-building and deepening their relationships. Complete agreement on the emotional reality (Base Reality First) creates a strong 'Group Mind' and allows the scene's comedy or drama to emerge naturally from the environment.
