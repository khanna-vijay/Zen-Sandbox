# Human Pillars

<div class="game-hero-ph" markdown>🎨 *Infographic coming soon*</div>

> Actors justify random words supplied by audience volunteers standing on stage as physical pillars.

## Overview
Two actors perform a scene while standing next to two audience volunteers who act as pillars. Whenever an actor needs a word to complete a sentence, they tap their pillar, who instantly provides a random word that the actor must seamlessly justify and integrate into the scene's reality.

## What It Trains
- **Domain:** D3 — The Scene
- **Principle(s):** Base Reality First; Yes, And; The Audience Is the Final Scene Partner
- **Skill(s):** Justification; Offer Reception; Stage Presence & Clarity
- **Technique(s):** Justify the absurd; Endowment-acceptance
- **Focus:** comedy_game

**Objective:** Develops rapid justification skills, active offer reception, and the ability to maintain a grounded base reality even when injected with absurd, unpredictable audience suggestions.

## Setup
Two actors stand center stage. Two audience volunteers are invited up to stand next to each actor, acting as their respective pillars. The facilitator gets a simple, grounded suggestion for a relationship and location to establish a base reality.

## How to Play
1. Invite two audience volunteers to the stage and position each one standing slightly behind and to the side of one of the two main actors.
2. Explain to the volunteers that their only job is to provide a single word or short phrase whenever their designated actor taps them on the shoulder.
3. Obtain a grounded suggestion from the remaining audience to establish the scene's base reality.
4. The two actors begin a standard, grounded scene, focusing on establishing their relationship, environment, and platform.
5. At any point, an actor can pause mid-sentence, tap their volunteer's shoulder, and wait for the volunteer to say a word.
6. The actor must immediately accept the word, complete their sentence using it, and instantly justify why that word makes perfect sense in the context of the scene.
7. The other actor must accept this new justified reality and continue the scene, building on the newly established information.
8. The scene continues with actors alternating taps to keep the narrative moving, culminating in a natural resolution after about three to five minutes.

## Facilitation Notes
- Encourage actors to establish a strong, normal base reality before tapping the pillars for the first time; this makes the justification of absurd words much funnier and more impactful.
- Side-coach actors to avoid winking at the audience or treating the word as a mistake; they must treat the volunteer's word as absolute, serious truth within the scene.
- If a volunteer freezes or whispers, the actor should confidently repeat what they think they heard, or gently prompt them with a supportive question to keep the energy high.
- Pitfall: Tapping the pillars too frequently, turning the scene into a rapid-fire word game rather than a cohesive story. Fix: Remind players to build the relationship and environment between taps.

## Variations
- Emotional Pillars: The volunteer provides an emotion instead of a word, which the actor must immediately adopt and justify.
- Sound Effect Pillars: The volunteer makes a random sound effect, which the actor must identify and justify as a real occurrence in the scene.
- Physical Pillars: The volunteer strikes a physical pose when tapped, which the actor must immediately mirror and justify.

## Debrief
- How did establishing a strong base reality early on help you justify the more bizarre words later?
- What strategies did you use to make an absurd word sound completely logical and natural to your character?
- How did it feel to share the stage and control with an audience member, and how did that affect your stage presence?

## Safety & Inclusion
Ensure volunteers are comfortable standing on stage and being gently tapped on the shoulder. If physical touch is a boundary, actors can point or bow to their pillar instead of tapping.

## Why It Works
By forcing actors to integrate completely random, external inputs into a structured scene, the game strips away planning and forces pure presence. The comedy and narrative satisfaction come from the tension between the absurd, random word and the actor's commitment to making it make sense within their established base reality.
