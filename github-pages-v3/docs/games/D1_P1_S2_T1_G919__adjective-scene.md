# The Adjective Dial

![The Adjective Dial — infographic](img/D1_P1_S2_T1_G919__adjective-scene.jpg){ .game-hero }

> Scale your commitment to a single descriptive trait from a whisper to a roar.

## Overview
Players perform a grounded scene while embodying a specific assigned adjective. As the scene unfolds, the facilitator calls out intensity levels from 1 to 10, prompting players to dynamically scale their physical and emotional commitment.

## What It Trains
- **Domain:** D1 — The Self
- **Principle(s):** Commit 100%; Yes, And; Show, Don't Tell
- **Skill(s):** Emotional Fluidity; Physicality & Space Work; Offer Reception; World-Building
- **Technique(s):** The Emotional Dial (1→10); Character Walks/Centers; Endowment-acceptance; C.R.O.W. (Character, Relationship, Objective, Where)
- **Focus:** skill_drill

**Objective:** Develops emotional fluidity and total commitment by challenging players to explore the extreme ranges of a single trait without losing narrative connection.

## Setup
A clear performance space. Two to four players on stage, with the remaining group acting as active observers. No props or special materials needed.

## How to Play
1. Ask the group or audience for one or two distinct adjectives (e.g., 'suspicious', 'ecstatic', 'melancholy').
2. Assign one adjective to each active player on stage.
3. Instruct the players to begin a simple, everyday scene (e.g., preparing a meal, waiting for a train) starting at a baseline intensity of 2 out of 10.
4. Players must express their assigned adjective through their physicality, vocal tone, and reaction to their partner, rather than explicitly naming the trait.
5. Once the scene's platform is established, the facilitator calls out a new number on the dial (e.g., 'Dial it up to 5!').
6. Players must immediately elevate their physical and emotional commitment to match the new level while continuing the scene's narrative.
7. The facilitator continues to adjust the dial up and down (ranging from 1 to 10), challenging players to find the physical and vocal extremes of their assigned trait.
8. End the scene once the players have successfully sustained a level 10 commitment while maintaining their connection to the scene's reality.

## Facilitation Notes
- Coaching cue: 'Show it in your body, not just your words. How does a suspicious person chop an onion?'
- Coaching cue: 'Don't jump straight to 10. Explore the subtle differences between a level 3 and a level 4.'
- Pitfall: Players often talk about their emotion ('I am so anxious!') instead of showing it. Fix: Remind them to use 'Show, Don't Tell' by letting the emotion leak into their physical actions and subtext.
- Pitfall: Players lose the plot or stop listening when the intensity gets high. Fix: Side-coach them to 'Yes, and' their partner's reality, using the heightened emotion to fuel their reactions to the scene's environment.

## Variations
- Secret Adjectives: Players draw their adjectives secretly and must discover each other's traits purely through physical and verbal offers.
- Opposing Dials: One player's dial goes up while the other player's dial goes down, creating a dynamic power or emotional imbalance.
- The Shared Dial: The entire ensemble shares a single adjective, and the facilitator dials the entire room's atmosphere up and down together.

## Debrief
- How did changing the physical intensity of your adjective change the choices you made in the scene?
- What did it feel like to commit 100% to a level 10 emotion? Where did you feel that in your body?
- How did your partner's shifting intensity affect your own emotional state?

## Safety & Inclusion
Ensure players understand that high-intensity emotions (like anger or panic) should be expressed safely without physical contact or genuine distress. Encourage players to explore the physical and vocal boundaries of the emotion within their personal comfort levels.

## Why It Works
By using a numerical dial, players bypass the intellectual fear of 'overacting' and are given permission to commit 100% to extreme states. It teaches emotional fluidity by showing that any character trait can be dialed up or down without breaking the reality of the scene.
