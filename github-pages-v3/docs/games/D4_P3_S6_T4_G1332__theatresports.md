# Competitive Improv Showdown

> Two teams battle in a high-energy, judged arena of spontaneous challenges and audience-driven points.

## Overview
A structured, high-energy performance format where two teams face off in a series of improvised challenges, games, and scenes. Guided by an MC and evaluated by a panel of judges, players must balance competitive drive with excellent stagecraft, pacing, and audience engagement. The result is a fast-paced, self-correcting show that teaches players how to read the room and serve the overall performance.

## What It Trains
- **Domain:** D4 — The Ensemble
- **Principle(s):** Serve the Piece; The Audience Is the Final Scene Partner; Serve the Story
- **Skill(s):** Format Literacy; Audience-Energy Management; Pacing & Rhythm; Game Identification
- **Technique(s):** Longform vs. shortform mechanics; Tag-running (riding a laugh wave); Edits (Sweep, Tag-Out, Sound/Light)
- **Focus:** mixed

**Objective:** Develops format literacy, audience-energy management, and pacing. Players learn to transition smoothly between different shortform structures, recognize when a scene has peaked, and collaborate with the audience as their ultimate scene partner.

## Setup
A large open stage with a clear performance area. Two team benches on opposite sides. A central table for 1-3 judges equipped with a horn (or buzzer), scorecards, and a penalty basket (to place over a player's head or hold as a physical penalty marker). An MC/host with a microphone. An audience seated facing the stage.

## How to Play
1. Divide the players into two equal teams (typically 3 to 5 players per team) and seat them on opposing sides of the stage, with the judges positioned centrally in view of both the stage and the audience.
2. The MC warms up the audience, explains the rules of the showdown, and introduces the judges and the competing teams.
3. Determine the starting team via a coin toss. The winning team issues a specific challenge to the opposing team (e.g., a particular shortform game, a scene with a specific constraint, or a stylistic limitation).
4. The challenged team can either accept the challenge or 'balk' (protest) if the challenge is deemed unfair, repetitive, or physically impossible. The judges rule on the balk; if sustained, the challenging team must offer an alternative.
5. Both teams perform their version of the challenge within a strict time limit (typically 2 to 4 minutes per scene). The judges monitor the time and use the horn to signal warnings or to immediately end a scene if it loses momentum ('boring' horn).
6. Judges can issue penalties for stage infractions (such as blocking, excessive blue humor, or breaking format rules). Penalized players must wear the penalty basket or sit out for a designated period.
7. After both teams have completed the challenge, the MC calls for audience voting (via applause, cheering, or scorecards). The judges interpret the audience's response and award points (typically 5 points for a win, 0 for a loss).
8. The teams alternate roles, with the previously challenged team now issuing a new challenge to their opponents. Repeat this cycle for a set number of rounds or until the 45-minute time limit is reached.
9. The MC and judges tally the final scores, declare the winning team, and lead a final bow for all performers to emphasize collaborative success over actual rivalry.

## Facilitation Notes
- The 'boring' horn is a tool for pacing, not punishment. Teach judges to honk when a scene has plateaued or lost its narrative drive, helping players learn to edit quickly.
- Ensure the competition remains 'good-naturedly fierce.' The secret of this format is that both teams must work together to make the entire show successful, even while pretending to fight for points.
- If a team is struggling with a challenge, the opposing team can 'save' the scene by entering as supporting characters, demonstrating high-level ensemble support over pure competition.
- Keep the MC's energy high and transitions tight. The momentum of the show relies on rapid shifts between challenges, performances, and scoring.

## Variations
- Judicial Challenges: Instead of teams challenging each other, the judges or the MC dictate the games and constraints for each round.
- The Ombuds Variant: Replace the panel of judges with a single 'Ombuds' who acts as MC, referee, and scorekeeper, simplifying the logistics for smaller casts.
- Audience Challenge: Allow members of the audience to write down challenges or constraints before the show, which are drawn from a hat by the MC.

## Debrief
- How did the competitive framing affect your commitment to the scenes? Did it make you play safer or take bigger risks?
- How did the presence of the horn and the threat of penalties change your awareness of pacing and scene edits?
- In what ways did you 'serve the piece' and support the overall show, even when your team wasn't the one performing?
- How did you read and respond to the audience's energy during the voting process?

## Safety & Inclusion
Establish clear boundaries for penalties and physical gags. The penalty basket should be used lightheartedly and never forced on a player who expresses discomfort. Ensure all challenges respect the physical safety and accessibility needs of all active players.

## Why It Works
By gamifying the performance, this format lowers the stakes of failure while raising the energy of the room. The explicit rules, time limits, and judicial interventions force players to focus on format literacy and tight editing. It teaches performers that the ultimate goal is not to win, but to co-create an entertaining, well-paced show with their opponents and the audience.
