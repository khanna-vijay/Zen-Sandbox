# The Game Blueprint

<div class="game-hero-ph" markdown>🎨 *Infographic coming soon*</div>

> Align your scene's comedic engine instantly by comparing silent blueprints with your partner.

## Overview
A structured, virtual-friendly exercise where one player initiates a scene with a hidden comedic premise, and their partner attempts to identify and play into that dynamic. After a brief exchange, both players freeze and silently write down what they believe the 'game of the scene' is, revealing their answers to test alignment and communication clarity.

## What It Trains
- **Domain:** D3 — The Scene
- **Principle(s):** Base Reality First; Yes, And; Make Your Partner a Genius
- **Skill(s):** Game Identification; Heightening & Exploration; Active Listening; Offer Reception; Active Gifting
- **Technique(s):** Finding & Playing the Game; If this is true, what else is true?; Meisner Repetition; Endowment-acceptance; Endowment-gifting drills
- **Focus:** skill_drill

**Objective:** To train players in establishing a clear, playable comedic pattern (the 'game') from a single initiation, and to develop the partner's ability to actively listen, identify, and co-sign that pattern immediately.

## Setup
Players work in pairs in a virtual meeting space. Each player must have a small physical whiteboard, a piece of paper, and a dark marker. The facilitator prepares a list of prompts categorized into three types: a distinct character trait, an unusual circumstance, or a strong opinion.

## How to Play
1. Assign one player in each pair as the 'Architect' and the other as the 'Builder.'
2. The facilitator privately sends the Architect a prompt (e.g., via direct message) from one of the three categories: Character Trait, Unusual Circumstance, or Strong Opinion. The Builder must not see this prompt.
3. The Architect takes 10 to 15 seconds to silently plan a 'game'—a repeatable, escalating pattern of behavior or conflict—that logically flows from this prompt.
4. The Architect initiates a two-person virtual scene, immediately embodying the prompt and establishing the comedic pattern within their first two lines.
5. The Builder enters the scene, accepting the reality, actively listening for the unusual behavior, and playing directly into the emerging pattern.
6. After 5 to 7 total dialogue exchanges (about 60 to 90 seconds), the facilitator calls 'Freeze!'
7. Without speaking, both players immediately write down a single, concise sentence on their whiteboard or paper defining what they believe the 'game of the scene' is.
8. On the facilitator's count of three, both players hold their whiteboards up to their webcams to reveal their written 'blueprints.'
9. The pair compares their answers to see if the Architect's intended game matches the Builder's perceived game, followed by a brief self-debrief on what specific choices led to alignment or misalignment.

## Facilitation Notes
- Coaching Cue: Encourage Architects to avoid hiding the prompt. Embody the choice boldly in the very first line so the Builder has immediate data to work with.
- Coaching Cue: Remind Builders not to invent a new game. Your job is to find the game your partner is already offering and make them look like a genius by heightening it.
- Pitfall: Players writing vague descriptions like 'we are arguing.' Fix: Instruct them to write the game as a specific pattern, such as 'One person is obsessed with X, while the other tries to normalize it.'
- Virtual Tip: Ensure players keep their cameras on and use thick markers so their written blueprints are easily readable on screen.

## Variations
- The Observer's Blueprint: Introduce a third player as an active observer who also writes down the game, providing an objective audience perspective on whether the dynamic was clear.
- The Silent Build: Run the scene entirely through physical action and object work (no dialogue) before the freeze and reveal, forcing players to communicate the game non-verbally.
- The Mid-Scene Pivot: If the blueprints do not match, unfreeze the scene and have the players immediately adjust to play the Builder's perceived game for another 30 seconds.

## Debrief
- For the Architect: What specific verbal or physical choices did you make to make your intended game obvious from the start?
- For the Builder: What was the exact moment or line that made the game click for you?
- If your blueprints didn't match, where did the communication break down, and how could a simpler choice have saved it?
- How does knowing your partner's 'blueprint' change how you support them in a scene?

## Safety & Inclusion
Since players are generating games based on strong opinions or unusual circumstances, remind them to keep choices grounded in comedic absurdity rather than punching down or targeting real-world marginalized groups. Ensure virtual chat is used respectfully for prompt delivery.

## Why It Works
This game works because it gamifies the feedback loop of scene initiation. By forcing a silent, written reveal, it strips away polite agreement and exposes the gap between what an improviser thinks they are communicating and what their partner is actually receiving. It directly reinforces the 'If this is true, what else is true' engine by requiring both players to align on the core comedic premise before they can successfully heighten it.
