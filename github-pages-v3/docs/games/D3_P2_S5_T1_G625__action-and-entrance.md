# Action and Entrance

![Action and Entrance — infographic](img/D3_P2_S5_T1_G625__action-and-entrance.jpg){ .game-hero }

> Establish a rich physical environment and a compelling relationship through committed action and emotional entry.

## Overview
Two players build a solid foundation for a scene by combining physical environment work with an emotionally charged entrance. One player initiates a silent, committed physical activity to define the space, while the second player enters with a distinct emotional attitude to establish their relationship. Together, they discover a scene where the dialogue focuses on their connection rather than merely narrating their physical actions.

## What It Trains
- **Domain:** D3 — The Scene
- **Principle(s):** Base Reality First; Show, Don't Tell; Fail Joyfully; Make Your Partner a Genius
- **Skill(s):** World-Building; Physicality & Space Work; Emotional Fluidity; Offer Reception
- **Technique(s):** C.R.O.W. (Character, Relationship, Objective, Where); Object work; Endowment-acceptance
- **Focus:** skill_drill

**Objective:** To master the C.R.O.W. framework (Character, Relationship, Objective, Where) by prioritizing a strong base reality, using physical space work to define the setting, and using emotional offers to establish relationships without relying on verbal exposition.

## Setup
An open performance space with a designated 'stage' area and an 'off-stage' wing. The rest of the group sits as an active audience. No physical props are used; all objects must be mimed.

## How to Play
1. Player A steps onto the stage and immediately begins a silent, highly detailed physical activity (e.g., folding laundry, painting a wall, preparing a meal), focusing on the weight, size, and placement of imaginary objects.
2. Player A must fully commit to this activity, behaving as if they are alone and unaware of any impending entrance, establishing the 'Where' of the scene.
3. Player B prepares off-stage, adopting a clear, strong emotional state or attitude (e.g., anxious, ecstatic, irritated) before they cross the threshold.
4. Player B enters the stage, carrying their pre-established emotion, and initiates contact with Player A to define their relationship ('Who') and 'Objective'.
5. Player B must accept and support Player A's physical reality, avoiding any criticism or denial of the activity Player A is performing.
6. Both players engage in dialogue that focuses on their relationship, history, or feelings, using the physical activity as a background reality rather than the sole topic of conversation.
7. The scene continues for two to three minutes, allowing the players to discover how the physical task and the emotional dynamic influence each other, before the facilitator calls 'scene'.

## Facilitation Notes
- Side-coaching cue: 'Give those objects weight! Don't let your imaginary coffee cup float or disappear.'
- Side-coaching cue: 'Player B, don't enter cold. Feel your emotion in your body before your foot touches the stage.'
- Common Pitfall: Talking only about the activity (e.g., 'You are washing that plate well. Yes, it is dirty.'). Fix: Encourage players to talk about their relationship or a shared history while their hands keep doing the work.
- Common Pitfall: Criticizing the activity (e.g., 'Why are you doing that so stupidly?'). Fix: Remind players to make their partner a genius by respecting their choices and finding a supportive way to connect.

## Variations
- Skill Level-Up: Have Player A decide beforehand whether they love or hate the activity, or whether they are highly skilled or completely incompetent at it, letting this shape their physical characterization.
- Emotional Shift: Have Player B enter with a specific primary emotion (fear, anger, joy, sadness) and require Player A to gradually mirror or contrast that emotion as the scene progresses.
- Silent Foundation: Run the first minute of the scene in complete silence, relying entirely on physical cooperation and emotional body language before any dialogue begins.

## Debrief
- How did having a physical activity to focus on affect your comfort level and spontaneity when the scene started?
- For the entering player, how did having a pre-established emotion shape your character's relationship to the other person?
- What happened when the dialogue moved away from describing the physical task and focused on the relationship instead?

## Safety & Inclusion
Ensure players choose physical activities that respect their own and others' physical boundaries. If a player has mobility constraints, the physical activity can be adapted to a seated or low-impact task (e.g., writing, sorting small items) without losing any of the dramatic value.

## Why It Works
This game works because it splits the cognitive load of scene-building. Player A establishes the physical 'Where' through committed object work, while Player B brings the emotional 'Who' and 'Relationship'. By keeping the dialogue focused on their connection rather than the physical task, players naturally avoid dry exposition and build a rich, believable base reality.
