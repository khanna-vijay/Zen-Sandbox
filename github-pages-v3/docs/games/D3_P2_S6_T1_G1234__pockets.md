# Pocket Lines

<div class="game-hero-ph" markdown>🎨 *Infographic coming soon*</div>

> Justify absurd, audience-written lines seamlessly into a grounded, two-person scene.

## Overview
Two players establish a grounded base reality before periodically drawing random, audience-written phrases from their pockets. The challenge is to deliver these non-sequiturs as their character's direct, intentional dialogue and immediately justify why they said them. This builds comedic tension as players work together to make the bizarre lines make perfect sense within their scene.

## What It Trains
- **Domain:** D3 — The Scene
- **Principle(s):** Base Reality First; Yes, And; The Audience Is the Final Scene Partner
- **Skill(s):** Justification; Offer Reception
- **Technique(s):** Justify the absurd; Endowment-acceptance
- **Focus:** comedy_game

**Objective:** To develop advanced justification skills (specifically justifying the absurd) and reinforce the importance of establishing a strong base reality before introducing chaotic elements.

## Setup
Provide the audience with small slips of paper and pens before the session or show. Ask them to write down single, complete, and relatively mundane or slightly unusual sentences. Fold these slips and distribute three to each of the two performing players. Players must place these slips into their pockets (or designated containers) without looking at them. The performance space requires a clear stage area for two players and seating for the audience.

## How to Play
1. Ask the audience for a simple, grounded suggestion to start the scene, such as a relationship or a mundane location.
2. The two players begin the scene by establishing a strong, believable base reality, focusing on their relationship, environment, and a clear platform.
3. Once the scene's reality is stable, either player may choose a moment of high stakes or transition to reach into their pocket and pull out one slip of paper.
4. The player must read the slip aloud exactly as written, delivering it as their character's direct, active dialogue rather than quoting someone else.
5. The speaking player, or their scene partner, must immediately justify the line, treating it as a completely logical and meaningful statement within their current reality.
6. The players continue the scene, taking turns drawing and integrating their remaining slips of paper at natural dramatic intervals.
7. The scene concludes once all six slips have been drawn, justified, and the narrative reaches a satisfying resolution.

## Facilitation Notes
- Side-coaching cue: 'Own the line!' Remind players not to use distancing language like 'My grandmother used to say...' or 'I read a book once that said...'. The character must mean the words directly.
- Pitfall: Drawing slips too quickly. Fix: Coach players to establish at least 30-45 seconds of solid, grounded base reality before the first slip is drawn, and space subsequent draws out to allow for deep justification.
- Side-coaching cue: 'Yes-and the reaction.' The partner must react honestly to the bizarre statement, treating it as a real choice that reveals character or plot.
- Pitfall: Treating the line as a joke and breaking character. Fix: Encourage players to maintain high emotional stakes; the more seriously the character takes the absurd line, the funnier and more satisfying the justification becomes.

## Variations
- Blind Exchange: Instead of pockets, the slips are scattered face-down on the stage floor, and players must pick them up when they physically touch or interact with an object in the space.
- The Interrogator: One player plays a grounded detective who does not have slips, while the suspect has five slips in their pocket and must use them to answer the detective's hard-hitting questions.

## Debrief
- How did establishing a strong base reality early on make it easier to justify the absurd lines later?
- What strategies did you use to make a completely random sentence sound like a natural, intentional choice for your character?
- How did your partner's reaction help validate the strange things you were forced to say?

## Safety & Inclusion
Ensure the audience is instructed to keep written suggestions respectful and free of offensive content. If a player draws a slip that makes them or their partner uncomfortable, they have full permission to quietly discard it and draw another, or simply ignore the mechanic and continue the scene normally.

## Why It Works
This game works because it forces players to practice extreme offer reception and immediate justification. By stripping away the player's control over their own dialogue, it bypasses intellectual planning and forces them to find logical connections in real-time. Grounding the scene first ensures that the absurd lines disrupt a stable world, which maximizes the comedic payoff for the audience.
