# Sounds Like a Song

> Transform ordinary spoken dialogue into high-energy, spontaneous musical numbers on a single cue.

## Overview
In this high-energy game, players initiate a standard spoken scene until an off-stage player or facilitator calls out "Sounds like a song!" The active players must instantly transition into an improvised musical number inspired by the very last line spoken. It challenges players to listen intently to every word and commit fully to sudden, unscripted musicality.

## What It Trains
- **Domain:** D2 — The Partner
- **Principle(s):** Yes, And; Commit 100%; The First Thought Is a Gift
- **Skill(s):** Active Listening; Unfiltered Spontaneity; Vocal Craft; Heightening & Exploration
- **Technique(s):** Last Word Response; Vocal characterization; The 'ladder' (escalating beats)
- **Focus:** comedy_game

**Objective:** To develop active listening and immediate agreement (Yes, And) by using the last spoken line as the launchpad for a spontaneous song, fostering vocal confidence and unfiltered spontaneity.

## Setup
An in-person playing space with two players on stage and the remaining players standing on the wings as the active audience/facilitators. No instruments or props are required; the music is entirely a cappella and self-generated.

## How to Play
1. Two players step forward to begin a standard, grounded scene based on a simple suggestion.
2. The players on stage build the scene naturally, focusing on clear relationship dynamics and specific dialogue.
3. At any point, an off-stage player or the facilitator yells, "Sounds like a song!"
4. The active players must immediately freeze their scene work and transition into singing.
5. The song's title, chorus, or opening line must be the exact last sentence spoken right before the interruption.
6. Both players collaborate on the song, supporting each other with backing vocals, physical choreography, or rhyming verses.
7. Once the song reaches a natural, high-energy climax, the players transition back into the spoken scene, treating the musical outburst as their reality.
8. The scene continues until another "Sounds like a song!" cue is called, or the facilitator edits the scene.

## Facilitation Notes
- Coaching Cue: "Sing the last line!" If players hesitate, remind them that the last spoken sentence is their instant chorus.
- Pitfall: Players trying to think of a clever song topic instead of using the exact last line. Fix: Force them to repeat the last line rhythmically to start the beat.
- Coaching Cue: "Support with movement!" Encourage the non-singing partner to establish a physical rhythm or dance step to ground the song's tempo.
- Pitfall: The song goes on too long and loses momentum. Fix: Coach the off-stage players to look for a strong button or final chord to wrap up the song and return to the scene.
- Coaching Cue: "Yes-and the style!" If one player starts singing opera, the other must match that genre or enthusiastically contrast it, rather than remaining silent.

## Variations
- Instrumental Accompaniment: If a musician is available, they can instantly provide a backing beat or melody matching the emotional tone of the last line.
- The Chorus Line: When the cue is called, the off-stage players rush on to act as backup dancers and singers, supporting the main duo.
- Genre Swap: The person calling the cue can specify a genre (e.g., "Sounds like a country song!" or "Sounds like a heavy metal song!").

## Debrief
- How did knowing a song could happen at any moment change how closely you listened to your partner's exact words?
- What strategies did you use to support your partner when they took the lead on a verse?
- How does committing 100% to a silly or unexpected musical choice make the scene feel safer and more fun?

## Safety & Inclusion
Ensure players are aware they do not need to be "good" singers; vocal play, chanting, or rhythmic speaking are completely valid. Encourage physical safety during spontaneous choreography, keeping the stage clear of tripping hazards.

## Why It Works
By forcing players to use the exact last line spoken as the song's foundation, the game operationalizes the Last Word Response technique. It eliminates the anxiety of invention by giving players their first lyric, proving that the first thought is indeed a gift. The sudden shift in medium demands total commitment, which bypasses the analytical mind and unlocks pure, playful spontaneity.
