# The Aligned Architects

<div class="game-hero-ph" markdown>🎨 *Infographic coming soon*</div>

> Build an imaginary structure while dynamically negotiating personal boundaries and practicing radical creative honesty.

## Overview
A collaborative physical and conceptual building game where players co-create an imaginary structure. The true measure of success is not the final architectural marvel, but how fluidly and honestly players navigate, communicate, and respect each other's unspoken boundaries in real time.

## What It Trains
- **Domain:** D2 — The Partner
- **Principle(s):** Consent & Boundaries; Yes, And; Truth Over Pandering
- **Skill(s):** Boundary Navigation; Offer Reception
- **Technique(s):** Check-ins; Cut calls; Negotiating physical contact; Yes, And… sentence games
- **Focus:** skill_drill

**Objective:** To develop active boundary navigation, real-time consent check-ins, and the principle of 'Truth Over Pandering' by integrating personal limits directly into the collaborative 'Yes, And' process.

## Setup
An open, safe physical space with clear boundaries. No props are needed. Players stand in a circle or semi-circle, ready to step into the active playing area.

## How to Play
1. Set Private Blueprints: Before starting, each player privately decides on one to three personal boundaries for the round (e.g., 'no physical contact above the shoulders' or 'my section must remain perfectly symmetrical'). These remain secret until triggered.
2. Initiate the Build: One player steps into the space and initiates the construction of an imaginary structure by making a physical or verbal offer (e.g., placing a hand at waist height and saying, 'I am laying down a heavy stone foundation here').
3. Add with Awareness: A second player steps in to add to the structure, using 'Yes, And' to build upon the first player's offer while internally checking if the offer or their own planned addition violates their private boundaries.
4. Execute Perfect Alignment: If the previous offer and the new addition align perfectly with the player's private boundaries, they seamlessly add to the structure with a standard physical or verbal offer.
5. Navigate Boundary Triggers: If an offer or action triggers a player's private boundary, they must not ignore it. Instead, they respond with a 'Yes, And... with a boundary negotiation,' stating their limit and offering an alternative that honors both their safety and the scene's momentum.
6. Utilize Active Check-ins: At any point, if an offer feels ambiguous or a player senses unspoken discomfort, any participant can call a brief out-of-character 'Check-in' to clarify comfort levels before resuming.
7. Employ the Cut Call: If a boundary is directly crossed or safety is compromised, any player can immediately call 'Cut!' to halt all action, neutrally state the boundary crossed, and reset the scene without judgment.
8. Rotate and Iterate: Continue adding players to the build one by one, allowing the structure to grow dynamically for 5 to 7 minutes before resetting with new private boundaries.

## Facilitation Notes
- Normalize Boundary Setting: Frame the 'Cut' call and boundary negotiations as creative gifts that enhance trust, rather than interruptions or failures.
- Side-Coach Active Listening: Encourage players to pay close attention to subtle physical cues, hesitation, or changes in vocal tone that might indicate a boundary is being approached.
- Model the Negotiation: Demonstrate how to deliver a 'Yes, And... with a boundary negotiation' so players see how to pivot an offer constructively without shutting down the scene's energy.
- Monitor Group Safety: Keep a close eye on physical proximity and intensity. If you notice a player looking uncomfortable but not speaking up, gently prompt a group 'Check-in'.
- Keep Rounds Brief: Limit each building phase to 5-7 minutes to maintain high focus and allow ample time for a deep, reflective debrief.

## Variations
- The Emotional Blueprint: Instead of physical boundaries, players set emotional or narrative boundaries (e.g., 'I will not accept offers that make my character feel incompetent' or 'I will not engage in high-status conflict').
- Blind Architect: One player is designated as the lead architect who cannot see the physical build but must direct it through verbal check-ins, forcing the team to communicate spatial boundaries purely through dialogue.
- Rapid Shifts: Every two minutes, the facilitator calls 'Shift!', prompting players to instantly adopt a new private boundary mid-build.

## Debrief
- How did it feel to hold onto your private boundaries when an exciting or high-energy offer came your way?
- What strategies did you use to negotiate your boundaries while still keeping the collaborative momentum of the build alive?
- How did the presence of explicit check-ins and the 'Cut' option affect your sense of creative freedom and trust in your partners?
- Did you experience the urge to 'pander' or ignore your boundary to keep the scene smooth? How did you overcome that impulse?

## Safety & Inclusion
This game is highly safety-sensitive. Establish a firm, non-judgmental atmosphere before playing. Ensure all players understand that 'Cut' is an absolute, non-negotiable safety tool. Respect all physical and emotional boundaries without requiring players to justify or explain their personal limits beyond stating the boundary itself.

## Why It Works
By gamifying the negotiation of boundaries, this exercise removes the social stigma of saying 'no' in collaborative spaces. It teaches players that true 'Yes, And' is not blind compliance, but rather the active acceptance of your partner's presence combined with an honest, safe contribution of your own.
