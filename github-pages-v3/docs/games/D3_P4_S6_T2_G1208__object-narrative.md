# Object Perspectives

<div class="game-hero-ph" markdown>🎨 *Infographic coming soon*</div>

> Three distinct characters weave a unified narrative web around a single, ordinary physical object.

## Overview
In this exercise, three players deliver sequential monologues inspired by a single everyday object resting on a central chair. Each player adopts a unique character perspective, building upon the narrative details established by the previous speakers. The goal is to create a cohesive, surprising story where the object serves as the connective tissue.

## What It Trains
- **Domain:** D3 — The Scene
- **Principle(s):** Serve the Story; Yes, And
- **Skill(s):** Justification; Narrative Architecture; Active Listening; Unfiltered Spontaneity
- **Technique(s):** Reincorporation-as-justification
- **Focus:** narrative

**Objective:** To develop narrative justification and reincorporation skills by listening deeply to previous monologues and discovering unexpected, logical connections between different characters and a central object.

## Setup
Place a single chair in the center of the playing area. Position the rest of the group in a semi-circle facing the chair. Select a random, mundane physical object from the room (such as a stapler, a shoe, or a coffee mug) and place it clearly on the chair.

## How to Play
1. The facilitator places a random object on the central chair to serve as the physical anchor for the scene.
2. Player One steps forward, assumes a distinct character, and delivers a short monologue of approximately one minute that establishes their relationship to the object and sets a narrative in motion.
3. Player One steps back. Player Two, inspired by the first monologue, steps forward to deliver a monologue from a completely different character's point of view.
4. Player Two must actively listen to Player One's details and justify how their new character is connected to the same object or situation, avoiding the most obvious relationship choice.
5. Player Two steps back. Player Three steps forward to deliver the final monologue, assuming a third distinct character.
6. Player Three must reincorporate elements from both previous monologues, providing a satisfying resolution or a unifying justification that ties all three perspectives together.
7. Once the cycle of three is complete, the facilitator resets the stage with a new object, and a new trio of players steps up to perform.

## Facilitation Notes
- Side-coaching cue: 'Listen for the details, not just the object.' Encourage players to latch onto specific emotional or factual details dropped by the previous speaker.
- Pitfall: Players making the object the literal talking point of every sentence. Fix: Remind them that the object is a catalyst or a symbol; the monologue should be about the character's life, relationships, and stakes.
- Side-coaching cue: 'Avoid the obvious connection.' If the first character is a doctor, the second shouldn't automatically be the patient; perhaps they are the florist who delivered the flowers to the recovery room.
- Pitfall: Player Three ignoring Player Two's contributions to focus only on Player One. Fix: Coach Player Three to actively synthesize details from both preceding monologues to achieve true narrative reincorporation.

## Variations
- The Silent Witness: The object must remain untouched, but characters can interact with the space around it, treating it as a sacred, dangerous, or forgotten item.
- The Passing Relic: Instead of the object staying on the chair, each character physically handles the object during their monologue and leaves it in a new state or position for the next player.
- Time Jump: Each monologue takes place in a different era (e.g., past, present, and future), tracing the object's journey through time.

## Debrief
- How did choosing a less obvious character relationship help expand the world of the story?
- What specific details from the first monologue did you find easiest or most exciting to justify and reincorporate?
- How did focusing on the characters' emotional stakes prevent the monologues from becoming just a list of facts about the object?

## Safety & Inclusion
Ensure the physical object chosen is safe to handle and does not carry any loaded or triggering associations for the group. If players have mobility constraints, the chair can be placed at a height or position that is easily accessible, or the object can be held up by the facilitator.

## Why It Works
This game works because it forces players to practice 'Yes, And' through narrative justification rather than direct dialogue. By requiring different character perspectives, it prevents the story from moving in a straight, predictable line, instead building a rich, multi-dimensional world where every detail introduced is treated as an essential, justified truth.
