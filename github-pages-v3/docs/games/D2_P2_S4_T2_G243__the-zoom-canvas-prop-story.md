# The Virtual Prop Relay

> Transform your video frames into a collaborative, physical chain reaction of imaginary objects.

## Overview
A dynamic virtual game where players use physical space and mime to pass a single, evolving imaginary object from screen to screen. By treating their individual video frames as a shared physical canvas, participants must closely observe and physically accept the weight, texture, and properties of the object established by the previous player before adding their own narrative twist.

## What It Trains
- **Domain:** D2 — The Partner
- **Principle(s):** Commit 100%; Yes, And; Show, Don't Tell; Group Mind
- **Skill(s):** Physicality & Space Work; Active Listening; Offer Reception; World-Building
- **Technique(s):** Object work; Endowment-acceptance; C.R.O.W. (Character, Relationship, Objective, Where)
- **Focus:** mixed

**Objective:** To develop deep visual offer reception and physical endowment-acceptance in a virtual environment, training players to listen with their eyes and commit fully to physical space work.

## Setup
Conducted on a video conferencing platform. All participants must enable their cameras and switch to a grid or gallery view so everyone is visible. The facilitator acts as the director, utilizing the platform's spotlight or pin feature to highlight the active player. No physical props are required, but players should ensure their physical background is relatively clear and their upper body is fully visible in the frame.

## How to Play
1. Instruct all players to turn on their cameras and switch their display to a grid view so they can see the entire group.
2. Explain the core rule of Visual Yes-And: players must physically accept the exact state, size, weight, and location of the imaginary object left by the previous player before changing or adding to it.
3. The facilitator begins the game by miming the creation of a highly specific imaginary object, such as a heavy, fragile crystal vase or a buzzing, high-voltage device, within their video frame.
4. The facilitator interacts with the object to establish its physical properties like weight, temperature, or texture, and delivers a brief, one-sentence verbal cue to set the narrative context.
5. The facilitator then uses the platform's spotlight feature to highlight the next player, signaling that the object has been passed to their screen.
6. The spotlighted player must immediately receive the object, physically matching its established weight and location as it enters their frame.
7. The active player then adds a new physical complication or development to the object, such as it starting to melt, getting incredibly heavy, or transforming, and narrates the change with a single sentence.
8. The facilitator un-spotlights the current player and spotlights a new player, continuing the chain to build a cohesive, physical narrative arc.
9. After every player has contributed once or twice, the facilitator spotlights a final player to deliver a dramatic, physical resolution to the story.

## Facilitation Notes
- Coaching Cue: 'Show the weight!' Remind players to use their muscles and resistance to make the imaginary object look real; if it is heavy, their shoulders and arms should reflect that strain.
- Pitfall: Players often ignore the previous player's physical setup and invent a completely new, unrelated object. Fix: Intervene immediately and ask the player to rewind and physically touch or interact with the previous object first.
- Coaching Cue: 'Keep it in the frame!' Ensure players perform their actions close enough to the camera so their hands and the imaginary object are clearly visible to the rest of the group.
- Technical Tip: If the spotlight feature has latency, verbally announce the next player's name a split second before spotlighting them to keep the physical momentum seamless.

## Variations
- Silent Canvas: Run the entire game in absolute silence, relying 100% on physical expression, facial reactions, and body language to convey the narrative and object properties.
- Environmental Shift: The object changes the physical environment of the player's room, such as the room becoming freezing cold if the object is a block of ice, affecting how they move.
- Speed Run: Once the object's journey is established, run the exact same sequence again at double speed, forcing rapid physical transitions and high-energy commitment.

## Debrief
- How did you have to adjust your physical movement to make the imaginary object feel real to the person watching you?
- What visual cues did you look for to understand the weight, size, and danger of the object before it was passed to you?
- How does physical 'Yes, And' differ from verbal 'Yes, And' when building a shared reality online?
- In what ways can we apply this level of active visual observation to our daily virtual communication?

## Safety & Inclusion
Ensure players who may have physical mobility limitations are encouraged to adapt the physical work to their comfort level; the weight and properties of the object can be expressed through facial expressions, head movements, or smaller hand gestures. Remind everyone to stay within a safe physical range of motion in their home workspace.

## Why It Works
This game works because it forces players to move away from verbal overthinking and ground their choices in physical reality. By utilizing the spotlight feature, it creates a shared focal point that mimics a physical stage, while the strict rule of endowment-acceptance ensures that players must actively receive and honor their partner's physical offer to keep the story moving forward.
