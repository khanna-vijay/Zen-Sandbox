# The Invisible Blueprint

<div class="game-hero-ph" markdown>🎨 *Infographic coming soon*</div>

> Build an imaginary monument while navigating secret personal boundaries and practicing explicit, real-time consent.

## Overview
In this physical, collaborative exercise, players work together to assemble a complex, imaginary machine or structure. Each player holds a secret, personal boundary card that dictates their physical or social limits during the scene. Players must actively check in, negotiate proximity, and prioritize personal safety over comedic momentum, learning that saying no can unlock deeper creative solutions.

## What It Trains
- **Domain:** D2 — The Partner
- **Principle(s):** Consent & Boundaries; Yes, And; Make Your Partner a Genius
- **Skill(s):** Boundary Navigation; Active Listening; Offer Reception; Support Work
- **Technique(s):** Check-ins; Cut calls; Negotiating physical contact
- **Focus:** skill_drill

**Objective:** To develop practical boundary navigation and real-time consent check-ins, training players to prioritize personal comfort over performative agreement while maintaining collaborative play.

## Setup
An open, safe physical space with room to move. Prepare a set of pre-written Boundary Cards (index cards with specific physical or social boundaries, e.g., 'No physical contact on the shoulders', 'Must maintain a two-foot distance', 'No high-status commands'). Shuffle the cards and place them face down.

## How to Play
1. Each player secretly draws one Boundary Card from the deck, reads it, internalizes it as their absolute truth for the round, and pockets it.
2. The facilitator provides a collaborative construction prompt, such as building a giant, delicate clockwork engine or assembling a deep-sea research vessel.
3. Players enter the performance space and begin physically, but invisibly, constructing the object, which naturally requires close proximity and cooperative movement.
4. Before initiating any action involving close proximity (under arm's length), physical touch, directing another's body, or introducing sensitive themes, the initiator must perform an explicit check-in.
5. Check-ins can be direct ('May I place my hand here to support this beam?') or integrated into the scene ('Partner, can you hold this heavy gear with me if your back is up for it?').
6. The receiving player must respond honestly based on their secret boundary: they either accept the action if it is safe, or decline/redirect it if it violates their boundary (e.g., 'I can't hold it there, but I can support it from the bottom').
7. If a boundary is crossed, or if any player feels genuine discomfort, anyone can call 'Cut!' to immediately pause the scene with zero judgment or explanation required.
8. Once the construction is complete or the scene reaches a natural conclusion, players step out of character to debrief before starting a new round with fresh cards.

## Facilitation Notes
- Normalize Redirection: Actively praise players who decline or redirect offers. Frame a boundary assertion as a creative win that forces interesting, alternative staging.
- Praise Proactive Checks: Call out and celebrate subtle, seamless check-ins (both verbal and clear, non-verbal cues) that keep partners safe without stalling the scene's energy.
- Enforce the 'Cut' Rule: Ensure that if 'Cut' is called, all action stops instantly. Model this by immediately stepping in to support the player without demanding justification.
- Avoid the 'Yes-And' Trap: Remind players that 'Yes, And' does not mean sacrificing physical safety or personal comfort; true collaboration requires mutual safety.

## Variations
- Open Blueprints: In round two, players reveal their previous boundary card to the group before drawing a new secret one, practicing how to navigate known versus unknown boundaries.
- Non-Verbal Signals: Restrict check-ins to specific, pre-agreed non-verbal cues (like an open palm or a questioning nod) to build deep physical awareness and eye-contact connection.

## Debrief
- How did it feel to prioritize your secret boundary over the natural urge to agree and keep the scene moving?
- What strategies did you use to redirect an offer creatively without shutting down the collaborative momentum?
- How did receiving a 'no' or a redirection change your physical choices and make you a more attentive partner?
- For those who called 'Cut' or witnessed it, how did the immediate pause affect your sense of safety in the space?

## Safety & Inclusion
This game is highly safety-sensitive. Before playing, establish that players can write their own boundary cards if they prefer, or choose a 'no-contact' card. Emphasize that 'Cut' is a sacred tool of empowerment, and no player will ever be questioned or penalized for using it. Ensure the physical space is clear of actual tripping hazards.

## Why It Works
By gamifying boundaries through secret cards, it removes the social pressure of saying 'no' in an improv setting. It forces players to practice the mechanics of consent (checking in, listening, redirecting) in a low-stakes, fictional environment, building the muscle memory needed to protect themselves and support their partners in high-stakes scenes.
