# The Consent Stone

> Pause scenes to explicitly negotiate physical, emotional, and narrative boundaries using a physical token.

## Overview
A structured skill drill where players use a physical token to pause a scene and explicitly negotiate high-impact offers. It creates a safe, low-stakes environment to practice setting boundaries, making invitational offers, and prioritizing personal comfort over scene momentum. Players learn that saying 'no' or modifying an offer is a collaborative gift that enhances trust.

## What It Trains
- **Domain:** D2 — The Partner
- **Principle(s):** Consent & Boundaries; Truth Over Pandering
- **Skill(s):** Boundary Navigation; Offer Reception; Active Gifting
- **Technique(s):** Check-ins; Cut calls; Negotiating physical contact
- **Focus:** skill_drill

**Objective:** To develop practical boundary navigation and active check-in techniques, allowing players to confidently propose, modify, or decline high-impact offers without sacrificing psychological safety or scene momentum.

## Setup
Gather 4 to 8 players in a circle. Place a small, soft object (like a beanbag or stress ball) in the center of the space to act as the 'Consent Stone.' Define 'high-impact offers' for the group: physical contact, entering close personal space, dictating another character's intense emotional state, or revealing sensitive narrative secrets. Explain that players may use their real-world comfort levels or pre-determine a 'fictional' boundary for their character to practice the mechanics safely.

## How to Play
1. Begin a standard two-person scene based on a simple relationship or location suggestion, with the remaining players observing.
2. When a performing player (Player A) wants to make a high-impact offer (such as a physical hug or a major narrative revelation), they must pause the scene by picking up the Consent Stone and placing it near the other player (Player B).
3. Player A then steps slightly out of character to make their offer 'invitationally,' asking a clear, non-coercive question (e.g., 'I would like to hug you here. How does that land?').
4. Player B picks up the Consent Stone and must choose one of three responses: Accept, Modify, or Decline.
5. To Accept, Player B says 'Yes,' puts the stone down, and both players immediately resume the scene, integrating the agreed-upon action.
6. To Modify, Player B proposes an alternative that respects their boundary (e.g., 'I don't want a hug, but we can shake hands'), puts the stone down, and the scene resumes with the modified action.
7. To Decline, Player B says 'Cut' or 'No, thank you,' and puts the stone down.
8. If a Decline occurs, the scene immediately rewinds five to ten seconds; Player A must make a completely different, low-impact offer, and play continues without any in-the-moment discussion or justification of the decline.
9. After a few minutes or after two to three stone negotiations, the facilitator calls 'Scene' to rotate players, ensuring everyone gets a turn as both the offerer and the responder.

## Facilitation Notes
- Keep Negotiations Swift: Remind players that the pause is a brief, functional check-in, not a lengthy out-of-character discussion. Keep the momentum snappy.
- Normalize the 'Cut': Actively celebrate when a player declines an offer. Ensure the group treats a 'Cut' as a neutral, positive tool for safety rather than a failure or a creative rejection.
- Coach Invitational Phrasing: If Player A makes a demanding offer (e.g., 'I'm going to grab you now, okay?'), pause and coach them to rephrase it invitationally (e.g., 'I'd like to reach for your hand. Are you comfortable with that?').
- Encourage Fictional Boundaries: For players hesitant to share real-world boundaries, encourage them to adopt a fictional boundary (e.g., 'My character hates being touched on the shoulder') to practice the mechanics of saying 'no' without personal vulnerability.
- Watch for Pandering: If you notice a player accepting an offer with visible physical hesitation, gently pause and remind them that prioritizing their comfort over 'yes-and' is the ultimate goal of this drill.

## Variations
- The Invisible Stone: Once players are comfortable with the physical prop, remove the stone. Players must now use a verbal cue or a specific hand gesture to initiate the same explicit check-in process.
- Secret Boundaries: Before the scene starts, the receiving player is secretly assigned a specific physical or emotional boundary by the facilitator. The initiating player must navigate the scene and use the stone to discover and respect this boundary.

## Debrief
- How did it feel to explicitly pause the scene to check in? Did it hinder or actually liberate your creativity?
- For those who modified or declined an offer, did you feel any internal pressure to 'pander' or say yes anyway? How did the mechanics of the game help you overcome that?
- How did making an 'invitational offer' change how you thought about your partner's physical and emotional space?
- How can we carry the spirit of the 'Consent Stone' into our regular, un-propped improv scenes?

## Safety & Inclusion
This game is highly safety-sensitive. It is designed to build agency and psychological safety. Facilitators must explicitly state that players have absolute sovereignty over their bodies and comfort levels. No player is ever required to justify a boundary or a 'Cut' call. Using fictional boundaries is highly recommended as an accessibility tool for players who may feel exposed or anxious about asserting real-world personal boundaries in a group setting.

## Why It Works
By physicalizing the check-in process with a tangible object, the game lowers the social friction of pausing a scene to negotiate consent. It reframes 'no' from a scene-killer into a collaborative boundary-setting tool, teaching players that true 'yes-and' requires the freedom to say 'no.' This builds deep mutual trust, allowing players to take greater creative risks because they know their partners will actively check in rather than assume consent.
