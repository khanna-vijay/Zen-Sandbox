# The Feedback Agreement

> Transform your audience into a living dashboard of real-time performance feedback.

## Overview
This exercise turns the passive training audience into an active, structured scene partner that provides immediate, non-verbal, and subtle auditory feedback. By dividing the room into specialized observation zones, performers learn to read the room dynamically and adjust their physical, vocal, and energetic choices mid-scene.

## What It Trains
- **Domain:** D5 — The Audience
- **Principle(s):** The Audience Is the Final Scene Partner; Play for the Back Row
- **Skill(s):** Room Reading; Audience-Energy Management; Stage Presence & Clarity; Physicality & Space Work; Vocal Craft
- **Technique(s):** Energy-calibration; Tag-running (riding a laugh wave); Landing/cushioning a beat; Breaking the 4th Wall / Direct Address; Cheating out; Projection; Make the choice readable
- **Focus:** skill_drill

**Objective:** To develop advanced room-reading and energy-calibration skills, training performers to treat audience reactions as direct, actionable scene information rather than passive background noise.

## Setup
Arrange the space with a clear stage area and seating for at least six audience members. Divide the audience seating into three distinct geographic zones: Front Row (Close), Middle Row (Mid-distance), and Back Row (Far). No props are required.

## How to Play
1. Divide the non-performing participants into three distinct audience zones: the Front Row, the Middle Row, and the Back Row, while two to three performers take the stage.
2. Assign the Front Row the role of the Fourth Wall Guard, instructing them to monitor eye contact and direct address by leaning forward when engaged, leaning back when ignored, and raising a flat palm if the address is too overbearing.
3. Assign the Middle Row the role of the Energy Monitor, instructing them to track emotional stakes and comedic momentum by offering a soft approving hum for clear choices, whispering quietly if the scene loses energy, and chuckling to build comedic momentum.
4. Assign the Back Row the role of the Distance Beacon, instructing them to monitor projection and physical readability by nodding slowly when clear, cupping a hand to their ear when quiet, and shrugging if a major choice is completely lost.
5. Have the audience practice their cues once with the facilitator to ensure they can deliver them subtly, instantly, and without disrupting the flow of the scene.
6. Establish the core contract: performers must treat all audience cues as direct, real-time stage directions and immediately adjust their performance to correct negative feedback or amplify positive feedback.
7. Begin a three-to-five-minute scene based on a simple suggestion, requiring the performers to split their focus between their scene partner and the physical and auditory cues of the audience.
8. Allow the scene to run, with the facilitator occasionally calling a brief freeze to ask performers what cues they are currently receiving and how they plan to adapt.

## Facilitation Notes
- Ensure the audience's cues remain subtle and organic so the exercise does not devolve into a comedy game rather than a calibration drill.
- If performers ignore the audience, pause the scene and ask them to look at a specific section to identify what feedback they are missing.
- Encourage performers to ride the wave of the Middle Row's chuckle by pausing their dialogue slightly and letting the energy peak before continuing.
- Watch out for performers who over-correct and break character entirely, reminding them to integrate the adjustments seamlessly into the reality of the scene.

## Variations
- Silent Mode: Run the entire exercise with absolutely no vocal cues from the audience, forcing performers to rely entirely on physical posture, eye contact, and subtle gestures.
- The Solo Calibration: A single performer delivers a monologue or tells a story, navigating all three audience zones alone to practice direct engagement and vocal projection.
- Dynamic Zones: Mid-scene, the facilitator claps and the audience zones swap roles, forcing performers to quickly re-map their spatial awareness.

## Debrief
- Performers: Which audience zone was the easiest to read, and which did you find yourself accidentally ignoring?
- Performers: How did it feel to adjust your vocal projection or physical choices in direct response to a physical cue like a cupped ear?
- Audience: Did you notice a moment where a performer successfully shifted their energy or staging to win back your engagement?
- How does treating the audience as an active scene partner change your physical presence on stage?

## Safety & Inclusion
Ensure that the Front Row's direct address cues do not pressure performers into unwanted physical proximity or intense, prolonged eye contact if it makes them uncomfortable. Establish that performers can always choose to break eye contact or step back to re-establish their personal boundaries.

## Why It Works
This game works because it gamifies the feedback loop between the stage and the house. By codifying the audience's natural, often invisible reactions into explicit physical and vocal signals, it removes the guesswork from reading the room. Performers receive immediate, low-stakes consequences for dropping their projection or ignoring the fourth wall, building the muscle memory needed to calibrate their energy dynamically in live shows.
