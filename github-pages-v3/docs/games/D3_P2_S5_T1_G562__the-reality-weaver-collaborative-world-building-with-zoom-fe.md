# The Reality Weavers

<div class="game-hero-ph" markdown>🎨 *Infographic coming soon*</div>

> Co-create and instantly adapt to a dynamically shifting visual world on a shared digital stage.

## Overview
The Reality Weavers is a virtual improv game where players collaboratively build and alter their physical environment in real-time using a shared digital canvas. One player acts as the Architect, managing a library of visual assets, while performing players initiate scenes and dynamically inject visual elements into the space. The entire ensemble must instantly 'Yes, And' these sudden visual changes, treating them as absolute physical truths that redefine their scene's base reality.

## What It Trains
- **Domain:** D3 — The Scene
- **Principle(s):** Base Reality First; Yes, And; Group Mind
- **Skill(s):** World-Building; Offer Reception; Support Work
- **Technique(s):** C.R.O.W. (Character, Relationship, Objective, Where); Endowment-acceptance; Playing architecture/objects
- **Focus:** mixed

**Objective:** To master the C.R.O.W. framework (Character, Relationship, Objective, Where) by establishing a strong base reality and dynamically expanding it through collaborative, visual world-building.

## Setup
Players join a virtual meeting room. One player is designated as the 'Architect' and prepares a shared digital canvas (such as a collaborative whiteboard or slide presentation) loaded with a diverse library of background environments, objects, and abstract textures. All players rename themselves to reflect their character's name and role (e.g., 'Dr. Aris - Botanist'). The Architect shares their screen displaying the blank canvas.

## How to Play
1. Assign one player as the Architect, who shares their screen showing a blank digital canvas and has the asset library ready.
2. Two active players step forward to begin a scene, immediately establishing their Characters, Relationship, Objective, and initial Where (C.R.O.W.) through dialogue and physical object work.
3. At any point, any player (active or off-screen) can introduce a physical change to the environment by making an 'environmental offer' (e.g., declaring that a sudden storm is rolling in or a high-tech console has appeared).
4. To materialize this offer, the offering player sends a designated reaction emoji (like a clap or hand raise) to the Architect.
5. The Architect immediately grants temporary remote screen control to the offering player, or swiftly drags the corresponding visual asset (an image, icon, or drawing) onto the shared canvas themselves.
6. The offering player places, resizes, or annotates the asset on the shared screen to represent their environmental addition, then immediately relinquishes control back to the Architect.
7. The active players must instantly 'Yes, And' this new visual reality, adjusting their physical posture, eye-line, and dialogue to treat the digital asset as a tangible, high-stakes element of their immediate surroundings.
8. The scene continues to evolve with players weaving new visual elements, shifting locations, or tagging in and out to explore new relationships within the established base reality.

## Facilitation Notes
- Coaching Cue: 'See it, touch it, react to it!' Remind players to physically align their eye-lines with the shared screen assets to make the virtual space feel three-dimensional.
- Pitfall: Players get bogged down in the technical aspect of dragging and dropping images, stalling the scene. Fix: Keep the asset placement rapid and imperfect; a rough placement is better than a slow, precise one. The Architect can also place assets on behalf of players based on verbal cues to maintain momentum.
- Coaching Cue: 'Ground the change in C.R.O.W.' Ensure every visual addition directly impacts the characters' relationships or objectives, rather than being random visual noise.
- Pitfall: Overloading the screen with too many assets too quickly. Fix: Encourage players to let each visual offer breathe and be fully integrated into the dialogue before introducing the next one.

## Variations
- The Blind Architect: The active players cannot see the shared canvas, but the audience and off-screen players can. Off-screen players add assets, and the active players must discover and describe them based solely on physical hints from a third player.
- Sensory Overload: The Architect actively changes environmental assets (like weather, lighting, or background textures) without warning, forcing the active players to instantly justify the sudden environmental shift within their current scene.

## Debrief
- How did having a literal, visual representation of the environment change how you established your C.R.O.W. elements?
- What strategies did you use to ensure that a sudden visual change supported the scene's emotional reality rather than disrupting it?
- How did the physical act of aligning your gaze and movement to the shared screen assets affect your sense of connection with your scene partner?

## Safety & Inclusion
Ensure all visual assets in the library are accessible, clear, and free of flashing or high-contrast elements that could cause sensory distress. If remote control features present accessibility barriers for some players, they can verbally describe their environmental offer, and the Architect or a designated buddy can place the asset for them.

## Why It Works
By externalizing the imagined environment onto a shared digital canvas, this game removes the cognitive load of tracking a purely mental space. It forces players to physically and verbally commit to a shared, undeniable 'Where' (the W in C.R.O.W.), which instantly solidifies the scene's base reality. The physical interaction with digital assets bridges the gap of remote isolation, creating a unified group mind through shared visual focus.
