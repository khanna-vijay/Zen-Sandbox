# Pure Gibberish

<div class="game-hero-ph" markdown>🎨 *Infographic coming soon*</div>

> Communicate deep relationships and clear narratives using only nonsense language and physical commitment.

## Overview
Two players perform a fully realized scene using entirely made-up, nonsense language with no translator present. By stripping away literal English, players must rely on vocal variety, physical environment work, and emotional commitment to make the narrative and relationships instantly recognizable. The result is a masterclass in non-verbal communication where the subtext becomes the text.

## What It Trains
- **Domain:** D1 — The Self
- **Principle(s):** Commit 100%; Show, Don't Tell; Base Reality First
- **Skill(s):** Vocal Craft; Physicality & Space Work; Active Listening; World-Building
- **Technique(s):** Gibberish; Object work; C.R.O.W. (Character, Relationship, Objective, Where)
- **Focus:** skill_drill

**Objective:** To develop vocal craft and physical commitment by removing the crutch of literal language, forcing players to establish a clear base reality and emotional truth through tone, gesture, and active listening.

## Setup
Two active players stand in the performance space. The remaining group acts as observers. No props or chairs are needed, though players should utilize imaginary object work to define their environment.

## How to Play
1. Ask the observers or facilitator for a simple, everyday location to establish the base reality.
2. The two players enter the space and immediately begin establishing their physical environment and relationship using object work and posture.
3. Players must speak exclusively in gibberish—fluent, expressive, nonsense sounds that mimic the cadence, rhythm, and punctuation of real speech.
4. Avoid silent pantomime; players must continuously use their voices to express thoughts, reactions, and commands.
5. Keep the narrative progression simple and linear, focusing on a clear, singular conflict or collaborative task.
6. Listen actively to the emotional tone and pitch of your partner's gibberish, responding with matching or contrasting vocal energy to build the scene's tension.
7. End the scene once a clear resolution or emotional climax is reached, typically within two to three minutes.

## Facilitation Notes
- Side-coach players to avoid 'empty' gibberish. Encourage them to use distinct consonants and vowels that match their character's emotional state.
- Pitfall: Slipping into silent mime. Fix: Remind players that this is a vocal exercise; they must speak as much as they would in a standard English scene.
- Pitfall: Making the plot too complex. Fix: Side-coach players to focus on a simple physical task or relationship dynamic rather than trying to explain intricate backstory.
- Encourage 100% commitment to the reality of the gibberish words. If a player points to an object and says 'Zibble!', that object is now a 'zibble' for the rest of the scene.

## Variations
- Gibberish Switch: The facilitator can call out 'English!' or 'Gibberish!' mid-scene to force players to instantly translate their current emotional state between languages.
- The Expert and Interviewer: One player is a world-renowned expert speaking in gibberish, and the other is an interviewer who must translate their points to the audience, though the scene still relies on the expert's physical clarity.

## Debrief
- How did removing English words affect your ability to read your partner's emotional state?
- What physical choices did you have to make to ensure the audience understood the objects you were interacting with?
- How does committing 100% to nonsense sounds actually make a scene feel more grounded and truthful?

## Safety & Inclusion
Ensure players are aware that gibberish should not mimic or caricature real-world accents, cultures, or existing languages. Focus on abstract, cartoonish, or entirely synthetic sounds to maintain a respectful and inclusive environment.

## Why It Works
By removing literal language, players cannot hide behind clever dialogue or stall with verbal waffling. They are forced to show rather than tell, relying on the fundamental building blocks of performance: vocal dynamics, physical environment, and emotional vulnerability. This builds a strong foundation for base reality and active listening.
