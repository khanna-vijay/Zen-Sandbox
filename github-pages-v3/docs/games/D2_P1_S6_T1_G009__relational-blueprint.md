# Relational Blueprint

> Co-create dynamic scenes while actively negotiating physical, emotional, and narrative boundaries in real time.

## Overview
Relational Blueprint is an active scene-work exercise where players treat boundary negotiation as a collaborative, creative engine rather than a limitation. Players navigate secret character constraints and physical contact requests, learning to check in and adjust their relational agreements mid-scene. The experience balances the playful spontaneity of improv with the grounded, mindful practice of mutual consent.

## What It Trains
- **Domain:** D2 — The Partner
- **Principle(s):** Consent & Boundaries; Yes, And; Make Your Partner a Genius; Truth Over Pandering
- **Skill(s):** Boundary Navigation; Active Listening; Offer Reception
- **Technique(s):** Check-ins; Cut calls; Negotiating physical contact
- **Focus:** connection

**Objective:** To develop advanced boundary navigation and active check-in techniques, training players to prioritize truth over pandering by integrating consent negotiations directly into the narrative fabric of a scene.

## Setup
A clear, open playing space with room for active scene work and an observing circle. Prepare a set of 'Scene Prompt' cards (scenarios with inherent relational tension) and 'Boundary Modifier' cards (secret character directives that challenge spatial, emotional, or physical comfort). Optional: Red, Yellow, and Green traffic light cards for visual check-ins.

## How to Play
1. Begin with a collective 'My Lines' check-in where players state any personal, non-negotiable physical or thematic boundaries for the session, establishing a shared safety baseline.
2. Have players briefly share their general comfort level with physical contact for the day (e.g., high, medium, or minimal touch) to calibrate group expectations.
3. Cast two players into the performance space and provide a relational scene prompt, such as estranged siblings meeting at a train station.
4. Discreetly hand one player a secret 'Boundary Modifier' card, such as 'Your character desperately craves physical comfort but must respect any refusal' or 'Your character is highly protective of their personal space'.
5. As the scene plays, any player wishing to initiate physical contact beyond casual, low-impact touch must explicitly ask for permission in-character.
6. The receiving player must respond truthfully in-character by accepting, declining, or offering an alternative, immediately weaving this choice into the story.
7. If a thematic or emotional line feels uncomfortable, players must practice 'Truth Over Pandering' by asserting an in-character boundary instead of blindly agreeing.
8. If any player feels a boundary is crossed or feels unsafe, they call 'Cut!' to immediately freeze the scene, step out of character, and spend 30-60 seconds negotiating a 'blueprint adjustment' before resuming play.
9. The facilitator calls 'Scene!' after 3-5 minutes once a significant boundary has been successfully navigated, integrated, and resolved.

## Facilitation Notes
- Actively celebrate 'Cut!' calls as successful acts of self-advocacy and creative calibration, never as failures or interruptions.
- Help players distinguish between productive character discomfort (dramatic tension) and genuine improviser discomfort (boundary crossing); when in doubt, encourage calling 'Cut!'.
- Keep the out-of-character 'Relational Re-calibration' strictly time-boxed to 30-60 seconds to maintain momentum and show that safety adjustments do not ruin the scene's flow.
- Define 'pedestrian touch' clearly as brief, low-stakes contact like a high-five or shoulder tap. Anything lingering, intimate, or high-energy requires an in-character check-in.
- Empower observing players to watch for subtle non-verbal cues of discomfort and, in advanced groups, call 'Cut!' on behalf of a partner who seems frozen or unable to advocate for themselves.

## Variations
- Non-Verbal Calibration: Play the scene entirely silently, requiring players to negotiate physical boundaries and check-ins purely through eye contact, breath, and physical gestures.
- The Traffic Light System: Give players physical Red, Yellow, and Green cards to hold. They can silently flash these cards mid-scene to signal their comfort level with the current direction without stopping the dialogue.
- The Audience Blueprint: Have the audience or observing players suggest the specific boundary modifiers or relationship rules before the scene starts, making the negotiation transparent to everyone.

## Debrief
- How did having a secret boundary modifier change your awareness of your partner's physical and emotional cues?
- What did it feel like to explicitly ask for permission for physical contact in-character? How did the response shape the relationship?
- For those who called 'Cut!' or set an in-character boundary, how did it feel to prioritize your truth over the pressure to blindly 'yes-and' the offer?
- How did the quick 'Relational Re-calibration' affect your trust in your partner and your willingness to take creative risks afterward?

## Safety & Inclusion
This game is highly consent-sensitive. Ensure players know they have absolute autonomy over their bodies and emotional limits. If a player is uncomfortable with physical touch, they can participate entirely through verbal and spatial boundaries. Always establish that a player's real-world safety and comfort supersede any theatrical outcome.

## Why It Works
By gamifying boundary negotiation, this exercise strips away the stigma of saying 'no' in improv. It reframes boundary setting not as a scene-stopper, but as a rich, generative offer that deepens character relationships. Practicing explicit check-ins and rapid re-calibrations builds the muscle memory needed to maintain high trust and psychological safety in high-stakes scenework.
