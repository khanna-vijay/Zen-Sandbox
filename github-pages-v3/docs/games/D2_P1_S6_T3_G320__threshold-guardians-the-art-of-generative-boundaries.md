# Threshold Negotiators

![Threshold Negotiators — infographic](img/D2_P1_S6_T3_G320__threshold-guardians-the-art-of-generative-boundaries.jpg){ .game-hero }

> Transform physical boundaries into creative narrative offers through deliberate, real-time consent negotiation.

## Overview
A high-focus physical scene-work drill where players navigate a physically contested space. By using micro-physical offers, structured in-character pauses, and immediate out-of-character intent disclosures, players learn to treat boundary-setting not as a scene-stopper, but as a generative narrative engine.

## What It Trains
- **Domain:** D2 — The Partner
- **Principle(s):** Consent & Boundaries; Yes, And; Truth Over Pandering
- **Skill(s):** Boundary Navigation; Active Listening; Offer Reception
- **Technique(s):** Check-ins; Cut calls; Negotiating physical contact
- **Focus:** skill_drill

**Objective:** To develop advanced boundary navigation and physical contact negotiation skills, training players to read subtle physical cues, assert personal and character limits, and align artistic intent with physical impact.

## Setup
Use tape to mark a 4x4 foot 'Contested Zone' in the center of the playing space. Two primary players stand on opposite sides outside the zone. If available, one or two off-stage players act as environmental narrators. The facilitator stands nearby to monitor safety.

## How to Play
1. Establish Boundaries: Before starting, both active players state one specific physical boundary for their character (e.g., 'My character is highly protective of their personal space and flinches if approached quickly' or 'My character seeks physical comfort but needs verbal permission first').
2. Set the Scenario: The facilitator provides a high-stakes scenario that forces both characters to interact near or within the taped Contested Zone (e.g., retrieving a shared survival tool, seeking shelter from a toxic storm).
3. Initiate Micro-Offers: As players pursue their objectives, they must introduce 'micro-physical offers'—subtle, low-stakes physical movements like hovering a hand near a shoulder, leaning in, or placing a hand on a nearby wall—to test proximity and comfort.
4. Read and Check-In: The initiating player must actively observe their partner's non-verbal reactions (breath, muscle tension, eye contact) to gauge comfort in real-time before advancing any physical contact.
5. Respond with Consent: The receiving player can enthusiastically accept the physical offer, integrating it into their action if it aligns with their character's boundaries.
6. Negotiate In-Character: If an offer pushes a character's boundary, the receiver uses the phrase 'Hold that thought...' to pause the physical action in-character, state their boundary, and offer a creative alternative (e.g., 'Hold that thought... I don't like being crowded, but you can hand me the map from there').
7. Execute Out-of-Character Safety: If any offer crosses an actual player-level comfort boundary, any player can call 'Cut!' to immediately freeze the scene, state their personal boundary briefly, and reset the action safely without judgment.
8. Reveal Intent: Immediately following any physical interaction or boundary negotiation, the initiator steps out-of-character to state their character's exact motivation (e.g., 'My character's intent was to offer reassurance, not to threaten you'). The receiver acknowledges this, and the scene resumes.

## Facilitation Notes
- Demonstrate First: Run a slow-motion demonstration of 'Hold that thought...' and 'Cut!' with a volunteer before starting, showing the clear difference between character-level negotiation and player-level safety.
- Celebrate the 'Cut': When a player calls 'Cut!', pause, validate their boundary immediately, and lead a brief round of applause to reinforce that safety is a creative win, not a failure.
- Coach the Micro-Offers: Remind players that micro-offers should be slow and legible. If a player moves too fast, side-coach: 'Slow down the approach, let them see the hand coming.'
- Avoid Pandering: Watch for players who silently tolerate physical discomfort just to keep the scene moving. Side-coach: 'Honor your character's truth—if they want space, demand it.'
- Manage Cognitive Load: The combination of in-character play and out-of-character intent reveals can be taxing. Keep scenes short (2-3 minutes) to prevent mental fatigue.

## Variations
- Environmental Pressures: Introduce 1-2 off-stage players who narrate external shifts (e.g., 'The room is getting colder,' 'The floor begins to tilt') to force the active players to renegotiate their physical proximity under new constraints.
- Silent Negotiation: Run the entire scene in complete silence, forcing players to rely entirely on micro-physical offers, eye contact, and physical gestures to negotiate boundaries.

## Debrief
- How did it feel to use 'Hold that thought...' to set a boundary in-character? Did it stall the scene, or did it give you new narrative material to play with?
- For the initiators, how did stating your character's intent out-of-character change how you viewed the physical impact of your action?
- What physical or non-verbal cues did you find most reliable for reading your partner's comfort level before making physical contact?
- How does prioritizing 'Truth Over Pandering' in this exercise change your perspective on the traditional 'Yes, And' rule?

## Safety & Inclusion
This is a high-contact, consent-sensitive exercise. Participation must be entirely voluntary. Ensure all players understand that 'Cut!' is an absolute, unarguable boundary marker. If a player has physical mobility or touch limitations, adapt the 'Contested Zone' and boundaries to focus entirely on vocal proximity or eye contact.

## Why It Works
By separating character boundaries from player boundaries, and intent from impact, this game demystifies physical contact in improv. It replaces the anxiety of 'doing the wrong thing' with a structured, collaborative vocabulary for real-time negotiation, proving that boundaries actually generate richer dramatic tension than automatic compliance.
