# Synchronized Clap

<div class="game-hero-ph" markdown>🎨 *Infographic coming soon*</div>

> Pass a single, simultaneous clap across a circle using only eye contact and shared timing.

## Overview
Synchronized Clap is a high-focus ensemble warm-up where players stand in a circle and pass a clap from person to person. Instead of a sequential pass, the sender and receiver must execute a single, perfectly synchronized clap together before the receiver passes it to someone else. This exercise builds intense focus, non-verbal connection, and a shared sense of rhythm.

## What It Trains
- **Domain:** D4 — The Ensemble
- **Principle(s):** Group Mind; Follow the Follower; Make Your Partner a Genius

- **Technique(s):** Timing exercises; Mirror exercise

**Objective:** To develop group mind, peripheral awareness, and precise non-verbal timing by aligning physical actions with a partner in real-time.

## Setup
Players stand in a comfortable circle facing inward. No props or special materials are required. Ensure everyone has a clear line of sight to all other players.

## How to Play
1. Form a standing circle with all players facing inward.
2. Player A initiates the game by making clear, direct eye contact with Player B across the circle.
3. Once eye contact is established, Player A and Player B must clap their hands together at the exact same moment, creating a single, unified sound.
4. Player B, now holding the pass, turns their attention to a new player, Player C, and establishes eye contact.
5. Player B and Player C then execute their synchronized clap.
6. The pass continues around the circle in this manner, with the receiver of the previous clap becoming the sender of the next.
7. The group strives to maintain a steady, unbroken rhythm, gradually increasing the speed of the passes as their connection deepens.

## Facilitation Notes
- Coaching cue: Look for the micro-movements. Breathe together to find the exact moment to strike.
- Coaching cue: Don't rush the setup. The magic is in the shared agreement, not just the speed.
- Pitfall: Players clapping out of sync because they are rushing. Fix: Instruct them to slow down, exaggerate their preparatory hand movement like a conductor's upbeat, and prioritize the shared connection over speed.
- Pitfall: Players only passing to their immediate neighbors. Fix: Encourage players to look across the circle to engage the entire space and build peripheral awareness.

## Variations
- Double Pass: Introduce a second clap into the circle so two pairs are synchronizing simultaneously, requiring high peripheral awareness to avoid collisions.
- Sound and Motion: Replace the clap with a unique sound and physical gesture initiated by the sender and mirrored instantly by the receiver.
- Blind Synchro: Players try to execute the synchronized clap with minimal eye contact, relying purely on physical breath cues and peripheral vision.

## Debrief
- What did it feel like when a clap was perfectly synchronized versus slightly off?
- How did you signal to your partner that you were ready to clap without using words?
- How does this exercise help us 'follow the follower' in an active improv scene?

## Safety & Inclusion
Ensure players with limited mobility or hand-use can participate by substituting the clap with a nod, a vocal sound, or any mutually agreed-upon simultaneous gesture.

## Why It Works
It strips away verbal communication, forcing players to rely entirely on non-verbal cues, breath, and micro-expressions. By requiring two people to act as one, it physically manifests the concept of Group Mind and trains players to anticipate and support their partner's timing.
