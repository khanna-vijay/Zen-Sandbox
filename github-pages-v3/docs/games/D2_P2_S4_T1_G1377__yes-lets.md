# Yes, Let's!

<div class="game-hero-ph" markdown>🎨 *Infographic coming soon*</div>

> Enthusiastically agree to every physical suggestion and jump into action together as a unified group.

## Overview
A high-energy, physical warm-up where players collectively build a shared environment or activity. Every suggestion is met with instant, unanimous agreement and immediate physical play, training players to embrace ideas without hesitation.

## What It Trains
- **Domain:** D2 — The Partner
- **Principle(s):** Yes, And; Group Mind; Follow the Follower
- **Skill(s):** Offer Reception; Support Work; Physicality & Space Work
- **Technique(s):** Yes, And… sentence games; Object work
- **Focus:** connection

**Objective:** To develop radical agreement and instant offer reception by physically committing to every suggestion made by peers, bypassing intellectual filtering.

## Setup
Players stand in a loose circle in an open room with enough space to move around safely. No props or materials are required.

## How to Play
1. Gather the group into a circle and establish a broad, collaborative theme, such as throwing a party, going on a camping trip, or preparing for a space mission.
2. One player steps forward or calls out from their spot with an active suggestion starting with the phrase, 'Let's...' (e.g., 'Let's inflate the balloons!').
3. Immediately, the entire group responds in unison with high energy: 'Yes, let's!'
4. Without delay, every player physically acts out the suggested activity, using their whole body and engaging in object work to bring the action to life.
5. After a few seconds of collective physical play, another player calls out a new suggestion that builds on the current scenario (e.g., 'Let's taste-test the punch!').
6. The group instantly stops the previous action, shouts 'Yes, let's!', and immediately transitions into the new physical activity.
7. Continue this rapid-fire cycle of suggestion, enthusiastic agreement, and physicalization until everyone has contributed multiple ideas and the energy is peaking.

## Facilitation Notes
- Encourage immediate physical action: players should start moving as they say 'Yes, let's!', rather than thinking about how to do it first.
- Watch out for 'spectating' where players watch others instead of committing to their own physical interpretation of the offer. Side-coach: 'Find your own way to do it!'
- Keep the pace brisk. If a physical action goes on for too long, prompt the group: 'Who has the next step? Let's hear it!'
- Ensure suggestions are active and physical rather than passive (e.g., 'Let's paint the walls' is better than 'Let's think about what color to use').

## Variations
- Silent Yes, Let's: Play the game entirely in pantomime. One player initiates a physical action, and the group must instantly copy and expand on it without verbal cues.
- The Ad Campaign: Use the same mechanic to pitch a ridiculous, imaginary product, where every 'Let's...' adds a feature, a slogan, or a marketing stunt.
- Emotional Shifts: Each new 'Let's...' suggestion must be delivered with a specific, extreme emotion (e.g., terrified, ecstatic, suspicious) which the entire group must adopt.

## Debrief
- How did it feel to have every single one of your suggestions met with instant, enthusiastic agreement?
- What did you notice about your brain's tendency to filter or judge your own ideas before speaking them?
- How does immediate physical commitment change your confidence when receiving an offer?

## Safety & Inclusion
Ensure the physical space is clear of tripping hazards. Remind players to be mindful of personal boundaries and physical limitations; all actions can be adapted or scaled to match individual mobility levels.

## Why It Works
By pairing verbal agreement ('Yes, let's!') with immediate physical action, the game bypasses the analytical mind. It forces players to support their partners instantly, building deep trust and a shared 'group mind' where no offer can fail.
