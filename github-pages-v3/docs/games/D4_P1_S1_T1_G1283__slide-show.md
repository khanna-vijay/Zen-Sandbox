# The Living Slideshow

![The Living Slideshow — infographic](img/D4_P1_S1_T1_G1283__slide-show.jpg){ .game-hero }

> Co-create an epic journey through the push-and-pull of physical stage pictures and spontaneous narration.

## Overview
One player acts as a presenter sharing a slideshow of a grand adventure, while the remaining players physically assemble themselves into frozen 'slides' that represent the photos. The magic of the game lies in the collaborative feedback loop between the narrator's words and the performers' physical compositions. It is a highly visual exercise that demands physical commitment, spatial awareness, and narrative agility.

## What It Trains
- **Domain:** D4 — The Ensemble
- **Principle(s):** Group Mind; Follow the Follower; Serve the Story; Yes, And
- **Skill(s):** Peripheral Awareness; Support Work; Narrative Architecture; World-Building; Offer Reception

- **Focus:** narrative

**Objective:** To develop group mind and peripheral awareness by co-creating balanced stage pictures that advance a narrative without verbal negotiation.

## Setup
A clear stage area with moderate space. One player (the Narrator) stands to one side facing the audience, holding an imaginary remote control. The other players (the Slide Actors) stand ready at the back or sides of the playing area.

## How to Play
1. Assign one player to be the Narrator and the remaining three to five players to be the Slide Actors.
2. Obtain a suggestion from the group for a grand, active event, such as 'an expedition to the center of the Earth' or 'building a high-tech skyscraper.'
3. The Narrator begins the presentation by introducing the topic and clicking the imaginary remote control to bring up the first slide.
4. Upon the click, the Slide Actors quickly enter the stage and freeze in a cohesive, multi-level physical composition that represents the scene.
5. The Narrator observes the physical offer made by the actors and describes the slide, adding narrative details that justify and expand upon the actors' physical choices.
6. The Narrator clicks the remote for the next slide, prompting the actors to immediately break their pose and form a brand-new frozen picture that advances the story chronologically.
7. Rather than just illustrating the Narrator's previous words, the actors must make bold physical offers that introduce new narrative elements, which the Narrator must then accept and explain.
8. Continue this back-and-forth for five to six slides, building to a logical climax and conclusion before the Narrator wraps up the presentation.

## Facilitation Notes
- Coaching cue: 'Look at the levels! If someone is high, go low. If someone is forward, go back.' This trains peripheral awareness and stage composition.
- Coaching cue: 'Don't just wait for the narrator to speak. Actors, make a physical choice that changes the story!'
- Pitfall: Actors standing in a flat line facing forward. Fix: Remind them to use depth, physical contact (with consent), and varied angles to create a dynamic 3D stage picture.
- Pitfall: The Narrator steamrolling the actors by describing exactly what they want before the actors pose. Fix: Encourage the Narrator to click, wait for the picture to form, and then discover what the slide is.

## Variations
- Blind Slides: The Narrator turns their back to the stage. The actors form a slide, and only then does the Narrator spin around and instantly narrate what they see.
- Moving Slides: The Narrator can hit 'play' on the remote, allowing the frozen slide to come alive with sound and motion for five seconds before hitting 'pause' to freeze them again.
- The Clicker Swap: Every time the slide changes, a different actor steps out to become the narrator, and the previous narrator joins the slide pool.

## Debrief
- How did it feel to let the physical shapes dictate the story rather than planning the plot ahead of time?
- How did you use your peripheral vision to balance the stage picture when forming a new slide?
- What made the collaboration feel seamless between the narrator and the physical performers?

## Safety & Inclusion
Ensure players are mindful of physical boundaries when posing close together. Encourage players to use levels that are comfortable for their bodies, substituting kneeling or sitting for crouching if they have joint or mobility limitations.

## Why It Works
This game forces a tight feedback loop between physical offer and verbal justification. Because the actors cannot speak, they must rely entirely on peripheral awareness and spatial composition to communicate with each other, while the narrator must practice deep offer reception to weave those physical shapes into a coherent story.
