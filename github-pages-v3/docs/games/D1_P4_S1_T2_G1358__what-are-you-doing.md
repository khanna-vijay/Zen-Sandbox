# What Are You Doing?

<div class="game-hero-ph" markdown>🎨 *Infographic coming soon*</div>

> Bypass your analytical brain by physically doing one thing while verbally declaring another.

## Overview
A high-energy, fast-paced physical warm-up where players must dissociate their physical actions from their verbal declarations. It challenges players to embrace immediate, unfiltered impulses under pressure, creating a playful environment where hesitation is bypassed and mistakes are celebrated.

## What It Trains
- **Domain:** D1 — The Self
- **Principle(s):** The First Thought Is a Gift; Fail Joyfully; Yes, And
- **Skill(s):** Unfiltered Spontaneity; Physicality & Space Work; Offer Reception; Active Listening
- **Technique(s):** First Thought drills; Object work; Endowment-acceptance
- **Focus:** skill_drill

**Objective:** To develop unfiltered spontaneity and physical commitment by training players to accept their very first verbal impulse without self-censoring, while instantly translating verbal suggestions into physical space work.

## Setup
Have all players form a single-file line facing a designated performance space. No props or special materials are required; just a clear space for movement.

## How to Play
1. The first player in line steps into the performance space and begins performing a clear, repetitive physical mime (e.g., painting a wall).
2. The second player in line steps up to them and asks clearly, 'What are you doing?'
3. Without hesitating, the first player must verbally state an entirely different activity (e.g., 'I'm walking a tightrope') while continuing to mime the original action (painting).
4. As soon as the first player states the new activity, they exit to the back of the line.
5. The second player must immediately begin physically miming the new activity (walking a tightrope) with full physical commitment.
6. The third player in line then steps up and asks the second player, 'What are you doing?'
7. The second player verbally names a new, unrelated activity (e.g., 'I'm baking a giant cake') and exits to the back of the line.
8. The cycle continues down the line, keeping the physical and verbal transitions as seamless and rapid as possible.

## Facilitation Notes
- Coaching cue: 'Don't think, just speak! Let the first word out of your mouth surprise you.'
- Pitfall: Players freeze trying to think of a 'clever' or 'funny' activity. Fix: Encourage them to say the most mundane thing possible (e.g., 'I'm eating an apple') to keep the momentum going.
- Coaching cue: 'Commit to the physical work! Make your mime clear and specific before the next person asks.'
- Pitfall: Players stop miming the moment they are asked the question. Fix: Remind them to keep their hands moving and stay in the physical action until they have finished speaking and exited.

## Variations
- Rapid-Fire Duo: Two players face each other and trade off back-and-forth at high speed. If anyone hesitates, says 'um,' or repeats an action, they swap out with a new player from the sidelines.
- Emotional Overlay: The person asking the question asks it with a specific emotion (e.g., angrily, joyfully), and the responder must answer in that same emotional state while naming their new action.

## Debrief
- How did it feel when you tried to plan your answer ahead of time versus when you just let the first word slip out?
- What happened to your physical commitment when you were trying to think of a verbal answer?
- How does this game help us practice 'failing joyfully' when our brain short-circuits?

## Safety & Inclusion
Ensure the physical space is clear of tripping hazards. Players with limited mobility can adapt the physical mime to use upper-body movements, facial expressions, or verbal descriptions of physical states. Encourage a supportive, low-pressure atmosphere where 'brain freezes' are met with laughter and applause.

## Why It Works
By forcing the brain to perform one physical task while generating an unrelated verbal concept, the game disrupts the analytical 'editor' mind. This cognitive overload makes it impossible to plan ahead, forcing the player to rely entirely on their unfiltered, spontaneous first thoughts.
