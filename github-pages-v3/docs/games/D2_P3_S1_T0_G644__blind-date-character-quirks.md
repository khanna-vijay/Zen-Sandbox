# The Quirky Dating Show

![The Quirky Dating Show — infographic](img/D2_P3_S1_T0_G644__blind-date-character-quirks.jpg){ .game-hero }

> A hilarious matchmaking game of active listening, bold character choices, and supportive guessing.

## Overview
In this classic short-form comedy game, a single dater attempts to find love among three mysterious contestants, each harboring a bizarre, audience-generated quirk or secret identity. Guided by an engaging host, the dater must ask questions, listen intently to the clues hidden in the answers, and guess the contestants' eccentricities. The joy of the game lies in the playful banter, the bold physical and vocal choices of the contestants, and the dater's enthusiastic, unfiltered guesses.

**Objective:** Develops active listening, supportive hosting, and spontaneous character commitment. It teaches players to make their partners look brilliant by treating every offer as a gift and embracing mistakes with joy.

## Setup
Arrange five chairs on stage: three side-by-side for the contestants, one for the host on the side, and one for the dater facing the contestants. The remaining workshop participants act as the live studio audience. No props are required.

## How to Play
1. Assign the five roles: one Host, one Dater, and three Contestants (Contestant 1, 2, and 3).
2. Send the Dater out of the room or have them cover their ears so they cannot hear the stage.
3. The Host solicits three distinct, highly active quirks or secret identities from the audience—one for each contestant (e.g., 'is secretly a werewolf,' 'can only speak in questions,' or 'thinks they are a famous historical figure').
4. Bring the Dater back to the stage and have them sit in their designated chair, ready to begin the mock dating show.
5. The Host introduces the show, sets a playful tone, and invites the Dater to ask questions to the contestants.
6. The Dater asks a series of questions (ideally the same question to all three contestants in a round) to compare their answers and observe their behavior.
7. The Contestants answer the questions while fully committing to their assigned quirks, dropping subtle to obvious clues through their dialogue, physical posture, and vocal delivery.
8. The Host actively supports the scene by highlighting funny moments, prompting contestants to elaborate, or giving the Dater gentle nudges if they are struggling to pick up on a clue.
9. The Dater makes playful, active guesses throughout the rounds rather than waiting until the very end, celebrating incorrect guesses and building on the comedic momentum.
10. The game concludes once the Dater successfully identifies all three quirks, or when the Host calls a high-energy wrap-up.

## Facilitation Notes
- Encourage the Dater to guess early and often. Waiting for the 'perfect' guess slows down the comedic momentum; wrong guesses are often funnier and give contestants more material to play with.
- Remind Contestants to show, not just tell. Instead of explicitly stating their quirk, they should embody it through physical posture, vocal tone, and behavioral reactions.
- The Host's primary job is to make everyone look good. If a contestant's quirk is too obscure, the Host can ask a leading question to help them showcase it more clearly.
- Pitfall: Contestants becoming too subtle, making it impossible to guess. Fix: Side-coach them to 'turn the volume up' on their quirk by 20%.

## Variations
- Reverse Roles: The contestants are normal, and the Dater is the one with a secret quirk that the contestants must figure out through their questions.
- Emotional Quirks: Instead of physical or situational quirks, contestants are assigned specific, extreme emotional states or relationship dynamics toward the Dater.
- Themed Dating Show: Set the dating show in a specific genre or era, such as a medieval court, a sci-fi space station, or a 1920s noir film, forcing all players to adapt their characters to the setting.

## Debrief
- For the Dater: How did active listening help you piece together the clues, and how did it feel to make incorrect guesses?
- For the Contestants: How did committing to a physical or vocal quirk make it easier to improvise your answers spontaneously?
- For the Host: What strategies did you use to support your fellow players and keep the energy of the show high?
- How does treating your partner's mistakes as gifts make the overall performance more entertaining for the audience?

## Safety & Inclusion
Ensure that audience suggestions for quirks are respectful and do not rely on offensive stereotypes, physical disabilities, or mental health conditions. Encourage players to establish physical boundaries before the game starts, especially if a quirk might involve physical proximity or touch.

## Why It Works
This game works because it shifts the pressure of 'being funny' away from the individual and onto the collaborative dynamic. The Dater relies entirely on active listening to discover clues, while the Contestants rely on physical and vocal commitment to deliver those clues. By framing the guessing process as a playful dialogue rather than a test, players learn to fail joyfully, turning incorrect guesses into comedic gold and making their partners look like geniuses.
