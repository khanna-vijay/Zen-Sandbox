# Spontaneous Presents

<div class="game-hero-ph" markdown>🎨 *Infographic coming soon*</div>

> Instantly receive and celebrate imaginary gifts using the very first word that pops into your head.

## Overview
A rapid-fire, paired warm-up where players exchange invisible gifts, naming them instantly without hesitation. By celebrating whatever absurd or mundane object is named, players bypass their internal editor and practice radical acceptance. The focus is on speed, joy, and trusting your immediate subconscious impulses.

## What It Trains
- **Domain:** D1 — The Self
- **Principle(s):** The First Thought Is a Gift; Yes, And; Make Your Partner a Genius
- **Skill(s):** Unfiltered Spontaneity; Offer Reception; Active Gifting
- **Technique(s):** First Thought drills; Endowment-acceptance; Endowment-gifting drills
- **Focus:** skill_drill

**Objective:** To develop unfiltered spontaneity and trust in one's first instinct, training players to treat their initial thoughts as valuable gifts rather than filtering or judging them.

## Setup
Players stand in pairs facing each other. No physical props are needed. Ensure there is enough space between pairs to allow for comfortable physical gesturing.

## How to Play
1. Divide the group into pairs and have partners stand facing one another.
2. Player A initiates by miming the physical act of handing over an invisible, unspecified object of any size or shape, without deciding what it is.
3. Player B immediately takes the invisible object, looks at it, and names it with the absolute first word or phrase that enters their mind (e.g., 'Wow, a rusty bicycle!').
4. Player B must express genuine, enthusiastic gratitude for the gift, no matter how strange, useless, or mundane it might seem.
5. Without pausing to admire the gift further, Player B immediately mimes handing a new invisible gift back to Player A.
6. Player A receives the gift, instantly names it (e.g., 'Oh my gosh, a single blueberry!'), expresses delight, and immediately passes a new gift back.
7. Keep the exchange moving at a rapid, breathless pace for two to three minutes, leaving no time for hesitation or planning.

## Facilitation Notes
- Side-coaching cue: 'Don't think, just speak! The first word is the right word!'
- Pitfall: Players trying to make 'clever' or 'funny' choices, which causes a delay. Fix: Remind them that mundane gifts (like 'a shoe' or 'a brick') are just as wonderful as bizarre ones. Speed beats cleverness.
- Pitfall: The giver deciding what the gift is before handing it over. Fix: Instruct givers to keep their hands completely neutral and let the receiver do 100% of the naming.
- Side-coaching cue: 'Celebrate every single gift like it is the one thing you have wanted your entire life!'

## Variations
- Emotional Gifts: Instead of physical objects, players hand over abstract concepts or emotions (e.g., 'A sense of peace' or 'Mild confusion'), reacting with the corresponding emotion.
- The 'Why' Add-on: After naming the gift, the receiver must instantly add one sentence explaining why it is perfect (e.g., 'A dead leaf! Perfect, I needed a bookmark!'). Keep this fast to avoid stalling.
- Physicalized Gifts: The giver uses distinct physical shapes (very heavy, tiny, sticky) to dictate the physical nature of the gift, but the receiver still names it instantly.

## Debrief
- How did it feel to say the very first word that came to mind without filtering it?
- What happened when you tried to be clever or plan your gift ahead of time?
- How does expressing instant, enthusiastic gratitude change your relationship to an unexpected offer?

## Safety & Inclusion
Ensure players are mindful of physical boundaries when miming the exchange of gifts. If players have limited mobility, the physical hand-off can be represented purely through eye contact and vocal cues.

## Why It Works
This drill works by removing the fear of making a 'bad' choice. By demanding extreme speed and pairing it with unconditional positive reinforcement (enthusiastic gratitude), the brain's analytical editor is bypassed. This teaches players that their first instinct is not only sufficient but is a delightful gift to the scene.
