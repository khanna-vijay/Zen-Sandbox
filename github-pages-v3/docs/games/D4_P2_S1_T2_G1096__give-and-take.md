# Give and Take: Sound and Motion

![Give and Take: Sound and Motion — infographic](img/D4_P2_S1_T2_G1096__give-and-take.jpg){ .game-hero }

> Pass the invisible baton of movement and sound using pure group awareness.

## Overview
In this ensemble exercise, players distribute their focus across a shared space to ensure that, at any given moment, exactly one person is moving and exactly one person is making sound. Players must dynamically pass these roles back and forth without verbal cues, relying entirely on peripheral vision and auditory sensitivity. The result is a fluid, improvised dance of silence, sound, stillness, and motion.

**Objective:** To develop deep peripheral awareness, group mind, and the ability to 'follow the follower' by sharing focus seamlessly without explicit leadership.

## Setup
An open room cleared of obstacles. Players scatter randomly throughout the space, standing in stillness and silence, facing any direction they choose.

## How to Play
1. Instruct all players to stand scattered and still in the space, establishing a baseline of silence.
2. Introduce the two active roles: the 'Mover' (who must walk through the space) and the 'Sounder' (who must make a continuous or repeating vocal sound).
3. Explain the core constraint: at any given moment, there must be exactly one Mover and exactly one Sounder in the entire room.
4. Clarify that these roles can be held by the same person (one person moving and making sound) or by two different people (one person moving, another making sound).
5. To initiate play, any player may spontaneously start walking (becoming the Mover) or start making sound (becoming the Sounder).
6. To 'give' focus, an active player stops their action (e.g., the Mover freezes), signaling to the group that someone else must immediately take over that role.
7. To 'take' focus, an inactive player starts an action (e.g., a new player starts walking), which obligates the currently active player of that role to immediately stop.
8. Encourage players to experiment with both giving and taking, aiming for seamless transitions where the sound or movement never fully dies out and never doubles up.

## Facilitation Notes
- Side-coaching cue: 'Feel the space behind you. Use your peripheral vision and hearing, not just your eyes.'
- Pitfall: Multiple people moving or making sound at once. Fix: Remind players to yield instantly when they sense a double-up. It is better to have a moment of complete stillness than a chaotic double-up.
- Side-coaching cue: 'Vary the quality of your sound and movement. A slow crawl invites a different sound than a brisk march.'
- Pitfall: The same two or three confident players dominating the roles. Fix: Challenge the group to let the quieter voices initiate, or instruct dominant players to actively 'give' and wait before 'taking' again.

## Variations
- Double Trouble: Increase the complexity by requiring exactly two Movers and two Sounders at all times.
- Emotional Resonance: The Sounder must express a specific, clear emotion through their vocalization, and the Mover must physicalize that same emotion in their movement style.
- Virtual Adaptation: In a video grid, 'moving' is replaced by moving close to or away from the camera, while 'sounding' remains vocal.

## Debrief
- How did you know when it was your turn to take or give focus without looking directly at everyone?
- What did it feel like when there was a gap of silence or stillness? Did you feel rushed to fill it?
- How did the quality of the sound influence the movement of the walker, and vice versa?

## Safety & Inclusion
For players with mobility challenges, 'moving' can be adapted to any physical gesture, head movement, or upper-body motion. For non-verbal players, 'sounding' can be adapted to clapping, snapping, or using an assistive device or instrument.

## Why It Works
This game works because it strips away explicit verbal communication, forcing players to rely on their non-verbal channels of connection. By practicing 'follow the follower,' players learn to yield their own impulses to the needs of the collective, building a shared rhythm and a heightened state of ensemble presence.
