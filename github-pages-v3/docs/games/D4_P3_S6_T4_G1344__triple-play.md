# The Triptych Play

<div class="game-hero-ph" markdown>🎨 *Infographic coming soon*</div>

> Interweave three distinct three-act plays, testing narrative memory, pacing, and structural discipline.

## Overview
The Triptych Play is an advanced longform performance format where an ensemble improvises three completely unrelated stories simultaneously. The performance is structured chronologically by act: the audience witnesses Act I of all three stories, followed by Act II of all three, and finally Act III of all three. This structural challenge demands high narrative tracking, distinct characterization, and sharp editing from the ensemble.

## What It Trains
- **Domain:** D4 — The Ensemble
- **Principle(s):** Serve the Piece; Serve the Story; The Audience Is the Final Scene Partner
- **Skill(s):** Format Literacy; Pacing & Rhythm; Narrative Architecture; Room Reading
- **Technique(s):** Longform vs. shortform mechanics; Edits (Sweep, Tag-Out, Sound/Light); Story Spine
- **Focus:** narrative

**Objective:** Develops format literacy, narrative architecture, and ensemble-wide pacing by managing multiple parallel storylines and understanding how to structure clear beginnings, middles, and ends under strict structural constraints.

## Setup
An in-person performance space with a clear stage area and an audience area. The ensemble of 6 to 12 proficient players stands offstage or on the back line. No props or set pieces are required. A host or facilitator is present to introduce the format and gather suggestions.

## How to Play
1. Solicit three distinct, completely unrelated suggestions from the audience to inspire three separate plays, which we will call Play A, Play B, and Play C.
2. Establish the core casts for each play from the ensemble, ensuring players make distinct physical and vocal choices to help the audience track the different stories.
3. Perform Act I of Play A, focusing on establishing the platform, characters, and the inciting incident, then edit the scene cleanly.
4. Immediately perform Act I of Play B, followed by Act I of Play C, editing each scene once the initial platform and conflict are established.
5. Transition to the second acts, performing Act II of Play A to raise the stakes and introduce complications, followed by Act II of Play B, and then Act II of Play C.
6. Conclude the performance with the third acts, performing Act III of Play A to resolve its central conflict, followed by Act III of Play B, and finally Act III of Play C.
7. Use decisive, clean edits such as sweep edits or designated host transitions between each act to signal to the audience which narrative is active.

## Facilitation Notes
- Coaching Tip: Encourage players to make bold, memorable physical and vocal choices in Act I. Because of the time gap between acts, these distinct offers help the audience instantly recognize which story has returned.
- Pitfall & Fix: Story bleeding, where players accidentally mix narrative elements or characters from different plays. Fix: Have offstage players act as active editors, keeping a mental log of each world and calling out transitions if onstage players falter.
- Coaching Tip: Focus on narrative economy. Each act should only be two to three minutes long. Do not try to resolve conflicts early; trust the three-act structure and leave unresolved cliffhangers at the end of Act II.
- Coaching Tip: Read the room. Use the audience's reactions to gauge which narrative threads need the most clarity or emotional payoff in the final acts.

## Variations
- Genre Triptych: Assign a distinct theatrical or cinematic genre to each of the three plays, such as Shakespearean tragedy, Film Noir, and Sci-Fi space opera, to make the worlds highly contrasting.
- The Monoscene Anchor: One of the three plays must take place in real-time in a single location, while the other two are free-roaming narratives, creating a contrast in pacing.
- Audience Director: The host checks in with the audience between acts, letting them vote on a minor complication or twist to inject into the upcoming Act II or III.

## Debrief
- How did the delay between acts affect your ability to track your character's emotional journey and narrative arc?
- What techniques did you use to instantly re-establish the world of your play when returning for Act II and Act III?
- How does serving the overall structure of the piece differ from simply following individual scene momentum?

## Safety & Inclusion
Ensure that players establish clear physical boundaries before the show, especially since they will be jumping in and out of three different high-energy narratives. If intense themes are suggested, the ensemble should have a pre-agreed-upon signal to de-escalate or redirect the narrative safely.

## Why It Works
By slicing three narratives into parallel acts, this format forces improvisers to abandon immediate gratification and think structurally. It builds format literacy by demonstrating how narrative tension is sustained over time and how clean edits and distinct character choices serve the larger artistic piece rather than just the immediate scene.
