# The Tag-Out Edit

![The Tag-Out Edit — infographic](img/D4_P3_S4_T1_G1319__tagout.jpg){ .game-hero }

> Transform scenes instantly by freezing action and swapping players to shift time, place, or perspective.

## Overview
A foundational editing game where players transition between scenes by physically tagging an active performer. The tagged player exits, while the remaining player freezes in place, instantly adopting a new character, relationship, or setting initiated by the incoming player. It builds a seamless, high-energy chain of interconnected scenes.

## What It Trains
- **Domain:** D4 — The Ensemble
- **Principle(s):** Serve the Piece; Start in the Middle
- **Skill(s):** Pacing & Rhythm; Support Work; World-Building
- **Technique(s):** Edits (Sweep, Tag-Out, Sound/Light); Tap-ins
- **Focus:** mixed

**Objective:** To master the physical tag-out edit, developing sharp pacing, rhythmic transitions, and the ability to instantly support a partner's physical offer.

## Setup
An open performance space with a clear stage area and a backstage line where off-stage players stand ready to edit. No props or chairs are needed.

## How to Play
1. Two players step into the performance space and begin a standard, relationship-focused scene with active physical object work.
2. The remaining players stand in a line at the back or sides of the stage, actively watching the scene for edit opportunities.
3. At any point, an off-stage player can decide to edit by running forward, physically tapping one of the active players on the shoulder, and calling out 'Tag!'
4. Upon being tagged, the tagged player must immediately exit the stage, while the untagged player must freeze instantly in their exact physical posture.
5. The incoming player assumes a physical position relative to the frozen player and initiates a brand-new scene, immediately establishing a new location, time, or relationship.
6. The remaining player must instantly drop their previous character, accept their physical posture as the starting point for a new character, and 'yes-and' the incoming player's initiation.
7. Play continues in this chain-reaction style, with players constantly tagging in and out to keep the rhythm brisk and the edits clean.

## Facilitation Notes
- Side-coach players to tag with purpose: don't wait for a scene to die; tag when a scene is at a high point or when a physical posture looks interesting to play with.
- Pitfall: The remaining player tries to keep their old character. Fix: Remind them that a tag-out completely resets the universe; they are a brand-new character in a brand-new world, using only their physical shape as inspiration.
- Encourage light, safe physical contact. A gentle tap on the shoulder is all that is needed to signal the edit.
- Side-coaching cue: 'Freeze instantly! Don't adjust your arms or stand up straight before the new scene starts.'

## Variations
- Character Continuity: The remaining player keeps their exact character, but the incoming player tags in to play a different person from that character's life, shifting the scene to a different time or place (e.g., a flashback or flash-forward).
- Transformation Tag: The incoming player tags both players out, taking the exact physical pose of one of them, while a second incoming player takes the pose of the other, completely resetting the scene with the same physical composition.
- Speed Rounds: Challenge the group to make edits every 30 seconds, forcing rapid-fire physical initiations and high-tempo pacing.

## Debrief
- How did freezing in a physical shape help you discover your next character?
- What made an edit feel satisfying and well-timed versus disruptive?
- How does keeping the physical posture of the remaining player help build immediate world-building in the next scene?

## Safety & Inclusion
Ensure all players consent to light physical contact (a tap on the shoulder). If a player prefers not to be touched, establish a non-contact alternative, such as stepping into their line of sight and calling 'Tag!' or clapping loudly to signal the freeze.

## Why It Works
By forcing one player to remain frozen, the game removes the cognitive load of deciding 'who' or 'where' to start. The physical shape dictates the new character's status and emotion, while the incoming player's initiation provides the context. This mechanical constraint ensures rapid, high-agreement transitions that keep the ensemble's momentum moving forward.
