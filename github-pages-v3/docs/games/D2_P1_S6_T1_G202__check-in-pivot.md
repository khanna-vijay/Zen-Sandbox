# Check-In and Pivot

![Check-In and Pivot — infographic](img/D2_P1_S6_T1_G202__check-in-pivot.jpg){ .game-hero }

> Pause the action, negotiate boundaries in real-time, and seamlessly pivot the narrative together.

## Overview
A structured skill drill where players pause active scenes to explicitly negotiate physical, emotional, or narrative boundaries. By stepping momentarily out of character, players co-create a safe playing space before seamlessly resuming the action with newly agreed-upon parameters. This practice transforms safety protocols into active, collaborative improv tools.

## What It Trains
- **Domain:** D2 — The Partner
- **Principle(s):** Consent & Boundaries; Yes, And; Truth Over Pandering
- **Skill(s):** Boundary Navigation; Active Listening; Offer Reception
- **Technique(s):** Check-ins; Cut calls; Negotiating physical contact; Yes, And… sentence games
- **Focus:** skill_drill

**Objective:** To build practical boundary navigation and active listening skills, normalizing real-time consent check-ins as a collaborative tool rather than a creative disruption.

## Setup
An open playing space with no props. Players stand in a circle or line, with two players stepping into the center to perform a scene based on a simple relationship prompt provided by the facilitator.

## How to Play
1. Two players begin a character-driven scene based on a simple relationship prompt provided by the facilitator.
2. At any point during the scene, if a player feels a boundary is being approached—whether physical, emotional, or narrative—they must call out 'Check-in!' and freeze the action.
3. Both players immediately step physically out of character (e.g., dropping their posture, making direct eye contact as themselves) to signal a brief meta-conversation.
4. The player who initiated the check-in clearly and concisely states their boundary or question (e.g., 'I want to hug you here, are you comfortable with physical contact?' or 'This argument is getting very intense, are you okay with this level of anger?').
5. The partner must respond honestly using one of three structured options: 'Yes, and...' (fully comfortable, let's proceed), 'Yes, but...' (comfortable with specific limits), or 'No, and...' (not comfortable, proposing a specific alternative direction).
6. Once both players agree on the boundary and the new direction, the initiator says 'Continue!' and both players step back into character, immediately integrating the negotiated terms.
7. If a check-in cannot be resolved comfortably, or if an immediate safety concern arises, any player (or the facilitator) can call 'Cut!' to instantly end the scene without penalty or explanation.

## Facilitation Notes
- Normalize the pause: Remind players that checking in is a sign of high-level partnership, not a failure of flow. Praise early check-ins.
- Keep it brief: Ensure the meta-conversation lasts no longer than 30 seconds. If players begin debating or over-explaining, step in and prompt them to choose a 'No, and...' pivot.
- Enforce the structured responses: If a partner responds vaguely, coach them to use the exact phrasing ('Yes, and', 'Yes, but', or 'No, and') to keep the negotiation clean and actionable.
- Celebrate the 'No, and...': Explicitly highlight 'No, and...' as a highly creative tool that protects the player while offering a fresh, exciting narrative gift to the scene.

## Variations
- Blind Check-ins: The facilitator calls 'Check-in!' at random intervals, forcing players to practice assessing their current comfort levels and negotiating the next beat proactively.
- Physical-Only Focus: Restrict the check-ins specifically to physical proximity and touch, training players to negotiate spatial boundaries before making physical contact.

## Debrief
- How did it feel to explicitly state a boundary mid-scene? Did it disrupt your creative flow as much as you expected?
- How did receiving a 'No, and...' response affect your sense of trust with your partner?
- In what ways did negotiating a boundary actually open up new, unexpected narrative directions?

## Safety & Inclusion
This game is highly safety-sensitive. Establish a firm rule that no player is ever required to explain why they have a boundary. A 'No, and...' or a 'Cut!' must be accepted immediately and without question or debate. Ensure players know they can step out of the exercise at any time.

## Why It Works
By formalizing the check-in as an explicit game mechanic, it removes the social stigma of stopping a scene to address comfort. The structured response system ('Yes, and', 'Yes, but', 'No, and') reframes boundary setting as an act of collaborative playwriting, proving that respecting personal limits actually fuels creative variety rather than restricting it.
