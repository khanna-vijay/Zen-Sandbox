# Watching You, Watching Me

![Watching You, Watching Me — infographic](img/D2_P3_S1_T1_G737__i-m-watching-you-watching-me.jpg){ .game-hero }

> Build deep connection and hyper-awareness by repeating a simple phrase while maintaining unbroken eye contact.

## Overview
This intimate, low-energy exercise pairs players up to sit face-to-face and exchange a single, repeating phrase without breaking eye contact. By stripping away the need to invent dialogue, players become acutely aware of their partner's micro-expressions, breathing patterns, and emotional shifts. It creates a shared space of vulnerability and deep presence, grounding players in the reality of the moment.

## What It Trains
- **Domain:** D2 — The Partner
- **Principle(s):** Make Your Partner a Genius; Vulnerability
- **Skill(s):** Active Listening; Single-Partner Empathy & Mirroring; Silence & Stillness
- **Technique(s):** Meisner Repetition; Mirror exercise
- **Focus:** connection

**Objective:** To develop active listening, empathy, and presence through Meisner-style repetition, training players to read and respond to subtle physical cues rather than intellectualizing their next line.

## Setup
Arrange chairs in pairs throughout the room, facing each other directly. The chairs should be close enough that partners' knees are nearly touching, creating an intimate, focused space. No props or materials are required.

## How to Play
1. Divide the group into pairs and have each pair sit facing one another, maintaining comfortable but close physical proximity.
2. Instruct partners to establish and maintain continuous eye contact, letting go of the need to look away or break the connection.
3. Player A begins by looking at Player B and saying, 'I am watching you, watching me,' delivering the line with genuine presence.
4. Player B immediately responds by looking back and repeating the exact same phrase: 'I am watching you, watching me.'
5. Continue this back-and-forth exchange rhythmically, allowing the tone, speed, and emotional quality of the phrase to shift naturally based on what is observed in the partner.
6. Encourage players to welcome any natural impulses—such as laughter, sighs, or shifts in posture—without trying to suppress them or force a specific performance.
7. After approximately two minutes, call freeze, have players thank their partners, and rotate to a new partner to repeat the process.

## Facilitation Notes
- Side-coaching cue: 'Notice the tiny shifts. Is their breath shallow? Did their eyes soften? Let those details affect how you say your next line.'
- Pitfall: Players might turn the exercise into a staring contest or a joke. Fix: Remind them that blinking is allowed and encouraged; this is about connection, not endurance or dominance.
- Side-coaching cue: 'If you feel the urge to laugh, let it out. Don't try to be stoic or dramatic. Just be present with whatever arises.'
- Pitfall: Rushing the phrase to get it over with. Fix: Coach them to slow down and truly hear the partner's delivery before responding.

## Variations
- Silent Observation: Run the exercise for one minute in complete silence before introducing the spoken phrase, focusing entirely on breath and micro-movements.
- Physical Mirroring: Allow players to subtly mirror the physical posture or breathing rhythm of their partner as they repeat the phrase.
- Behavioral Observation: Instead of the fixed phrase, players state a simple, objective physical observation about their partner (e.g., 'Your shoulders dropped' or 'You smiled'), which the partner then repeats back to confirm.

## Debrief
- What did you notice about your partner's face or body that you might normally miss in a standard scene?
- How did it feel to have your only job be looking and listening, without needing to invent new words?
- How did the meaning or feeling of the phrase change over the course of the two minutes?

## Safety & Inclusion
Because prolonged eye contact and close physical proximity can feel intense or uncomfortable for some participants (including neurodivergent players or those with trauma histories), explicitly state that players may look at their partner's nose, forehead, or general facial area if direct eye contact becomes overwhelming. Establish a non-verbal signal (like a hand on the heart) to step back or take a break if needed.

## Why It Works
By removing the cognitive load of inventing dialogue, the exercise forces players to focus entirely on the 'other.' It leverages Meisner-style repetition to bypass the analytical mind, training improvisers to respond to the subtext of their partner's physical reality. This builds the foundational habit of making the partner the most important element in the room.
