# The Genre Cauldron

<div class="game-hero-ph" markdown>🎨 *Infographic coming soon*</div>

> Toss obvious tropes into an imaginary pot to rapidly distill the essence of any genre.

## Overview
Players stand in a circle around an imaginary cauldron, taking turns rapidly tossing in physicalized tropes, clichés, and archetypes from a chosen genre. By shouting out the first obvious association that comes to mind, the group quickly builds a shared vocabulary and demystifies genre-based storytelling.

## What It Trains
- **Domain:** D1 — The Self
- **Principle(s):** The First Thought Is a Gift; Group Mind
- **Skill(s):** Unfiltered Spontaneity; Suggestion Deconstruction (A-to-C); World-Building
- **Technique(s):** First Thought drills; Premise brainstorm rounds
- **Focus:** skill_drill

**Objective:** To train unfiltered spontaneity and rapid association, helping players trust their first thoughts and embrace the obvious clichés that define narrative genres.

## Setup
Players stand in a circle in an open space. No props or materials are required. The facilitator stands as part of the circle to introduce the first genre.

## How to Play
1. Gather the group in a standing circle around an imaginary cauldron in the center of the floor.
2. The facilitator or a player names a specific genre, such as Sci-Fi Space Opera, Noir Detective, or Romantic Comedy.
3. Players take turns stepping forward slightly, making a physical gesture of throwing something into the central cauldron, and calling out a single trope, cliché, character archetype, or visual element of that genre.
4. Encourage players to speak instantly without filtering, hesitation, or trying to be clever—the goal is to name the most obvious elements.
5. As each item is thrown in, the rest of the group physically mimics stirring the cauldron or reacting to the ingredient to maintain high energy and group focus.
6. Once the cauldron is bubbling over with tropes after about one to two minutes, the facilitator calls out a new genre, and the process repeats with a fresh pot.

## Facilitation Notes
- Side-coach players to avoid editing their thoughts. If they think of laser gun for Sci-Fi, they should say it immediately, even if someone else just said blaster.
- Address the pitfall of players pausing to think of a unique or funny trope by reminding them that the most obvious, cliché elements are the most useful for building a recognizable genre world.
- Keep the pace brisk. If a player hesitates, encourage them to make a sound or a physical gesture first, and let the word follow naturally.
- Encourage physicalization: don't just say the word, mime tossing it in with the weight or energy of that specific object.

## Variations
- Stirring the Soup: After a few ingredients are thrown in, a player must step forward, taste the soup, and act out a five-second silent scene incorporating those elements.
- A-to-C Cauldron: Instead of direct tropes, players must throw in an association of the previous player's ingredient, stretching the genre boundaries.
- Character Cauldron: Focus entirely on character archetypes and emotional states rather than physical objects or plot points.

## Debrief
- How did it feel to say the most obvious thing instead of searching for a clever or unique idea?
- How does establishing these common clichés help us when we improvise a full scene in a specific genre?
- Did you notice any patterns in how quickly the group aligned on the vibe of the genre once the first few ingredients were thrown in?

## Safety & Inclusion
Ensure the physical space in the center of the circle is clear of obstacles. Players with limited mobility can gesture or throw ingredients verbally from their spot in the circle without stepping forward.

## Why It Works
This drill bypasses the analytical brain's filter by demanding high-speed, low-stakes contributions. By celebrating the obvious instead of the original, players realize that shared clichés are the building blocks of collaborative world-building and instant group mind.
