# Identity Check

<div class="game-hero-ph" markdown>🎨 *Infographic coming soon*</div>

> Craft rich characters and scenes inspired directly by the real-world IDs of your audience.

## Overview
In this game, players borrow physical identification cards from audience members to inspire their characters. By directly addressing the crowd and weaving real-world details into the performance, players build a highly collaborative bridge between the stage and the seats. The resulting scene blends truth and fiction into a grounded, high-energy narrative.

## What It Trains
- **Domain:** D5 — The Audience
- **Principle(s):** The Audience Is the Final Scene Partner; Base Reality First; The First Thought Is a Gift
- **Skill(s):** Audience-Energy Management; World-Building; Physicality & Space Work
- **Technique(s):** Breaking the 4th Wall / Direct Address; C.R.O.W. (Character, Relationship, Objective, Where); Character Walks/Centers
- **Focus:** mixed

**Objective:** To develop audience-energy management and direct address techniques by treating the audience's real-world artifacts as the ultimate scene partners and building a grounded base reality from them.

## Setup
A performance space with a clear boundary between the stage and the audience. The facilitator or host needs to collect two to three physical IDs (such as driver's licenses, library cards, or student IDs) from willing audience members before the scene starts.

## How to Play
1. The host asks for two or three volunteers from the audience who are willing to temporarily lend a physical ID for the duration of the scene.
2. The host or players receive the IDs, ensuring they handle them respectfully, and read out key details like the name, hometown, or a description of the photo to the entire audience.
3. The players briefly interview the ID owners, asking one or two low-stakes questions to gather additional inspiration about their lives.
4. Each performing player adopts one of the IDs as their character's official identity, committing to the name, age, and physical vibe suggested by the card.
5. The players initiate a scene, establishing a clear base reality that connects these distinct characters in a shared environment.
6. Throughout the scene, players can break the fourth wall to check back in with the actual ID owners, asking for clarification or reacting to their real-time expressions.
7. The scene concludes once a satisfying narrative arc is reached, and the players return the IDs to the audience members with a warm round of applause.

## Facilitation Notes
- Side-coaching cue: Look at the photo on the ID and let that facial expression dictate your character's physical posture and attitude.
- Side-coaching cue: Don't just play the facts; play the feeling of the card. If it is an old library card, maybe they are deeply nostalgic.
- Pitfall: Players might make fun of the audience member's photo or personal details. Fix: Remind players to treat the audience as their ultimate partner; the goal is to celebrate and elevate the details, not mock them.
- Pitfall: Getting bogged down in too many biographical details. Fix: Use the First Thought Is a Gift principle—take one or two striking details from the ID and immediately jump into action rather than trying to memorize the whole card.

## Variations
- The Lost Wallet: Instead of individual IDs, one audience member lends a wallet, and the players reconstruct a single character's entire life based on the collection of cards and receipts inside.
- The ID Lineup: Players act as detectives trying to solve a mystery, using the borrowed IDs as the suspect lineup and interviewing the actual audience members as witnesses.

## Debrief
- How did having a physical, real-world artifact change how quickly you committed to your character's physicality and voice?
- What did you notice about the audience's engagement when their actual identities were woven into the fabric of the scene?
- How did breaking the fourth wall to check in with the ID owners help manage the energy in the room?

## Safety & Inclusion
Always obtain explicit consent before taking an ID, and never read sensitive personal information aloud, such as home addresses, license numbers, or exact birth years if the person is uncomfortable. Ensure physical cards are returned immediately after the game. Offer alternative options like digital loyalty cards or student IDs if someone is uncomfortable sharing a government-issued ID.

## Why It Works
By using real audience artifacts, the game instantly breaks down the barrier between the stage and the house. It forces players to establish a strong base reality grounded in truth, while the act of direct address keeps the audience actively invested as co-creators of the theatrical world.
