# Receiver's Right

<div class="game-hero-ph" markdown>🎨 *Infographic coming soon*</div>

> Track the target and launch the next offer from the periphery.

## Overview
A high-focus circle warm-up where the person who receives an energy pass is not the one who sends it. Instead, the player standing directly to the receiver's right must instantly launch the next pass, forcing the entire group to track the thread of focus rather than just their own turns.

## What It Trains
- **Domain:** D4 — The Ensemble
- **Principle(s):** Commit 100%; Group Mind; Follow the Follower
- **Skill(s):** Unfiltered Spontaneity; Active Listening; Offer Reception; Peripheral Awareness; Pacing & Rhythm
- **Technique(s):** Thread-tracking drills; Timing exercises
- **Focus:** connection

**Objective:** To build peripheral awareness and group mind by training players to track interactions they are not directly involved in, preparing them to support and build on moments instantly.

## Setup
Players stand in a comfortable, open circle with clear lines of sight to everyone else. No props or materials are required.

## How to Play
1. Gather the group into a standing circle and establish a steady, focused energy.
2. Demonstrate the 'Send' gesture: press your palms together at your chest, then push them outward toward another player while making a sharp 'Whoof!' sound.
3. Demonstrate the 'Receive' gesture: open your arms wide and pull them inward to your chest as if catching a beach ball, making a sharp inhaling 'Sip' sound.
4. Explain the core rule: when Player A sends a pass to Player B, Player B must catch it with the 'Sip' gesture and sound. However, Player B does not send the next pass.
5. Instead, the player standing immediately to Player B's right (Player C) must instantly initiate a new 'Whoof' pass to any other player in the circle.
6. Begin the game with a slow, deliberate pass to establish the pattern, ensuring everyone identifies who received the pass and who is to their right.
7. Encourage the group to gradually increase the speed and rhythm as the pattern becomes second nature.

## Facilitation Notes
- Side-coaching cue: 'Keep your eyes on the whole circle, not just the person with the energy!'
- Common Pitfall: The receiver tries to send the pass themselves out of habit. Fix: Have the receiver freeze in their 'catch' pose to clearly signal to their right-hand neighbor that it is their turn to launch.
- Side-coaching cue: 'Support your neighbor! If you are to the right, be ready to spring into action the moment the catch is made.'
- Common Pitfall: Hesitation from the launcher. Fix: Remind players that a messy, fast pass is better than a perfect, delayed one. Commit to the impulse.

## Variations
- Receiver's Left: Switch the launching duty to the player standing immediately to the receiver's left.
- Double Skip: The player standing two spots to the right (skipping one person) must launch the next pass.
- Blind Launch: The receiver catches the pass with their eyes closed, forcing the neighbor to rely purely on the audio cue of the 'Sip' sound to launch.

## Debrief
- How did it feel to have your action triggered by someone else's reception rather than your own?
- What did you have to do with your visual focus to ensure you didn't miss your cue?
- How does this level of peripheral awareness translate to supporting a scene from the sidelines?

## Safety & Inclusion
Ensure the physical gestures are adaptable; players with limited mobility can use a simple head nod, eye contact, or vocal cue instead of the full arm extension.

## Why It Works
By decoupling the act of receiving from the act of sending, this game disrupts the standard 'ping-pong' flow of circle games. It forces players to maintain active, split-second tracking of the entire group's spatial relationships, directly building the 'Group Mind' needed for ensemble-driven long-form improv.
