# The Counter-Current Wave

<div class="game-hero-ph" markdown>🎨 *Infographic coming soon*</div>

> Pass a physical wave and a vocal name-chain in opposite directions simultaneously.

## Overview
A high-energy focus warm-up where players stand in a circle and manage two distinct impulses traveling in opposite directions. One impulse is a physical arm wave, while the other is a spoken name-pass. When the two waves collide, players must split their attention to keep both tracks moving without dropping the rhythm.

## What It Trains
- **Domain:** D4 — The Ensemble
- **Principle(s):** Group Mind; Fail Joyfully
- **Skill(s):** Peripheral Awareness; Pacing & Rhythm; Unfiltered Spontaneity
- **Technique(s):** Thread-tracking drills; Timing exercises
- **Focus:** connection

**Objective:** To develop peripheral awareness and group mind by training players to track multiple physical and auditory threads simultaneously without losing focus or pacing.

## Setup
Have all players stand in a clear, open circle with enough space to raise their arms fully without hitting their neighbors. No props or materials are required.

## How to Play
1. Gather the group into a standing circle facing inward, ensuring everyone has clear sightlines to all other players.
2. Introduce the physical wave: One player starts by raising both arms overhead and lowering them, prompting the neighbor to their right to do the same as soon as the first player's arms reach the peak.
3. Practice this physical wave clockwise around the circle until it moves smoothly, fluidly, and at a consistent, rapid pace.
4. Stop the physical wave and introduce the vocal wave: One player turns to their left neighbor, points, and says their own first name. That neighbor immediately turns to their left, points, and says their own name, passing the vocal chain counter-clockwise.
5. Practice the vocal wave counter-clockwise until the group can pass the names quickly and rhythmically around the circle.
6. Now, initiate the physical wave going clockwise. Once it is established and moving, initiate the vocal wave going counter-clockwise from a different starting point in the circle.
7. Instruct players to keep both waves moving continuously. When a player receives both the physical wave and the vocal wave at the same time, they must execute both actions simultaneously (raising their arms while saying/pointing their name) and pass them along.
8. If a wave stalls or gets lost, encourage the group to laugh, reset, and immediately launch both waves again without over-analyzing the mistake.

## Facilitation Notes
- Side-coaching cue: 'Keep your eyes soft!' Encourage players to use peripheral vision rather than staring directly at the person next to them.
- Common pitfall: Players freeze when both waves hit them at once. Fix: Coach them to 'fail joyfully' and just make a silly sound or movement to keep the energy flowing rather than stopping to correct it.
- Side-coaching cue: 'Support your neighbors!' Remind players that their job is to set up the next person for success by passing the impulse clearly and rhythmically.
- If the physical wave loses its fluid 'wave' quality and becomes choppy, remind players to initiate their movement just as their neighbor's arms reach the highest point, creating a seamless ripple.

## Variations
- Sound Effects: Replace names with distinct, short sound effects (like 'Zip' or 'Boing') to increase the auditory complexity.
- Blind Wave: Once the group is highly proficient, challenge them to run both waves with their eyes closed, relying entirely on sound and physical proximity cues.
- Speed Rounds: Challenge the group to see how many complete rotations both waves can make in 60 seconds without dropping either thread.

## Debrief
- How did your focus shift when the two waves crossed paths?
- What did you have to do with your vision and attention to track both threads at once?
- How does 'failing joyfully' help the group maintain momentum when a mistake happens?

## Safety & Inclusion
Ensure players with limited shoulder or arm mobility are offered alternative physical gestures for the wave, such as a head nod, a bow, or a foot tap, which can be easily mirrored by the rest of the circle.

## Why It Works
This game works because it forces players out of analytical thinking and into a state of active, split-attention presence. By running two distinct tracks (kinesthetic and auditory) in opposite directions, the brain cannot rely on hyper-focusing on a single point; instead, players must expand their peripheral awareness and tap into the collective rhythm of the group mind to keep the system balanced.
