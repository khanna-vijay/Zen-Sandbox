# Sitting, Standing, Lying Down

> Maintain three distinct physical postures while justifying every sudden shift in your scene.

## Overview
A dynamic three-player scene where players must constantly maintain a strict physical balance: at any given moment, one player must be standing, one sitting, and one lying down. When one player changes their posture, the other two must instantly adjust their positions to restore the balance, all while justifying their physical movements within the context of the story.

## What It Trains
- **Domain:** D3 — The Scene
- **Principle(s):** Show, Don't Tell; Group Mind; Yes, And
- **Skill(s):** Justification; Peripheral Awareness; Offer Reception; Physicality & Space Work
- **Technique(s):** Justify the absurd; Stage-picture exercises
- **Focus:** comedy_game

**Objective:** To develop physical justification, peripheral awareness, and rapid offer reception by forcing players to find logical, character-driven reasons for sudden, arbitrary physical changes.

## Setup
Place one or two chairs on the stage. Three players step up to perform. The rest of the group watches as the audience/support.

## How to Play
1. Three players enter the performance space and establish their starting positions: one must be standing, one sitting, and one lying down.
2. The facilitator or audience provides a simple relationship or location suggestion to initiate the scene.
3. Players begin a scene, speaking and acting naturally while maintaining their initial physical postures.
4. At any point, a player may choose to change their posture (for example, the sitting player stands up).
5. The moment a player changes posture, the other two players must immediately adjust their own postures so that the rule of one standing, one sitting, and one lying down is instantly restored.
6. Every physical transition must be justified verbally or physically within the reality of the scene (for example, if a player suddenly lies down, they might claim they dropped a contact lens or felt dizzy).
7. The scene continues with players fluidly swapping positions, ensuring that no two players ever occupy the same posture for more than a brief second.

## Facilitation Notes
- Coaching cue: Justify the move! Why did you just lie down? Make it make sense to your character.
- Coaching cue: Keep your eyes on your scene partners. Use your peripheral vision to track their heights.
- Pitfall: Players focus so much on the physical rules that they stop listening to the dialogue. Fix: Remind them that the physical change is an offer that drives the scene's narrative forward, not just a mechanical chore.
- Pitfall: Lag time during transitions. Fix: Encourage immediate physical reaction; the justification can follow a split second after the movement.

## Variations
- Emotional States: Instead of physical postures, players must maintain three distinct emotional states (such as happy, sad, and angry). When one shifts, the others must shift to balance the emotional trio.
- Four-Player Expansion: Add a fourth player and a fourth posture: kneeling. The rule becomes one standing, one sitting, one kneeling, and one lying down.
- Silent Scene: Play the entire game in complete silence, relying purely on physical storytelling and object work to justify the posture changes.

## Debrief
- How did the physical restriction of changing postures help or hinder your narrative choices?
- What strategies did you use to justify a sudden, unexpected change in your physical position?
- How did keeping track of your partners' physical states affect your overall awareness on stage?

## Safety & Inclusion
Ensure the floor is clean and free of hazards before playing. Players with mobility or physical limitations can adapt the postures (such as sitting, leaning, raising hands, or tilting head) to ensure everyone can participate comfortably and safely.

## Why It Works
This game forces players to practice Show, Don't Tell by making physical offers the primary driver of the scene. By requiring immediate physical compliance followed by logical justification, it bypasses the analytical brain, leading to delightful, absurd, and highly creative narrative discoveries.
