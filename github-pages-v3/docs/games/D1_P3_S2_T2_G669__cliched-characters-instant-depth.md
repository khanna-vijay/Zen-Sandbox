# Stereotype to Soul

<div class="game-hero-ph" markdown>🎨 *Infographic coming soon*</div>

> Infuse highly exaggerated, comedic archetypes with genuine personal truths to create instant, vulnerable depth.

## Overview
Players begin a scene as highly exaggerated, cliched archetypes, committing fully to physical and vocal stereotypes. On the facilitator's cue, they must maintain their extreme physical and vocal choices while speaking from their own real-life experiences, worries, or joys. This juxtaposition instantly grounds the scene, transforming a flat caricature into a deeply human, vulnerable character.

## What It Trains
- **Domain:** D1 — The Self
- **Principle(s):** Vulnerability; Commit 100%; Yes, And

**Objective:** Develops emotional fluidity and vulnerability by using emotional substitution—injecting real, personal stakes and authentic self-reflection into highly stylized physical characters.

## Setup
An open playing space with room for physical movement. The group stands in a semi-circle (backline) facing the stage area. No props or chairs are needed.

## How to Play
1. Invite two players to step forward into the playing space.
2. Provide a highly recognizable, cliched character archetype (e.g., pirates, cowboys, vampires, medieval knights, high-society aristocrats).
3. Instruct the players to immediately initiate a scene, committing 100% to the physical, vocal, and behavioral stereotypes of that archetype.
4. Allow the players to establish the scene's physical environment and high-energy tropes for about 30 to 45 seconds.
5. Call out the side-coaching cue: 'Say something real!'
6. Upon hearing the cue, players must keep their established physical postures, voices, and accents, but immediately pivot their dialogue to express genuine, mundane personal truths from their own actual lives.
7. The players must 'yes-and' these real-life admissions, continuing the scene by blending the high-concept physical archetype with authentic, vulnerable conversation.
8. End the scene after a minute of this grounded interaction, then rotate in two new players with a different archetype.

## Facilitation Notes
- Side-coach with: 'Keep the body, change the brain!' to remind players not to drop their physical and vocal characterizations when they become vulnerable.
- Pitfall: Players sometimes share fake 'real' things (e.g., 'I'm worried about my dragon' instead of 'I'm worried about my rent'). Fix: Pause the scene and gently prompt, 'No, what is something actually true for you, the actor, today?'
- Encourage players to avoid intellectualizing. The first mundane truth that pops into their head—like being tired, feeling guilty about a skipped workout, or looking forward to a meal—is perfect.
- Ensure the partner actively listens and validates the vulnerability, rather than making fun of the contrast. The humor comes naturally from the juxtaposition, not from mocking the truth.

## Variations
- Monologue Version: A single player starts as a caricature, and on the cue, delivers a vulnerable, personal monologue while maintaining the physical posture.
- Emotional Dial: The facilitator can call out percentages of 'realness' (e.g., '20% real', '80% real') to help players practice sliding along the spectrum of emotional substitution.
- Silent Transition: Instead of a verbal cue, players must transition to a real personal topic organically when they feel the scene needs grounding.

## Debrief
- How did it feel to speak your actual personal truths while wearing the physical mask of a caricature?
- In what ways did maintaining the physical stereotype make it easier or harder to be vulnerable?
- How did the scene's stakes change once the dialogue shifted from generic tropes to real-life experiences?

## Safety & Inclusion
Because this game asks players to share real-life personal truths, establish a boundary before playing: players should only share truths they are comfortable sharing with the room. They do not need to share deep trauma; simple, everyday vulnerabilities (like being tired or anxious about a minor task) are highly effective and safe.

## Why It Works
By using a highly physical, cliched character as a 'shield,' players feel safer accessing genuine vulnerability. The physical commitment bypasses the analytical mind, while the emotional substitution technique allows the player to draw from their own lived experience, instantly giving a flat caricature three-dimensional depth and emotional resonance.
