# Action, Color, Emotion

> Balance plot, sensory detail, and feeling by shifting narrative focus on a dime.

## Overview
One storyteller weaves an impromptu tale while guided by three conductors who prompt them to pivot between plot progression, sensory world-building, and internal emotional states. This exercise creates a dynamic, multi-dimensional narrative that prevents stories from becoming flat or purely plot-driven.

## What It Trains
- **Domain:** D3 — The Scene
- **Principle(s):** The First Thought Is a Gift; Show, Don't Tell; Serve the Story; Follow the Follower
- **Skill(s):** Unfiltered Spontaneity; Emotional Fluidity; Narrative Architecture; World-Building; Support Work
- **Technique(s):** First Thought drills; The Emotional Dial (1→10); C.R.O.W. (Character, Relationship, Objective, Where)
- **Focus:** narrative

**Objective:** Develops narrative architecture by teaching players to balance the three essential pillars of storytelling: plot movement (Action), sensory environment (Color), and internal stakes (Emotion).

## Setup
One storyteller stands center stage. Three conductors stand in a line facing them, or slightly to the side. Each conductor is assigned one of the three prompts: Action, Color, or Emotion.

## How to Play
1. Assign roles: one Storyteller, three Conductors (one for Action, one for Color, one for Emotion), and the rest of the group as the audience.
2. Get a simple, mundane suggestion from the audience to start the story, such as 'washing the dishes' or 'buying a postcard'.
3. The Storyteller begins telling a story in the first or third person based on the suggestion.
4. At any point, a Conductor can call out their assigned word ('Action!', 'Color!', or 'Emotion!') to force the Storyteller to immediately pivot their focus.
5. When 'Action' is called, the Storyteller must advance the plot, describing what happens next or what physical steps are taken.
6. When 'Color' is called, the Storyteller must pause the plot and describe sensory details—smells, textures, sounds, weather, or visual aesthetics of the environment.
7. When 'Emotion' is called, the Storyteller must dive into the internal state, feelings, or psychological reactions of the characters involved.
8. Conductors should keep the transitions rapid, challenging the storyteller to switch modes mid-sentence without losing the thread of the narrative.
9. Run the story for two to three minutes, then rotate roles so everyone gets a chance to tell a story and conduct.

## Facilitation Notes
- Coaching cue: 'Don't restart your sentence when a new prompt is called; seamlessly transition the thought.'
- Pitfall: Storytellers getting stuck in 'Action' because it is the default mode. Fix: Conductors should aggressively call 'Color' and 'Emotion' to break the habit of dry plot-listing.
- Coaching cue: 'Color is not just visual; use all five senses—temperature, smell, texture, and ambient sound.'
- Pitfall: Conductors waiting too long to switch. Fix: Encourage rapid-fire switching (every 10-15 seconds) to keep the storyteller in a state of unfiltered spontaneity.

## Variations
- Physical Tags: Instead of calling out words, conductors stand behind the storyteller and tap them on the left shoulder (Action), center (Color), or right shoulder (Emotion) to trigger the shift physically.
- The Scene Version: Two players perform a scene, and two off-stage conductors call out 'Action', 'Color', or 'Emotion' to guide the dialogue and physical choices of both players simultaneously.
- Audience Conducted: The entire audience is divided into three sections, and the facilitator points to a section to trigger that specific narrative element.

## Debrief
- Which of the three modes (Action, Color, Emotion) felt most natural to you, and which felt most challenging?
- How did adding 'Color' and 'Emotion' change the pacing and stakes of the story compared to a standard plot-driven narrative?
- As a conductor, how did you decide when the story needed a shift?

## Safety & Inclusion
Ensure emotional prompts remain within safe, creative boundaries; players should explore a wide range of emotions (joy, awe, curiosity) rather than solely focusing on high-trauma or distressing states.

## Why It Works
It externalizes the internal editing process of a writer. By separating narrative into three distinct channels, it bypasses the cognitive overload of trying to do everything at once, allowing the storyteller to practice deep focus on one narrative element at a time.
