# Cue and Justify

![Cue and Justify — infographic](img/D3_P2_S6_T1_G697__enter-and-exit.jpg){ .game-hero }

> Justify sudden entrances and exits triggered by secret verbal cues spoken by your scene partners.

## Overview
In this dynamic scene-work drill, four players navigate a single scene where their physical presence is dictated by secret verbal triggers. Two players start on stage and two off stage, each assigned a specific category word that forces them to instantly enter or exit when spoken by another player. The challenge lies in seamlessly justifying these abrupt transitions to maintain a grounded, believable base reality.

## What It Trains
- **Domain:** D3 — The Scene
- **Principle(s):** Base Reality First; Yes, And; Make Your Partner a Genius
- **Skill(s):** Justification; Active Listening; Offer Reception; Peripheral Awareness; Pacing & Rhythm
- **Technique(s):** Justify the absurd; Endowment-acceptance
- **Focus:** skill_drill

**Objective:** To develop rapid justification skills and active listening, ensuring that sudden physical shifts are integrated logically into the scene's established base reality.

## Setup
Clear a performance space with designated off-stage areas on either side. Gather four players. Assign each player a unique category cue word (e.g., a color, a number, an animal, or a household object). Two players start on stage in an active scene, while the other two wait off-stage, listening intently.

## How to Play
1. Assign each of the four players a distinct, simple cue word from different categories, ensuring everyone knows which word belongs to whom.
2. Position two players on stage to begin the scene, and place the other two players off-stage in the wings.
3. The two on-stage players begin a grounded, realistic scene based on a simple location suggestion.
4. All players must listen closely to the dialogue; when a player's assigned cue word is spoken by another player on stage, that triggered player must immediately change their location.
5. If the triggered player is off-stage, they must enter the scene; if they are on-stage, they must exit the scene.
6. The entering or exiting player must immediately deliver a verbal justification that explains why they are suddenly arriving or leaving, keeping it consistent with the scene's reality.
7. A player cannot say their own cue word to trigger their own movement; the trigger must always come from someone else's dialogue.
8. The remaining players must accept the new arrival or departure and adapt the scene's narrative to accommodate the change without breaking character.

## Facilitation Notes
- Coaching cue: 'Don't just run on or off—give us the character reason for your movement before or during your transition!'
- Pitfall: Players sometimes drop the scene's reality to acknowledge the game mechanic (e.g., saying 'Oh, I guess I have to leave now'). Fix: Remind them to justify the movement through character motivation (e.g., 'I left my oven on!' or 'I heard someone screaming out here!').
- Coaching cue: 'Keep the base reality strong. If you enter a kitchen scene, you are a plumber, a neighbor, or a family member, not a random alien.'
- Pitfall: Players off-stage stop paying attention. Fix: Encourage active physical listening in the wings so they can enter mid-sentence with high energy and immediate relevance.

## Variations
- Emotional Triggers: Instead of physical movement, hearing your cue word requires you to instantly adopt a specific, extreme emotion and justify the sudden mood swing.
- Chain Reaction: Increase the player count to six, with three on stage and three off stage, creating faster-paced, chaotic transitions.
- Silent Triggers: Instead of spoken words, triggers are physical actions, such as touching an elbow, checking a watch, or sitting down.

## Debrief
- How did it feel to have your physical presence controlled by words you didn't say?
- What strategies helped you make an abrupt entrance or exit feel natural and grounded?
- How did active listening in the wings change how you entered the scene compared to entering cold?

## Safety & Inclusion
Ensure the off-stage wings are clear of physical hazards, as players may need to enter or exit quickly. Remind players to move safely and avoid running blindly onto the stage.

## Why It Works
This game forces players to practice justifying the absurd in real-time. By making physical movement mandatory and arbitrary, it strips away the hesitation of when to enter or exit, leaving the player to focus entirely on why. This strengthens the muscle of treating every unexpected offer as a logical truth within the scene's base reality.
