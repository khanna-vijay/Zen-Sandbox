# The Guided Traveler

![The Guided Traveler — infographic](img/D2_P3_S1_T0_G963__blind-lead.jpg){ .game-hero }

> Guide a blindfolded partner safely through an obstacle course using only clear, supportive vocal cues.

## Overview
One player is blindfolded and must navigate a room filled with safe obstacles. The remaining players stand at the far end of the space and must work together to verbally direct the blindfolded player to safety. It is a high-trust exercise that demands precise communication, deep empathy, and acute active listening.

## What It Trains
- **Domain:** D2 — The Partner
- **Principle(s):** Make Your Partner a Genius; Group Mind
- **Skill(s):** Active Listening; Active Gifting; Peripheral Awareness; Support Work; Physicality & Space Work
- **Technique(s):** Give them the answer
- **Focus:** connection

**Objective:** To develop active listening, precise vocal gifting, and partner support by shifting focus entirely to the partner's physical safety and success.

## Setup
Clear a moderate-sized room. Scatter several safe obstacles (such as plastic chairs, soft bags, or sweaters) across the floor to create a winding path. One player is blindfolded at one end of the room, while the other players stand at the opposite end.

## How to Play
1. Designate one player as the Traveler and have them put on a blindfold at the starting line.
2. Position the remaining players at the far end of the room to act as the Guides.
3. The facilitator quietly arranges the safe obstacles between the Traveler and the Guides to form a unique path.
4. The Guides must verbally direct the Traveler through the obstacle course using clear, step-by-step instructions.
5. All directions must be given from the Traveler's physical perspective (e.g., 'turn to your left' rather than 'turn to my left').
6. The Traveler moves slowly and deliberately, listening closely to the voices and trusting the guidance.
7. The round is successful when the Traveler safely reaches the Guides without touching any obstacles.
8. Rotate roles so multiple players experience being both the Traveler and the Guide.

## Facilitation Notes
- Coaching cue: 'Speak from their perspective, not yours! Put yourself in their shoes.'
- Coaching cue: 'One voice at a time. Don't crowd them with noise.'
- Pitfall: Multiple guides shouting conflicting directions at once. Fix: Have the guides designate a single speaker at a time, or establish a rhythm where they pass the leadership role seamlessly.
- Pitfall: The Traveler moving too fast out of anxiety. Fix: Remind them to take slow, deliberate 'baby steps' and feel the space before committing to a step.

## Variations
- Single Guide: Only one designated guide can speak, while the others must remain silent but can point or gesture to help the active guide see the path.
- The Whispering Gallery: The guides must deliver instructions in a whisper, forcing the Traveler to tune out ambient noise and focus deeply on the sound.
- No Directional Words: Guides cannot use the words 'left,' 'right,' 'forward,' or 'backward.' They must invent alternative descriptive cues (e.g., 'turn toward the window,' 'step toward my voice').

## Debrief
- How did it feel to completely surrender control and rely entirely on your partner's voice?
- As a guide, how did you adapt your language to ensure your partner felt safe and successful?
- What happened to the group's listening dynamic when multiple people tried to guide at once?

## Safety & Inclusion
Ensure all obstacles are soft or rounded with no sharp edges. The facilitator should act as a physical 'spotter' to step in and gently stop the Traveler if they are about to trip or collide with something hard. If a player is uncomfortable being blindfolded, they may simply close their eyes or keep their gaze fixed on the ceiling.

## Why It Works
This game strips away visual cues, forcing the Traveler to practice absolute active listening. It forces the Guides to practice 'making their partner a genius' by prioritizing the Traveler's perspective, safety, and comfort over their own cleverness. It builds a profound sense of shared responsibility and group mind.
