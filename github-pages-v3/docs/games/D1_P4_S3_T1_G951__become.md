# Embodied Objects

![Embodied Objects — infographic](img/D1_P4_S3_T1_G951__become.jpg){ .game-hero }

> Transform your entire body instantly to discover the physical essence of any object or concept.

## Overview
This is a fast-paced physical spontaneity drill where players instantly transform their bodies to represent inanimate objects, elements, or abstract concepts. Guided by rapid side-coaching, players move from literal representations to deep, specific physical choices that explore weight, texture, history, and environment. It is an active, high-energy exercise that gets players out of their heads and into their bodies.

## What It Trains
- **Domain:** D1 — The Self
- **Principle(s):** Commit 100%; The First Thought Is a Gift; Show, Don't Tell
- **Skill(s):** Unfiltered Spontaneity; Physicality & Space Work; World-Building
- **Technique(s):** First Thought drills; Character Walks/Centers; C.R.O.W. (Character, Relationship, Objective, Where)
- **Focus:** skill_drill

**Objective:** To develop immediate physical commitment and space work by bypassing intellectual filtering, trusting the first physical impulse, and using the whole body to express character centers and physical states.

## Setup
An open, clear room where players can move freely without colliding. No props or materials are required. Players stand scattered throughout the space, facing any direction.

## How to Play
1. Have all players spread out across the room, standing comfortably in an active, neutral stance.
2. Explain that you will call out an object, element, or concept, and they must instantly take a physical shape representing it using their entire body.
3. Call out the first prompt (e.g., 'A jar of mayonnaise') and instruct players to immediately freeze in a shape representing it, without planning or hesitating.
4. Once players are in their initial shapes, deliver rapid-fire side-coaching questions to help them find specificity (e.g., 'Are you brand new or nearly empty?', 'Are you expired?', 'Where are you sitting?').
5. Instruct players to adjust their physical shape, weight distribution, and muscle tension in response to these questions, deepening their physical commitment.
6. Call out a new, contrasting prompt (e.g., 'A grandfather clock' or 'A gust of wind') and have them instantly shed the old shape and snap into the new one.
7. Gradually transition from concrete objects to abstract concepts (e.g., 'The color neon green' or 'An unpaid bill'), encouraging players to find a physical center or movement quality for these ideas.
8. In the final rounds, challenge players to self-coach, instantly choosing their own specific details (history, location, state) the moment the prompt is given.

## Facilitation Notes
- Coaching cue: 'Don't think, just shape! Let your body make the decision before your brain can analyze it.'
- Common pitfall: Players making small, safe gestures with just their hands. Fix: Coach them to use their entire spine, level changes, and weight distribution. 'If your big toe isn't part of this mayonnaise jar, you aren't fully in it!'
- Coaching cue: 'Find the history of the object. Is it dusty, broken, loved, or forgotten? Show us through your physical tension.'
- Common pitfall: Players waiting for the side-coaching before making a choice. Fix: Remind them that the first shape is a gift; the side-coaching is just to polish what is already there.

## Variations
- Object Interactions: Have players pair up. Call out two related objects (e.g., 'A hammer and a rusty nail') and have them physically interact while maintaining their object identities.
- The Human Element: Have one player step into the space as a human character and interact with the embodied objects (e.g., opening the jar of mayonnaise), requiring the objects to react physically to being handled.
- Emotional Objects: Add an emotional layer to the prompt (e.g., 'A depressed umbrella' or 'An arrogant alarm clock') to bridge physical space work with character development.

## Debrief
- How did it feel to instantly commit to a shape before you had a logical plan for how to represent it?
- What physical details (weight, tension, center of gravity) helped you feel more like the object and less like a person pretending?
- How does finding the 'history' or 'environment' of an object change how you physically present it to an audience?

## Safety & Inclusion
Ensure players are mindful of their physical boundaries and those of others in the space. Offer modifications for players with limited mobility, emphasizing that physical embodiment can be expressed through tension, facial expression, breath, or vocal quality rather than large spatial movements.

## Why It Works
By demanding instant physicalization, the game bypasses the analytical mind, forcing players to rely on their first creative impulse. The side-coaching teaches them that specificity builds reality; by answering questions about the object's state and environment physically, they learn to build rich, detailed worlds and characters from simple starting points.
