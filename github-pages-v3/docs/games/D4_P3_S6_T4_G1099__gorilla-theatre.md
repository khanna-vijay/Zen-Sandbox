# The Gorilla's Choice

> Direct your fellow actors to win the audience's favor—and avoid the gorilla's forfeit.

## Overview
A high-energy, audience-interactive showcase where players take turns directing their peers in spontaneous scenes. Instead of judging the actors, the audience evaluates the director's choices, awarding cardboard bananas for success or demanding hilarious forfeits for failure. This format shifts the focus to narrative architecture, pacing, and the delicate art of side-coaching.

## What It Trains
- **Domain:** D4 — The Ensemble
- **Principle(s):** Serve the Piece; The Audience Is the Final Scene Partner; Serve the Story
- **Skill(s):** Format Literacy; Pacing & Rhythm; Audience-Energy Management; Narrative Architecture
- **Technique(s):** Longform vs. shortform mechanics; Edits (Sweep, Tag-Out, Sound/Light); Breaking the 4th Wall / Direct Address; Platform/Tilt
- **Focus:** mixed

**Objective:** Develops format literacy, pacing, and narrative architecture by putting players in the director's chair. It teaches performers how to serve the piece as a whole, manage audience energy, and understand when to intervene or let a scene breathe.

## Setup
An open stage with a visible scoreboard, a collection of cardboard bananas, a gorilla suit (worn by one player), and a master list of potential scene setups or shortform games. An MC facilitates, while 5 to 8 proficient players sit on stage ready to act or direct.

## How to Play
1. Assign roles: One player hosts as the Master of Ceremonies (MC), one player wears the gorilla suit (acting as the silent, mischievous mascot and ultimate prize), and the remaining players form the ensemble.
2. The MC introduces the premise: The director with the most cardboard bananas at the end of the show wins custody of the Gorilla. The MC trains the audience to vote after each scene: shouting 'Banana!' for a well-directed piece, or 'Forfeit!' if the direction was lacking.
3. A player steps forward to act as the Director for a round. They select a game, prompt, or style from the master list and cast fellow ensemble members to perform the scene.
4. The Director stands at the edge of the stage, actively guiding the scene. They can pause action, introduce 'tilts' (sudden plot twists), edit the scene, or side-coach the actors to adjust their emotional states or physical choices.
5. Once the Director calls 'Scene!' to end the piece, the MC steps forward to conduct the audience vote. The audience loudly chooses between 'Banana' or 'Forfeit'.
6. If the audience awards a 'Banana', the Director pins a cardboard banana to the scoreboard under their name.
7. If the audience demands a 'Forfeit', the Director must immediately perform a quick, playful penalty designed by the MC or the Gorilla (e.g., singing a brief apology song, doing twenty push-ups, or confessing a minor secret).
8. The Gorilla acts as an agent of chaos throughout the show—bringing props, occasionally entering scenes as a silent character, or playfully tormenting directors who receive forfeits.
9. Rotate directors so every ensemble member has an equal opportunity to direct. After approximately 90 minutes, the MC tallies the bananas and crowns the winning director.

## Facilitation Notes
- Coaching the Directors: Remind directors that they are responsible for the scene's success. If a scene is failing, they must intervene with a bold choice rather than letting the actors struggle.
- Managing the Gorilla: Ensure the player in the gorilla suit understands their role is supportive chaos. They should enhance the comedy and tension without completely hijacking the narrative focus of the scenes.
- Pacing the Forfeits: Keep the forfeits brief, high-energy, and lighthearted. They should feel like a fun penalty, not a genuine punishment, maintaining a supportive atmosphere.
- Pitfall - Passive Directing: Sometimes directors just watch the scene play out. Side-coach them to use their power: 'Change the genre!', 'Freeze them!', or 'Give them a secret!'

## Variations
- The Silent Gorilla: The gorilla player is completely mute and must communicate entirely through physical comedy and object work, raising the stakes for physical storytelling.
- Blind Directing: The director is given a secret constraint or genre by the audience that the actors do not know, forcing the director to guide them into the style purely through side-coaching.
- The Cooperative Gorilla: The gorilla can be tagged into a scene by the director as a wild-card character, but must follow the established rules of the scene.

## Debrief
- How did stepping into the director's role change your perspective on when to edit or push a scene forward?
- What did you notice about the relationship between the director's intervention and the audience's engagement?
- How does knowing the director is on the hook affect how the actors support each other on stage?

## Safety & Inclusion
Ensure all physical forfeits are accessible to all players; offer alternative options (like verbal or creative challenges) so no one is excluded due to physical limitations. Establish clear boundaries for physical contact with the gorilla character before the show begins.

## Why It Works
By shifting the burden of success from the actors to the director, this format removes the typical stage fright of performing and replaces it with a collaborative puzzle. Actors feel safe to take big risks because the director has their back, while the director learns the structural mechanics of storytelling, pacing, and audience-energy management in real-time.
