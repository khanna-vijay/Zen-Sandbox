# Three Melodies

> A structured musical scene where two characters sing their way from isolation to mutual resolution.

## Overview
Two players establish distinct characters, occupations, and personal dilemmas through individual musical solos, then meet to resolve their problems in a collaborative duet. This game uses a strict four-beat narrative structure to practice platforming, tilting, and active gifting through song.

## What It Trains
- **Domain:** D3 — The Scene
- **Principle(s):** Serve the Story; Yes, And; Make Your Partner a Genius
- **Skill(s):** Narrative Architecture; Active Gifting; Vocal Craft
- **Technique(s):** Platform/Tilt; Endowment-gifting drills; Vocal characterization
- **Focus:** narrative

**Objective:** To master narrative architecture (specifically Platform, Tilt, and Resolution) and active gifting by using musical structure to establish, complicate, and resolve a shared story.

## Setup
Two players stand on stage in front of an audience. No physical props or musical instruments are required, though an improvising accompanist can support them if available. The facilitator asks the audience for two distinct, unrelated occupations.

## How to Play
1. Ask the audience for two different occupations, assigning one to Player A and one to Player B.
2. Beat 1 (First Solo): Player A steps forward and sings a verse using a distinct melody (Melody 1). They establish their occupation, why they love it (the Platform), and the one critical thing they are missing or struggling with (the Tilt).
3. Beat 2 (Second Solo): Player B steps forward and sings a verse using a completely different melody (Melody 2). They establish their own occupation, its joys, and their own unique, unresolved dilemma.
4. Beat 3 (The Meeting): Both players step into a shared space (traditionally a neutral location like a bar or bus stop). They converse briefly in character, then each sings a single, clear summarizing line of their dilemma (e.g., 'I have plenty of X, but I desperately need Y').
5. Beat 4 (The Duet): The players discover that Player A's abundance (X) is the solution to Player B's need (Y), and vice versa. They launch into a collaborative duet using a brand-new third melody (Melody 3), singing about how they solve each other's problems and ending on a harmonious resolution.

## Facilitation Notes
- Encourage players to make their 'Tilt' (the problem) highly specific. Vague problems are harder to solve in Beat 4.
- Side-coach players to listen actively during their partner's solo. They must remember the specific resource their partner has and the specific thing they need to set up the final exchange.
- If players struggle to find a new melody for Beat 4, coach them to change the tempo or emotional quality (e.g., moving from melancholy solos to an upbeat, triumphant major-key duet).
- Pitfall: Players sometimes solve their own problems. Remind them that the magic of the game lies in 'making your partner a genius'—you must let your partner's resource solve your problem.

## Variations
- A Cappella Challenge: Play without an accompanist, forcing players to establish and hold their own distinct tempos and keys.
- Genre Shift: Assign specific musical genres to each melody (e.g., Melody 1 is a country ballad, Melody 2 is opera, Melody 3 is a pop anthem).
- The Trio: Add a third player and a fourth melody, requiring a three-way chain of problem-solving (A solves B, B solves C, C solves A).

## Debrief
- How did the structured musical beats help you track the narrative arc (Platform, Tilt, Resolution)?
- What did it feel like to actively listen for your partner's 'gift' (their resource) rather than planning your own solution?
- How did changing the melody for the final duet shift the emotional energy of the scene?

## Safety & Inclusion
Ensure players feel comfortable singing; emphasize that vocal quality or pitch accuracy is not evaluated—the focus is entirely on narrative structure and character expression. Allow players to speak-sing (rhythmic chanting) if they are anxious about singing.

## Why It Works
By dividing the narrative into three distinct musical movements, the game forces players to respect the pacing of a story. The first two melodies build the platform and introduce the tilt. The third melody acts as a structural container for the resolution, ensuring that players don't rush to solve the conflict until they have fully explored their characters' needs and actively gifted the solutions to one another.
