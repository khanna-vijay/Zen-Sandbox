# Spontaneous Finger Puppets

> Instantly bring two finger characters to life in a suggested location without overthinking.

## Overview
A fast-paced circle warm-up where players use their index fingers as miniature actors. Upon receiving a location, a player must immediately launch into a brief, two-character scene using their hands, training them to trust their very first instinct. It is a high-energy, low-stakes way to practice rapid world-building and spontaneous character creation.

**Objective:** To bypass the internal editor, practice unfiltered spontaneity, and establish a clear base reality instantly using physicalized characters.

## Setup
Players stand in a circle in an open space. No props, chairs, or materials are required.

## How to Play
1. Have all players stand in a circle. One player starts as the initiator.
2. The initiator clasps their hands together, leaving only their two index fingers pointing upward to represent two distinct characters.
3. The entire group chants a simple, rhythmic prompt: 'Where do the fingers go? Where do the fingers go?'
4. On the beat immediately following the chant, the initiator points their clasped hands at another player in the circle and calls out a specific location.
5. The selected player must immediately raise their own hands in the same clasped, two-finger position.
6. Without pausing to plan, the selected player performs a brief, 15-to-30-second scene where their two index fingers act as characters in the suggested location.
7. The player must establish a clear relationship and a basic reality right away, making active statements rather than asking questions.
8. Once the mini-scene reaches a quick button or natural conclusion, the group resumes the chant, and the player who just performed points to a new person with a new location.

## Facilitation Notes
- Side-coach players to speak immediately. If they hesitate, call out: 'First thought, best thought! Just start talking!'
- Watch out for players asking questions to stall (e.g., 'What are we doing here?'). Remind them to make bold, declarative statements instead (e.g., 'We finally made it to the top of Mt. Everest!').
- Encourage physical variety. Suggest that players give their two fingers distinct heights, angles, or movement styles to represent different personalities.
- Keep the pace brisk. Cut scenes off after 3 or 4 lines of dialogue to maintain high energy and prevent players from getting bogged down in plot.

## Variations
- Emotional States: The initiator provides both a location and an emotional prompt (e.g., 'A library, but they are terrified').
- Physical Obstacles: The fingers must interact with a tiny physical hazard in their environment, such as a slippery surface or a giant gust of wind.
- Gibberish Puppets: The characters must speak entirely in gibberish, relying on physical movement and vocal tone to convey their relationship and environment.

## Debrief
- How did it feel to speak before you had a plan for where the scene was going?
- What strategies helped you establish the location and characters instantly?
- How did physicalizing the characters through your fingers change the choices you made?

## Safety & Inclusion
Ensure players with limited hand or finger mobility know they can use any two physical markers—such as two thumbs, two elbows, or two small handheld objects—to represent their characters.

## Why It Works
By shrinking the performance scale down to two fingers, it lowers the stakes of acting and allows players to focus purely on spontaneous dialogue. The rhythmic group chant creates a supportive, ritualistic momentum that pushes players past their analytical minds directly into their first thoughts.
