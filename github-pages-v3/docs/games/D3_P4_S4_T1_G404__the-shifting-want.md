# The Shifting Want

![The Shifting Want — infographic](img/D3_P4_S4_T1_G404__the-shifting-want.jpg){ .game-hero }

> Drive narrative momentum by continuously evolving character objectives and raising what they stand to lose.

## Overview
A dynamic, turn-based scene-building exercise where players explicitly state and continuously adapt their characters' core desires. By linking every new objective to a heightened consequence, players construct a high-stakes narrative driven entirely by cause and effect. The experience is fast-paced and intellectually stimulating, transforming abstract stakes into concrete dramatic engine parts.

## What It Trains
- **Domain:** D3 — The Scene
- **Principle(s):** Serve the Story; Yes, And; Make Your Partner a Genius
- **Skill(s):** Stakes / The 'Want'; Narrative Architecture; Raising the Stakes; Justification; Offer Reception; Active Gifting
- **Technique(s):** What do they stand to lose? reps; Objective-laddering; Stakes-escalation reps; Justify the absurd; If this is true, what else is true?
- **Focus:** narrative

**Objective:** To develop the ability to identify, articulate, and escalate character wants and stakes in real-time, specifically practicing the technique of defining what characters stand to lose to serve the overall story structure.

## Setup
An in-person playing space with two chairs or a clear stage area. The rest of the group sits as active observers. No props or materials are required. The facilitator prepares to side-coach actively.

## How to Play
1. Select two players to step into the performance space while the remaining participants act as the audience.
2. Obtain a simple suggestion from the group, such as a location or a relationship, to establish the initial platform.
3. Player One initiates the scene and must explicitly state their character's primary, tangible want within their first three lines of dialogue.
4. Player Two enters or responds by directly acknowledging or obstructing Player One's want, while immediately stating their own conflicting want and introducing a consequence that raises the stakes.
5. Players alternate turns, ensuring that every line of dialogue directly reacts to the partner's previous statement using if this is true, what else is true logic.
6. With each exchange, players must evolve their character's want to be a logical progression of the new circumstances, while explicitly raising what they stand to lose if they fail.
7. The facilitator actively side-coaches during the scene, calling out Want! or Stakes! if a player's objective becomes vague or if the consequences flatline.
8. Continue the scene for four to six rounds of escalating exchanges, building to a natural peak of high tension or an impossible choice before calling scene.

## Facilitation Notes
- Side-Coaching Cues: Use direct, brief prompts like 'What do you stand to lose right now?' or 'State your new want clearly' to keep players focused without breaking their flow.
- Avoid Absurdity Overload: Watch out for players escalating stakes into ridiculous territory too quickly. Guide them to keep the consequences grounded in the established reality of the scene.
- Enforce Active Gifting: Ensure players are not just repeating their initial wants. Each turn must build on the partner's previous offer, treating their partner's stakes as absolute truth.
- The Want vs. Action Pitfall: Remind players that a want is an internal motivation or objective, not just a physical action.

## Variations
- Silent Stakes: Play the same structure but players must communicate their shifting wants and escalating stakes entirely through physical choices and subtext, without explicitly stating them.
- The Turning Point: Introduce a mid-scene prompt where the facilitator calls out 'Crisis!', forcing both players to instantly flip their wants to the exact opposite of what they previously desired.
- Multi-Player Escalation: Run the game with three or four players, where each new entering character must inherit the current stakes and introduce a new, even higher-stakes want that complicates the existing web.

## Debrief
- How did explicitly stating what your character stood to lose change the momentum of the scene?
- What challenges did you face when trying to evolve your want rather than just repeating your initial objective?
- How did knowing your partner's exact stakes make it easier to make them look good and serve the story?

## Safety & Inclusion
Because this game rapidly escalates stakes, players might instinctively reach for high-stress or traumatic scenarios. Establish a boundary beforehand that stakes should remain focused on relational, professional, or comedic consequences rather than physical violence or personal trauma, allowing players to opt out or redirect if the tension becomes genuinely uncomfortable.

## Why It Works
This game works because it operationalizes the narrative engine of cause and effect. By forcing players to explicitly state their wants and what they stand to lose, it strips away the vagueness that often stalls scenes. It teaches players that narrative progression is not about inventing random new plot points, but about exploring the deep, cascading consequences of the choices and desires already present in the room.
