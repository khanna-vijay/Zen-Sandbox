# Backdrop Odyssey

> Co-create an evolving narrative by matching verbal 'Yes, And' with instant virtual background shifts.

## Overview
Backdrop Odyssey is a virtual-first improv game where players collaboratively build an evolving world using their video platform's virtual background feature. Operating in gallery view, each player must immediately change their background to visually 'Yes, And' the previous player's setting while delivering a connecting line of dialogue. This creates a seamless, multi-modal chain of storytelling where the environment itself acts as an active character.

## What It Trains
- **Domain:** D2 — The Partner
- **Principle(s):** Yes, And; Make Your Partner a Genius; Show, Don't Tell; Base Reality First; Group Mind
- **Skill(s):** Physicality & Space Work; Active Listening; Offer Reception; World-Building; Peripheral Awareness
- **Technique(s):** Object work; Yes, And… sentence games; Endowment-acceptance; C.R.O.W. (Character, Relationship, Objective, Where)
- **Focus:** mixed

**Objective:** To develop advanced offer reception and active listening by forcing players to process and build upon both visual and verbal offers simultaneously, reinforcing the core principle of 'Yes, And' in a digital space.

## Setup
Players join a virtual meeting platform in Gallery View. Before starting, every participant must pre-load a diverse folder of 10-15 virtual backgrounds (such as landscapes, interiors, abstract spaces, or historical sites) into their platform settings so they can switch them instantly with one click.

## How to Play
1. Instruct all players to turn on their cameras, switch to Gallery View, and open their virtual background selection menu so it is ready for instant switching.
2. The facilitator establishes the starting premise of the journey, such as exploring a newly discovered planet or searching for a lost temple.
3. The first player immediately changes their virtual background to match the starting premise and delivers one or two lines of dialogue establishing their character's immediate reaction to this environment.
4. The next player must immediately change their own background to a setting that logically or creatively follows the previous player's environment.
5. Simultaneously, this next player delivers a line of dialogue that 'Yes, Ands' the previous verbal offer, connecting their new visual environment to the unfolding story.
6. Players are encouraged to physically interact with their chosen background, using object work to lean against virtual walls, look out virtual windows, or react to elements in their image.
7. Play continues sequentially through the group, with each participant waiting for the previous player to finish their line before instantly changing their background and speaking.
8. The facilitator monitors the flow, ensuring players do not lag during background transitions, and guides the narrative to a satisfying conclusion after everyone has contributed at least once.

## Facilitation Notes
- Side-coaching cue: 'See the background first, then speak!' Remind players to let the visual change inspire their verbal offer, rather than deciding what to say beforehand.
- Pitfall: Technical lag or hesitation while searching for a background. Fix: Encourage players to pick the first background that catches their eye rather than searching for the perfect match; speed and spontaneity trump precision.
- Side-coaching cue: 'Interact with your space!' Remind players to use physical space work, such as ducking under a low ceiling shown in their background, to ground their performance.
- Pitfall: Disconnected storylines where players ignore the previous verbal offer to fit their pre-selected background. Fix: Coach players to adapt their verbal line to bridge any visual gap, making the transition part of the narrative logic.

## Variations
- Blind Choice: Players must change their background without looking at the preview, then instantly justify whatever random image appears behind them.
- Two-Person Scenes: Run the game as a standard scene between two players who must constantly shift their backgrounds to represent a fast-paced chase or a teleportation battle.
- Emotional Echo: Players must choose backgrounds that represent their character's internal emotional state rather than a literal physical location, translating feelings into visual metaphors.

## Debrief
- How did having to process both a visual offer and a verbal offer change how you listened?
- What strategies did you use to justify a sudden or unexpected visual shift in the story?
- How did physically interacting with your virtual background help you feel more grounded in the scene?

## Safety & Inclusion
Ensure all pre-selected backgrounds are appropriate for the group. For players with older hardware that cannot support virtual backgrounds without a green screen, allow them to use physical household items held up to the camera, or colored sticky notes over their camera lens to change the lighting and color of their frame as their visual offer.

## Why It Works
This game works because it gamifies the technical constraints of virtual platforms, turning latency and physical separation into creative assets. By requiring a simultaneous visual and verbal 'Yes, And', it forces players out of their heads and into a state of high peripheral awareness. The physical act of changing the background serves as a clear, non-verbal baton pass, which naturally mitigates the audio overlap common in online improv.
