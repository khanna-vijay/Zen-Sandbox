# The Living Agreement

![The Living Agreement — infographic](img/D2_P1_S6_T1_G143__the-living-contract.jpg){ .game-hero }

> Co-create a dynamic safety roadmap before playing, then navigate boundaries in real-time.

## Overview
A structured pre-scene negotiation game where players collaboratively design a customized safety and physical contact agreement before stepping into a scene. During play, improvisers actively practice real-time check-ins and boundary navigation, learning to prioritize personal comfort and artistic truth over the pressure to please.

## What It Trains
- **Domain:** D2 — The Partner
- **Principle(s):** Consent & Boundaries; Truth Over Pandering
- **Skill(s):** Boundary Navigation; Active Listening
- **Technique(s):** Check-ins; Cut calls; Negotiating physical contact
- **Focus:** connection

**Objective:** To develop proactive boundary navigation, active listening, and real-time consent check-ins, ensuring players can confidently establish physical and emotional safety parameters without sacrificing creative momentum.

## Setup
An open, comfortable space for small groups of 3 to 5 players. No props are required. The facilitator prepares a list of emotionally complex or high-stakes scene premises (e.g., estranged business partners meeting, a tense family reunion, or strangers stuck in a stalled train).

## How to Play
1. Divide players into small groups of three to five and assign each group a high-stakes scene premise.
2. Instruct each group to spend five minutes co-creating their 'Living Agreement' by first identifying any off-limits themes, emotional triggers, or sensitive topics they wish to avoid entirely.
3. Have players establish their 'Physicality Palette' by explicitly stating absolute 'No' zones (areas or types of touch that are completely off-limits) and absolute 'Yes' zones (unconditional, safe physical contact like handshakes or high-fives).
4. Define the 'Conditional Zones'—actions or physical contact that are acceptable only if preceded by an active, in-scene verbal or non-verbal check-in (e.g., a hug, holding hands, or intense shouting).
5. Establish the 'Cut' anchor, ensuring every player understands that calling 'Cut' immediately stops the scene with zero explanation required or expected.
6. Begin the scene play, with two or three players performing while the remaining group members observe as supportive active listeners.
7. During the scene, players must actively execute verbal check-ins (e.g., asking 'Can I hold your hand?') or non-verbal check-ins (e.g., pausing and offering an open palm) before entering any designated Conditional Zone.
8. Prioritize 'Truth Over Pandering' by allowing characters to naturally decline physical contact or redirect the scene if a boundary is approached, rather than forcing a 'yes-and' that compromises comfort.
9. Immediately halt the scene if any player calls 'Cut' or uses a non-verbal stop signal, resetting the space without judgment.

## Facilitation Notes
- Coaching Cue: If a scene starts to feel tense or approaches a conditional zone, call out: 'Check in with your partner now—verbal or non-verbal!'
- Pitfall: Players may rush through the pre-scene agreement phase due to social pressure to appear 'easy to work with.' Fix: Remind the group that setting clear boundaries is an act of generosity that actually unlocks greater creative freedom.
- Coaching Cue: Encourage subtle, character-integrated check-ins. For example, a character asking 'May I sit next to you?' keeps the narrative flow while securing explicit consent.
- Pitfall: Players might feel they ruined the scene if they call 'Cut.' Fix: Celebrate 'Cut' calls during the debrief as successful demonstrations of boundary navigation and mutual care.

## Variations
- The Silent Agreement: Conduct the pre-scene phase using only written notes or non-verbal gestures to establish boundaries, focusing heavily on reading physical cues during the scene.
- The Slider: Introduce a scale of 1 to 5 for emotional intensity, where players can verbally call out a number in-character to request their partner dial the intensity up or down.

## Debrief
- How did co-creating the agreement beforehand affect your sense of creative freedom and willingness to take risks during the scene?
- What did you notice about the transition into 'Conditional Zones'—did the check-ins feel clunky, or did they integrate naturally into the story?
- How did it feel to prioritize your personal comfort ('Truth Over Pandering') over the traditional pressure to always say 'yes' to every physical or emotional offer?
- What strategies did you find most effective for making non-verbal check-ins clear and readable to your partner?

## Safety & Inclusion
This game is highly consent-sensitive. Ensure all participants know they have absolute autonomy over their bodies and emotional boundaries. If a participant is uncomfortable discussing specific boundaries in a group, they may write them down privately for their scene partners or step out of the physical contact portion of the exercise without penalty. Respect all 'No' boundaries unconditionally without asking for justification.

## Why It Works
By transforming consent from a static, reactive safety rule into an active, collaborative design process, this game removes the stigma of saying 'no.' Defining 'Conditional Zones' teaches players that consent is not binary; it can be dynamically negotiated in real-time. This builds deep trust, allowing players to commit fully to high-stakes scenes because they know their partner is actively listening and respecting their boundaries.
