# Day in the Life

> Transform an audience member's daily routine into an extraordinary, narrative-driven theatrical journey.

## Overview
In this performance-oriented narrative game, players interview an audience member about their typical daily routine and then improvise a structured story based on those details. The resulting performance balances truth and playful exaggeration, honoring the volunteer's real-world experiences through a cohesive narrative arc.

## What It Trains
- **Domain:** D3 — The Scene
- **Principle(s):** Serve the Story; Serve the Piece; The Audience Is the Final Scene Partner
- **Skill(s):** Narrative Architecture; World-Building; Format Literacy; Room Reading
- **Technique(s):** Story Spine; C.R.O.W. (Character, Relationship, Objective, Where); Longform vs. shortform mechanics; Reading the suggestion's intent
- **Focus:** narrative

**Objective:** Develops narrative architecture, active listening, and world-building by translating real-world interview details into a structured, satisfying story using the Story Spine framework.

## Setup
An in-person performance space with a stage area and seating for an audience. Place a single chair on stage for the volunteer. The performing cast of 4 to 8 players stands ready at the sides of the stage, prepared to take notes or listen intently.

## How to Play
1. The host invites a volunteer from the audience to join them on stage and sit in the designated interview chair.
2. The host conducts a brief, friendly interview, asking about the volunteer's morning routine, commute, job, quirky colleagues, hobbies, and evening habits.
3. The performing cast listens actively from the sidelines, identifying key details, relationships, and potential narrative hooks to establish the status quo.
4. Once the interview is complete, the host thanks the volunteer and sends them back to their audience seat to watch the performance.
5. The players take the stage and begin the scene by establishing the volunteer's normal daily routine, using the Story Spine prompt: 'Once upon a time... and every day...'
6. The players introduce a clear inciting incident based on the interview details, using the prompt: 'Until one day...'
7. The narrative progresses through the consequences of this disruption, building tension and comedy with sequential 'And because of that...' beats.
8. The players resolve the story with 'Until finally...' and establish a new status quo with 'And ever since that day...', celebrating the volunteer's journey.

## Facilitation Notes
- Coaching cue: Encourage players to 'listen for the unusual in the usual'—mundane details like a specific coffee order or a weird commute route make the best comedic fodder.
- Common pitfall: Caricaturing the volunteer maliciously. Fix: Remind players that the volunteer is the hero of the story; the portrayal must be affectionate and celebratory, not mocking.
- Side-coach with narrative prompts like 'And because of that...' if the scene becomes a series of disconnected jokes rather than a sequential story.
- Ensure players use physical object work to bring the volunteer's specific environment and daily tools to life, enhancing the world-building.

## Variations
- The Nightmare Version: Instead of a normal day, the players dramatize a heightened, comedic worst-case scenario day based on the volunteer's minor pet peeves or daily anxieties.
- The Musical Edition: Key moments of the volunteer's day are heightened and transitioned into spontaneous, fully-staged musical numbers.
- Split-Screen Day: Two players play the same day simultaneously—one representing the external reality of the day, and the other representing the internal monologue or fantasy life of the volunteer.

## Debrief
- How did active listening during the interview shape the specific narrative choices we made on stage?
- How did using the Story Spine framework help us transition from a list of facts into a cohesive, satisfying story?
- What strategies did we use to keep the volunteer feeling respected and celebrated while still finding comedy in their routine?

## Safety & Inclusion
Ensure the volunteer feels safe and respected at all times. Avoid asking highly personal, invasive, or sensitive questions during the interview, such as those regarding finances, deep relationships, or personal trauma. Always allow the volunteer to pass on any question, and keep the stage portrayal supportive and good-natured.

## Why It Works
This game anchors the narrative engine in concrete, real-world truths, preventing players from getting lost in abstract invention. By applying the Story Spine framework to a real person's life, players learn to transition smoothly from world-building to action, ensuring a satisfying narrative arc that resonates deeply with both the volunteer and the audience.
