# The Shared Ascent

> Navigate a high-stakes crisis together where every action must build upon the last.

## Overview
A collaborative narrative game where four to six players work in unison to overcome a single, high-stakes crisis. By operating under strict rules of interdependence, players must build a cohesive story of survival or triumph, ensuring no single character solves the crisis alone.

## What It Trains
- **Domain:** D3 — The Scene
- **Principle(s):** Serve the Story; Group Mind; Make Your Partner a Genius
- **Skill(s):** Narrative Architecture; Stakes / The 'Want'; World-Building; Game Identification; Support Work; Active Listening
- **Technique(s):** C.R.O.W. (Character, Relationship, Objective, Where); If this is true, what else is true?
- **Focus:** narrative

**Objective:** To develop narrative architecture and collective story-building by forcing players to subordinate individual comedic or heroic impulses to the overarching needs of a shared group objective.

## Setup
An open performance space. The facilitator gathers four to six players on stage. No physical props are used. The facilitator prompts the players for a high-stakes, complex crisis that requires a team to solve (e.g., escaping a collapsing mine, disarming a complex mechanism, or navigating a ship through a cosmic storm). The facilitator also establishes a unifying relationship among the characters.

## How to Play
1. Begin the scene in media res (in the middle of the action), with all characters already fully aware of the crisis and actively dealing with its immediate pressure.
2. The first player initiates by establishing a specific, immediate physical obstacle or a critical piece of information related to the overarching crisis.
3. Subsequent players must make moves that directly build upon the established reality, alternating between introducing new sub-obstacles, offering resources, or taking physical actions.
4. Enforce the 'No Solo Heroics' rule: no single character can unilaterally resolve a major problem or save the day. Any attempt to do so must be met by other characters complicating the action or pointing out why it requires the whole team.
5. Practice the 'Selfless Hand-Off' by ending every line or physical action with an open-ended challenge or a clear physical cue that invites another player to step in and build.
6. Maintain narrative tension by tracking the group's progress; every successful action should reveal a new, logical complication (the 'if true, what else is true' principle applied to danger).
7. Conclude the scene organically when the group either definitively achieves their super-objective or conclusively fails, resulting in a shared, dramatic climax.

## Facilitation Notes
- Side-coaching cue: 'Pass the baton!' Use this when a player starts taking multiple consecutive actions without letting others contribute.
- Pitfall: Players instantly solving the crisis (e.g., 'I found the key and unlocked the door, we are safe!'). Fix: Immediately side-coach, 'Yes, but the door opens into a flooded corridor!' or have the other players point out a new complication.
- Side-coaching cue: 'Raise the stakes!' If the scene feels flat, prompt a player to discover a ticking clock or a failing resource (e.g., 'We only have two minutes of oxygen left!').
- Encourage physical object work. When players physically struggle with heavy levers, tight spaces, or delicate tools, it slows down the narrative and makes the obstacles feel real and earned.

## Variations
- Internal Friction: Give one or more characters a secret personal agenda or a relationship conflict that makes cooperation harder, though they must still work toward the shared goal.
- The Ticking Clock: The facilitator physically counts down or uses a visible timer, forcing players to accelerate their narrative pacing and decision-making.
- Silent Crisis: Play the entire game in complete silence, relying entirely on physical theater, eye contact, and shared object work to communicate obstacles and solutions.

## Debrief
- How did it feel to surrender your individual control over the story's resolution?
- What specific moves made you feel most supported by your teammates?
- How did the 'No Solo Heroics' constraint change the way you listened to your scene partners?
- At what point did the narrative feel like it was truly moving forward, and what caused that momentum?

## Safety & Inclusion
Ensure that the chosen crisis does not trigger real-world trauma for the participants (e.g., avoid realistic medical emergencies or claustrophobic scenarios if players express discomfort). Establish a non-verbal 'pause' signal if the physical intensity or tension of the crisis becomes too high for any player.

## Why It Works
This game works because it operationalizes the 'Yes, And' philosophy into a structural narrative engine. By stripping away the option of individual heroism, players are forced to listen actively and treat their partner's contributions as essential building blocks. The alternating rhythm of obstacle and solution naturally generates a classic dramatic arc, teaching players how to build tension and satisfy audience expectations through collaborative cause-and-effect.
