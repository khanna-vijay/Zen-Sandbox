# Attunement Waves

![Attunement Waves — infographic](img/D2_P3_S3_T2_G336__resonance-rebalance.jpg){ .game-hero }

> Sync physically, echo emotionally, and weave status dynamics to build deep, intuitive partner connection.

## Overview
A progressive three-phase partner exercise designed to build deep interpersonal attunement. Players transition systematically from silent physical synchronization to structured emotional echoing, culminating in a spoken scene where they consciously manipulate and balance status dynamics. This progression scaffolds vulnerability, helping players ground their scene work in the immediate reality of their partner.

## What It Trains
- **Domain:** D2 — The Partner
- **Principle(s):** Yes, And; Make Your Partner a Genius; Assume Competence; Vulnerability
- **Skill(s):** Active Listening; Status Modulation; Single-Partner Empathy & Mirroring; Offer Reception; Active Gifting; Emotional Fluidity
- **Technique(s):** Mirror exercise; Emotional-echo drills; Meisner Repetition; Yes, And… sentence games; Endowment-gifting drills; Endowment-acceptance; Status Seesaw; Give them the answer
- **Focus:** connection

**Objective:** To cultivate deep partner empathy, active listening, and responsive status modulation by layering physical, emotional, and verbal attunement, training players to make their partner the focal point of the scene.

## Setup
An open, quiet space where pairs can stand or sit facing each other at a comfortable distance. No props are required. For online play, partners pin each other's video feeds to maintain direct, uninterrupted eye contact.

## How to Play
1. Divide the group into pairs and have partners stand or sit facing each other, establishing soft, steady eye contact in silence.
2. Phase 1 (Silent Mirroring): Instruct pairs to begin moving their hands, torsos, or facial expressions in unison without designating a leader or follower, aiming for a fluid, shared physical state where every movement is mutually initiated.
3. Phase 2 (Emotional Echo): Transition to verbal play. Partner A shares a brief, honest statement about their current internal state or a physical sensation (e.g., 'I feel a slight tension in my shoulders' or 'I feel quiet anticipation').
4. Partner B receives the statement, echoes it back exactly to validate it, and then adds their own present-moment emotional or physical statement (e.g., 'You feel quiet anticipation. I feel a sense of calm curiosity').
5. Continue this structured emotional exchange back and forth for several rounds, maintaining the physical connection and eye contact established in Phase 1.
6. Transition Bridge: To transition to Phase 3, the facilitator calls out a simple, low-stakes relationship or setting (e.g., 'two neighbors gardening' or 'co-workers organizing a desk'). Partners must carry their final emotional states from Phase 2 directly into these characters.
7. Phase 3 (Status Weave): Begin the spoken scene. The first line of the scene must directly reference or build upon the last emotional echo. As the dialogue continues, players must consciously choose to either elevate or lower their partner's status.
8. Throughout the scene, players must actively gift status to their partner (treating them as the expert or authority) or gracefully accept gifted status, keeping the power dynamic fluid, collaborative, and balanced.

## Facilitation Notes
- Coaching Cue: During Phase 1, if one person is clearly leading, call out: 'Dissolve the leader. Let the movement live in the space between you, as if you are both reacting to a single current.'
- Pitfall: In Phase 2, players often overthink or invent dramatic, fictional emotions. Fix: Remind them to report simple, honest physical or emotional sensations like 'My breathing is shallow' or 'I feel relaxed.'
- Transition Coaching: During the transition to Phase 3, remind players not to drop the physical and emotional connection. Say: 'Keep the same eye contact and physical proximity as you enter the scene. The scene is just an extension of your current connection.'
- Coaching Cue: In Phase 3, encourage physical status work. Remind players that status is expressed through posture, eye contact, and physical space, not just verbal sparring.
- Pitfall: Players treating status as a competition to win. Fix: Remind them that the goal is to make their partner look brilliant by accepting their status offers and playing into them.

## Variations
- Online Adaptation: Conduct the exercise over video call. In Phase 1, players mirror facial expressions, head movements, and hand gestures within the camera frame. In Phase 3, use vocal tone and proximity to the camera to modulate status.
- Status Seesaw: In Phase 3, require players to alternate status roles with every single line exchange (high to low, low to high), creating a dynamic, rhythmic power shift.
- Vocal Mirroring (Accessibility): For players with limited mobility or visual impairments, replace physical mirroring in Phase 1 with vocal mirroring, matching the pitch, tempo, and volume of breathing or simple non-verbal sounds.

## Debrief
- How did the physical attunement in Phase 1 change how you listened to your partner's words in Phase 2?
- What did it feel like to have your emotional state echoed back to you before your partner responded? How did that affect your sense of safety?
- How did carrying your Phase 2 emotional state into Phase 3 ease the transition into the scene?
- How did gifting status to your partner make them look like a genius, and how did it affect the flow of the scene?

## Safety & Inclusion
Ensure players know they can share simple, low-stakes physical sensations (like 'My feet feel warm') if sharing deep emotional states feels too vulnerable. For physical accessibility, players can remain seated throughout. If eye contact is uncomfortable or inaccessible, partners can focus on a neutral point nearby or mirror breathing patterns instead.

## Why It Works
By scaffolding the interaction from non-verbal to emotional, and finally to narrative status, the game bypasses intellectual overthinking. The explicit transition bridge ensures that the emotional reality established in the early phases directly fuels the scene, preventing players from dropping their connection. It forces players to ground their scene work in the actual, present-moment reality of their partner, making 'Yes, And' a physical and emotional truth rather than just a cognitive rule.
