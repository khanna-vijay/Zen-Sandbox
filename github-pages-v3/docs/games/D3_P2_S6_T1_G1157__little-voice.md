# Voices of the Room

![Voices of the Room — infographic](img/D3_P2_S6_T1_G1157__little-voice.jpg){ .game-hero }

> Bring your physical environment to life and justify the absurd things your surroundings say.

## Overview
One player initiates a solo scene, establishing a clear physical environment through detailed object work. Off-stage players provide the voices of inanimate objects or tiny elements within that space, forcing the main actor to accept, adapt, and justify these absurd interactions within their base reality.

## What It Trains
- **Domain:** D3 — The Scene
- **Principle(s):** Base Reality First; Yes, And; Make Your Partner a Genius
- **Skill(s):** Justification; World-Building; Offer Reception; Active Gifting
- **Technique(s):** Justify the absurd; C.R.O.W. (Character, Relationship, Objective, Where); Endowment-acceptance; Endowment-gifting drills
- **Focus:** skill_drill

**Objective:** Develops the skill of justifying the absurd while maintaining a strong base reality and practicing active offer reception.

## Setup
A clear performance space with no physical props. One player stands in the playing area, while two to five players stand on the sidelines ready to provide off-stage voices.

## How to Play
1. The main player enters the stage and establishes a clear, mundane environment using detailed physical object work, such as making coffee, brushing teeth, or walking through a forest.
2. Once the base reality of who, what, and where is established, an off-stage player speaks as an inanimate object or tiny entity in the scene, such as a coffee mug, a toothbrush, or an ant.
3. The off-stage voice must immediately make their identity clear, either through their dialogue or by the main player naming them upon hearing the voice.
4. The main player must instantly accept the voice as a real, physical occurrence in their world, avoiding denial or treating it as a hallucination.
5. The main player justifies why this object is talking or why it holds its specific perspective, integrating the absurd element into the scene's logic.
6. Other off-stage players can chime in as different objects, but they must space out their offers to let the main player react and build a relationship with one object at a time.
7. The scene concludes once a satisfying resolution or a heightened comedic dynamic is established between the human and their vocal environment.

## Facilitation Notes
- Side-coaching cue: 'Don't play crazy! Treat the talking toaster as a completely real, logical part of your morning.'
- Pitfall: The main player ignores the object or explains it away as a dream. Fix: Remind them that in improv, everything is real. Yes-and the reality of the talking object.
- Side-coaching cue: 'Off-stage voices, keep your offers short and active. Give the main player something specific to react to.'
- Pitfall: Too many objects talk at once, overwhelming the main player. Fix: Coach the off-stage players to listen and edit themselves, allowing only one or two objects to speak per scene.

## Variations
- The Emotional Object: The objects don't just talk; they have extreme emotional states, such as a highly anxious seatbelt or a passive-aggressive mirror, which the main player must soothe or match.
- Two-Player Human Scene: Two players are in the scene together, and the objects speak to both of them, forcing them to collectively justify the shared absurdity.
- The Secret Agenda: The off-stage voice has a hidden motive or secret they are trying to get the main player to confess.

## Debrief
- How did establishing a strong physical environment first make it easier to justify the talking objects?
- What strategies did you use to make the absurd voices feel grounded and logical within your character's world?
- How did the off-stage players help 'make their partner a genius' with their vocal offers?

## Safety & Inclusion
Ensure that off-stage voices do not use startlingly loud noises or jump-scare tactics. Keep the physical space clear of actual obstacles so the main player can safely pantomime their environment without tripping.

## Why It Works
By anchoring the scene in a strong, physical base reality first, the absurd elements do not collapse the scene into chaos. Instead, they act as active gifts that the player must justify, which naturally builds a rich, imaginative world through the core engine of Yes-And.
