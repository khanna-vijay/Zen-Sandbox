# Anatomy Assembly

<div class="game-hero-ph" markdown>🎨 *Infographic coming soon*</div>

> Rapidly cluster by matching body parts to build physical connection and sharp peripheral awareness.

## Overview
Anatomy Assembly is a high-energy physical warm-up where players move dynamically through the space and must instantly form clusters based on a specific number and body part called out by the facilitator. It breaks the ice, encourages physical playfulness, and demands sharp spatial awareness to ensure everyone is included.

## What It Trains
- **Domain:** D4 — The Ensemble
- **Principle(s):** Fail Joyfully; Consent & Boundaries; Group Mind
- **Skill(s):** Physicality & Space Work; Boundary Navigation; Peripheral Awareness
- **Technique(s):** Negotiating physical contact
- **Focus:** connection

**Objective:** Develops peripheral awareness, group mind, and physical comfort within the ensemble by making rapid, collaborative spatial adjustments under pressure.

## Setup
A large, open room free of obstacles. Players stand scattered throughout the space, ready to move. No props are required.

## How to Play
1. Instruct all players to begin walking around the room at a moderate pace, filling the empty spaces and maintaining soft-focus peripheral vision.
2. Explain that the facilitator will periodically call out a number and a body part, such as Three elbows or Four knees.
3. Upon hearing the call, players must immediately scramble to form groups of exactly that size, connecting the specified body parts together in a central point.
4. Emphasize that players must work together to ensure no one is left out; if a group is too large or small, they must quickly adjust or help others find a cluster.
5. Once the clusters are formed and stabilized, the facilitator calls Walk, and players disperse to resume moving through the space.
6. Repeat the process with different numbers and body parts, gradually increasing the speed and complexity of the prompts.

## Facilitation Notes
- Side-coaching cue: Keep your eyes open! Look for the person who needs a group and pull them in.
- Common Pitfall: Players getting too exclusive or competitive about forming their immediate group. Fix: Remind them that the goal is for the entire room to succeed, not just their individual cluster.
- Side-coaching cue: If you have an extra person, help them find another group or split your group to accommodate them.
- Common Pitfall: Physical discomfort or boundary crossing. Fix: Establish clear boundaries before starting, allowing players to hover or point near the body part if they prefer not to make direct physical contact.

## Variations
- Silent Assembly: Run the game in complete silence, requiring players to use only visual cues, eye contact, and physical gestures to form groups.
- Non-Human Parts: Call out imaginary or metaphorical parts, such as Three wings or Five antennae, to encourage creative physical expression.
- Mathematical Split: Call out numbers that do not divide evenly into the total group size, forcing players to negotiate how to handle the remainder creatively.

## Debrief
- How did you use your peripheral vision to find open groups or help others find a spot?
- What did it feel like when a group had to quickly adjust or split up to include someone else?
- How does this physical quick-decision making translate to supporting your scene partners on stage?

## Safety & Inclusion
Establish a clear opt-in boundary rule before playing. Players can always substitute a hand-tap or a simple gesture/pointing instead of direct contact with sensitive areas. Avoid calling out highly sensitive or invasive body parts to maintain a safe, professional, and comfortable environment for all participants.

## Why It Works
This game bypasses intellectual overthinking by forcing immediate physical action. The rapid spatial reorganization builds group mind as players stop thinking about their individual movement and start viewing the entire room as a single, fluid organism that needs to balance itself.
