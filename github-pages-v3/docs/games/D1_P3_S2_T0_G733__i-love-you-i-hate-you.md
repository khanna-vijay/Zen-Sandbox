# Degrees of Connection

<div class="game-hero-ph" markdown>🎨 *Infographic coming soon*</div>

> Rapidly shift emotional intimacy levels with passing classmates to build vulnerability and spontaneous connection.

## Overview
In this fast-paced, low-stakes movement exercise, participants walk freely through the space and exchange progressively more intense emotional statements with whoever they pass. By cycling through extreme declarations of affection and rejection, players bypass their intellectual filters and experience sudden, visceral shifts in emotional proximity.

## What It Trains
- **Domain:** D1 — The Self
- **Principle(s):** Vulnerability; Make Your Partner a Genius
- **Skill(s):** Emotional Fluidity; Unfiltered Spontaneity; Single-Partner Empathy & Mirroring
- **Focus:** connection

**Objective:** To develop emotional fluidity and vulnerability by practicing the rapid delivery and reception of high-stakes emotional statements without overthinking or hesitation.

## Setup
An open, clear room where participants can walk freely in all directions. No props or chairs are needed.

## How to Play
1. Instruct all participants to walk around the room at a moderate pace, filling the empty spaces and maintaining soft eye contact with those they pass.
2. Announce Phase 1: As players pass each other, they must make brief eye contact and say a simple, neutral greeting like 'Hello.'
3. After about 30 seconds, call out Phase 2: Instruct players to upgrade their greeting to a warm, positive statement like 'You're great' or 'I'm glad you're here.'
4. Transition to Phase 3: Instruct players to look each other in the eye as they pass and say, with genuine intent, 'I love you.'
5. Transition to Phase 4: Instruct players to shift completely and say, with equal conviction but without physical aggression, 'I hate you.'
6. Transition to Phase 5: Bring the exercise back to a positive, vulnerable state by having players pass each other and say 'I love you' once more.
7. Call a freeze to end the exercise, allowing players to take a deep breath and shake off the emotional residue.

## Facilitation Notes
- Side-coaching cue: 'Commit to the words. Don't laugh them off, make them a joke, or apologize immediately after.'
- Pitfall: Players might rush past each other to avoid eye contact. Fix: Remind them to slow down, make brief eye contact, and deliver the line directly to the person.
- Side-coaching cue: 'Notice how your body and breath react to saying "I hate you" versus "I love you".'
- Pitfall: Over-acting or cartoonish delivery. Fix: Encourage players to speak the lines as simple, honest truths rather than theatrical caricatures.

## Variations
- Physical Mirroring: Add a physical gesture (a nod, a hand on the heart, a slight turn away) that matches the emotional tone of the phrase.
- Gibberish Translation: Deliver the same progression of subtexts using gibberish, relying entirely on tone, facial expression, and body language.
- Status Shifts: Instead of emotional phrases, players pass each other and silently establish high or low status relative to the person they are passing.

## Debrief
- Which phrase felt the most difficult or vulnerable to say out loud, and why?
- How did your physical posture or breathing change as we shifted between love and hate?
- How does practicing these extreme statements in a low-stakes way help us when we are improvising scenes?

## Safety & Inclusion
Ensure participants know they can adjust the words if 'I love you' or 'I hate you' triggers discomfort, substituting with 'I value you' or 'I reject this' if needed. Emphasize that physical boundaries must be respected—no touching or aggressive physical posturing is allowed during the 'hate' phase.

## Why It Works
By rapidly cycling through extreme emotional statements in a low-stakes, repetitive format, the brain's analytical filter is bypassed. This builds emotional fluidity because players must instantly inhabit a feeling without needing a narrative justification, training them to accept and express vulnerability on demand.
