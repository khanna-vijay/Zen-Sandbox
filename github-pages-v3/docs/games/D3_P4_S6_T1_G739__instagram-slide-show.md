# Instant Travelogue

![Instant Travelogue — infographic](img/D3_P4_S6_T1_G739__instagram-slide-show.jpg){ .game-hero }

> Present a hilarious vacation slideshow using random, real-time images as your unexpected holiday photos.

## Overview
Players portray travelers returning from an unusual trip, presenting a slideshow to an audience. Instead of prepared slides, a tech operator projects random, real-time images from a public feed or curated folder. The presenters must instantly justify how each absurd, unrelated image represents a real moment from their journey, weaving a cohesive narrative on the fly.

## What It Trains
- **Domain:** D3 — The Scene
- **Principle(s):** Serve the Story; Yes, And; The Audience Is the Final Scene Partner
- **Skill(s):** Justification; World-Building; Offer Reception; Stage Presence & Clarity
- **Technique(s):** Justify the absurd; C.R.O.W. (Character, Relationship, Objective, Where); Endowment-acceptance; Breaking the 4th Wall / Direct Address
- **Focus:** comedy_game

**Objective:** Develops rapid justification of absurd offers, narrative cohesion, active listening, and collaborative world-building.

## Setup
A stage area with a projection screen or large monitor visible to both the audience and the players. A tech operator with an internet-connected device ready to pull up a random image feed or pre-selected folder of unusual photos. Two to three players stand on stage.

## How to Play
1. Ask the audience for a suggestion of an unusual destination or the purpose of the players' recent trip.
2. The players step forward, establish their relationship, and introduce the slideshow to the audience.
3. The tech operator projects the first random image onto the screen.
4. The active presenter must immediately accept the image as a literal photograph from their trip, justifying the absurd or mundane visual elements to fit their narrative.
5. The other players must 'yes-and' this justification, adding personal memories, emotional context, or explaining background details.
6. Once the current slide has been fully explored and justified, a player calls 'Next slide, please,' prompting the tech operator to display a new random image.
7. The players continue this process for four to six slides, building a progressive narrative arc with a clear beginning, middle, and climax.
8. The players wrap up their presentation with a final concluding slide and thank their audience.

## Facilitation Notes
- Coaching cue: 'Don't deny the image! If there is a random dog in a business suit, that is your tour guide.'
- Pitfall: Players spending too much time describing what is on the screen instead of why it happened or how they felt. Fix: Coach them to focus on the emotional backstory and their personal relationship to the image.
- Coaching cue: 'Share the mic. Let your partner explain the background details while you set up the main action.'
- Pitfall: Tech operator moving too fast or slow. Fix: Instruct the tech operator to wait for the verbal cue 'Next slide' and to choose images with distinct, clear visual elements.

## Variations
- The Expert Panel: Instead of a vacation, players are world-renowned experts presenting a groundbreaking scientific discovery or historical lecture using random diagrams and charts.
- The Blind Artist: One player acts as the 'photographer' who took all the photos and must defend their terrible artistic choices to their frustrated travel companions.
- Audience Camera Roll: With consent, use photos submitted live by audience members from their own phone libraries.

## Debrief
- How did you find a logical connection between a completely random image and your established story?
- What strategies helped you share the narrative load with your partner instead of steamrolling?
- How does committing 100% to an absurd explanation make it believable and funny for the audience?

## Safety & Inclusion
Ensure the image source is pre-filtered or moderated to avoid displaying inappropriate, offensive, or highly personal content. If using audience photos, establish clear boundaries regarding privacy and consent before displaying any images.

## Why It Works
It forces players to practice extreme 'Yes, And' by treating external, uncontrollable visual inputs as absolute truth. By justifying the absurd elements of a random photo, players learn to trust their immediate instincts and discover that the funniest moments come from logical explanations of illogical situations.
