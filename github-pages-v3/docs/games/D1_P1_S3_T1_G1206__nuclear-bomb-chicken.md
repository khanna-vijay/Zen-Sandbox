# The Henhouse Paradox

![The Henhouse Paradox — infographic](img/D1_P1_S3_T1_G1206__nuclear-bomb-chicken.jpg){ .game-hero }

> Maintain absolute character commitment even when the world is about to end.

## Overview
A high-stakes, single-use physical exercise where players must embody simple animals unaware of an impending, catastrophic human event. It challenges players to separate their own knowledge from their character's reality, demanding 100% physical commitment to mundane behaviors under extreme external pressure.

## What It Trains
- **Domain:** D1 — The Self
- **Principle(s):** Commit 100%; Base Reality First
- **Skill(s):** Physicality & Space Work; World-Building
- **Technique(s):** Character Walks/Centers; C.R.O.W. (Character, Relationship, Objective, Where)
- **Focus:** skill_drill

**Objective:** To develop deep character commitment and physical space work by separating the player's meta-knowledge from the character's immediate, lived reality.

## Setup
An open, safe room representing a chicken coop. No props are needed. Players spread out across the space.

## How to Play
1. Instruct all players to scatter throughout the space and fully embody chickens in a standard, peaceful henhouse.
2. Emphasize that they must commit 100% to the physical reality of being a chicken: pecking, nesting, clucking, and interacting with the environment using detailed space work.
3. Once they are fully immersed in their physical characterizations, announce to the room: 'An atomic bomb has been launched and will detonate directly on this henhouse in exactly thirty seconds.'
4. Start a silent or spoken countdown from thirty.
5. Observe the players' physical choices as the countdown progresses, looking for any signs of human panic, looking at the sky, or reacting to the concept of a 'bomb.'
6. Call 'Freeze' when the countdown reaches zero.
7. Identify who successfully maintained the pure, simple reality of a chicken (e.g., continuing to peck at seeds or lay an egg) versus who let their human knowledge of the threat break their character's reality.

## Facilitation Notes
- Side-coaching cue: 'What does a chicken care about right now? Focus on the grain, the dirt, the feathers.'
- Side-coaching cue: 'Commit to the physical center of the animal. Where does your movement originate?'
- Pitfall: Players immediately panic, run around, or look up at the sky. Fix: Gently remind them during the debrief that chickens have no concept of modern warfare or physics; their base reality is entirely immediate and sensory.
- Note: This exercise can really only be played once with its surprise element intact, so make the setup count.

## Variations
- The Modern Office: Players are 19th-century historical figures suddenly told a Wi-Fi outage is occurring; they must remain in their character's reality, completely ignoring the modern terminology.
- The Pet Shop: Players are domestic pets in a shop while a stock market crash is announced over the radio.

## Debrief
- Why is it so difficult to ignore high-stakes information that we, as actors, know is happening?
- How did focusing on physical space work (pecking, nesting) help you stay grounded in your character's reality?
- What is the difference between playing the 'plot' versus playing the 'character's truth'?

## Safety & Inclusion
Ensure players are mindful of their physical boundaries while moving around as animals. Since players may be looking down or moving erratically, establish a slow-motion rule if the space is tight to prevent collisions.

## Why It Works
It forces a stark, undeniable boundary between actor consciousness and character consciousness. By using a high-stakes threat that the character cannot comprehend, it highlights how easily we abandon 'Base Reality First' in favor of dramatic, unearned panic.
