# The Consent Compass

![The Consent Compass — infographic](img/D2_P1_S6_T1_G113__the-consent-compass-navigating-the-narrative.jpg){ .game-hero }

> Transform boundary navigation into a dynamic, collaborative engine for rich and safe narrative storytelling.

## Overview
A structured narrative game where players actively practice real-time boundary negotiation and collaborative scene redirection. By treating consent as a generative creative constraint, players learn to check in fluidly and pivot stories constructively when boundaries are reached. The result is a highly supportive play space where safety directly inspires deeper, more truthful dramatic choices.

## What It Trains
- **Domain:** D2 — The Partner
- **Principle(s):** Consent & Boundaries; Truth Over Pandering
- **Skill(s):** Boundary Navigation; Narrative Architecture; Support Work
- **Technique(s):** Check-ins; Cut calls; Negotiating physical contact
- **Focus:** mixed

**Objective:** To develop advanced boundary navigation and active check-in techniques, transforming safety interventions from disruptive halts into collaborative, narrative-building pivots.

## Setup
Arrange a moderate playing space with 2 to 3 players on stage and 1 to 2 players seated off-stage as Navigators who actively watch and support. No props are required. Before beginning, conduct a brief container setup to establish the group's baseline boundaries and review the verbal cues.

## How to Play
1. Assign 2 to 3 players to start a scene based on a simple relationship or location suggestion, while the remaining 1 to 2 players act as off-stage Navigators.
2. During play, any on-stage player who senses an approaching physical or emotional boundary must initiate an in-character Consent Check by pairing a subtle physical pause with an embedded verbal inquiry.
3. The receiving player must respond immediately in-character, either affirming the action or expressing hesitation or declination through their dialogue and body language.
4. If the partner declines or hesitates, the players must immediately pivot the narrative to respect that boundary, using the resistance as a realistic, character-driven plot point.
5. If any player or off-stage Navigator feels a boundary is crossed or at imminent risk without consent, they must call out Safety Pivot to pause the action.
6. Upon hearing the call, all players immediately freeze in place, step out of character, and maintain their physical positions while the caller neutrally states their concern without blame.
7. The entire group of on-stage players and Navigators must quickly collaborate to propose two distinct, safer narrative paths that still honor the scene's emotional stakes.
8. The group selects one path, players step back into character at their frozen positions, and seamlessly resume the scene along the newly chosen trajectory.

## Facilitation Notes
- Side-coach players to keep Consent Checks integrated; the pause should feel like a natural dramatic beat rather than a complete break in momentum.
- Pitfall: Players using the Safety Pivot to rewrite narrative choices they simply did not like. Fix: Ensure pivots are strictly reserved for boundary, safety, or comfort concerns, not creative disagreement.
- Encourage Navigators to be active but selective; their role is to offer an objective safety net, not to micromanage the scene's direction.
- Remind players that a 'no' in a Consent Check is a gift that reveals character depth; side-coach with: Play the truth of their hesitation!

## Variations
- Silent Compass: Conduct the Consent Checks entirely through non-verbal cues, such as eye contact, hand placement, and physical distance, to build heightened physical awareness.
- Blind Navigators: The off-stage Navigators close their eyes and listen only to the vocal tone and dialogue, calling Safety Pivots based purely on auditory boundaries and emotional escalation.

## Debrief
- How did integrating Consent Checks in-character affect the dramatic tension and truthfulness of your scenes?
- What did it feel like to collaboratively re-author a scene during a Safety Pivot? Did the new path feel more or less creative than the original direction?
- How can we apply the practice of treating a partner's boundary as a creative gift in our standard, unconstrained improv play?

## Safety & Inclusion
This game is highly safety-sensitive. Establish a clear, shared understanding of personal boundaries before playing. Ensure players know they have absolute autonomy to decline any physical or emotional escalation, and that calling a Safety Pivot is always treated as a supportive, non-punitive contribution to the ensemble.

## Why It Works
By formalizing check-ins and boundary navigation as active play mechanics, this game removes the stigma of saying no on stage. It aligns with the principle of Truth Over Pandering by proving that respecting boundaries leads to more complex, realistic, and compelling narrative architecture than pushing past comfort zones for cheap theatrical payoffs.
