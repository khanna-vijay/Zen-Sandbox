# The Boundary Lab

<div class="game-hero-ph" markdown>🎨 *Infographic coming soon*</div>

> Practice navigating real-time boundary challenges and empowering 'Cut' calls in a supportive, structured environment.

## Overview
A high-stakes, safety-focused skill drill where one player is secretly tasked with introducing a subtle boundary challenge into a scene. The remaining players must actively listen, prioritize their personal comfort over scene compliance, and practice setting boundaries or calling a halt to the action. It shifts the focus from making the scene work at all costs to practicing real-time agency and mutual support.

## What It Trains
- **Domain:** D2 — The Partner
- **Principle(s):** Consent & Boundaries; Vulnerability; Truth Over Pandering
- **Skill(s):** Boundary Navigation; Active Listening
- **Technique(s):** Check-ins; Cut calls; Negotiating physical contact
- **Focus:** skill_drill

**Objective:** To develop practical boundary navigation and active listening skills, specifically training players to prioritize personal safety and comfort over polite compliance, and to confidently execute and respect 'Cut' calls.

## Setup
An in-person space with a clear stage area and seating for 4 to 6 players. No props are required. The facilitator prepares a few secret prompts on slips of paper or is ready to whisper them to the designated Pusher player.

## How to Play
1. Establish the Safety Framework: The facilitator gathers the 4-6 players and establishes that this is a safety-first training drill. Introduce the 'Cut' call as an immediate, non-negotiable stop that is celebrated as a success, not a failure.
2. Assign Roles: Select one player to be the 'Pusher' for the round. The other 3-5 players are the 'Responders.'
3. Deliver the Secret Prompt: Take the Pusher aside and whisper or hand them a secret, subtle boundary-testing task, such as trying to get everyone to join in a specific physical ritual, or repeatedly bringing up a topic their character knows makes another character uncomfortable.
4. Set the Scene: Give the entire group a simple, low-stakes scenario to establish a baseline reality, such as planning a group vacation or waiting in a lobby.
5. Begin the Scene: The Responders start the scene naturally. The Pusher enters and begins to subtly work toward their secret objective, paying close attention to the physical and verbal reactions of their partners.
6. Navigate and Respond: Responders must actively listen to their gut. If they feel their personal or character boundaries being pushed, they must practice 'Truth Over Pandering' by redirecting, setting verbal limits, checking in with partners, or calling 'Cut!' to stop the scene immediately.
7. Conclude the Action: The scene ends either when a player or the facilitator calls 'Cut!', or when the Pusher has successfully tested the boundaries and the Responders have successfully navigated the challenge to a natural resolution.
8. Reset and Rotate: Run multiple rounds, ensuring different players take on the Pusher and Responder roles to experience both sides of the dynamic.

## Facilitation Notes
- Frame 'Cut' as a Win: Actively celebrate whenever a player calls 'Cut!' Remind the group that stopping a scene to protect a boundary is a highly skilled, professional choice, not a failure of the improv.
- Calibrate the Prompts: Start with very mild, low-stakes prompts before moving to physical proximity or touch prompts. Only escalate if the group demonstrates high trust and competence.
- Monitor the Pusher: Ensure the Pusher understands their role is to test boundaries, not to violate them or be malicious. They must be highly sensitive to 'soft nos' and back off immediately if a boundary is set.
- Intervene Early: As the facilitator, do not hesitate to call 'Cut!' yourself if you observe a player experiencing genuine distress or freezing up. Use these moments as gentle, supportive teaching points.
- Avoid Performance Pressure: Remind players that this is a clinical skill drill, not a show. There is no pressure to be funny, clever, or to keep the scene going if it feels uncomfortable.

## Variations
- Traffic Light System: Instead of only having a hard 'Cut,' introduce 'Yellow' (slow down/re-evaluate) and 'Red' (hard stop) verbal cues to practice nuanced, real-time boundary adjustments.
- The Safety Observer: Designate one non-playing participant as a dedicated 'Safety Observer' whose sole job is to watch for subtle non-verbal cues of discomfort and call 'Cut!' on behalf of the players if they notice someone freezing.
- The Rewind: After a 'Cut' is called, debrief briefly, then replay the last 30 seconds of the scene, allowing the Responder to practice a different, more assertive boundary-setting strategy.

## Debrief
- Responders: What was the very first internal signal (physical or emotional) that told you a boundary was being tested, and how did you react to it?
- Pushers: What non-verbal or verbal cues did you observe from your partners, and how did those cues affect how hard you pushed?
- For those who wanted to set a boundary or call 'Cut' but hesitated: What was the internal narrative or pressure that held you back?
- How can we carry this practice of 'Truth Over Pandering' into our regular, high-energy comedic scenes to keep each other safe?

## Safety & Inclusion
This exercise is highly safety-sensitive. Before starting, conduct a group agreement check-in where players can state any hard physical or emotional boundaries for the session. Participation must be entirely voluntary, and players must have the right to pass on being the Pusher or to step out of the exercise at any moment without explanation.

## Why It Works
Improv training often over-indexes on yes-and compliance, which can accidentally train players to ignore their own discomfort for the sake of the scene. By gamifying the act of saying no or setting limits, this drill builds the psychological safety and muscle memory needed to navigate real-time discomfort, transforming boundary setting from a scene-killer into an empowering act of collaborative trust.
