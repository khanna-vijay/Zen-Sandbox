# The Shared Blueprint

<div class="game-hero-ph" markdown>🎨 *Infographic coming soon*</div>

> Build abstract physical connections through explicit, real-time boundary negotiation and enthusiastic consent.

## Overview
In this exercise, players collaboratively construct a metaphorical physical sculpture or sequence representing an abstract concept. By introducing secret personal boundaries and a mandatory pause-and-ask mechanic, players learn to navigate physical contact with absolute transparency, prioritizing personal comfort over theatrical momentum.

## What It Trains
- **Domain:** D2 — The Partner
- **Principle(s):** Consent & Boundaries; Truth Over Pandering; Vulnerability
- **Skill(s):** Boundary Navigation; Active Listening; Offer Reception
- **Technique(s):** Check-ins; Cut calls; Negotiating physical contact
- **Focus:** connection

**Objective:** To develop advanced boundary navigation and physical contact negotiation skills, training players to prioritize authentic personal comfort (Truth Over Pandering) while maintaining creative collaboration.

## Setup
An open, quiet playing space. Distribute index cards and pens to all players. Have players sit or stand in a circle, with a clear performance area in the center.

## How to Play
1. Each player privately writes down a specific, actionable physical boundary on an index card. This boundary should represent a minor, real-world preference that causes mild discomfort if crossed (e.g., 'no unexpected shoulder touches' or 'requires arm-length distance when facing someone'), but must not be deeply triggering.
2. Keep these cards secret. Two players step into the center of the space to build a 'Blueprint' based on an abstract prompt provided by the facilitator, such as 'A Gesture of Deep Compassion' or 'The Foundation of Trust'.
3. Player A initiates the first physical or spatial 'block' of the blueprint by moving toward Player B, but they must pause their movement exactly three inches before making physical contact or entering Player B's personal space.
4. While paused, Player A must make eye contact and explicitly state their physical intention (e.g., 'To build trust, I would like to place my hand on your forearm. May I do that?').
5. Player B assesses their secret boundary card and their actual comfort level. They must respond in one of three ways: an enthusiastic 'Yes', a 'Conditional Yes' that negotiates an alternative (e.g., 'You can't touch my arm, but you can rest your hand on my shoulder'), or a clear 'Cut' if they feel genuine discomfort.
6. If Player B negotiates, Player A must actively listen and adapt, finding a creative alternative that respects the boundary while still contributing to the abstract prompt.
7. Once an action is agreed upon, it is executed. Player B then initiates the next physical block toward Player A, repeating the pause, check-in, and negotiation loop.
8. If at any point a player feels genuine discomfort or if an action is rushed without consent, they call 'Cut!' to immediately freeze the action, step out of character, explain the boundary breach, and reset safely.

## Facilitation Notes
- Emphasize the 'Pause before Contact': Ensure players do not rush the physical approach. The three-inch pause is a non-negotiable safety buffer that gives the receiver time to process.
- Praise Creative Redirection: Highlight moments where a player receives a boundary restriction and successfully navigates it with a creative physical alternative, showing that boundaries enhance rather than block play.
- Monitor Boundary Selection: Before starting, remind players to choose boundaries that provide 'real data' (mild discomfort) rather than extreme triggers, keeping the learning environment safe but honest.
- Normalize the 'Cut' Call: If a player calls 'Cut', celebrate it as a successful application of the rules. Never treat a freeze as a failure of the scene.

## Variations
- Blind Blueprints: The initiator must close their eyes while the receiver guides them physically using only verbal, consent-based directions.
- Group Blueprint: Gradually add more players to the space, requiring multi-way consent checks as three or four players integrate into the physical structure.

## Debrief
- How did it feel to explicitly pause and ask for permission before making a physical move?
- For the receivers, did you feel any internal pressure to say 'yes' just to keep the scene moving? How did you overcome that?
- How did negotiating a boundary spark more creative physical choices than simply doing the first thing that came to mind?
- What did this exercise reveal about how we communicate comfort and safety on stage?

## Safety & Inclusion
This game is highly safety-sensitive. Participation must be entirely voluntary. Players have absolute autonomy over their physical boundaries. If a player is uncomfortable with physical touch, their secret boundary can be 'no physical contact,' forcing their partner to negotiate purely through spatial proximity and gesture. Always allow players to pass or observe.

## Why It Works
By gamifying the negotiation of physical space, this exercise strips away the pressure of theatrical performance and replaces it with explicit communication. The secret boundary mechanic forces players to practice 'Truth Over Pandering,' proving that scenes become more intimate and compelling when built on a foundation of absolute, enthusiastic consent rather than assumed compliance.
