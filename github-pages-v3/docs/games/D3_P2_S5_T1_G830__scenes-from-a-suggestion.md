# C.R.O.W. Launchpad

<div class="game-hero-ph" markdown>🎨 *Infographic coming soon*</div>

> Transform any audience suggestion into a rich, fully realized scene foundation in seconds.

## Overview
This foundational scene-work drill challenges two players to take a single audience suggestion and immediately establish a stable, believable base reality. By focusing on the core elements of Character, Relationship, Objective, and Where (C.R.O.W.), players learn to build a solid platform before chasing conflict or comedy.

## What It Trains
- **Domain:** D3 — The Scene
- **Principle(s):** Base Reality First; The Audience Is the Final Scene Partner
- **Skill(s):** World-Building; Room Reading
- **Technique(s):** C.R.O.W. (Character, Relationship, Objective, Where); Reading the suggestion's intent
- **Focus:** skill_drill

**Objective:** To develop the ability to rapidly establish a clear, grounded base reality (C.R.O.W.) using an audience suggestion, ensuring both players and the audience are aligned from the very first line.

## Setup
Two active players stand on stage in a clear performance space. The remaining players sit as the audience to provide suggestions and observe.

## How to Play
1. Ask the audience for a simple, non-geographic suggestion, such as an everyday object, an occupation, or a mundane activity.
2. The two active players take a brief moment of silence to let the suggestion settle, then step forward to begin the scene.
3. Player A initiates the scene with physical action (object work) that clearly establishes the 'Where' (the physical environment) and hints at their 'Character.'
4. Player B enters the scene, immediately accepting the physical environment established by Player A and defining their 'Relationship' to Player A through dialogue or physical interaction.
5. Within the first three exchanges, both players must collaborate to make the 'Objective' (what they are doing or what they want right now) clear to the audience.
6. The players continue the scene for two to three minutes, focusing on exploring and deepening this established base reality rather than introducing sudden twists or high-stakes conflict.
7. The facilitator calls 'Scene' once a stable, clear platform has been successfully built and maintained.

## Facilitation Notes
- Side-coaching cue: 'Show us where you are before you speak!' This encourages players to use physical environment work to establish the 'Where.'
- Side-coaching cue: 'Who are you to each other?' Use this to push players to define their relationship early in the dialogue.
- Pitfall: Players ignore the suggestion or use it only as a quick, throwaway joke. Fix: Challenge players to make the suggestion central to their physical environment or their character's immediate objective.
- Pitfall: Rushing into high-stakes conflict or arguments before establishing the base reality. Fix: Side-coach players to 'find the normal day first' to build a platform before introducing any major problems.

## Variations
- The Silent Launch: The first 45 seconds of the scene must be entirely silent, relying solely on physical environment and character posture to establish the 'Where' and 'Relationship' before any dialogue begins.
- C.R.O.W. Freeze: If the facilitator or audience feels a specific C.R.O.W. element is missing after 30 seconds, they call 'Freeze!' and the players must immediately adjust their next line to establish that missing element.

## Debrief
- How did having a clear physical environment (Where) make it easier to discover your relationship?
- What physical or verbal clues did your partner give you that helped you instantly understand who you were to each other?
- Why does establishing a strong base reality first make the rest of the scene feel easier and more sustainable to play?

## Safety & Inclusion
Ensure suggestions requested from the audience are safe, respectful, and inclusive. If an inappropriate suggestion is given, the facilitator should calmly and warmly ask for a different one without shaming the audience member.

## Why It Works
By focusing on C.R.O.W. immediately after receiving a suggestion, players avoid the 'talking heads' trap. It grounds the suggestion in physical reality and human connection, turning an abstract word into a lived-in world that the audience can instantly understand and engage with.
