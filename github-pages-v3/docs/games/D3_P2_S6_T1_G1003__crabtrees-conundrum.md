# Rhyme Retraction

<div class="game-hero-ph" markdown>🎨 *Infographic coming soon*</div>

> Justify sudden, rhyming shifts in your scene's reality whenever your partner mishears you.

## Overview
Two players initiate a grounded scene, establishing a clear relationship and environment. At any point, one player can challenge their partner's last line by asking, 'What did you say?', prompting the partner to instantly replace their last line with a rhyming alternative. The players must then immediately justify this new, absurd reality and continue the scene.

## What It Trains
- **Domain:** D3 — The Scene
- **Principle(s):** Base Reality First; The First Thought Is a Gift
- **Skill(s):** Justification; Unfiltered Spontaneity
- **Technique(s):** Justify the absurd; First Thought drills
- **Focus:** comedy_game

**Objective:** To develop rapid justification skills and unfiltered spontaneity by forcing players to seamlessly integrate unexpected, phonetically-driven dialogue choices into a grounded base reality.

## Setup
Two players stand in the performance space. The remaining participants act as an active audience. No props or special staging are required.

## How to Play
1. Two players begin a scene by establishing a clear, grounded base reality with a defined relationship, setting, and activity.
2. The players engage in natural dialogue to build the platform of the scene.
3. At any point, Player A can interrupt Player B's last spoken line by asking, 'What did you say?'
4. Player B must immediately retract their last line and replace it with a new sentence that rhymes with the original line but carries a completely different meaning.
5. Player B must deliver this new rhyming line with absolute commitment, treating it as what they intended to say all along.
6. Both players must instantly accept this new rhyming line as the absolute truth of the scene.
7. The players use justification to make this sudden, absurd shift make sense within their established reality, continuing the scene from this new point.

## Facilitation Notes
- Coaching cue: 'Don't stall to think of the perfect line. Grab the first rhyming word that comes to mind and build the sentence around it.'
- Pitfall: Players often pause too long trying to make the rhyme logical before speaking. Fix: Encourage them to speak the rhyme immediately and figure out the logic afterward.
- Coaching cue: 'Ground the absurd. If your line suddenly changed from "I bought a clock" to "I fought a hawk," explain why there is a hawk in the room.'
- Pitfall: Overusing the challenge. Fix: Remind players to let the scene breathe and establish a solid base reality before throwing in a rhyme challenge.

## Variations
- Audience Call-Out: The audience or facilitator calls out 'What did you say?' instead of the scene partner, keeping both actors on their toes.
- Double Down: The challenger can ask 'What did you say?' twice in a row, forcing a double-rhyme chain before the scene can proceed.
- Emotional Pivot: The new rhyming line must be accompanied by a complete shift in the speaker's emotional state.

## Debrief
- How did establishing a strong base reality at the start help you justify the weird rhyming lines later?
- What strategies did you use to instantly justify an absurd line that you didn't plan on saying?
- How did it feel to treat your first spontaneous rhyming thought as a gift rather than a mistake?

## Safety & Inclusion
Ensure players feel safe to pause briefly if needed; do not mock slow responses. Keep the physical environment clear of obstacles as players may get physically expressive when justifying absurd lines.

## Why It Works
This game works because it forces players to practice 'justification of the absurd' in real-time. By requiring a rhyming replacement, the brain is forced to bypass logical planning and rely on pure phonetic association (unfiltered spontaneity). Once the absurd line is spoken, the players must treat it as an absolute truth, reinforcing the principle of Base Reality First by weaving the new, bizarre detail back into the logical fabric of the scene.
