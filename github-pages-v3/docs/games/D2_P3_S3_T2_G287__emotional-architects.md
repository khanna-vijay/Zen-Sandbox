# Emotional Architects

<div class="game-hero-ph" markdown>🎨 *Infographic coming soon*</div>

> Co-create a rich emotional reality by gifting justifications for your partner's subtle physical offers.

## Overview
In this two-player exercise, one player acts as the 'Canvas,' subtly physicalizing an unspoken emotion, while the other acts as the 'Architect,' gifting specific narrative reasons for that feeling. Through a continuous cycle of emotional endowment and physical echo, players build a deeply connected, justified scene without ever naming the emotions directly.

## What It Trains
- **Domain:** D2 — The Partner
- **Principle(s):** Yes, And; Make Your Partner a Genius; Assume Competence; Show, Don't Tell
- **Skill(s):** Emotional Fluidity; Active Listening; Status Modulation; Single-Partner Empathy & Mirroring; Offer Reception; Active Gifting
- **Technique(s):** Meisner Repetition; Emotional-echo drills; Yes, And… sentence games; Endowment-acceptance; Endowment-gifting drills
- **Focus:** connection

**Objective:** To develop deep interpersonal attunement, active listening, and emotional fluidity by practicing precise endowment-gifting and empathetic mirroring, while exploring how emotional shifts naturally modulate status.

## Setup
Players work in pairs. They stand or sit facing each other in a shared space. No props are required. The facilitator should prepare a list of nuanced, active emotional states (e.g., 'quietly resentful', 'breathlessly expectant', 'defensively proud') to assign privately.

## How to Play
1. Establish a neutral, grounded physical activity for the pair (e.g., folding laundry, waiting for a train) to anchor the scene.
2. The facilitator privately assigns an active, nuanced emotional state to Player A (the Canvas), who begins to subtly physicalize this state through posture, breath, and object work without naming it.
3. Player B (the Architect) closely observes Player A's physical cues and gifts a specific, contextual justification (an endowment) for why Player A feels this way, focusing on sensory details or shared history rather than labeling the emotion.
4. Player A accepts the gift by echoing a key word or physical gesture from Player B (a Meisner-style emotional echo), internalizes the justification, and allows their emotional state to shift or intensify.
5. Player A verbalizes the physical or practical implications of this new emotional level (e.g., 'My hands won't stop shaking' or 'I feel like I'm about to jump out of my skin') to show, not tell, their state.
6. Player B observes this shift, noting how the emotional change has altered Player A's relative status, and offers a new, deeper endowment that matches this status shift.
7. After three to four exchanges, the facilitator calls 'Switch.' The final emotional state of Player A becomes the starting seed for Player B, who now becomes the Canvas, while Player A becomes the Architect.

## Facilitation Notes
- Side-coaching cue: 'Don't name the feeling, paint the picture of why it exists.' This prevents players from taking the easy way out by labeling emotions.
- Encourage the Canvas to use Meisner-style repetition: if the Architect says 'You're gripping that cup tightly,' the Canvas can repeat 'Gripping it tightly...' while letting the physical action deepen their internal feeling.
- Pitfall: The Architect ignores the Canvas's physical offers and imposes a pre-planned narrative. Fix: Remind the Architect to look at their partner's eyes, breath, and hands before speaking.
- Remind players of Status Modulation: a shift from irritation to fear naturally lowers status. Encourage players to let their physical posture and vocal volume reflect these status shifts organically.

## Variations
- Silent Architecture: Run the entire exercise non-verbally, using only physical gestures, touch (with consent), and breath to gift and accept emotional states.
- Status Seesaw: Explicitly instruct the Architect to gift endowments that force the Canvas to shift from high status to low status, or vice versa, with each exchange.

## Debrief
- How did it feel to have your partner justify your internal state? Did it make you feel supported or restricted?
- What physical cues did you notice your partner picking up on that you didn't realize you were offering?
- How did the restriction of not naming the emotion force you to communicate more deeply?
- In what ways did your relative status shift as the emotions evolved?

## Safety & Inclusion
Ensure players establish physical boundaries before beginning, especially if using the non-verbal variation. Remind players that they have full agency over how intensely they embody an emotion, and they can de-escalate the emotional intensity at any point if it feels overwhelming.

## Why It Works
By stripping away the ability to label emotions, players must rely entirely on physical attunement and narrative justification. The Architect's role forces active gifting that makes the partner look brilliant, while the Canvas's role builds deep empathy and emotional fluidity through immediate acceptance. This collaborative loop builds a shared reality where status and emotion are organically linked.
