# Consent Sculpture Ensemble

> Co-create a physical, non-verbal vocabulary for boundaries and safety through collaborative, silent group tableaus.

## Overview
This workshop activity establishes a shared, group-specific physical vocabulary for safety and consent. Over three distinct phases, players collaboratively define key safety terms and then translate them into abstract, silent physical sculptures. This process moves safety from an intellectual concept into an embodied, shared physical language that empowers confident play.

## What It Trains
- **Domain:** D2 — The Partner
- **Principle(s):** Consent & Boundaries; Group Mind; Truth Over Pandering
- **Skill(s):** Single-Partner Empathy & Mirroring; Boundary Navigation; Peripheral Awareness; Stage Presence & Clarity
- **Technique(s):** Check-ins; Cut calls; Negotiating physical contact; Stage-picture exercises
- **Focus:** connection

**Objective:** To develop practical boundary navigation, single-partner empathy, and non-verbal communication skills by establishing a clear, physical safety container.

## Setup
An open room with space to move. A whiteboard or flipchart with markers is placed at the front of the room. No other props are required.

## How to Play
1. Step 1: Gather the group around the whiteboard and introduce the goal of co-creating a custom safety and consent lexicon specific to this ensemble.
2. Step 2: Write four core concepts on the board: Check-In, Cut/Stop, Boundary, and Physical Contact Negotiation.
3. Step 3: Facilitate a brief group discussion for each term to define what it looks and sounds like in action, writing concrete examples on the board.
4. Step 4: Divide the players into small teams of three to five and assign one of the defined safety concepts to each team.
5. Step 5: Give the teams five minutes to design a silent, abstract, multi-person physical tableau that represents their assigned concept without telling a narrative story.
6. Step 6: Have each team present their silent sculpture to the rest of the group, holding the physical shape for ten seconds.
7. Step 7: After holding the shape, the presenting team explains their physical choices, and the facilitator invites the audience to share their observations.

## Facilitation Notes
- Frame this activity as an empowering tool that unlocks bolder play, rather than a set of restrictive rules.
- Encourage players to be completely honest about their actual comfort levels during the definition phase, prioritizing truth over polite agreement.
- If a group starts acting out a narrative scene during the sculpture phase, coach them to strip away the story and focus purely on physical shapes, distance, and body language.
- Keep the initial brainstorming focused and moving quickly so that the group has ample time to physically build and discuss the sculptures.

## Variations
- Dynamic Sculptures: Allow the tableaus to have slow, repetitive movement or a single physical transition to show a boundary being crossed and restored.
- The Spectator's Touch: Allow audience members to step forward and gently adjust the sculpture's physical spacing to make the safety concept clearer.
- In-Scene Integration: Immediately follow the exercise with simple two-person scenes where players must actively use the physical check-ins and boundary signals developed in the sculptures.

## Debrief
- How did translating these abstract safety concepts into physical shapes change your understanding of them?
- What did you notice about the different ways people physically represent a boundary or a check-in?
- How does having a shared, physical vocabulary make you feel about taking creative risks in future scenes?
- Why is it important to prioritize honest personal boundaries over pleasing your partner or the audience?

## Safety & Inclusion
This exercise is highly sensitive as it directly addresses physical touch and personal boundaries. Ensure all participation in the physical sculptures is strictly opt-in, and explicitly state that players have absolute autonomy over how and where they are touched, or if they prefer to represent boundaries entirely without physical contact.

## Why It Works
By translating intellectual safety guidelines into physical, spatial relationships, this game builds muscle memory and visual cues that players can easily recognize in the heat of an improvisation. It bypasses the polite hesitation of yes-and dogma by establishing clear, physical protocols for boundary navigation, ultimately giving players the psychological safety needed to play with high commitment.
