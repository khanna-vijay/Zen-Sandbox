# Parallel Realities

<div class="game-hero-ph" markdown>🎨 *Infographic coming soon*</div>

> Weave multiple simultaneous storylines into a single, cohesive narrative tapestry across virtual breakout spaces.

## Overview
A virtual ensemble game where small groups simultaneously improvise distinct scenes in breakout rooms, all reacting to a single overarching prompt. Players periodically return to the main room to share concise summaries, then dive back in to cross-pollinate and synthesize their narratives into a unified, interconnected multiverse.

## What It Trains
- **Domain:** D4 — The Ensemble
- **Principle(s):** Group Mind; Serve the Story; Yes, And
- **Skill(s):** Thematic Synthesis; Active Listening; Narrative Architecture; World-Building
- **Technique(s):** Weave the threads; C.R.O.W. (Character, Relationship, Objective, Where); Callbacks & Mapping
- **Focus:** narrative

**Objective:** To develop thematic synthesis and group mind by training players to actively listen to external narratives and seamlessly weave disparate story threads into their own scene work.

## Setup
A virtual meeting platform with breakout room capabilities and gallery view enabled. No physical props or space required. The facilitator prepares a list of broad, evocative, open-ended narrative prompts (e.g., 'The day gravity fluctuated by ten percent' or 'An ancient, unidentifiable key is found in multiple locations').

## How to Play
1. Introduce the Premise: The facilitator presents a single, broad, overarching narrative prompt (the 'inciting incident') that will affect all parallel worlds simultaneously.
2. Divide into Breakout Rooms: Split the players into 2 to 4 breakout rooms (ideally 3-4 players per room) and instruct them to immediately begin a scene exploring how this prompt impacts their specific 'world.'
3. Improvise Round One: Players run their scenes in the breakout rooms for 3 to 4 minutes, establishing characters, a specific setting, and an immediate conflict stemming from the prompt.
4. Reconvene and Summarize: Bring all players back to the main gallery view. The facilitator calls on one representative from each group to deliver a strict 30-second summary of their world's characters, conflict, and latest development.
5. Active Listening: While each group shares, all other players must listen intently, tracking key narrative details, names, and thematic elements from the other worlds.
6. Re-assign or Deepen (Round Two): Send players back into breakout rooms. The facilitator can either keep the original groups to deepen their story using the new external knowledge, or shuffle the groups to force direct narrative cross-pollination.
7. Weave the Threads: In this second round, players must actively integrate at least one or two narrative elements, characters, or consequences heard from the other groups' summaries into their current scene.
8. Final Synthesis: Bring everyone back to the main room for a final round of summaries, highlighting how the separate worlds have begun to collide, echo, or influence one another.

## Facilitation Notes
- Keep Summaries Strict: Enforce the 30-second limit on summaries to prevent rambling and keep the energy high; use a visual timer or a gentle verbal cue.
- Crafting the Prompt: Ensure the initial prompt is broad enough to support multiple genres and interpretations (e.g., environmental changes, societal shifts, or mysterious discoveries) rather than a narrow, prescriptive event.
- Side-Coaching Integration: Remind players before Round Two that integration doesn't have to be literal; a character in World A could hear a radio broadcast about World B, or find an object left behind by a character from World C.
- Manage Platform Transitions: Keep transitions between the main room and breakout rooms swift. Broadcast time-remaining warnings to the breakout rooms so players can find a natural stopping point or cliffhanger.

## Variations
- The Butterfly Effect: A major event or decision made in Room 1 during the first round is broadcast by the facilitator as a mandatory disruption that immediately impacts all other rooms in Round 2.
- Character Migration: In Round 2, swap exactly one player from each room to another. That player must enter the new room as their original character, physically or narratively 'falling' into the parallel dimension.
- Genre Clash: Assign a different genre (e.g., sci-fi, film noir, soap opera, high fantasy) to each breakout room, forcing them to synthesize wildly different tones during the integration phase.

## Debrief
- How did hearing the other groups' summaries change your perspective on your own scene's world?
- What strategies did you use to naturally weave another group's narrative thread into your scene without derailing your own story?
- How did this exercise shift your focus from individual joke-making to collective, ensemble-wide world-building?

## Safety & Inclusion
Ensure players know they can pause or adjust any narrative elements that feel uncomfortable, especially when themes of global disruption or crisis are used as prompts. Encourage accessible, verbal-heavy world-building so players who cannot use video or have limited mobility can fully participate.

## Why It Works
By separating players and then forcing them to synthesize external information, the game breaks the insular nature of isolated scene work. It demands high-level active listening and cognitive flexibility, teaching players that their scene does not exist in a vacuum. This builds Group Mind across physical distances, showing how disparate narrative threads can be woven into a unified thematic tapestry.
