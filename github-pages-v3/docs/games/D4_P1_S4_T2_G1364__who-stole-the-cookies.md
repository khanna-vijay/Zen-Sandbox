# The Cookie Jar Rhythm

![The Cookie Jar Rhythm — infographic](img/D4_P1_S4_T2_G1364__who-stole-the-cookies.jpg){ .game-hero }

> A rhythmic name-passing game that builds group mind, active listening, and collective tempo.

## Overview
Players stand in a circle and establish a steady, collective beat. Through a call-and-response chant, they pass the accusation of a missing cookie from person to person without breaking the rhythm. It is a high-focus, playful warm-up that sharpens peripheral awareness and group timing.

## What It Trains

**Objective:** To develop group mind, active listening, and precise rhythmic timing by maintaining a shared tempo while dynamically passing focus.

## Setup
Have all players stand in a circle facing inward. No props or special materials are needed. Ensure everyone can see and hear each other clearly.

## How to Play
1. Establish a steady, collective rhythm. Have the entire group begin clapping their hands or patting their thighs in a simple four-beat pattern.
2. Once the rhythm is stable, the entire group chants the opening line in unison: 'Who stole the cookie from the cookie jar?'
3. The facilitator or a designated starting player immediately names another player in the circle on the next beat, chanting: '[Name] stole the cookie from the cookie jar!'
4. The named player must respond instantly in rhythm, saying: 'Who, me?'
5. The rest of the group responds in unison on the beat: 'Yes, you!'
6. The named player replies: 'Couldn't be!'
7. The group chants back: 'Then who?'
8. The named player immediately passes the accusation to a new player by chanting: '[New Name] stole the cookie from the cookie jar!'
9. The cycle repeats seamlessly with the newly named player, keeping the rhythm unbroken.

## Facilitation Notes
- Coaching cue: 'Keep the beat steady! Don't let the rhythm speed up or slow down when names are called.'
- Pitfall: Players hesitate when named, causing the rhythm to stutter. Fix: Encourage players to prioritize the beat over perfection; making a rhythmic mistake is fine, just keep the pulse going.
- Coaching cue: 'Use eye contact when accusing someone to help them prepare for their turn.'
- Pitfall: The group chant drowns out the individual's response. Fix: Remind the group to keep their unison chant at a supportive volume so the solo player can be heard.

## Variations
- Double Time: Gradually increase the tempo of the clapping and chanting to challenge the group's focus and reaction times.
- Silent Rhythm: Perform the game without clapping, relying entirely on an internal, felt pulse and the spoken rhythm of the words.
- Character Accusations: Players must adopt a specific emotion or character voice (e.g., melodramatic, suspicious, robotic) when accusing or defending themselves, while still maintaining the rhythm.

## Debrief
- How did it feel when the rhythm faltered, and how did we collectively recover it?
- What did you have to pay attention to in order to keep the timing seamless?
- How does maintaining a shared physical rhythm help us connect as an ensemble?

## Safety & Inclusion
Ensure that names are pronounced correctly and that players use the names others have asked to be called. If physical clapping or patting is difficult for any participant, they can nod, blink, or tap a foot to keep the rhythm.

## Why It Works
The game relies on a shared physical and auditory pulse, forcing players to subvert their individual timing to the collective tempo. By combining active listening with immediate vocal response, it builds a state of 'group mind' where the ensemble functions as a single, synchronized organism.
