# Follow the Leaver

<div class="game-hero-ph" markdown>🎨 *Infographic coming soon*</div>

> Edit scenes dynamically by tracking a single departing character into their next narrative world.

## Overview
An ensemble-driven long-form format where scenes transition whenever a character exits the stage. The audience or offstage players call out to follow the departing character, instantly clearing the current stage and launching a brand-new scene centered on that character's next destination. It creates a chain of interconnected vignettes that explore the hidden lives of minor and major characters alike.

## What It Trains
- **Domain:** D4 — The Ensemble
- **Principle(s):** Serve the Piece; Serve the Story; The Audience Is the Final Scene Partner
- **Skill(s):** Pacing & Rhythm; Format Literacy; Narrative Architecture; Audience-Energy Management
- **Technique(s):** Edits (Sweep, Tag-Out, Sound/Light); Longform vs. shortform mechanics
- **Focus:** narrative

**Objective:** To master organic editing, narrative pacing, and seamless transitions by using character exits as the primary engine for scene changes.

## Setup
An open performance space with a clear stage area and an offstage/backline area for waiting players. An audience (or the rest of the workshop group acting as the audience) sits facing the stage.

## How to Play
1. Begin with two or more players on stage establishing a grounded, character-driven scene.
2. Each player on stage must fully commit to a distinct character, establishing clear relationships and a specific setting.
3. At any point during the scene, a character may make a justified exit from the space (e.g., leaving an office, walking out of a restaurant, storming out of a room).
4. As the character exits, the audience or offstage ensemble yells 'Follow the leaver!' to trigger the edit.
5. Upon hearing the edit call, all other players remaining on stage must immediately sweep off, leaving only the departing character in transition.
6. The departing character enters a new location, maintaining their established identity, emotional state, and history.
7. Offstage players immediately enter this new space as brand-new characters to initiate a fresh storyline with the traveling character.
8. Repeat this cycle of character-driven exits and edits, creating a continuous, linear chain of scenes that explore different facets of the world.

## Facilitation Notes
- Coaching Cue: 'Justify the exit!' Remind players that leaving a scene should feel natural to the narrative, not just a mechanical trigger.
- Pitfall: Players staying on stage too long out of fear of ending the scene. Fix: Encourage bold, early exits to keep the rhythm brisk and energetic.
- Coaching Cue: 'New characters, new energy!' When entering to support the 'leaver', bring in high-contrast characters to immediately define the new environment.
- Pitfall: The 'leaver' changing their character traits in the new scene. Fix: Remind them that the joy of this format is seeing the same character react to completely different situations.

## Variations
- Internal Edits: Instead of the audience calling 'Follow the leaver', the offstage players or the exiting player themselves initiate the transition seamlessly through physical movement.
- The Return Loop: After 4-5 transitions, the final 'leaver' exits and walks back into the very first scene, closing the narrative loop.
- Tag-Out Fusion: Allow players to use standard tag-outs to explore sub-plots, but use 'Follow the Leaver' as the primary macro-edit to shift locations.

## Debrief
- How did knowing you could be 'followed' change how you played your character's motivations and exits?
- What did you notice about the pacing of the edits? When did an exit feel most satisfying?
- How does this format help us practice 'serving the piece' rather than just holding onto our own scene ideas?

## Safety & Inclusion
Ensure the physical space is clear of obstacles, as players will be entering and exiting the stage rapidly. Encourage players to call out physical movements if lighting is dim or if anyone has mobility considerations.

## Why It Works
This game works because it gamifies the edit, removing the hesitation often associated with long-form transitions. By tying the edit directly to a character's physical action (leaving), it teaches players to listen with their eyes and respond instantly to narrative momentum, building a shared sense of rhythm and ensemble trust.
