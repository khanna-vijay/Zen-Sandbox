# Make It Personal

<div class="game-hero-ph" markdown>🎨 *Infographic coming soon*</div>

> Transform dry, objective facts into high-stakes personal drama through instant emotional justification.

## Overview
A rapid-fire pairing drill where players practice receiving dry, objective facts and instantly treating them as deeply personal, high-stakes revelations. By reacting emotionally first and justifying second, players learn to accept any offer as a direct endowment. This shifts scenes from dry information exchanges into rich, character-driven relationships.

## What It Trains
- **Domain:** D2 — The Partner
- **Principle(s):** Yes, And; Base Reality First
- **Skill(s):** Offer Reception; Justification; Emotional Fluidity; Stakes / The 'Want'
- **Technique(s):** Endowment-acceptance; Justify the absurd
- **Focus:** skill_drill

**Objective:** To develop immediate offer reception and endowment-acceptance. Players practice treating neutral external information as a high-stakes personal offer, building skills in justification, emotional fluidity, and establishing a strong personal 'want'.

## Setup
Divide the group into two equal lines facing each other in the center of the room, creating pairs. Line A will act as the 'Fact Givers' and Line B as the 'Personalizers'. No props or special staging are required, just enough space for pairs to hear each other clearly.

## How to Play
1. Instruct Line A to think of a simple, objective, and completely neutral fact that has nothing to do with themselves or their partner (e.g., 'The post office closes at five' or 'Bananas contain potassium').
2. Explain that Line A will deliver their neutral fact to their partner in Line B with a neutral, matter-of-fact delivery.
3. Instruct Line B that their job is to instantly receive this fact as if it is a deeply personal, high-stakes statement directed at them, their character, or their relationship.
4. Direct Line B to react with an immediate, strong emotional choice (e.g., joy, devastation, guilt, anger) before they even know why they are reacting.
5. Have Line B immediately justify their emotional reaction with a spoken response that connects the neutral fact to their personal life or relationship (e.g., 'How dare you bring up bananas after what happened at the zoo!').
6. Run the drill down the line, allowing each pair to execute one exchange, then have the entire group rotate so everyone gets a new partner.
7. After a few rounds, swap the roles of the lines so Line B becomes the Fact Givers and Line A becomes the Personalizers.

## Facilitation Notes
- Side-coach players to 'react first, think second.' If a player hesitates, call out an emotion like 'Grief!' or 'Ecstasy!' to bypass their analytical brain.
- Pitfall: Players in Line B sometimes respond with a clever joke or a detached observation instead of making it personal. Fix: Remind them to use 'I', 'me', or 'we' in their very first sentence.
- Encourage Fact Givers to keep their facts truly mundane. If they offer a high-drama fact, it robs the Personalizer of the work of transforming it.
- Remind players that 'personal' doesn't have to mean tragic; a fact can be personally thrilling, insulting, or a long-awaited relief.

## Variations
- Continuous Scene: Instead of a single exchange, let the pair continue the scene for 3-4 lines, maintaining the high personal stakes established by the initial reaction.
- The Worst Person: Instruct the Personalizer to actively choose to be the absolute worst possible person to hear that specific fact, raising the immediate conflict.
- Physical Endowment: The Fact Giver performs a simple, neutral physical action (like tying a shoe or checking a watch), and the Personalizer must interpret and react to it as a deeply personal gesture.

## Debrief
- How did reacting emotionally before figuring out the reason help you justify the offer?
- What happened to the energy of the interaction when a dry fact was treated as a high-stakes personal attack or gift?
- How does this drill help us avoid 'talking heads' scenes where characters just exchange information?

## Safety & Inclusion
Because this game invites high emotional stakes, remind players that 'personal' refers to their fictional characters, not their actual private lives. Encourage players to play with a wide range of emotions (like absurd joy or professional outrage) rather than defaulting solely to heavy trauma or genuine distress.

## Why It Works
This game works because it bypasses the analytical mind's tendency to negotiate or reject dry offers. By forcing an immediate emotional reaction, the player is compelled to 'yes-and' the reality of the offer through instant justification. It teaches that any line of dialogue, no matter how mundane, can be the foundation of a high-stakes relationship if we choose to let it affect us.
