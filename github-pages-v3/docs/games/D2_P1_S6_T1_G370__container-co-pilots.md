# Container Co-Pilots

![Container Co-Pilots — infographic](img/D2_P1_S6_T1_G370__container-co-pilots.jpg){ .game-hero }

> Navigate boundaries collectively using active observation lenses and shared, low-stakes check-ins.

## Overview
A structured exercise where two active players improvise a scene while the remaining ensemble members act as supportive observers assigned to specific safety lenses. By using subtle physical cues to signal rising intensity or proximity, the group practices pausing for brief, non-judgmental check-ins. This shifts the responsibility of maintaining a safe creative space from the individual to the entire team.

## What It Trains
- **Domain:** D2 — The Partner
- **Principle(s):** Consent & Boundaries; Truth Over Pandering
- **Skill(s):** Boundary Navigation; Peripheral Awareness
- **Technique(s):** Check-ins; Cut calls; Negotiating physical contact
- **Focus:** connection

**Objective:** To build a shared vocabulary for dynamic consent, practice proactive boundary navigation, and prioritize personal comfort over narrative momentum (Truth Over Pandering).

## Setup
Players stand or sit in a semi-circle facing a designated performance space. No props are required. The facilitator assigns two players to be the active performers ('Scene Pilots') and divides the remaining players ('Co-Pilots') into specific observational roles, each focusing on a different lens: Physical Proximity, Emotional Escalation, Power Dynamics, or Non-Verbal Comfort Cues.

## How to Play
1. Assign two players to enter the scene as 'Scene Pilots' and give them a simple, low-stakes relationship prompt (e.g., two neighbors discussing a shared fence).
2. Assign each remaining player in the audience a specific 'lens' to monitor, such as physical distance, emotional volume, or subtle physical signs of tension.
3. Establish a subtle, non-disruptive physical gesture (like touching one's collarbone or tapping a chin) that Co-Pilots will use to signal when a scene is approaching a boundary.
4. Begin the scene; the Scene Pilots improvise naturally while the Co-Pilots watch intently through their assigned lenses.
5. If a Co-Pilot notices the scene approaching a boundary or sees a performer showing subtle tension, they quietly hold their designated physical gesture.
6. If two or more Co-Pilots hold their gesture, or if either active performer feels a boundary approaching, anyone may call a soft pause by saying 'Container Check-in'.
7. During the check-in, the scene pauses instantly, and the Co-Pilots who flagged share brief, objective observations based strictly on their lens (e.g., 'I noticed the physical distance closed to under a foot').
8. The active performers then check in with each other honestly, stating their actual comfort levels and negotiating how to adjust the scene's trajectory (e.g., 'I'd like to keep some physical distance, but let's keep the emotional intensity').
9. Once a consensual path is agreed upon, the performers resume the scene from where they paused, integrating the new boundary.
10. Remind players that a standard 'Cut' call remains available to anyone at any time for an immediate, unquestioned stop if a hard boundary is crossed.

## Facilitation Notes
- Ensure Co-Pilot feedback is strictly descriptive and objective (e.g., 'I saw shoulders tense' rather than 'You made them uncomfortable').
- Actively protect 'Truth Over Pandering' by validating performers who choose to back away or lower the intensity during a check-in.
- Keep the check-ins rapid and clinical; do not let them turn into long creative discussions or notes sessions.
- If the non-verbal gestures are being missed, the facilitator can gently call out 'I see two flags' to prompt the check-in.

## Variations
- Silent Adjustments: Run the game where the non-verbal flag prompts the active players to self-adjust their physical distance or volume in real-time without pausing the scene.
- Blind Lenses: Do not announce which Co-Pilot has which lens, forcing the performers to simply trust that the ensemble is holding space for them holistically.

## Debrief
- How did it feel to have the audience actively sharing the responsibility of monitoring boundaries?
- Did the check-in make it easier to speak your truth instead of pandering to the scene's momentum?
- How did observing through a single, specific lens change your awareness of your partners' physical and emotional states?
- How can we carry this collective vigilance into our regular, unpaused scene work?

## Safety & Inclusion
This exercise is highly safety-sensitive. Establish a clear, pre-discussed baseline of physical touch boundaries before starting. Ensure players know they have absolute agency to pause or stop the scene at any point without needing to justify their decision to the group.

## Why It Works
By distributing the cognitive load of boundary monitoring across the entire ensemble, performers are freed from the pressure of hyper-vigilance. The structured, objective feedback format removes shame from boundary setting, transforming consent from a defensive, reactive measure into a collaborative, creative tool.
