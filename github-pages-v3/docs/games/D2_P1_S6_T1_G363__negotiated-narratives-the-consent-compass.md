# The Consent Compass

<div class="game-hero-ph" markdown>🎨 *Infographic coming soon*</div>

> Navigate physical, emotional, and thematic boundaries in real-time through structured, active verbal check-ins.

## Overview
A structured scene-work exercise where players explicitly pause the action to negotiate boundaries before introducing physical touch, high-intensity emotions, or sensitive themes. By integrating real-time, out-of-character check-ins directly into the performance, players learn to prioritize personal comfort and mutual safety over theatrical momentum.

## What It Trains
- **Domain:** D2 — The Partner
- **Principle(s):** Consent & Boundaries; Yes, And; Truth Over Pandering
- **Skill(s):** Boundary Navigation; Offer Reception; Active Listening
- **Technique(s):** Check-ins; Cut calls; Negotiating physical contact; Yes, And… sentence games
- **Focus:** connection

**Objective:** To build practical skills in boundary navigation, active listening, and honest offer reception, ensuring players can confidently advocate for their own comfort while supporting their partner's boundaries.

## Setup
A moderate open space. Players stand in a circle or semi-circle. Two players step into the center to perform a scene, while the remaining players act as supportive observers. No props or materials are required.

## How to Play
1. Begin a standard two-person scene based on a simple relationship and location prompt provided by the facilitator.
2. Before initiating any physical contact, escalating to an intense emotional state (like extreme anger or intimacy), or introducing a sensitive thematic topic, you must pause the scene by saying 'Consent Compass!'
3. Clearly state your theatrical intention to your partner out-of-character (e.g., 'I would like to grab your hand to comfort you' or 'I want to escalate my character's anger and stand very close to you').
4. The receiving partner must immediately respond out-of-character using one of three options: 'Yes, And' (full agreement), 'Yes, But' (conditional agreement with a modified boundary), or 'No, Thanks' (declining the offer entirely).
5. If the partner responds with 'Yes, And', execute the action as proposed and seamlessly transition back into the scene.
6. If the partner responds with 'Yes, But', negotiate the modified boundary (e.g., 'You can't grab my hand, but you can pat my shoulder') and continue the scene respecting this new limit.
7. If the partner responds with 'No, Thanks', immediately accept the boundary without requiring any justification, redirect the narrative focus, and continue the scene along a different path.
8. At any point during the scene, any player can call 'Cut!' to immediately halt the action if they feel genuine discomfort, bypassing all negotiation to prioritize safety.

## Facilitation Notes
- Normalize the Slowdown: Remind players that pausing the scene to check in is the primary goal of the exercise. Speed and narrative flow are secondary to building conscious competence in boundary negotiation.
- Differentiate Character vs. Player: Emphasize that 'Yes, But' and 'No, Thanks' responses represent the actual player's comfort level, which always overrides what their character might logically agree to.
- Celebrate the 'No': Actively praise players who set firm boundaries or call 'Cut!'. Frame these choices as acts of strength and high-level partner support rather than scene-killing mistakes.
- Watch for Hesitation: If a player hesitates before agreeing to a proposal, gently pause the scene and ask, 'Are you saying yes for the scene, or does this genuinely feel comfortable for you?' to combat the urge to pander.

## Variations
- Silent Signals: For advanced players, replace the verbal 'Consent Compass' cue with a specific non-verbal gesture (like an open palm) to initiate the verbal negotiation, making the transition slightly more seamless.
- Thematic Focus: Restrict the exercise specifically to practicing physical touch boundaries in one round, and then switch to practicing high-stakes emotional boundaries in the next.

## Debrief
- How did it feel to explicitly ask for permission before making a physical or emotional leap in the scene?
- For those who set boundaries, did you feel any internal pressure to 'pander' to the scene, and how did you overcome it?
- How did knowing you had the power to say 'No' or call 'Cut' affect your overall sense of freedom and playfulness during the scene?
- In what ways can we carry this practice of active, verbal check-ins into our regular, unconstrained improv scenes?

## Safety & Inclusion
This exercise is highly safety-sensitive. Before starting, establish a collective agreement that personal boundaries are absolute and require no explanation. Ensure players understand that calling 'Cut!' is a tool for self-care and will be met with immediate support and zero judgment. If a player calls 'Cut!', the facilitator should quietly check in with them privately to ensure they feel safe before resuming group play.

## Why It Works
This game works because it dismantles the toxic misconception that 'Yes, And' requires blind compliance. By gamifying the negotiation process, it provides a low-stakes, structured vocabulary ('Yes, But' and 'No, Thanks') that transforms boundary setting from a potential conflict into a collaborative, creative act of mutual care.
