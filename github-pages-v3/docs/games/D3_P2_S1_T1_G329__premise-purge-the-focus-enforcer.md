# Premise Purge

![Premise Purge — infographic](img/D3_P2_S1_T1_G329__premise-purge-the-focus-enforcer.jpg){ .game-hero }

> Strip away narrative clutter to isolate, commit to, and relentlessly escalate a single comedic game.

## Overview
A structured scene-work drill designed to eliminate narrative wandering and train hyper-focused commitment. Players begin with an open-ended scene to discover an unusual pattern, pause to explicitly define that core dynamic, and then restart the scene with a mandate to play only that game. The facilitator actively polices the boundaries of the scene, calling out distractions to keep the focus razor-sharp.

## What It Trains
- **Domain:** D3 — The Scene
- **Principle(s):** Base Reality First; Yes, And; The First Thought Is a Gift
- **Skill(s):** Game Identification; Heightening & Exploration; Justification; Active Listening; Offer Reception; Unfiltered Spontaneity
- **Technique(s):** Finding & Playing the Game; If this is true, what else is true?; The 'ladder' (escalating beats); Exploring the 'why'; Justify the absurd
- **Focus:** skill_drill

**Objective:** To develop the ability to quickly identify a scene's core comedic or thematic engine (the 'game') and build the discipline to explore and heighten that single pattern while actively filtering out distracting, unrelated narrative threads.

## Setup
An open performance space. No props or special materials are required. The group (ideally 6 to 12 players) sits as an audience, with two players stepping up to the stage. The facilitator is positioned where they can easily observe and call out cues.

## How to Play
1. Select two players to step onto the stage and provide them with a simple, open-ended situational suggestion that establishes a basic environment without dictating a plot.
2. Instruct the players to begin an open scene (the Discovery Phase) for approximately two minutes, focusing on establishing a solid base reality, exploring characters, and letting unusual behaviors or patterns naturally emerge.
3. Once a clear, repeatable pattern or unusual dynamic is established, call out 'PURGE!' to pause the scene.
4. Lead a rapid 30-to-60-second discussion with the players to explicitly define the single most compelling 'game' or pattern that emerged.
5. Direct the players to reset to their starting positions and restart the scene from the very beginning.
6. In this second run, players must immediately establish the base reality and launch directly into the identified game, dedicating every line, reaction, and physical choice to heightening this single premise.
7. Instruct players to actively ignore or reframe any off-game details from the first run, filtering out anything that does not serve the chosen dynamic.
8. If the scene begins to drift, introduce new unrelated conflicts, or lose focus, call out 'TOO WIDE!' to signal the players to immediately pivot their next line back to the core game.
9. End the scene once the game has been heightened to a satisfying peak or fully explored.

## Facilitation Notes
- When calling 'PURGE!', ensure you have a clearly articulable game in mind. A good game is a repeatable pattern of behavior or a specific, unusual point of view, not just a plot point.
- Use the 'TOO WIDE!' intervention supportively but firmly. It is not a punishment, but a real-time boundary marker to help players recognize when they are default-building plot instead of heightening the game.
- If players struggle to find a game during the pause, ask them: 'What was the first unusual thing that happened, and how did the other person react to it?'
- Encourage the non-active partner (the 'normal' character) to justify the active partner's unusual behavior, which grounds the scene in a believable base reality and keeps the game sustainable.

## Variations
- Silent Purge: Instead of pausing for a discussion, the facilitator calls out 'PURGE!' and names the game aloud, and the players must immediately restart and adapt without a verbal huddle.
- Tag-In Purge: When 'TOO WIDE!' is called, an audience member can tag in for one of the players to demonstrate how to pivot the scene back to the core game.
- Three-Player Purge: Run the exercise with three players, where two players establish the core game and the third player enters later to either feed the game or act as the voice of reason.

## Debrief
- How did it feel to restart the scene with a singular, pre-determined focus compared to improvising completely in the dark?
- What strategies did you use to reframe unexpected or 'off-game' offers so they fit the established premise?
- How does establishing a strong, believable base reality first make it easier to identify and play the game?

## Safety & Inclusion
Ensure that the 'TOO WIDE!' intervention is framed as a collaborative coaching tool rather than a critique of player capability. If players feel self-conscious about being interrupted, remind the group that boundaries actually free up creativity by reducing cognitive load.

## Why It Works
By separating the discovery of a game from its execution, this game lowers the cognitive load of trying to find and play a game simultaneously. The 'TOO WIDE!' mechanism provides immediate, real-time feedback, training the brain to recognize narrative drift instantly. This builds muscle memory for 'if this is true, what else is true?' and reinforces the value of a strong base reality as the launchpad for comedic escalation.
