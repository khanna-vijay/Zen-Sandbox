# Vector Tag

![Vector Tag — infographic](img/D4_P1_S1_T2_G1352__virus.jpg){ .game-hero }

> Pass the transmission source to corner and tag the remaining healthy players in real-time.

## Overview
A high-energy, physical warm-up where a group of infected players attempts to tag the remaining healthy players. The catch is that only the player physically holding a plastic bottle can tag someone, forcing the chasing team to pass the bottle and coordinate their movements silently.

## What It Trains
- **Domain:** D4 — The Ensemble
- **Principle(s):** Group Mind; Commit 100%
- **Skill(s):** Peripheral Awareness; Physicality & Space Work
- **Technique(s):** Thread-tracking drills
- **Focus:** connection

**Objective:** Develops peripheral awareness, group mind, and spatial tracking. Players must monitor multiple moving targets and teammates simultaneously, practicing non-verbal coordination under high-energy conditions.

## Setup
A large, open room clear of obstacles. One empty, clean plastic bottle (2-liter or 20-ounce soft drink bottle). All players scatter across the space.

## How to Play
1. Designate one player to start as the Vector and hand them the plastic bottle; all other players are Healthy.
2. On the facilitator's signal, the Healthy players run or walk quickly to avoid being tagged, while the Vector attempts to tag them with their free hand.
3. When a Healthy player is tagged, they immediately join the Vector team as an active chaser.
4. Only the player currently holding the plastic bottle has the power to tag and convert Healthy players.
5. To tag new players, the Vector team must pass the bottle hand-to-hand among themselves to trap and tag the remaining Healthy players.
6. Throwing the bottle is strictly prohibited; all passes must be direct hand-to-hand handoffs, requiring close physical proximity and coordination.
7. The game continues at high energy until every player has been tagged and joined the Vector team.

## Facilitation Notes
- Encourage eye contact and non-verbal cues among the Vector team to coordinate passes without shouting.
- Pitfall: Players throwing the bottle. Fix: Remind them that throwing is a safety hazard and invalidates the tag; the bottle must be handed off.
- Side-coaching: Track the bottle! Healthy players, keep your eyes on the vector. Vectors, cut off their escape routes!
- Ensure players are moving at a safe speed for the space, adjusting to fast-walking if the room is small or slippery.

## Variations
- Silent Vector: The entire game must be played in absolute silence, forcing pure visual connection and spatial awareness.
- Multiple Vectors: Introduce a second plastic bottle once the group size or skill level increases, requiring players to track two moving threads of infection.
- Slow-Motion Transmission: Play the entire game in exaggerated slow motion, emphasizing physical control, balance, and deliberate spatial strategy.

## Debrief
- How did your focus shift once you transitioned from being a runner to being part of the chasing team?
- What non-verbal signals did you use to coordinate passes with your teammates?
- How did tracking the physical bottle help you anticipate where the danger or opportunity was?

## Safety & Inclusion
Establish a no-running or fast-walking only rule if the floor is slick or the space is tight. Ensure tags are light taps on the shoulder or back, never slaps or shoves. Offer a non-running role (like a stationary obstacle or boundary referee) for players with mobility limitations.

## Why It Works
This game acts as a physical thread-tracking drill. Because the tagging power is tied to a single, moving object, players cannot rely on individual effort; they must develop a collective group mind to corner targets. It forces players to expand their peripheral vision to track the bottle, their teammates, and their targets all at once.
