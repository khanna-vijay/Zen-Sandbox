# Split Focus

![Split Focus — infographic](img/D4_P2_S4_T1_G1296__split-focus.jpg){ .game-hero }

> Master the art of organic focus shifts and seamless scene transitions across the stage.

## Overview
Split Focus is an ensemble-building drill where two or more scenes share the stage simultaneously. Players must use heightened peripheral awareness to pass the focus back and forth without verbal cues, creating a rhythmic, cinematic flow. The experience challenges players to balance active performance with supportive silence.

## What It Trains
- **Domain:** D4 — The Ensemble
- **Principle(s):** Follow the Follower; Serve the Piece; Serve the Story
- **Skill(s):** Pacing & Rhythm; Peripheral Awareness; Stage Presence & Clarity
- **Technique(s):** Edits (Sweep, Tag-Out, Sound/Light); Thread-tracking drills; Make the choice readable
- **Focus:** skill_drill

**Objective:** Develops ensemble-wide peripheral awareness, intuitive pacing, and the ability to hand off or take focus organically (editing/transitioning) by reading the room and following the follower.

## Setup
Define two distinct performance zones on stage (e.g., Stage Left and Stage Right). The remaining players sit or stand in the audience area as an active support pool. No props or special materials are required.

## How to Play
1. Step 1: Assign two players to Zone A (Stage Left) and two players to Zone B (Stage Right).
2. Step 2: Give a simple suggestion to start the scene in Zone A, while the players in Zone B stand frozen or in low-energy, silent background action.
3. Step 3: Zone A begins their scene, establishing characters and a basic platform.
4. Step 4: Zone B must watch for an organic moment to take focus—either by starting their own dialogue, increasing their physical energy, or reacting to a natural pause in Zone A.
5. Step 5: The moment Zone B takes focus, Zone A must immediately yield, transitioning into a freeze, slow-motion, or silent background activity.
6. Step 6: The focus must pass back and forth multiple times, driven entirely by the players' mutual awareness of pacing, rhythm, and narrative tension.
7. Step 7: Introduce the 'Organic Edit': If a scene in one zone reaches a natural conclusion, a player from the off-stage pool can sweep or tag-out that zone to start a new scene, while the other zone continues to hold or react.

## Facilitation Notes
- Coaching cue: 'Feel the breath of the other scene. Don't just wait for them to stop talking; look for the high point of their tension to take over.'
- Pitfall: Stealing focus too early or interrupting a crucial narrative moment. Fix: Coach players to 'serve the piece' by actively listening to the other scene even while frozen, waiting for a natural dip or a cliffhanger.
- Coaching cue: 'Make your physical freezes active. Your stillness helps the audience know where to look.'
- Pitfall: Both scenes talking at once (clashing). Fix: Remind players that only one audio track can run at a time. If you hear the other side speak, instantly mute yours.

## Variations
- Three-Way Split: Add a third zone (Stage Center) to increase the complexity of focus management.
- Thematic Threads: The two scenes must be thematic mirrors or contrast each other (e.g., a high-stress corporate boardroom vs. a relaxed fishing trip).
- Audio-Only Overlaps: Allow the audio of one scene to bleed into the physical action of the other, creating a split-screen cinematic effect.

## Debrief
- How did you know it was the right moment to take the focus away from the other scene?
- What did it feel like to yield focus? How did you keep your character active while silent?
- How does this exercise help us make cleaner, more organic edits in a long-form set?

## Safety & Inclusion
Ensure physical freezes are comfortable and sustainable; players with physical limitations should adapt their 'frozen' states to comfortable seated or standing postures.

## Why It Works
It forces players to look outward rather than inward. By practicing 'follow the follower,' players learn that they do not control the stage alone; the rhythm of the entire piece is a collaborative dance of volume, movement, and silence.
