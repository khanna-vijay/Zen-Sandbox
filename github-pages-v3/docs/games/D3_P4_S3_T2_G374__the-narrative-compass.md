# The Narrative Compass

<div class="game-hero-ph" markdown>🎨 *Infographic coming soon*</div>

> Steer scenes through sudden narrative shifts by instantly justifying and integrating dramatic plot complications.

## Overview
Two players establish a stable base reality before a facilitator periodically injects disruptive plot prompts from a deck of physical cards. Players must immediately accept, justify, and build upon these narrative shifts, transforming sudden complications into the logical engine of their story.

## What It Trains
- **Domain:** D3 — The Scene
- **Principle(s):** Yes, And; Base Reality First; Serve the Story
- **Skill(s):** Offer Reception; Game Identification; Narrative Architecture; Stakes / The 'Want'; World-Building; Justification
- **Technique(s):** Endowment-acceptance; If this is true, what else is true?; Platform/Tilt; C.R.O.W. (Character, Relationship, Objective, Where); Justify the absurd
- **Focus:** narrative

**Objective:** To master the Platform/Tilt technique by establishing a solid base reality and seamlessly integrating sudden, high-stakes narrative disruptions without breaking character or story logic.

## Setup
An open performance space for two players. The facilitator stands nearby holding a prepared deck of 'Narrative Compass' cards, each containing a distinct narrative shift (e.g., a revealed secret, a sudden environmental threat, or a complete shift in relationship dynamics). The remaining participants observe as active listeners.

## How to Play
1. Select two players to take the stage and obtain a simple, non-transactional suggestion to initiate the scene.
2. The players begin the scene by establishing a clear Platform (C.R.O.W.): defining who they are, their relationship, their physical environment, and their immediate, low-stakes objectives.
3. Once a stable base reality is established (typically 45 to 60 seconds in), the facilitator calls out 'Compass!' and reads aloud one card from the Narrative Compass deck.
4. The active players must immediately accept the prompt as absolute truth, seamlessly integrating it into the scene's reality without pausing or breaking character.
5. Players must immediately justify the prompt, explaining why this new development is happening and how it connects logically to what was previously established.
6. Apply the principle of 'If this is true, what else is true?' to explore the emotional and physical consequences of the new development, letting it alter character motivations and elevate the stakes.
7. The facilitator repeats the 'Compass!' call 2 to 3 more times throughout the scene, layering new narrative demands to build a progressive, multi-act story arc.
8. Conclude the scene once the final prompt has been fully integrated and resolved, or when the characters reach a natural narrative climax.

## Facilitation Notes
- Timing the Tilt: Do not introduce the first Compass card too early. Ensure the players have built a solid, believable platform first; otherwise, the prompt will feel like a random joke rather than a meaningful narrative shift.
- Avoid 'Justifying Away': Watch out for players who acknowledge the prompt but immediately dismiss its impact (e.g., 'Yes, there is a bomb, but I already defused it'). Side-coach them to let the complication land emotionally and physically.
- The Power of 'Why': If players struggle to integrate a card, side-coach with: 'How does this make sense based on what we already know about your relationship?' to prompt active justification.
- Vary the Prompts: Ensure your deck contains a mix of internal shifts (secrets, relationship changes) and external shifts (environmental hazards, sudden tasks) to test different narrative muscles.

## Variations
- Silent Compass: Instead of reading the card aloud, the facilitator hands the card to one specific player, who must introduce the narrative shift through their own dialogue and actions, forcing the other player to discover it organically.
- Audience Compass: The audience holds the deck of cards, and a designated audience member yells 'Compass!' and reads a card when they feel the scene's momentum plateauing.
- The Domino Chain: When a Compass card is read, the off-stage players must instantly physicalize a background element or minor character mentioned in the prompt, expanding the world-building.

## Debrief
- How did establishing a strong base reality at the start make it easier (or harder) to justify the sudden narrative shifts?
- What was the difference between simply acknowledging a prompt and truly letting it change your character's core objective?
- How did we use the 'If this is true, what else is true?' rule to make the unexpected plot points feel inevitable rather than random?

## Safety & Inclusion
Because Compass cards can introduce sudden high-stakes scenarios, establish a 'red card' or 'skip' boundary beforehand. If a drawn card touches on a sensitive theme or personal boundary for any player, they can simply call 'Pass' or wave their hand, and the facilitator will immediately draw a different card without question.

## Why It Works
This game acts as a narrative engine by separating the generation of plot complications from the execution of the scene. By outsourcing the 'tilt' to the cards, players are freed from the pressure of inventing plot twists and can focus entirely on the high-level skills of justification, emotional reaction, and structural adaptation. This builds the muscle of treating every unexpected offer as a gift that serves the larger story.
