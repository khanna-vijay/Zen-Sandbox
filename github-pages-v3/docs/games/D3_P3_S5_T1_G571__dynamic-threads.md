# Relational Threads

<div class="game-hero-ph" markdown>🎨 *Infographic coming soon*</div>

> Launch scenes instantly with high-stakes, pre-declared relationship dynamics that drive immediate narrative action.

## Overview
Relational Threads is an active scene-work exercise where players establish complex, emotionally charged relationship dynamics before the first line is spoken. By declaring these relational 'threads' aloud, players enter the scene with built-in history, subtext, and urgent personal objectives. The resulting scene bypasses polite exposition, starting immediately in the thick of a pre-existing, high-stakes conflict.

## What It Trains
- **Domain:** D3 — The Scene
- **Principle(s):** Start in the Middle; Show, Don't Tell; Yes, And; Make Your Partner a Genius
- **Skill(s):** World-Building; Stakes / The 'Want'; Game Identification; Justification; Offer Reception; Active Gifting
- **Technique(s):** C.R.O.W. (Character, Relationship, Objective, Where); What do they stand to lose? reps; If this is true, what else is true?; Endowment-gifting drills; Endowment-acceptance
- **Focus:** narrative

**Objective:** To develop the ability to start scenes in the middle by establishing immediate C.R.O.W. foundations, specifically focusing on deep relationship dynamics and clear character objectives to drive narrative progression.

## Setup
Two to three players stand on stage in front of an audience. No props or special staging are required. The facilitator prepares to solicit an abstract mood from the audience and provide a simple, open-ended situational prompt.

## How to Play
1. Ask the audience for an abstract mood or concept (e.g., 'Resentment', 'Anticipation', or 'Obligation') to serve as the emotional undercurrent of the scene.
2. Before the scene begins, each player chooses one other player on stage and silently formulates a complex, emotionally charged relationship dynamic phrased as an adjective-noun combination (e.g., 'I am your overprotective shadow' or 'I am your unearned second chance').
3. Each player takes turns verbally declaring their chosen dynamic aloud to the audience and their scene partners, ensuring every player is connected to at least one dynamic thread.
4. The facilitator provides a simple, non-specific situational catalyst (e.g., 'You are waiting for a delayed train' or 'You have just closed a heavy book').
5. The scene begins, and players must immediately initiate action and dialogue that embodies their declared dynamic without explicitly stating or explaining it.
6. Players use the 'If this is true, what else is true?' principle to let their declared relationship color every physical choice, vocal tone, and reaction.
7. Focus on playing the subtext of the relationship, allowing the inherent tension and character 'wants' to naturally escalate the narrative without needing to invent external plot points.

## Facilitation Notes
- Side-coaching cue: 'Show us the dynamic, don't tell us!' If a player says 'Since I am your bitter reminder of failure...', pause the scene and ask them to express that feeling through an action or a loaded subtextual line instead.
- Pitfall: Players choosing passive or neutral dynamics (e.g., 'I am your friendly neighbor'). Fix: Guide them to choose dynamics with built-in friction, history, or high emotional stakes.
- Side-coaching cue: 'What do you want from them right now?' Remind players that their dynamic dictates their objective; an 'unacknowledged savior' wants recognition, while an 'obsessive protector' wants control.
- Encourage physical world-building and object work. A character playing an 'untrustworthy confidante' might physically hover or rifle through the other's belongings to ground the relationship in the space.

## Variations
- Blind Threads: Players write down their adjective-noun dynamics on slips of paper. They read their own dynamic aloud, but do not know what dynamic the other player has chosen for them, forcing deep listening and active offer reception.
- Three-Way Loop: In a three-player scene, Player A declares a dynamic for Player B, Player B for Player C, and Player C for Player A, creating a complex web of competing objectives.

## Debrief
- How did having a pre-declared dynamic make starting in the middle easier than starting a scene from scratch?
- In what ways did your character's 'want' or objective naturally emerge from the adjective-noun relationship you chose?
- How did you balance playing your own dynamic while actively receiving and justifying the dynamic your partner declared?

## Safety & Inclusion
Because this game invites high-stakes emotional dynamics, remind players before choosing their 'threads' to avoid dynamics that touch on real-world trauma, abuse, or personal boundaries of their scene partners. Encourage physical consent check-ins if a dynamic implies close physical proximity.

## Why It Works
By pre-establishing the 'Relationship' and 'Objective' elements of C.R.O.W., players are freed from the cognitive load of inventing who they are to each other mid-scene. This structural head-start allows them to immediately 'start in the middle' with high stakes. The adjective-noun constraint forces specific, active choices rather than generic labels, turning the relationship itself into a self-sustaining narrative engine.
