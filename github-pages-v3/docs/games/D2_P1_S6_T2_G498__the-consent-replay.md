# The Consent Replay

> Replay scenes to transform internal boundary awareness into active, empowering stage communication.

## Overview
A structured, two-round scene study where players first improvise a baseline scene while privately tracking their comfort levels, then debrief and replay the exact same scene. In the second round, players actively practice boundary navigation, explicit check-ins, and 'Cut' calls. This iterative process bridges the gap between theoretical consent and real-time stage safety.

## What It Trains
- **Domain:** D2 — The Partner
- **Principle(s):** Consent & Boundaries; Truth Over Pandering; Vulnerability
- **Skill(s):** Boundary Navigation; Active Listening; Offer Reception
- **Technique(s):** Check-ins; Cut calls; Negotiating physical contact
- **Focus:** skill_drill

**Objective:** To develop practical boundary navigation, active listening, and offer reception by practicing real-time 'Cut' calls and prioritizing personal truth over artistic pandering.

## Setup
An open, comfortable playing space. No props or materials are required. Players should be arranged in a circle or semi-circle so everyone can clearly observe the scenes and participate in the debriefs.

## How to Play
1. Divide the group into pairs or trios, with the remaining players acting as supportive observers, and designate a facilitator to guide the process.
2. Provide the active players with a simple, open-ended relationship prompt (e.g., two coworkers discussing a failed project, or family members sharing difficult news).
3. Instruct the players to run a standard 2-to-3-minute scene (Round 1: The Baseline) while maintaining heightened internal awareness of their own comfort levels, physical boundaries, and any impulses to 'pander' or ignore discomfort for the sake of the scene.
4. Call 'Time' to end the scene, then lead a structured, non-judgmental debrief where each player rates their overall comfort on a scale of 1 to 5 and identifies specific moments where a boundary was approached or crossed.
5. Collaboratively identify one or two specific boundary-navigation techniques to focus on for the next round, such as verbal check-ins, physical redirection, or calling a 'Cut'.
6. Instruct the players to replay the exact same scene (Round 2: The Application) from the same starting point, using the same characters, setup, and opening lines.
7. During the replay, empower players to actively negotiate physical contact using explicit verbal check-ins (e.g., 'Can I hug you?') and to use a clear 'Cut' call ('Cut', 'Pause', or 'Reset') if a boundary is crossed.
8. If a player calls 'Cut' during the replay, pause the scene immediately, allow the player to briefly state their boundary or request a reset, and then resume play without judgment.
9. Conclude with a final debrief focusing on how the active boundary negotiation and prioritizing truth over pandering affected the players' sense of trust and the creative quality of the scene.

## Facilitation Notes
- Frame 'Cut' calls as a gift to the ensemble: emphasize that stopping a scene to protect a player's safety builds deep trust and ultimately allows for bolder, more authentic play.
- Watch for 'pandering': look for moments where a player's body language signals hesitation or discomfort but they verbally agree anyway. Gently highlight these in the first debrief as valuable learning points.
- Maintain a strict non-judgmental container: ensure the debrief focuses on individual self-awareness and skill-building, never on blaming a partner for making an offer that crossed a boundary.
- Normalize clumsy check-ins: remind players that a verbal check-in during Round 2 might feel clunky at first, but practicing it explicitly builds the muscle memory needed to make it seamless later.

## Variations
- The Non-Verbal Compass: In Round 2, players must negotiate physical boundaries using subtle, non-verbal cues (like offering an open hand and waiting for eye contact) instead of verbal check-ins.
- The Audience Cut: Allow observing players to call a supportive 'Cut' if they notice a physical boundary being crossed without a check-in, helping the entire room practice vigilance.

## Debrief
- How did your internal experience change between the baseline scene and the replay once you had permission to set boundaries?
- What did it feel like to hear or use a 'Cut' call, and how did it affect your trust in your scene partner?
- How does prioritizing 'truth over pandering' actually make a scene more compelling and authentic for the audience?
- In what ways did explicit check-ins open up new, creative directions for the scene that you didn't expect?

## Safety & Inclusion
Because this game explicitly deals with physical and emotional boundaries, participation must be fully voluntary. Establish a clear, universal 'Cut' word before starting. Ensure players know they can step out of a scene at any time without explanation, and that all boundaries shared during the debrief are strictly confidential.

## Why It Works
By separating the experience into a baseline round and an application round, this game lowers the stakes of boundary setting. It directly counters the improv instinct to always say 'yes' at the expense of personal safety. Replaying the scene with explicit permission to use 'Cut' calls and check-ins builds physical and verbal muscle memory, proving to players that honoring boundaries actually deepens trust and enhances creative freedom.
