# Transforming Bench

> Two chairs constantly redefine their reality as players cycle through imaginative, high-agreement scenes.

## Overview
In this fast-paced scene-building exercise, two chairs on stage represent a single physical location or object. As players cycle in and out, the incoming player establishes a brand-new setting and relationship, which the remaining player must instantly accept and build upon.

## What It Trains
- **Domain:** D2 — The Partner
- **Principle(s):** Vulnerability; Yes, And; Base Reality First
- **Skill(s):** Unfiltered Spontaneity; Active Listening; Offer Reception; World-Building
- **Technique(s):** First Thought drills; Meisner Repetition; Endowment-acceptance; C.R.O.W. (Character, Relationship, Objective, Where)
- **Focus:** mixed

**Objective:** To develop rapid offer reception and endowment-acceptance, training players to instantly agree to physical and situational realities established by their partners.

## Setup
Place two chairs side-by-side in the center of the playing area, facing the audience. The rest of the group sits in an audience line or semi-circle facing the stage area. No other props are needed.

## How to Play
1. Two players begin the game by sitting on the two chairs, establishing a simple scene based on a shared physical location (e.g., a roller coaster, a dentist's waiting room, or a park bench).
2. The players engage in a brief scene, focusing on establishing their relationship and the physical reality of the space using the chairs.
3. After about 30 to 45 seconds, one of the players finds a natural, character-driven reason to exit the scene and leaves the stage.
4. The remaining player must stay on the stage, maintaining their physical position on the chair.
5. A new player from the audience line immediately enters the stage and initiates a completely new scene, instantly redefining what the two chairs represent (e.g., turning a car ride into a ski lift).
6. The remaining player must immediately accept this new endowment, adapting their posture, attitude, and relationship to match the new player's initiation.
7. This cycle repeats: after a short interaction, the older player exits, a new player enters with a fresh concept, and the remaining player accepts the new reality.
8. The game continues until everyone has had the opportunity to play in at least two different scenes.

## Facilitation Notes
- Side-coaching cue: 'Accept the physical reality immediately!' Encourage the remaining player to adjust their body language the moment the new player initiates.
- Pitfall: The entering player asks 'Where are we?' or 'What are we doing?' Fix: Coach entering players to make strong, declarative physical and verbal offers that clearly define the space.
- Side-coaching cue: 'Find a logical exit.' Remind players to leave based on the scene's context rather than just running away when they get bored.
- Pitfall: The remaining player tries to cling to the old scene. Fix: Remind them that the incoming player has absolute authority to redefine the space, and the old scene is completely gone.

## Variations
- Emotional Endowment: The incoming player not only redefines the physical space but also endows the remaining player with a specific, intense emotion (e.g., 'Why are you so angry at me?').
- Non-Verbal Initiation: The incoming player must establish the new location and object work entirely through physical action before any dialogue is spoken.
- The Truth Bench: Run the alternative version where players sit as themselves or close-to-self characters, practicing radical honesty and unfiltered spontaneity without theatrical high-concept scenarios.

## Debrief
- How did it feel to have your physical environment completely redefined in an instant?
- What strategies helped you immediately accept and support your partner's new endowment?
- How does committing fully to the physical reality of the chairs make the scene feel more grounded for the audience?

## Safety & Inclusion
Ensure the physical setup of the chairs allows for comfortable seating and easy access. Players should be mindful of physical boundaries when entering the space, avoiding sudden unprompted physical contact when establishing the new scene.

## Why It Works
By keeping the physical props (the chairs) constant while constantly shifting their meaning, the game forces players to rely on active listening and physical endowment. It strips away the hesitation of planning, demanding immediate 'Yes, And' commitment to the partner's offer to establish a stable base reality.
