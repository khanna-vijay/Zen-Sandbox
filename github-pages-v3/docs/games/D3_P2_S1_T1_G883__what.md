# What?

> Transform any ordinary line into an extraordinary comedic game with a single, shocked reaction.

## Overview
Two players initiate a grounded, slice-of-life scene to establish a clear base reality. At any point, one player responds to an innocuous statement with a sharp, surprised 'What?', instantly framing that statement as highly unusual and launching the game of the scene.

## What It Trains
- **Domain:** D3 — The Scene
- **Principle(s):** Base Reality First; Yes, And
- **Skill(s):** Game Identification; Heightening & Exploration; Active Listening; Offer Reception
- **Technique(s):** Finding & Playing the Game; If this is true, what else is true?
- **Focus:** skill_drill

**Objective:** To develop Game Identification and Heightening skills by deliberately choosing a moment to make a partner's ordinary statement important, transforming it into the scene's central comedic premise.

## Setup
Two players stand in the performance space. The rest of the group acts as an active audience. No props or special staging are required.

## How to Play
1. Two players step forward to begin a standard, relationship-focused scene, establishing a clear, grounded base reality of who, where, and what they are doing.
2. Both players speak in a natural, everyday tone, exchanging realistic dialogue and engaging in physical object work.
3. Within the first thirty to forty-five seconds, either player selects a line spoken by their partner—ideally a completely mundane or throwaway comment—and responds with a pointed, surprised, or intense 'What?'.
4. The player who said 'What?' must immediately justify why that specific line was shocking, absurd, or deeply meaningful, establishing the unusual thing or game of the scene.
5. The partner must 'Yes, And' this framing by defending, explaining, or leaning into their newly defined unusual behavior, rather than backing away from it.
6. Both players continue the scene, heightening this specific dynamic by finding new ways to explore the established game while maintaining their initial relationship and environment.

## Facilitation Notes
- Side-coaching cue: 'Don't wait for a weird line to say What. Say What to a normal line and make it weird!'
- Pitfall: Players say 'What?' too early before a base reality is established. Fix: Ensure at least three or four lines of normal, grounded dialogue occur first so the 'What?' has a solid foundation to disrupt.
- Pitfall: The partner gets defensive or denies the premise. Fix: Remind them to enthusiastically accept the accusation and treat their behavior as completely normal for them.
- Side-coaching cue: 'Heighten the reaction, not just the words. How does this change your relationship?'

## Variations
- The Silent 'What?': Instead of speaking the word, a player uses a dramatic physical or emotional reaction, such as a double-take, sudden tears, or freezing in place, to highlight the partner's last line.
- Third-Party 'What?': An observing player offstage yells 'What?!' to freeze the scene, pointing out the line that the onstage players must now treat as the game.

## Debrief
- How did treating a completely normal line as shocking help you discover the game of the scene?
- What did it feel like to have your mundane line suddenly become the most important thing in the scene?
- How does establishing a strong base reality first make the 'What?' moment more impactful?

## Safety & Inclusion
Ensure players are mindful of vocal volume; the 'What?' should be emotionally intense but does not need to be painfully loud for those with sensory sensitivities.

## Why It Works
By forcing a sudden pivot on an ordinary line, this game bypasses the struggle of searching for a game. It teaches players that any line can become the game of the scene through commitment and reaction. It relies heavily on Base Reality First, as the contrast between the normal setup and the sudden heightened reaction is what generates the comedic engine.
