# Collective Definition

![Collective Definition — infographic](img/D2_P2_S1_T0_G1197__mystery-word.jpg){ .game-hero }

> Discover a hidden object together by contributing one descriptive clue at a time.

## Overview
In this cooperative exercise, a small group of players collaboratively defines a secret object without knowing what it is beforehand. By taking turns offering single-word descriptors, players must listen intently to previous clues to narrow down and eventually reveal a single, logical item.

## What It Trains
- **Domain:** D2 — The Partner
- **Principle(s):** Yes, And; Group Mind
- **Skill(s):** Active Listening; Offer Reception; Thematic Synthesis
- **Technique(s):** Yes, And… sentence games; Weave the threads
- **Focus:** skill_drill

**Objective:** To develop deep active listening, offer reception, and thematic synthesis by forcing players to build directly on their partners' clues rather than pushing their own agenda.

## Setup
Arrange 2 to 4 players in a small circle facing each other. No props, stage space, or audience are required.

## How to Play
1. The facilitator instructs the group that they will collectively describe a single, mystery object that does not yet exist in anyone's mind.
2. The first player begins by stating a single descriptive word, strictly avoiding nouns, to establish the first characteristic of the object.
3. The next player in the circle must actively listen to the first word and offer a second descriptive word that logically pairs with or refines the first.
4. Players continue taking turns in a circle, each adding exactly one non-noun descriptor that builds upon the cumulative picture created by all previous words.
5. Players must avoid contradictory clues and instead practice 'Yes, And' by accepting all prior descriptors as absolute truth.
6. As the list of descriptors grows, the group synthesizes the clues until a clear, specific object naturally emerges.
7. Once the identity of the object becomes obvious to everyone, any player can call out the final noun to successfully complete the round.

## Facilitation Notes
- Side-coach players to avoid planning their words in advance; they must react only to the immediate word spoken before them.
- Pitfall: Players offering contradictory words. Fix: Pause the game and ask the group how they can reconcile the two, or restart the round emphasizing agreement.
- Pitfall: Throwing out nouns too early. Fix: Remind players to stick strictly to adjectives, sensory details, or attributes until the final reveal.
- Encourage slow, deliberate pacing so players have time to synthesize the growing list of attributes.

## Variations
- Category Constraint: Limit the mystery object to a specific category, such as 'something found in a kitchen' or 'an ancient artifact'.
- Emotional Attributes: Allow players to contribute emotional or abstract descriptors alongside physical ones.

## Debrief
- How did it feel to contribute a word without knowing what the final object would be?
- What strategies did you use to synthesize your partner's clues with your own ideas?
- When did the moment of alignment happen where everyone realized what the object was?

## Safety & Inclusion
Ensure players are comfortable with verbal-only processing. For players with processing differences, allow a few extra seconds of silence between turns without pressure.

## Why It Works
This game works because it strips away the ability to plan ahead. Since no single player knows the destination, everyone is forced to practice pure 'Yes, And'—treating every partner's word as a binding reality that must be integrated into a single, cohesive concept.
