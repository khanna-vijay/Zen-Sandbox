# Three-Line Platform

![Three-Line Platform — infographic](img/D3_P2_S5_T1_G1333__three-line-environment.jpg){ .game-hero }

> Establish a complete, physically rich base reality using only three total lines of dialogue.

## Overview
Two players collaborate to build a fully realized scene foundation—defining who they are, where they are, and what they are doing—using exactly three spoken lines. By combining precise physical object work with highly efficient dialogue, players learn to establish a clear C.R.O.W. (Character, Relationship, Objective, Where) without relying on lengthy exposition.

## What It Trains
- **Domain:** D3 — The Scene
- **Principle(s):** Base Reality First; Yes, And; Show, Don't Tell
- **Skill(s):** World-Building; Physicality & Space Work; Offer Reception; Active Gifting; Narrative Architecture
- **Technique(s):** C.R.O.W. (Character, Relationship, Objective, Where); Object work; Platform/Tilt
- **Focus:** skill_drill

**Objective:** To master the rapid establishment of a scene's base reality by integrating physical environment work with economical, high-impact verbal offers.

## Setup
Two players stand in the performance space. The rest of the group observes as the audience. No physical props or chairs are used; all objects and environmental elements must be mimed.

## How to Play
1. Two players step forward to begin a scene, designated as Player A and Player B.
2. Before any dialogue is spoken, both players must immediately engage in physical object work to establish the physical environment ('Where') and their individual activities.
3. Player A speaks the first line of dialogue, which should offer a specific detail about their relationship, the location, or the immediate situation.
4. Player B receives the physical and verbal offers, continues their own physical work, and delivers the second line of dialogue to validate and expand the reality.
5. Player A delivers the third and final line of dialogue, cementing the platform and clarifying any remaining elements of C.R.O.W. (Character, Relationship, Objective, Where).
6. Once the third line is spoken, the facilitator calls 'Freeze!' and the scene ends.
7. The facilitator or audience briefly identifies the established elements: Who they are, where they are, what their relationship is, and what is happening.

## Facilitation Notes
- Encourage players to start with physical action before speaking; silent physical work for 5 to 10 seconds sets a much stronger environmental foundation.
- Watch out for 'exposition dumping' where players try to cram too much information into a single line (e.g., 'Hello, my brother John, here at our family bakery in Boston!'). Instead, encourage natural, specific dialogue.
- Remind Player B to actively 'Yes, And' the physical space established by Player A, respecting the placement of mimed objects.
- Side-coaching cue: 'Show us the weight of the object!' or 'Who are you to each other in this space?'

## Variations
- Silent Start: Players must do physical work for a full 30 seconds before the first line is spoken.
- Two-Line Platform: Tighten the constraint even further to just two lines (one from each player) to establish the entire base reality.
- Blind Environment: Player A starts with physical work in a specific location. Player B enters, figures out the location through observation, and joins the physical work before either speaks.

## Debrief
- How did starting with physical movement change the type of dialogue you chose to use?
- Which of the C.R.O.W. elements felt easiest to establish in three lines, and which felt hardest?
- How does having a clear, physically grounded environment make the rest of a scene easier to improvise?

## Safety & Inclusion
Ensure players are mindful of physical accessibility; physical work can be adapted to any mobility level, focusing on facial expressions, posture, or small-scale gestural work if needed.

## Why It Works
By limiting verbal communication to exactly three lines, players are forced to abandon stalling tactics and vague talk. They must rely on physical environment work to do the heavy lifting of world-building, which naturally leads to a more grounded, visually interesting, and stable base reality.
