# The Open Offer

<div class="game-hero-ph" markdown>🎨 *Infographic coming soon*</div>

> Ground ambiguous physical or verbal prompts by collaboratively building a solid, immediate base reality.

## Overview
Two players work together to establish a clear scene foundation from a single, intentionally incomplete starting point. One player initiates with a vague physical action or ambiguous line, and the second player immediately steps in to define the Character, Relationship, Objective, and Where (C.R.O.W.). This exercise builds trust and rapid platform-setting skills.

## What It Trains
- **Domain:** D3 — The Scene
- **Principle(s):** Base Reality First; Yes, And; Make Your Partner a Genius
- **Skill(s):** World-Building; Offer Reception; Active Gifting; Narrative Architecture
- **Technique(s):** C.R.O.W. (Character, Relationship, Objective, Where); Endowment-acceptance; Platform/Tilt; Endowment-gifting drills
- **Focus:** skill_drill

**Objective:** To develop rapid world-building and collaborative platform-setting (C.R.O.W.) by training players to treat ambiguous offers as gifts rather than obstacles.

## Setup
An open playing space. Players stand in a circle or form a line-up. No props or special staging are required.

## How to Play
1. Player A steps into the playing space and initiates with a single, open offer, which can be a vague physical gesture or an ambiguous line of dialogue.
2. Player A must deliberately leave the context incomplete, avoiding defining who they are, where they are, or what they are doing.
3. Player B immediately enters the scene, accepting the open offer as an absolute truth and instantly providing the missing context.
4. Player B's response must establish at least two elements of C.R.O.W. to ground the scene immediately.
5. Player A enthusiastically yes-ands Player B's definition, cementing the base reality with the remaining C.R.O.W. elements.
6. The duo plays a brief three-to-four line exchange to solidify this newly established reality before the facilitator calls freeze.
7. The next pair steps up to repeat the process with a new open offer.

## Facilitation Notes
- Side-coach with: Don't guess what they meant, decide what it is! This keeps players from hesitating.
- Watch out for Player A making an offer that is too defined. Remind them to keep the initiation abstract, like a blank canvas waiting for color.
- If Player B hesitates trying to find the perfect answer, encourage immediate, instinctual responses. The first idea is the best idea.
- Remind players to make their partner look like a genius by fully committing to whatever interpretation their partner brings to the table.

## Variations
- Silent Start: Player A can only use physical object work or body language with no words allowed until Player B establishes the reality.
- Rapid Fire: Run the exercise in a line-up where Player A makes an offer, Player B completes it, then Player B becomes the new Player A for the next person in line.
- Blind Offer: Player A closes their eyes, makes a physical shape, opens their eyes, and Player B must immediately justify the shape.

## Debrief
- How did it feel to step into a scene with absolutely no pre-planned context?
- What strategies did you use to instantly define the C.R.O.W. elements?
- How does treating an ambiguous offer as a deliberate gift change your anxiety level on stage?

## Safety & Inclusion
Ensure physical offers respect personal space boundaries. If a player initiates with a physical gesture that requires proximity, the responding player should check in with eye contact or maintain a comfortable physical distance while verbally defining the space.

## Why It Works
It bypasses the analytical brain by forcing immediate justification. By separating the offer from the definition, players learn that any initiation is valid as long as the partner commits to grounding it. It builds trust and reinforces the C.R.O.W. framework as an active tool rather than a theoretical concept.
