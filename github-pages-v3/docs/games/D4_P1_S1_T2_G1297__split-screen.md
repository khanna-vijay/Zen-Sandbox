# Split Screen

> Two characters played by four actors across a split stage using seamless, portal-like transitions.

## Overview
A challenging ensemble game where four players portray only two characters across a split stage divided by an invisible central wall. When a character exits one side of the stage, they instantly emerge on the other side, embodied by a different player who must seamlessly inherit their physical, vocal, and emotional state.

## What It Trains
- **Domain:** D4 — The Ensemble
- **Principle(s):** Group Mind; Follow the Follower; Make Your Partner a Genius
- **Skill(s):** Peripheral Awareness; Support Work; Single-Partner Empathy & Mirroring; Physicality & Space Work
- **Technique(s):** Thread-tracking drills; Tap-ins; Mirror exercise; Character Walks/Centers
- **Focus:** mixed

**Objective:** To develop deep peripheral awareness, group mind, and thread-tracking skills by matching and maintaining a partner's physical and narrative choices across a physical divide.

## Setup
Divide the playing area into two distinct halves (Left Stage and Right Stage) with a clear, invisible vertical boundary running down the center. Four players participate: two start on the Left Stage, and two wait offstage on the Right Stage.

## How to Play
1. Establish the physical boundary: the center line of the stage is an impassable wall that players cannot cross directly.
2. Assign the roles: Player A and Player B start on the Left Stage as Character 1 and Character 2, while Player C and Player D wait offstage on the Right Stage.
3. Begin a standard two-person scene on the Left Stage, establishing clear physical traits, emotional states, and a narrative relationship.
4. To trigger a transition, a character on the Left Stage must exit the scene by walking off the far-left wing.
5. The moment a character exits the far-left wing, their counterpart must instantly enter from the far-right wing onto the Right Stage, played by the waiting actor.
6. The entering actor must perfectly match the exiting actor's physical posture, vocal tone, emotional energy, and current narrative objective.
7. The scene continues seamlessly across the split stage, with players exiting and entering to swap actors while maintaining only two continuous characters.
8. Encourage frequent swaps to keep all four players highly active, requiring constant tracking of physical and narrative threads.

## Facilitation Notes
- Coaching cue: 'Track the hand!' or 'Match the energy before you step on!' to help offstage players prepare for their entrance.
- Pitfall: Entering players resetting the character's emotional state. Fix: Have offstage players physically shadow the active players' movements from the wings to maintain physical empathy.
- Coaching cue: 'Keep the conversation fluid—the character doesn't reset just because the actor changed.'
- Pitfall: Confusion over who is playing whom. Fix: Assign clear pairings beforehand (e.g., Player A and Player C are Character 1; Player B and Player D are Character 2).

## Variations
- Emotional Shift: The transition through the portal alters the character's emotional intensity, requiring the entering player to amplify the established emotion.
- Object Continuity: The characters handle a specific physical prop (object work) that must be seamlessly passed through the portal and maintained with identical weight and shape.

## Debrief
- How did you maintain physical and emotional continuity when inheriting a character?
- What did you have to pay attention to while waiting offstage to ensure a seamless transition?
- How does this exercise build Group Mind and trust between ensemble members?

## Safety & Inclusion
Ensure the playing space is clear of obstacles, especially near the wings where rapid exits and entries occur. Players should maintain controlled physical movements to avoid collisions when entering the stage.

## Why It Works
It forces players to move away from individual ownership of a character and embrace a collective consciousness. By tracking the physical and narrative threads of their partner in real-time, players develop high-level peripheral awareness and practice 'following the follower,' resulting in a unified performance.
