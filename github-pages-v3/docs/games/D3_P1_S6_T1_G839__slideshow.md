# Holiday Slideshow

> Present a hilarious vacation travelogue by justifying the bizarre physical tableaus your castmates freeze into.

## Overview
Two presenters share memories of a recent trip using a virtual slideshow clicker. Behind them, the rest of the ensemble instantly freezes into physical tableaus representing the photos, forcing the presenters to creatively justify whatever absurd poses they see.

## What It Trains
- **Domain:** D3 — The Scene
- **Principle(s):** Show, Don't Tell; Yes, And; Make Your Partner a Genius; Group Mind
- **Skill(s):** Justification; Offer Reception; Physicality & Space Work; Support Work; Stage Presence & Clarity
- **Technique(s):** Justify the absurd; Endowment-acceptance; Playing architecture/objects; Cheating out
- **Focus:** mixed

**Objective:** To develop rapid justification skills and physical offer reception by connecting abstract or unexpected physical poses to a cohesive narrative.

## Setup
An open performance space. Two players stand downstage facing the audience (the presenters). The remaining players (three to six) stand upstage in a line (the slide-makers), ready to step forward and freeze.

## How to Play
1. Ask the group for a suggestion of an unusual vacation destination or a specific event to set the scene.
2. The two downstage presenters begin their presentation, introducing themselves and setting up the context of their trip.
3. One presenter mimics holding a remote control and makes a clicking sound to transition to the first slide.
4. Upon hearing the click, the upstage players quickly step forward and freeze in a collaborative, physical tableau, creating a silent photograph.
5. The presenters turn around, observe the frozen tableau, and must immediately justify the pose, explaining exactly what is happening in the photo and how it relates to their trip.
6. The presenters speak with confidence, treating the bizarre physical choices of their castmates as absolute, logical facts of their vacation.
7. Once the presenters have fully explained the slide, they click the remote again, prompting the upstage players to immediately break their pose and form a completely new, unrelated tableau.
8. Repeat this cycle for four to six slides, allowing the presenters to build a progressive narrative arc before concluding their presentation.

## Facilitation Notes
- Encourage the slide-makers to use different levels (high, medium, low) and physical contact (with consent) to make the tableaus visually dynamic.
- Side-coach presenters to avoid denying the slide. If someone is frozen on the floor, don't say 'Oh, that's just a rock.' Instead, justify it: 'This is when Uncle Bob slipped on the banana peel right outside the museum!'
- Remind the slide-makers to remain completely frozen and silent once they establish their pose, letting the presenters do all the verbal heavy lifting.
- If presenters get stuck, prompt them with questions: 'Who is that in the background?' or 'What happened right before this photo was taken?'

## Variations
- Blind Slideshow: The presenters stand facing the audience and are not allowed to look back at the slide. They must guess what the slide is based on audience laughter or clues before justifying it.
- Emotional Slideshow: Each click of the remote also dictates a specific emotion that the presenters must adopt when describing the next slide.
- Historical Archive: Instead of a vacation, the presenters are historians presenting newly discovered archival photos of a major historical event or a fictional ancient civilization.

## Debrief
- How did it feel to have to instantly justify a physical shape you did not plan?
- What strategies did the presenters use to make the most absurd poses seem completely logical?
- For the slide-makers, how did you collaborate silently to create a cohesive visual offer?

## Safety & Inclusion
Ensure players establish physical boundaries before the game begins, as forming rapid tableaus can lead to accidental physical contact. Encourage slide-makers to prioritize physical safety and avoid highly precarious or unstable physical balances.

## Why It Works
This game acts as a perfect engine for justification because it separates the physical offer from the verbal explanation. By forcing the presenters to treat the physical tableaus as objective truth, it trains the 'Yes, And' mindset at a fundamental level—turning accidental or chaotic physical shapes into brilliant, intentional narrative choices.
