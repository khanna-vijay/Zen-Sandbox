# Rewind and Reframe

> Pause scenes at potential boundaries to check in, adjust, and collaboratively discover safer creative paths.

## Overview
This exercise introduces a proactive yellow-light intervention tool that allows players to pause a scene before boundaries are crossed. When a moment of physical, emotional, or thematic ambiguity arises, the facilitator pauses the action to host a brief, non-judgmental check-in. Players openly discuss their comfort levels and collaboratively adjust their choices before rewinding and playing out the scene with a new, consensual approach.

## What It Trains
- **Domain:** D2 — The Partner
- **Principle(s):** Consent & Boundaries; Truth Over Pandering; Vulnerability
- **Skill(s):** Boundary Navigation; Offer Reception
- **Technique(s):** Check-ins; Cut calls; Negotiating physical contact
- **Focus:** connection

**Objective:** To develop advanced boundary navigation and active check-in techniques, ensuring players prioritize personal comfort and vulnerability over automatic agreement.

## Setup
A moderate playing space suitable for hybrid or in-person setups. No props are required. Players stand in a semi-circle with two players stepping forward to initiate a scene, while one designated facilitator monitors the action.

## How to Play
1. Two players step into the performance space and begin a standard, relationship-focused scene based on a simple suggestion.
2. The facilitator actively monitors the scene, looking not just for clear boundary violations, but for yellow-light moments such as ambiguous physical proximity, intense emotional escalation, or sensitive thematic choices.
3. At any point of potential discomfort or ambiguity, the facilitator calls out 'Rewind!' in a neutral, supportive tone.
4. Upon hearing the call, the players immediately freeze in place, stepping out of character to enter a collaborative, meta-communicative space.
5. The facilitator briefly and objectively states what they observed, such as noting that physical closeness escalated quickly, and invites a check-in.
6. The players conduct an honest, verbal check-in, sharing how the moment felt for them personally and clarifying their creative intentions without judgment.
7. The group collaboratively discusses alternative choices that respect everyone's boundaries while still serving the scene's narrative goals.
8. The players agree on a specific point in the scene to rewind to, typically a few lines or actions before the pause occurred.
9. The players reset physically and emotionally to that chosen point, then resume the scene, actively implementing the agreed-upon adjustments and consensual alternatives.

## Facilitation Notes
- Normalize the 'Rewind' call as a positive learning tool rather than a disciplinary measure, framing it as a gift of clarity for the ensemble.
- Focus on yellow-light moments rather than waiting for a red-light boundary breach, helping players build sensitivity to subtle shifts in comfort.
- Keep the meta-discussion brief and action-oriented, guiding players toward concrete physical or verbal adjustments rather than lengthy intellectual debates.
- Ensure the player who experienced the potential boundary crossing is given the floor first to express their comfort level without pressure to pander to the scene's momentum.
- If players struggle to find an alternative, suggest specific options like replacing physical contact with eye contact, or translating physical intimacy into verbal status play.

## Variations
- Player-Initiated Rewinds: Once players are comfortable with the mechanic, empower the active improvisers to call 'Rewind!' on themselves when they feel a personal boundary approaching.
- The Silent Reframe: After the check-in, instead of discussing the solution, players must immediately resume the scene and find a non-verbal, physical way to adjust their boundaries organically.

## Debrief
- How did it feel to have the scene paused? Did it feel like a disruption, or did it provide a sense of relief and safety?
- During the check-in, how did you balance your commitment to the character's choices with your actual comfort as a player?
- What did you notice about the quality of the scene after we reframed and resumed? How did clarity of consent affect your play?
- How can we carry this proactive mindset of checking in and adjusting into our regular, uninterrupted improv scenes?

## Safety & Inclusion
This game is highly consent-sensitive. Establish a firm rule before playing that any player can decline to answer a check-in question or request a scene change without needing to justify their decision. For virtual play, players can use a physical hand gesture or type 'pause' in the chat to initiate a rewind if audio lag occurs.

## Why It Works
By interrupting the automatic yes-and reflex, this game teaches players that personal safety and mutual consent override narrative momentum. The structured pause removes the social pressure to pander to uncomfortable offers, while the immediate reframe provides a safe sandbox to practice real-time boundary navigation and active check-ins, building deep trust within the ensemble.
