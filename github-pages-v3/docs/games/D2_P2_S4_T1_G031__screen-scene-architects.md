# Virtual Scene Architects

> Co-create immersive digital worlds and characters using video platform features and physical props.

## Overview
Virtual Scene Architects is a remote-first improv game where players collaboratively build a scene's environment and characters using virtual backgrounds, profile renaming, and physical props. Two players are spotlighted at a time to perform, dynamically swapping out when a new player is called in to 'Yes, And' the visual or verbal reality.

## What It Trains
- **Domain:** D2 — The Partner
- **Principle(s):** Yes, And; Make Your Partner a Genius; Base Reality First; Group Mind
- **Skill(s):** Active Listening; Offer Reception; Active Gifting; World-Building; Peripheral Awareness; Support Work
- **Technique(s):** Meisner Repetition; Yes, And… sentence games; Endowment-acceptance; Endowment-gifting drills; C.R.O.W. (Character, Relationship, Objective, Where)
- **Focus:** mixed

**Objective:** Develops rapid offer reception, active listening, and multi-modal 'Yes, And' skills by integrating digital tools into active scene-building.

## Setup
A virtual meeting room with renaming and virtual background features enabled. Participants should have 2-3 simple desk props (e.g., a pen, a mug, a book, a scarf) nearby. The facilitator must be ready to manage the platform's spotlight or pinning features.

## How to Play
1. Step 1: Ensure all players have renaming permissions enabled and know how to change their virtual backgrounds. Have everyone gather 2-3 simple desk props.
2. Step 2: Start the Environmental Architecture phase. The facilitator selects a starting player who names a location and sets a virtual background to match.
3. Step 3: The starting player calls on another player, who must 'Yes, And' the environment by changing their background to a complementary location and briefly describing it. Repeat with 3-4 players to build a shared visual world.
4. Step 4: Begin the Character Genesis phase. The facilitator selects a player to introduce a character. This player renames their profile to their character's name and establishes a physical posture or holds a prop.
5. Step 5: This player calls on another player, who renames themselves to a complementary character and establishes their physical presence. Repeat until all players have a character name and a physical hook.
6. Step 6: The facilitator spotlights the first two characters to begin the scene. They initiate a brief dialogue, establishing their relationship and using their backgrounds and props.
7. Step 7: To swap players, one active character calls out another character's name. The facilitator immediately spotlights the new player and removes the calling player.
8. Step 8: The newly spotlighted player must instantly enter the scene by either visually 'Yes, Anding' (changing their background or presenting a prop) or verbally 'Yes, Anding' (delivering a line that builds on the last spoken dialogue).
9. Step 9: Players not currently spotlighted use the chat or non-verbal reaction emojis to offer silent support, which the active players can choose to integrate.
10. Step 10: The facilitator calls 'Scene!' once everyone has cycled through the spotlight multiple times and the narrative reaches a natural climax.

## Facilitation Notes
- Run a quick 1-minute tech check before starting to ensure everyone can change their background and rename themselves without technical lag.
- Keep the spotlight swaps fast. Side-coach: 'Don't wait for the perfect background—if you can't change it instantly, use your physical prop or body to make the offer!'
- If players get stuck trying to find the 'perfect' background image, remind them that a mismatched background can be justified as a hilarious narrative twist (e.g., a beach background in a space station is a holographic simulation room).
- Encourage off-screen players to keep their cameras on and actively listen so they can jump in seamlessly when called.

## Variations
- The Prop Pass: When a player is swapped out, they must 'pass' their physical prop off-screen, and the incoming player must 'receive' it on-screen, transforming its use.
- Background Roulette: The facilitator randomly changes a spotlighted player's background mid-scene, and the player must immediately justify why their character is suddenly in that new location.

## Debrief
- How did incorporating visual elements like backgrounds and props change how you received and built upon your partner's offers?
- What strategies did you use to keep track of the scene's reality when you weren't spotlighted?
- How did we justify 'mistakes' or slow tech transitions to make them part of the story?

## Safety & Inclusion
Ensure all players are comfortable with having their cameras on. If a player cannot use virtual backgrounds due to hardware limitations, allow them to use physical drawings held up to the camera, or describe their background verbally as their 'Yes, And' contribution.

## Why It Works
It translates the core improv principle of 'Yes, And' into a multi-sensory digital environment. By separating the scene into environmental, character, and narrative layers, it prevents cognitive overload. The spotlight mechanic solves the classic virtual improv issue of audio latency by limiting active speakers to two, while keeping others engaged as active observers and potential incoming architects.
