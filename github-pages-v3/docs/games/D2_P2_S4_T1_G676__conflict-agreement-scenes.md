# The Agreement Pivot

<div class="game-hero-ph" markdown>🎨 *Infographic coming soon*</div>

> Transform high-tension, conflict-prone setups into unexpected moments of deep collaborative agreement and connection.

## Overview
Two players are placed in a scenario that typically triggers immediate theatrical conflict, such as a car accident or a sudden firing. Instead of arguing, they must actively receive every offer and find a surprising, collaborative point of agreement. This shift prioritizes relationship-building and shared reality over cheap combativeness.

## What It Trains
- **Domain:** D2 — The Partner
- **Principle(s):** Yes, And; Make Your Partner a Genius; Base Reality First; Serve the Story
- **Skill(s):** Active Listening; Offer Reception; World-Building; Justification
- **Technique(s):** Yes, And… sentence games; Endowment-acceptance; C.R.O.W. (Character, Relationship, Objective, Where); Justify the absurd
- **Focus:** connection

**Objective:** Develops advanced offer reception and active listening by training players to bypass defensive instincts, accept high-stakes initiations, and discover collaborative justification.

## Setup
Two chairs are placed center stage representing the playing area. The remaining participants sit as an active audience. The facilitator prepares a list of high-stakes, potentially conflict-prone scenarios.

## How to Play
1. Invite two players to the stage and assign them a classic high-conflict prompt, such as a manager firing an employee.
2. Instruct the players that they must accept the premise of the conflict but immediately pivot to mutual agreement and support.
3. The first player initiates the scene by delivering the high-stakes offer clearly and directly, establishing the tension.
4. The second player receives the offer with total agreement, validating the first player's perspective instead of defending themselves.
5. The second player adds a 'Yes, And' detail that justifies why they agree, reframing the situation from an adversarial fight into a shared problem.
6. For example, if Player A says, 'You completely ruined my wedding cake!', Player B responds, 'I did, and honestly, looking at it now, those neon green frosting skulls were a terrible design choice on my part.' Player A then builds on this: 'Yes, and I'm so glad you see it too, because it actually matches our gothic theme way better than the pastel flowers I originally asked for!'
7. Both players continue the scene by building on this shared reality, using 'Yes, And' to discover a deeper bond or a collaborative solution.
8. The scene concludes once a strong, cooperative relationship is established and the initial crisis has been transformed into a connection.

## Facilitation Notes
- Side-coach with: 'Agree with the reality, but love the person.' This helps players separate the bad news from their relationship.
- Watch out for passive compliance where one player simply submits. Remind them to 'Yes, And' by adding new information that actively justifies the agreement.
- If players stall, coach them to find a shared external focus, like blaming a poorly designed intersection after a car crash.
- Encourage players to keep the stakes high; agreement does not mean the situation is suddenly easy, it means they are facing it together.

## Variations
- The Literal Yes-And: Players must begin every single line of dialogue with the phrase 'Yes, and...' to strictly enforce the agreement mechanic.
- The Disarming Pivot: One player is secretly instructed to try to start a conflict, while the other player must disarm them using radical agreement.
- Remote Adaptability: Playable on video calls by having players use direct eye contact with the camera. The 'accused' player can use physical objects near their desk to justify the agreement (e.g., holding up a mug to justify why they spilled coffee).

## Debrief
- How did it feel to bypass the instinct to argue when given a high-conflict prompt?
- What happened to the depth of the relationship when you chose agreement over conflict?
- How does agreeing with a difficult offer make your partner look like a genius?

## Safety & Inclusion
Because high-stakes prompts can easily veer into sensitive personal territory, establish clear boundaries before playing. Explicitly ban prompts involving real-world trauma, systemic oppression, domestic abuse, or personal phobias. Implement a 'pass' or 'reset' rule where any player can instantly veto a prompt or scene direction with no explanation required, allowing the group to pivot to a safer scenario immediately.

## Why It Works
Conflict in improv often leads to defensive blocking. By forcing agreement in a high-conflict setup, players must listen deeply and collaborate on a creative justification, building a strong base reality and prioritizing connection.
