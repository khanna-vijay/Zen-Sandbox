# Setting Kaleidoscope

<div class="game-hero-ph" markdown>🎨 *Infographic coming soon*</div>

> Explore the hidden depths of a single location through contrasting, thematic scenes.

## Overview
In this exercise, players perform a series of rapid-fire, independent scenes that all take place within the exact same physical location. By cycling through different characters, eras, and genres, the ensemble learns to look past the obvious tropes of a suggestion and discover its hidden dramatic and comedic potential.

## What It Trains
- **Domain:** D4 — The Ensemble
- **Principle(s):** Serve the Piece; Base Reality First
- **Skill(s):** Suggestion Deconstruction (A-to-C); World-Building; Thematic Synthesis
- **Technique(s):** What's interesting about this? mining; C.R.O.W. (Character, Relationship, Objective, Where); Montage
- **Focus:** skill_drill

**Objective:** Develops suggestion deconstruction (A-to-C) and thematic synthesis by training players to mine a single location for diverse, non-obvious scenarios using the 'What's interesting about this?' technique.

## Setup
Players stand in a line or semi-circle facing the stage area. No physical props or chairs are used; all environments and items must be established through pantomime.

## How to Play
1. The facilitator solicits a single, broad location suggestion from the group (e.g., 'a public library' or 'a subway platform').
2. Two players step forward to initiate the first scene, establishing a grounded base reality that represents a common, everyday use of that location.
3. After a brief, complete scene of 1 to 2 minutes, the facilitator edits the scene by calling 'Scene!' or clapping.
4. A new pair of players immediately steps forward to start a completely different scene in the exact same location.
5. This second scene must contrast sharply with the first in tone, relationship, stakes, or time period (e.g., moving from a quiet study session to a high-stakes spy handoff).
6. Subsequent pairs continue to step out and initiate new scenes, with each pair challenging themselves to find a totally unique angle or hidden pocket of the same setting.
7. The cycle concludes after 4 or 5 distinct scenes have fully mapped out the diverse possibilities of the suggested location.

## Facilitation Notes
- Coaching cue: 'What else happens in this space when no one is looking? Think about different times of day or different centuries!'
- Pitfall: Players repeat the same tone or character dynamics (e.g., multiple scenes of people arguing in a bank). Fix: Side-coach the next pair before they step out by giving them a specific genre prompt like 'Give us a sci-fi scene in this bank!'
- Coaching cue: 'Play with scale! Zoom in on a tiny detail like an ant on the floor, or zoom out to a massive historical event happening in this exact spot.'
- Pitfall: Players forget the setting entirely and do a generic talking-heads scene. Fix: Remind them to use physical object work to interact with the specific environment established in previous scenes.

## Variations
- Chronological Shift: The scenes must progress chronologically through time, showing the same location in the distant past, the present day, and the far future.
- Thematic Echoes: A specific physical object or a ghostly presence remains in the space across all the different scenes, connecting the disparate characters.
- Simultaneous Corners: Each scene takes place in a different physical corner of the same large location, with characters occasionally noticing the background action of the other scenes.

## Debrief
- How did forcing yourself to stay in the same location push you to make more creative, non-obvious choices?
- What clues did you take from your teammates' physical object work to help define your own version of the space?
- How does establishing a strong, grounded base reality first make the unusual elements of a scene stand out more clearly?

## Safety & Inclusion
Ensure the performance space is clear of physical obstacles. Encourage players to adapt their physical object work to accommodate different mobility levels, ensuring everyone can comfortably interact with the imagined environment.

## Why It Works
By restricting the physical setting, players are forced to move past their first-thought ideas (the 'A' association) and make lateral leaps (to 'B' and 'C' associations). This process of mining 'what is interesting' about a location teaches the ensemble to serve the larger piece by building a rich, multi-faceted world from a single seed.
