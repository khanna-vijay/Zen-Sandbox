# Dialogue and Doing

<div class="game-hero-ph" markdown>🎨 *Infographic coming soon*</div>

> Ground verbal scenes by layering in physical tasks without talking about the action.

## Overview
Two players begin a scene focused entirely on a verbal conversation topic. Mid-scene, the facilitator introduces a physical activity that the players must immediately execute with detailed object work, all while seamlessly continuing their original conversation without verbally acknowledging the task.

## What It Trains
- **Domain:** D3 — The Scene
- **Principle(s):** Show, Don't Tell; Commit 100%; Yes, And
- **Skill(s):** World-Building; Physicality & Space Work; Active Listening
- **Technique(s):** C.R.O.W. (Character, Relationship, Objective, Where); Object work
- **Focus:** skill_drill

**Objective:** To develop the ability to split focus between verbal storytelling and physical world-building, training players to show their environment and relationships through action rather than explaining them through dialogue.

## Setup
A clear stage area or classroom space. No props are needed. Players can work in pairs in front of the group, or the entire room can practice simultaneously in pairs.

## How to Play
1. Pair up players and have one pair step into the performance space.
2. Ask the group or facilitator for a simple, non-dramatic topic of conversation, such as cooking tips or childhood pets.
3. The players begin a natural, face-to-face conversation about this topic, establishing their characters and relationship through their dialogue.
4. Once the conversation is established and flowing, usually after thirty to forty-five seconds, the facilitator calls out a specific physical activity like washing a car or baking a cake.
5. The players must immediately begin performing this physical activity together using detailed, mime-based object work.
6. The players must continue their verbal conversation about the original topic without interruption, making no verbal reference to the physical task they are performing.
7. The physical actions should influence the delivery, pacing, and emotion of the dialogue, letting physical strain or cooperation affect the vocal tone.
8. End the scene after one to two minutes of combined verbal and physical play once a strong balance is achieved.

## Facilitation Notes
- Side-coach to keep the physical work active: Keep talking about the topic, but scrub that imaginary car harder!
- Watch out for players who stop talking to focus on the physical task. Remind them to let their hands move independently of their mouths.
- Address the pitfall of players narrating their actions. If a player says, 'Pass me that wrench,' side-coach them to ask for the wrench physically while talking about their childhood dog.
- Encourage players to let the physical effort affect their voice. If they are lifting something heavy, the audience should hear it in their breath and pacing.

## Variations
- Character-Driven Tasks: The facilitator calls out an activity that directly challenges or heightens the established relationship, such as performing surgery or packing a suitcase to leave.
- Silent Start: Start with the physical activity first, then introduce the verbal topic later, reversing the split-focus challenge.
- Emotional Overlay: The physical activity must be performed with a specific shared emotion that contrasts with the mundane conversation topic.

## Debrief
- How did adding a physical task change the dynamic of your verbal conversation?
- What did you notice about your physical commitment when you were not allowed to talk about what you were doing?
- How does having a physical task help establish the 'Where' and 'Relationship' of a scene without relying on expository dialogue?

## Safety & Inclusion
Ensure the physical activities called out are accessible to all players' physical abilities. Encourage players to adapt the physical scale of the task to their comfort level, focusing on precision of object work rather than high-impact exertion.

## Why It Works
It forces a cognitive split that bypasses the analytical brain. When the mouth is busy with one topic and the body is busy with another, players stop overthinking their dialogue. This creates highly realistic, grounded scenes where the physical environment and physical interactions are shown naturally, fulfilling the 'Show, Don't Tell' principle.
