# The Ping-Pong Ballad

> An improvised duet conducted entirely by the shifting gaze of an audience volunteer.

## Overview
Two performers improvise a cohesive song dedicated to an audience volunteer who has been invited on stage. The twist is that the volunteer acts as the conductor: whichever performer they look at must sing, requiring the singers to seamlessly pass the melody and lyrics back and forth mid-sentence.

## What It Trains
- **Domain:** D2 — The Partner
- **Principle(s):** Make Your Partner a Genius; The First Thought Is a Gift; The Audience Is the Final Scene Partner
- **Skill(s):** Active Listening; Unfiltered Spontaneity; Vocal Craft; Room Reading
- **Technique(s):** Last Word Response; First Thought drills; Vocal characterization
- **Focus:** mixed

**Objective:** To develop hyper-vigilant active listening, spontaneous vocal support, and the ability to seamlessly pick up a partner's unfinished thoughts, rhymes, and musical rhythms.

## Setup
Two performers stand downstage left and downstage right. An audience volunteer is invited to sit or stand in the center, facing the audience but positioned so they can easily look at either performer. No props or instruments are required, though a live keyboardist or simple backing beat can be used.

## How to Play
1. Invite an audience volunteer to join the two performers on stage, positioning them centrally between the singers.
2. Conduct a brief, warm interview with the volunteer to gather details about their life, such as their name, occupation, hobbies, or a recent minor triumph.
3. Explain the rules to the volunteer: they are the conductor of their own tribute song, and whichever singer they look at must sing immediately, while the other must stop.
4. Instruct the singers to begin the song, establishing a steady rhythm, tempo, and musical style based on the interview details.
5. As the volunteer shifts their gaze from one singer to the other, the active singer must freeze mid-lyric, and the newly targeted singer must instantly pick up the song.
6. The receiving singer must match the established rhythm, melody, and rhyme scheme, ideally completing the unfinished word or sentence of their partner.
7. Continue the song for two to three minutes, building to a collaborative, high-energy musical climax before ending on a final, shared note.

## Facilitation Notes
- Side-coach the volunteer to look back and forth deliberately rather than scanning rapidly like a fan, allowing each singer to establish a line or two before switching.
- Encourage the silent singer to actively mouth along or physically support the active singer's rhythm so they are ready to jump in at the exact tempo.
- Pitfall: Singers waiting for a clean sentence break to switch. Fix: Coach them to cut off and pick up mid-word, forcing the partner to complete the syllable (e.g., Singer A: 'She loves to gar-', Singer B: '-den in the yard!').
- Remind performers to make their partner look like a genius by setting up easy rhymes or clear narrative paths rather than trying to trap them with difficult words.

## Variations
- A Cappella Harmony: The silent singer hums a backing harmony or provides a vocal beatbox rhythm while the other sings, switching roles instantly when the gaze shifts.
- Emotional Shift: The volunteer's gaze not only switches the singer but also changes the emotional tone of the song (e.g., Singer A is joyful, Singer B is melodramatic).
- The Trio: Add a third singer, forming a triangle around the volunteer, increasing the complexity of the gaze-tracking.

## Debrief
- How did it feel to have your sentence cut off and completed by your partner? How did you maintain the song's momentum?
- What strategies did you use to make sure your partner was set up for success when the gaze shifted to them?
- How did the volunteer's physical presence and eye contact shape the rhythm and energy of the performance?

## Safety & Inclusion
Ensure the volunteer feels safe and celebrated, not mocked. Clearly explain that they have total control over the song's pacing and can look at whoever they want. If the volunteer has mobility or neck-movement limitations, they can point a finger or hold a physical prop (like a flashlight or card) to direct the singers instead of using eye contact.

## Why It Works
This game forces absolute presence. Because the handoff is dictated by an external force (the volunteer), performers cannot pre-plan their lines. They must listen to the exact syllable, rhythm, and pitch of their partner to ensure a seamless transition, embodying the 'last word response' technique in a musical context.
