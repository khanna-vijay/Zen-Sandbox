# Do You Like Your Neighbor?

<div class="game-hero-ph" markdown>🎨 *Infographic coming soon*</div>

> A high-energy, seat-swapping game of rapid observation, spontaneous sharing, and group connection.

## Overview
Players sit in a circle with one person standing in the center. By asking a seated player if they like their neighbors, the center player triggers a chaotic scramble for seats based on either physical proximity or shared personal traits. The goal is to find a seat, leaving a new player in the center to keep the momentum going.

## What It Trains
- **Domain:** D4 — The Ensemble
- **Principle(s):** Group Mind; The First Thought Is a Gift
- **Skill(s):** Peripheral Awareness; Unfiltered Spontaneity
- **Technique(s):** First Thought drills
- **Focus:** connection

**Objective:** Develops peripheral awareness, rapid physical reaction times, and unfiltered spontaneity while building ensemble cohesion and discovering shared commonalities.

## Setup
Arrange chairs in a tight circle facing inward. There must be exactly one fewer chair than the total number of players. One player starts standing in the center of the circle, while all other players sit.

## How to Play
1. The player standing in the center approaches any seated player and asks, 'Do you like your neighbor?'
2. The seated player must choose one of two answers: 'Yes' or 'No, but I like people who...'
3. If the player answers 'Yes', the asked player and their two immediate neighbors (the players sitting directly to their left and right) must immediately stand up and swap seats with each other.
4. If the player answers 'No, but I like people who [insert characteristic, e.g., wear blue socks, have traveled to Asia, love spicy food]', then every player in the circle who matches that characteristic must stand up and find a new seat.
5. During any seat scramble, the center player attempts to quickly sit in any vacated chair.
6. Players scrambling for seats cannot immediately sit back down in their own chair or their immediate neighbor's chair if they were part of a 'Yes' swap.
7. The player left standing without a chair becomes the new center player and initiates the next round.

## Facilitation Notes
- Encourage the center player to make eye contact and commit to their question quickly to keep the energy high.
- Pitfall: Players taking too long to think of a characteristic. Side-coach: 'Go with your first instinct! It can be as simple as wearing shoes or having brown hair.'
- Ensure safety during the scramble: remind players no diving, pushing, or pulling chairs out from under someone.
- If a 'Yes' is answered, remind the three active players that they must change seats; they cannot simply stand up and sit back down in the same spot.

## Variations
- Strictly No-Yes: Eliminate the 'Yes' option entirely to focus purely on finding shared characteristics and group commonalities.
- Silent Scramble: Play the entire game in complete silence, using gestures or written signs for the characteristics, forcing deep visual peripheral awareness.
- Double Trouble: Have two players in the center and two fewer chairs, increasing the chaos and requiring even sharper spatial awareness.

## Debrief
- How did you use your peripheral vision to spot open seats before others did?
- What did you learn about your ensemble members that surprised you?
- How does hesitating to share a 'first thought' affect the rhythm of the group?

## Safety & Inclusion
Ensure the path between chairs is clear of bags and tripping hazards. For players with mobility constraints, the game can be played with a 'pointing' or 'hand-raising' mechanic, or players can walk at a designated safe pace (e.g., giant steps or slow motion) to ensure equal access and physical safety.

## Why It Works
This game builds 'Group Mind' by combining physical play with personal disclosure. The physical urgency bypasses the analytical brain, forcing players to rely on unfiltered spontaneity. Meanwhile, scanning the circle for open seats trains peripheral awareness and instant spatial adaptation.
