# Consent Architects

> Build safer, bolder scenes by making explicit boundary negotiation a mandatory, active game mechanic.

## Overview
A structured, low-physicality skill drill where players integrate real-time verbal check-ins and boundary adjustments directly into their scene work. By treating consent as an active, creative mechanic rather than an interruption, players learn to prioritize personal comfort over scene momentum. The result is a highly collaborative environment where boundaries inspire, rather than block, narrative discovery.

## What It Trains
- **Domain:** D2 — The Partner
- **Principle(s):** Consent & Boundaries; Truth Over Pandering
- **Skill(s):** Boundary Navigation
- **Technique(s):** Check-ins; Cut calls; Negotiating physical contact
- **Focus:** skill_drill

**Objective:** To master real-time boundary navigation and verbal check-ins, training players to honor their own and others' comfort levels without sacrificing collaborative play.

## Setup
An intimate, in-person playing space with minimal footprint. Arrange 3 to 5 players in a semi-circle. Two players step forward to perform, while the remaining players act as active observers/facilitators. No props or materials are required.

## How to Play
1. Establish a simple, neutral scene premise involving two characters in close proximity (e.g., two coworkers organizing a small storage closet).
2. Optionally, secretly assign one or both players a minor character boundary (e.g., 'your character is highly sensitive to sudden movements' or 'your character dislikes sharing personal items').
3. Begin the scene. Before any player initiates physical contact, enters their partner's immediate personal space, or introduces a highly sensitive emotional topic, they must pause and ask an explicit, out-of-character check-in question (e.g., 'May I put my hand on your shoulder?' or 'Are you comfortable if my character brings up your character's past?').
4. The receiving player must immediately respond with one of three explicit options: 'Yes' (full consent to proceed), 'No' (clear boundary, requiring the initiator to pivot to a different action), or 'Adjust' (proposing a modified version, such as 'You can't touch my shoulder, but you can pat my back').
5. The initiating player must immediately adapt to the response, treating 'No' or 'Adjust' as a creative gift that shifts the scene's direction rather than a narrative block.
6. At any point, if a player feels a boundary has been crossed without a check-in, or if they feel genuine discomfort, they (or any observer) can call out 'Structural Integrity Breach!' to immediately freeze the action.
7. Upon a breach call, the scene resets to the moment right before the boundary was crossed. The players re-play the moment, this time executing a proper check-in and negotiation, with no penalty or shame.

## Facilitation Notes
- Celebrate 'No' and 'Adjust' responses enthusiastically; frame them as exciting narrative pivots that keep the scene safe and unpredictable.
- Ensure players do not over-explain or apologize when stating a boundary or calling a 'Structural Integrity Breach!'; comfort requires no justification.
- Watch for 'consent fatigue' or performative compliance. If players are checking in mechanically without actually assessing their comfort, pause and remind them to connect with their genuine feelings.
- If a player struggles to pivot after a 'No', side-coach them with: 'What is a completely different, non-physical way to show your character's support right now?'

## Variations
- Blind Boundaries: The observers know the players' secret character boundaries, but the players themselves do not, forcing them to rely entirely on real-time check-ins to discover them.
- Non-Verbal Attunement: For advanced players, transition the explicit verbal check-ins into subtle, physical offers and pauses, while keeping the 'Structural Integrity Breach!' call active as a safety net.

## Debrief
- How did it feel to treat 'No' or 'Adjust' as a creative offer rather than a rejection or a block?
- Did you find yourself wanting to pander to the scene's momentum instead of honoring your boundary? What helped you overcome that urge?
- How did calling or hearing 'Structural Integrity Breach!' affect the level of trust you felt with your scene partner?
- In what ways can we bring this level of explicit care and check-in into our regular, unconstrained improv scenes?

## Safety & Inclusion
This game is highly safety-sensitive. Establish a firm rule that personal boundaries always override character boundaries. Players are fully empowered to say 'No' or call a breach based on their real-world comfort at any moment, without explanation or social penalty. Ensure the tone remains supportive, non-judgmental, and focused on mutual care.

## Why It Works
By gamifying consent, this drill removes the social awkwardness of boundary setting. It reframes 'No' from a theatrical block into a generative constraint, proving that scenes can thrive and pivot creatively when players feel entirely safe. This directly reinforces 'Truth Over Pandering' by rewarding authentic comfort over superficial compliance.
