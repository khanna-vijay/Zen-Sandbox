# Restricted Word Count

![Restricted Word Count — infographic](img/D3_P2_S6_T1_G897__word-restriction.jpg){ .game-hero }

> Navigate a scene where every line you speak must meet an exact, unchanging word count.

## Overview
Three players perform a scene where each is assigned a strict, unchanging number of words they must use for every single line of dialogue. The challenge is to build a coherent, grounded base reality while justifying why each character speaks in their specific, restricted pattern.

## What It Trains
- **Domain:** D3 — The Scene
- **Principle(s):** Base Reality First; Yes, And
- **Skill(s):** Justification; Active Listening; Offer Reception; Unfiltered Spontaneity
- **Technique(s):** Justify the absurd
- **Focus:** comedy_game

**Objective:** Develops the ability to justify absurd constraints within a scene, encourages deep active listening, and forces players to make highly deliberate, spontaneous choices under pressure.

## Setup
Three players stand on stage. The facilitator assigns each player a specific, distinct number (typically one low number like 1 or 2, one medium number like 4 or 5, and one higher number like 7 or 8). Get a suggestion for a mundane, non-geographical location to establish the base reality.

## How to Play
1. Assign each of the three players their individual, fixed word count (e.g., Player A has 1 word, Player B has 4 words, Player C has 7 words).
2. Obtain a mundane location suggestion to ground the scene's base reality (e.g., a laundromat, a dentist's waiting room).
3. Begin the scene. Each player must speak only when it is their turn, and every single line they utter must contain exactly their assigned number of words—no more, no less.
4. Focus the initial lines on establishing who the characters are, where they are, and what they are doing, rather than immediately commenting on the restriction.
5. As the scene progresses, players must naturally justify their character's speech pattern through their behavior, emotional state, or physical environment.
6. Listen intently to the preceding line to ensure the dialogue flows logically, adapting your pre-planned word count to directly answer or build upon the previous offer.
7. Continue the scene until a satisfying narrative arc or comedic climax is reached, maintaining the strict word counts throughout.

## Facilitation Notes
- Side-coach players to avoid counting on their fingers visibly; encourage them to internalize the rhythm or justify the finger-counting if they must do it.
- If a player miscounts, do not stop the scene. Encourage them to justify the mistake in-character (e.g., 'I lost my train of thought') or simply keep going without breaking character.
- Remind players to establish a strong base reality first. If they focus entirely on the gimmick, the scene loses its narrative weight.
- Encourage physical action and object work. When dialogue is restricted, physical choices can convey massive amounts of context and subtext.

## Variations
- Dynamic Shift: Halfway through the scene, the facilitator calls out 'Switch!', and players must pass their word counts to the person to their left.
- Emotional Justification: Players must choose an emotion that perfectly explains their word count (e.g., the 1-word player is furious, the 8-word player is anxious and rambling).
- The Silent Partner: One player has a word count of zero, relying entirely on physical offers and object work to drive the scene.

## Debrief
- How did having a strict word count affect your listening? Did you find yourself planning ahead or staying present?
- What strategies did you use to justify why your character spoke in that specific way?
- How did physical action help fill the gaps left by the restricted dialogue?

## Safety & Inclusion
Ensure players feel comfortable with cognitive processing pressure. If a player struggles with rapid counting, allow them to use a lower, easier number, or pair them with a physical-only role.

## Why It Works
By imposing a rigid structural constraint, the brain is forced out of its standard conversational habits. This restriction creates an organic 'game' within the scene. To make the scene work, players must use justification—the core tool of comedy—to turn a mechanical limitation into a believable, funny character trait.
