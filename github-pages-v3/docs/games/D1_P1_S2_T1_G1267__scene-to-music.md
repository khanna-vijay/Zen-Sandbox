# Emotional Soundtrack

![Emotional Soundtrack — infographic](img/D1_P1_S2_T1_G1267__scene-to-music.jpg){ .game-hero }

> Let changing musical genres dictate your emotional intensity and physical choices in a silent scene.

## Overview
Players perform a completely silent scene where their emotional states, physical movements, and relationship dynamics are entirely guided by a changing musical soundtrack. As the facilitator shifts the music, players must instantly adapt their internal emotional dial to match the new auditory landscape.

## What It Trains
- **Domain:** D1 — The Self
- **Principle(s):** Commit 100%; Show, Don't Tell
- **Skill(s):** Emotional Fluidity; Physicality & Space Work; Active Listening
- **Technique(s):** The Emotional Dial (1→10); Character Walks/Centers
- **Focus:** skill_drill

**Objective:** To develop emotional fluidity and physical commitment by using external auditory cues to instantly shift emotional states from 1 to 10 on the emotional dial without relying on verbal explanation.

## Setup
A clear performance space with a moderate footprint. A music player (phone, laptop, or speaker system) preloaded with 4-5 distinct instrumental tracks of contrasting emotional tones (e.g., whimsical, suspenseful, melancholic, triumphant, chaotic). Two to four players stand in the playing area.

## How to Play
1. Two to four players step into the performance space and establish a simple, mundane physical activity, such as folding laundry, preparing a meal, or waiting at a bus stop.
2. The facilitator starts the first music track at a low-to-moderate volume.
3. Players immediately adopt an emotional state that matches the tone of the music, expressing it purely through physicality, facial expressions, and object work.
4. No spoken dialogue is allowed; players must communicate their relationship and reactions entirely through silent action and emotional expression.
5. The facilitator gradually increases the volume or calls out a number from 1 to 10 to prompt players to dial up the intensity of their current emotion.
6. After a minute, the facilitator crossfades or abruptly switches to a completely different musical genre or tempo.
7. Players must instantly transition their emotional state to match the new track, justifying the sudden shift through their physical actions and interactions.
8. The scene continues through three to four musical transitions before the facilitator fades the music to end the scene.

## Facilitation Notes
- Side-coach the physical connection: 'Let the music move your body first, then let the emotion follow.'
- Use the emotional dial: 'Dial it up to an 8! Show us that intensity in your fingertips and posture.'
- Watch out for players trying to mouth words or pantomime complex verbal plots. Remind them to focus on how they are doing the action (the emotional quality) rather than what they are trying to say.
- Address slow, tentative transitions when the music changes by encouraging immediate, impulsive physical shifts the second the beat or tone drops.

## Variations
- Gibberish Soundtrack: Allow players to speak, but only in gibberish, letting the music dictate the vocal tone, volume, and emotional cadence.
- Individual Soundtracks: Assign different musical cues to different players using headphones so they are operating on contrasting emotional wavelengths.
- The Volume Dial: Tie the emotional intensity directly to the physical volume of the music—as the facilitator turns the volume knob up, the players' emotional dial must rise to a 10; as it fades, they drop to a 1.

## Debrief
- How did the music change your physical posture and the speed of your movements?
- What was it like to transition instantly to a new emotion without needing a logical, narrative reason?
- How did you use your partner's physical shifts to support your own emotional journey?

## Safety & Inclusion
Ensure the volume levels are comfortable for all participants, especially those with sensory sensitivities. Encourage players to express high-intensity emotions (like anger or fear) through controlled physical posture and facial expressions rather than sudden, unsafe physical movements or invading personal space.

## Why It Works
By removing verbal dialogue, players are forced to rely on their bodies and faces to communicate. The external stimulus of music bypasses the analytical brain, allowing players to access deep, committed emotional states instantly. This builds the emotional dial muscle, teaching improvisers that they can shift their emotional intensity on a dime simply by committing to a physical state.
