# Zoom In, Zoom Out

![Zoom In, Zoom Out — infographic](img/D1_P1_S3_T2_G1383__zoom-in-zoom-out.jpg){ .game-hero }

> Instantly scale your characters from full-bodied humans to tiny finger puppets without losing dramatic commitment.

## Overview
Players perform a standard relationship-driven scene that is periodically interrupted by a facilitator's call to shift scale. On command, the actors must instantly transition their characters into miniature finger-puppet versions of themselves, then back to full-scale, maintaining the exact narrative thread, emotional stakes, and spatial relationships throughout.

## What It Trains
- **Domain:** D1 — The Self
- **Principle(s):** Commit 100%; Show, Don't Tell
- **Skill(s):** Physicality & Space Work; Vocal Craft; World-Building; Pacing & Rhythm
- **Technique(s):** Object work; Vocal characterization; Edits (Sweep, Tag-Out, Sound/Light)
- **Focus:** mixed

**Objective:** To develop absolute physical commitment and precise object work by translating large-scale physical choices into micro-movements, while training vocal flexibility and narrative continuity under sudden constraints.

## Setup
A clear performance space for two to three active players, with the remaining players observing as an active audience. No props or special materials are required.

## How to Play
1. Select two to three players to step into the performance space and initiate a grounded, relationship-focused scene based on a simple suggestion.
2. Instruct the players to establish clear physical environments, using deliberate object work and full-body movement to define the space.
3. At any point during the scene, the facilitator calls out 'Zoom Out!' to trigger an immediate shift in scale.
4. Upon hearing 'Zoom Out', the players must instantly freeze their physical bodies and bring one or both hands into their personal eye-line, treating their fingers as miniature puppets representing their characters.
5. Players continue the scene seamlessly from the exact line of dialogue where they left off, but now their physical actions, eye contact, and spatial relationships are expressed entirely through their fingers.
6. During the 'Zoomed Out' state, players must adapt their vocal craft—using higher pitches, altered tempos, or distinct vocal textures—while keeping the emotional stakes of the scene completely real.
7. When the facilitator calls 'Zoom In!', players instantly drop their hands and return to full-body physicalization, resuming the scene at normal scale with the same emotional intensity and narrative focus.
8. The facilitator continues to alternate between these two states, challenging the players to maintain narrative momentum and emotional commitment regardless of their physical scale.

## Facilitation Notes
- Side-coach players to keep the stakes high: the finger-puppet version shouldn't become a silly cartoon unless the original scene was already headed there; the emotional truth must remain intact.
- Watch for the 'narrative pause' pitfall: players often stop talking when the scale shifts. Encourage them to treat the transition like a camera cut, continuing the dialogue mid-sentence.
- Remind players to maintain spatial awareness in the micro-scale: if character A was standing to the left of character B in the wide shot, their finger-puppets must mirror that exact spatial relationship.
- Encourage extreme physical commitment: when zoomed in, use the whole body and the stage; when zoomed out, focus intense eye contact on the tiny 'finger' characters to make the audience believe in their reality.

## Variations
- Extreme Zoom: Introduce a third level, 'Super Zoom,' where players must focus on a single extreme close-up, acting using only their facial expressions or eyes to convey the scene.
- Player-Led Zoom: Instead of a facilitator calling the shifts, players can initiate the zoom-in or zoom-out dynamically based on the emotional needs of the scene.
- Object-Centric Zoom: The zoom-out focuses on a specific object in the scene (e.g., a letter being read), and the fingers play the characters interacting on or with that tiny object.

## Debrief
- How did shrinking your physical presence affect the emotional intensity and stakes of your dialogue?
- What did you notice about your vocal choices when you were forced to express your character through your fingers?
- How does committing 100% to a ridiculous physical constraint actually make the scene more engaging for the audience?

## Safety & Inclusion
Ensure players who may have physical limitations with fine motor skills or prolonged standing can adapt the 'Zoom Out' to use larger hand gestures, arm movements, or facial expressions that represent the scale shift without physical strain.

## Why It Works
This game works because it forces players to isolate and translate their physical choices. By shrinking the physical canvas to the size of a hand, players must distill their character's essence, status, and emotion into micro-movements. This extreme contrast highlights the importance of physical commitment and vocal variety, proving that compelling acting relies on focus and intent rather than the size of the stage.
