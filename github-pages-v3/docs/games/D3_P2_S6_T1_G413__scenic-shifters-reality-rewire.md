# Virtual Horizon Shifters

<div class="game-hero-ph" markdown>🎨 *Infographic coming soon*</div>

> Instantly adapt to sudden environmental shifts by justifying the absurd in a virtual space.

## Overview
A fast-paced virtual scene-work game where players use dynamic virtual background changes to disrupt and evolve the physical reality of a scene. Active players must instantly accept these radical environmental shifts, using justification to keep the scene grounded while maintaining their characters' core relationships and objectives. It is an exhilarating exercise in high-speed adaptability and visual yes-and.

## What It Trains
- **Domain:** D3 — The Scene
- **Principle(s):** Yes, And; Base Reality First; Group Mind
- **Skill(s):** Active Listening; Offer Reception; World-Building; Justification; Peripheral Awareness
- **Technique(s):** Endowment-acceptance; C.R.O.W. (Character, Relationship, Objective, Where); Justify the absurd
- **Focus:** mixed

**Objective:** To develop the skill of immediate justification of absurd or sudden environmental changes, reinforcing the establishment of a strong base reality that can withstand and integrate unexpected physical offers.

## Setup
Played on a video conferencing platform in Gallery View. Before starting, each player must pre-load 10 to 15 highly distinct virtual backgrounds (such as a desert, an underwater scene, a medieval castle, or a giant kitchen sink). Players should select 5 power backgrounds that offer high visual contrast and clear narrative stakes. In the meeting chat, establish a clear, sequential order of players who will act as the shifters.

## How to Play
1. Establish the Base: The first player in the chat order activates a distinct virtual background to set the initial location, and a second player joins them on screen.
2. Define the Reality: These two active players quickly establish their characters, their relationship, and their immediate objective, grounding themselves in this initial where.
3. The Shift: While the scene is in progress, the next player in the chat queue prepares their next background. When they spot a narrative opportunity, they instantly change their virtual background to a radically different environment.
4. Immediate Acceptance: The active players must instantly accept this new visual reality without breaking character or pausing the scene's momentum.
5. Justify the Absurd: The active players must immediately justify why their current scene is now happening in this new location (for example, if a cozy living room suddenly becomes a volcanic crater, they might justify that their home heating system has severely malfunctioned).
6. The Shifter's Entry: The player who initiated the shift must immediately deliver a line of dialogue or physical action that connects their character to this new environment, entering the scene as a supporting character or environmental force.
7. Maintain the Core: Throughout these environmental disruptions, players must keep their characters' original emotional relationships and objectives intact, letting the environment transform how they pursue their goals, not what those goals are.
8. Scene Resolution: After 3 to 5 major environmental shifts, the facilitator calls Scene or Wipe to bring the scene to a close.

## Facilitation Notes
- Side-Coaching Cue: Don't deny the change! If you are suddenly underwater, start swimming or explain why you aren't wet yet!
- Pitfall and Fix: Players sometimes abandon their characters' relationships when the background changes. Remind them: The environment changes, but your feelings about each other stay the same. Use the new space to express those feelings.
- Side-Coaching Cue: Justify immediately! The first explanation that pops into your head is the right one. Commit to it fully.
- Pitfall and Fix: Technical lag can stall the game. Fix this by instructing players to use the chat to type SHIFTING right before they change their background, giving a clear visual cue to the active players.

## Variations
- The Interruption Shift: Allow any player to jump the queue and change their background out of order if they have an incredibly high-stakes visual offer, moving to the back of the line afterward.
- Emotional Landscapes: Instead of literal locations, players use abstract color or pattern backgrounds, and the active players must instantly shift the emotional temperature of the scene to match the new visual aesthetic.
- Object Manifestation: Players use backgrounds featuring a single giant object (such as a massive rubber duck) and must justify how that object has suddenly appeared in their space and how they must interact with it.

## Debrief
- How did it feel to have your physical reality completely rewritten mid-sentence, and what strategies helped you justify the change instantly?
- How did maintaining your character's core relationship help ground the scene when the environment became absurd?
- What did you notice about the balance between verbal dialogue and visual offers during the shifts?

## Safety & Inclusion
Ensure all players are comfortable with virtual backgrounds and that their hardware supports them without causing severe system lag. If a player's system cannot support virtual backgrounds, they can participate by holding up physical drawings, colored paper, or household objects close to the camera to create manual environmental shifts.

## Why It Works
By forcing players to accept sudden, uncontrollable visual changes, the game bypasses the analytical mind and demands instant justification. It teaches that any offer—no matter how absurd or disruptive—can be integrated into a scene if the players remain committed to their base reality and emotional relationships. The visual nature of the platform becomes an asset, training peripheral awareness and active offer reception in a digital space.
