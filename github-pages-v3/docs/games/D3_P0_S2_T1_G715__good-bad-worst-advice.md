# Good, Bad, Worst Advice

![Good, Bad, Worst Advice — infographic](img/D3_P0_S2_T1_G715__good-bad-worst-advice.jpg){ .game-hero }

> Escalate comedic solutions to an audience member's problem through a structured ladder of absurdity.

## Overview
A panel of three experts takes a real, minor problem from the audience and offers solutions in sequence. The advice escalates from genuinely helpful, to terribly misguided, to utterly catastrophic. It is a fast-paced comedy game that teaches players how to build comedic tension and heighten a premise step-by-step.

## What It Trains
- **Domain:** D3 — The Scene
- **Principle(s):** The First Thought Is a Gift; The Audience Is the Final Scene Partner
- **Skill(s):** Heightening & Exploration; Unfiltered Spontaneity; Stage Presence & Clarity
- **Technique(s):** The 'ladder' (escalating beats); First Thought drills; Projection

**Objective:** Develops the ability to heighten and explore a premise using the 'ladder' technique, while practicing unfiltered spontaneity and maintaining strong stage presence.

## Setup
Three players sit or stand in a line facing the audience. A facilitator stands to the side to host and gather suggestions. No props are required, though three chairs can be used for the panel.

## How to Play
1. Position three players shoulder-to-shoulder facing the audience, designating them from left to right as Good, Bad, and Worst Advice.
2. Ask the audience to suggest a minor, everyday problem or inconvenience, such as losing car keys or oversleeping.
3. Have the first player offer genuinely helpful, practical, and logical advice to solve the problem.
4. Have the second player offer bad advice that is unhelpful, slightly mischievous, or counterproductive, but still grounded in the original problem.
5. Have the third player deliver the worst advice possible, escalating the premise to an absurd, chaotic, or catastrophic extreme.
6. Repeat the process with a new audience suggestion, optionally rotating the players' roles so everyone experiences a different level of the ladder.

## Facilitation Notes
- Coaching cue: 'Don't peak too early!' Remind the Bad Advice player to leave room for the Worst Advice player to escalate.
- Coaching cue: 'Commit to the expertise.' The comedy lands best when the absurd advice is delivered with absolute professional confidence.
- Pitfall: The Good Advice player tries to be funny. Fix: Insist that the first response must be completely boring and practical to establish a strong baseline.
- Pitfall: The Worst Advice player goes completely off-topic. Fix: Remind them that the final escalation must still directly address the original problem, just in the most disastrous way possible.

## Variations
- Role Rotation: Shift players one position to the right after each round so they rotate through Good, Bad, and Worst roles.
- Expert Personas: Assign specific character archetypes to each panelist to filter their advice through, such as a drill sergeant or a Victorian aristocrat.
- Audience Cross-Examination: Allow the audience member to ask a single follow-up question to one panelist, forcing them to justify their absurd advice on the spot.

## Debrief
- How did having a grounded, realistic starting point make the final escalation funnier?
- For the Bad Advice player, how did it feel to deliberately hold back your wildest ideas to support the Worst Advice player?
- How does maintaining a confident, expert persona help the audience accept highly absurd ideas?

## Safety & Inclusion
Ensure the audience suggestions are kept to minor, low-stakes inconveniences. The facilitator should explicitly steer the audience away from heavy personal crises, medical issues, or sensitive relationship problems to keep the play space safe and light.

## Why It Works
This game physicalizes the 'ladder' technique of comedic heightening. By structuring the responses into distinct, escalating tiers, players learn the value of patience in comedy, discovering that the biggest laugh relies on a solid, grounded setup and a disciplined step-by-step escalation.
