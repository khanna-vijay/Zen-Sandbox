# Shared Adventure

![Shared Adventure — infographic](img/D2_P2_S4_T1_G835__shared-holiday.jpg){ .game-hero }

> Co-create a wild, fictional memory with your partner using the ultimate rule of agreement.

## Overview
In this exercise, pairs of players collaborate to recall a fictional trip or event they supposedly took together in the past. By starting every single sentence with the phrase 'Yes, and...', they instantly validate their partner's contributions and build an increasingly imaginative, shared history.

## What It Trains
- **Domain:** D2 — The Partner
- **Principle(s):** Yes, And; Make Your Partner a Genius; The First Thought Is a Gift
- **Skill(s):** Offer Reception; Active Listening; Unfiltered Spontaneity; World-Building
- **Technique(s):** Yes, And… sentence games; First Thought drills; C.R.O.W. (Character, Relationship, Objective, Where)
- **Focus:** connection

**Objective:** To practice absolute agreement and immediate offer reception, training players to accept their partner's ideas as absolute truth and build upon them without hesitation.

## Setup
Players stand or sit in pairs facing each other. No props or special staging are required; the game works in any open space.

## How to Play
1. Divide the group into pairs and have them stand or sit facing one another.
2. Player A initiates the conversation by establishing a fictional shared memory from a past trip or event.
3. Player B must respond by starting their sentence with the exact words, 'Yes, and...' followed by a new detail that builds on Player A's premise.
4. Player A then responds with 'Yes, and...' adding another detail, accepting everything Player B just established as absolute truth.
5. The partners continue to alternate back and forth, with every single line beginning with 'Yes, and...' to construct a detailed, chronological narrative of their shared adventure.
6. Encourage players to keep their sentences relatively short to allow for rapid-fire exchange and to prevent one person from dominating the narrative.
7. Run the exercise for two to three minutes, or until the story reaches a natural, high-energy climax.

## Facilitation Notes
- Coaching Cue: Listen to the last word your partner said and build directly from it, rather than planning your next line while they speak.
- Pitfall: Players saying 'Yes, but...' or subtly blocking by changing the subject. Fix: Remind them that 'Yes, and' means expanding the current reality, not shutting it down or rushing to escape it.
- Coaching Cue: Treat your partner's ideas as absolute genius. If they say you fought a giant squid, you fought a giant squid!
- Pitfall: Hesitation or trying to think of the 'perfect' funny line. Fix: Encourage players to use their very first thought, no matter how mundane or bizarre.

## Variations
- Emotional Shift: Play the same game, but each 'Yes, and...' must escalate a specific shared emotion, such as growing increasingly terrified, excited, or suspicious.
- Physicalized Memory: Have the pairs physically act out the activities they are describing as they recall them, using object work to match the verbal narrative.
- The Worst Trip Ever: Instead of a fun vacation, players recall a disastrous event, but still must enthusiastically 'Yes, and...' every terrible detail.

## Debrief
- How did it feel to have every single one of your contributions immediately accepted and validated?
- Did you find yourself planning ahead, and what happened to the flow of the story when you did?
- How does starting with 'Yes, and...' change your mental state compared to how we normally negotiate or converse in daily life?

## Safety & Inclusion
While this game is low-risk, remind players to keep the shared memories safe and comfortable for both participants, avoiding topics that touch on real-world trauma or personal boundaries unless explicitly agreed upon.

## Why It Works
By forcing the literal use of 'Yes, and...', the game bypasses the analytical brain's tendency to edit, judge, or block. It establishes a safe, collaborative space where there are no mistakes, only building blocks, demonstrating how shared agreement naturally generates rich world-building.
