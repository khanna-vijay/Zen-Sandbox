# Silly, Stinky, Sexy

![Silly, Stinky, Sexy — infographic](img/D2_P3_S5_T1_G1275__silly-stinky-sexy.jpg){ .game-hero }

> Endow your scene partners with distinct, hilarious traits and watch them react to your gifts.

## Overview
In this high-energy endowment game, four players enter a shared social scene where they must treat each of their scene partners as either silly, stinky, or sexy. Players do not announce their choices; instead, they must show these perceptions through physical reactions, dialogue, and status play, forcing their partners to discover and inhabit their assigned traits.

**Objective:** Develops active gifting, endowment, and offer reception by forcing players to define their partners' characteristics through physical and verbal reactions rather than self-generating their own characters.

## Setup
Four players stand on stage. The facilitator establishes a simple, high-occupancy social setting (e.g., a backyard barbecue, a gallery opening, or a waiting room). Before starting, each player secretly decides which of the three traits—Silly, Stinky, or Sexy—they will project onto each of the other three players (one unique trait per partner).

## How to Play
1. Assign the four players to the stage and gather the remaining group as the audience.
2. Select a common social setting where characters can easily mingle and interact in pairs or small groups.
3. Instruct each player to secretly assign one of the three target traits—Silly, Stinky, or Sexy—to each of their three scene partners, ensuring every partner is assigned a different trait.
4. Begin the scene with players interacting naturally within the setting, but making sure their behavior toward each partner heavily reflects the secretly assigned trait.
5. Show, don't tell: Players must not explicitly name the traits. Instead, use physical reactions (like plugging a nose or swooning) and suggestive dialogue.
6. As players receive these endowments, they must accept them and lean into the behavior they are being gifted, adjusting their physicality and tone to match how they are being treated.
7. The scene continues for three to five minutes, allowing all players to interact with one another and establish the web of relationships.
8. Freeze the scene and ask the audience to identify which traits were projected onto whom, then have the players share what they felt they were being endowed with.

## Facilitation Notes
- Side-coach physical commitment: Remind players to use their whole bodies to show their reactions, such as holding breath for stinky or giggling for silly.
- Pitfall: Players explicitly naming the trait. Fix: If a player says 'You smell bad,' pause the scene and ask them to show it physically instead.
- Encourage variety: Ensure players do not just focus on one partner; they must actively cycle through all three partners to establish all three endowments.
- Side-coach the receiver: If a player is being treated as sexy, they should start behaving more confidently or seductively, validating their partner's gift.

## Variations
- Blind Endowment: The players do not choose. Instead, the audience secretly assigns one trait to each player, and players must discover their own identity solely based on how others treat them.
- Emotional Triad: Replace Silly, Stinky, Sexy with three distinct emotional states, such as Terrified, Furious, and Joyful.
- Status Triad: Replace the traits with High Status, Equal Status, and Low Status to practice subtle power dynamics.

## Debrief
- How did it feel to have your character's traits defined entirely by how others treated you?
- What physical or verbal cues were the most effective in communicating an endowment without using explicit words?
- How does making your partner look silly, stinky, or sexy make them look like a genius on stage?

## Safety & Inclusion
Because 'sexy' is one of the traits, establish clear boundaries before playing. Remind players that 'sexy' should be portrayed through safe, non-contact choices (such as swooning, dramatic sighing, verbal flattery, or maintaining a respectful physical distance) rather than unwanted physical touch. Players can opt to substitute 'sexy' with 'genius' or 'terrifying' if it fits the group's comfort level better.

## Why It Works
This game works because it shifts the burden of character creation from the individual to the group. By focusing entirely on making their partners look a certain way (Active Gifting), players bypass their own analytical minds. It embodies the principle of 'Make Your Partner a Genius' by proving that a character is defined not by what they say about themselves, but by how the world reacts to them.
