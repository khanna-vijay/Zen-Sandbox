# Ecosystem

<div class="game-hero-ph" markdown>🎨 *Infographic coming soon*</div>

> Embody a shifting environment collectively through non-verbal sound and movement to build deep ensemble connection.

## Overview
In this immersive ensemble exercise, players work together to physically and sonically manifest a complex, changing environment without using traditional characters or dialogue. By shifting from subtle ambient textures to dynamic, synchronized events, the group tells the story of a location solely through its physical and auditory evolution.

## What It Trains
- **Domain:** D4 — The Ensemble
- **Principle(s):** Group Mind; Follow the Follower; Serve the Piece; Show, Don't Tell
- **Skill(s):** Peripheral Awareness; Support Work; Suggestion Deconstruction (A-to-C); Pacing & Rhythm; Thematic Synthesis; Physicality & Space Work; World-Building
- **Technique(s):** Playing architecture/objects; A-to-C drills; Object work
- **Focus:** connection

**Objective:** To cultivate Group Mind and advanced Support Work by training players to surrender individual narrative control, play the physical architecture of a space, and respond instantly to collective shifts in rhythm and atmosphere.

## Setup
An open, clear floor space where 5 to 12 players can stand in a loose circle or semi-circle, ensuring everyone has a clear line of sight to all other participants. No props or materials are required.

## How to Play
1. The facilitator provides a broad, evocative environmental prompt (such as a decaying clockwork factory or an ancient, deep-sea trench) and asks players to stand in a loose circle.
2. Players close their eyes for ten seconds to mentally visualize the textures, temperatures, sounds, and rhythms of this specific environment.
3. Upon opening their eyes, players simultaneously begin generating subtle, ambient sounds and minimal, repetitive physical movements that establish the baseline atmosphere of the space.
4. Players must avoid creating distinct human characters or speaking words; instead, they become the physical architecture, weather, machinery, or flora of the location.
5. Once the baseline atmosphere is established, players begin to introduce more specific, localized elements (such as a sudden metallic clank or a branch bending under weight) while maintaining the overall ambient texture.
6. The group initiates collective shifts in the environment's state—such as a sudden storm, a power surge, or the transition from day to night—by observing and instantly mirroring or complementing the physical and sonic changes initiated by others.
7. The narrative of the space progresses through these shared, non-verbal transitions, building to a natural climax of energy or decay.
8. The piece concludes organically when the group collectively senses the narrative arc is complete, gradually fading their sounds and movements into absolute stillness and silence.

## Facilitation Notes
- Side-coaching cue: 'Listen to the whole room, not just your immediate neighbor. Let your sound fill the gaps rather than competing for volume.'
- Side-coaching cue: 'If someone initiates a physical shift, like a sudden gust of wind, let it ripple through your body. Follow the follower.'
- Pitfall: Players trying to play human characters or using dialogue. Fix: Remind them that they are the environment itself, not the people visiting it. Redirect them to object work or abstract sound.
- Pitfall: The environment remains static and monotonous. Fix: Introduce side-coaching prompts to trigger change, such as 'The temperature suddenly drops ten degrees' or 'Night is falling fast.'

## Variations
- Silent Ecosystem: Run the entire exercise with zero vocal or physical sound, relying entirely on physical movement, breath, and spatial relationships.
- External Catalyst: The facilitator periodically calls out external events (such as 'A heavy shadow passes overhead' or 'An earthquake begins') to test how the ecosystem collectively adapts and absorbs the shock.
- Emotional Weather: Assign a shifting emotional arc to the environment (such as starting with serene calm, escalating to high anxiety, and resolving in melancholy) to guide the physical and sonic progression.

## Debrief
- How did we know when to transition the environment's state without anyone speaking or leading?
- What did it feel like to support the scene by being the physical architecture rather than a character with dialogue?
- How did you use your peripheral vision and hearing to ensure your contribution blended with the group instead of standing out?
- At what point did you feel the 'Group Mind' take over, where the environment seemed to move as a single organism?

## Safety & Inclusion
Ensure players are mindful of physical boundaries as they move and sway. Offer low-impact alternatives for players with mobility limitations, emphasizing that subtle, localized movements (like finger taps or head tilts) or purely vocal contributions are incredibly valuable to the collective texture.

## Why It Works
By stripping away dialogue and individual character agendas, the game forces players to rely entirely on sensory attunement. Embodying the architecture of a space requires players to practice 'A-to-C' suggestion deconstruction, translating a simple prompt into rich, physicalized details. Because there is no single leader, the group must practice 'follow the follower' mechanics, which builds a profound sense of shared responsibility and Group Mind.
