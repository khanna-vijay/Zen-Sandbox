# The Digital Canvas

> Co-create rich, immersive virtual environments using everyday desk objects, custom names, and digital backdrops.

## Overview
The Digital Canvas is a fast-paced, collaborative world-building game designed specifically for virtual spaces. Players work together to construct a detailed base reality by sequentially adding environmental details, characters, and physical props. By utilizing the native features of video conferencing platforms, participants transform their immediate physical and digital surroundings into a shared, imaginative landscape.

## What It Trains
- **Domain:** D3 — The Scene
- **Principle(s):** Yes, And; Make Your Partner a Genius; Base Reality First; Group Mind
- **Skill(s):** Active Listening; Offer Reception; Active Gifting; World-Building; Justification; Support Work
- **Technique(s):** Last Word Response; Endowment-acceptance; Endowment-gifting drills; C.R.O.W. (Character, Relationship, Objective, Where); Endowment chains; Justify the absurd; Playing architecture/objects
- **Focus:** mixed

**Objective:** To establish a robust, detailed Base Reality First using the C.R.O.W. framework (Character, Relationship, Objective, Where) in a virtual environment, while practicing active listening and rapid offer endowment.

## Setup
Players join a video call in gallery view. Each player should have two to three mundane household or office objects (like a pen, mug, or stapler) within arm's reach. Ensure all participants know how to rename themselves, change their virtual background, and access the text chat. The facilitator acts as the director and queue manager.

## How to Play
1. The facilitator establishes the initial 'Where' by announcing a specific, evocative location, such as the control room of an underwater research station or an ancient, dusty library vault.
2. To enter the scene and contribute, players type a designated keyword like 'SCULPT' into the meeting chat to join a virtual queue.
3. The facilitator calls on players in the order of the chat queue, spotlighting the active player to focus the group's attention.
4. When spotlighted, the player has up to 45 seconds to make a single, clear 'Yes, And' contribution that builds directly on what has already been established.
5. The player must choose one of three ways to contribute: change their virtual background to represent a specific environmental detail, rename themselves to embody a new character with a quick line of dialogue, or hold up a mundane physical object and endow it as a fantastical prop.
6. Every contribution must explicitly reference and validate the previous player's addition, ensuring the C.R.O.W. elements remain connected and coherent.
7. The facilitator maintains a brisk, energetic pace, gently calling 'Next!' if a player hesitates or overthinks their contribution.
8. Once a rich, multi-layered scene has been fully established with characters, relationships, and a clear environment (typically after 5 to 7 minutes), the facilitator calls 'Scene!' to reset for a new round.

## Facilitation Notes
- Encourage players to focus on the 'Where' and 'Character' early in the round to ground the scene before introducing complex props.
- If a player struggles to find a virtual background quickly, coach them to focus on renaming themselves or using a physical desk prop instead to avoid technical lag.
- Remind players to 'endow' their physical objects with sensory details like weight, temperature, or smell to make the virtual prop feel real to the audience.
- If players introduce random, disconnected elements, side-coach with 'How does that connect to the previous element?' to enforce the Base Reality.

## Variations
- C.R.O.W. Focus: Assign specific roles to the queue where the first player must establish the 'Where', the second the 'Character', the third the 'Relationship', and the fourth the 'Objective'.
- Blind Props: Players must grab an object behind their back without looking, bring it to the screen, and immediately justify its presence in the scene.

## Debrief
- How did anchoring our scene in a clear 'Where' first make it easier to introduce characters and props?
- What strategies did you use to ensure your digital or physical offer directly supported the previous player's contribution?
- How did the visual elements like backgrounds, physical props, and name changes affect your ability to actively listen and react?

## Safety & Inclusion
Ensure players are comfortable showing their physical spaces or using virtual backgrounds. If a player cannot use virtual backgrounds due to hardware limitations, encourage them to focus entirely on renaming or physical prop endowment. Remind players to only use safe, non-hazardous household items as props.

## Why It Works
By forcing players to make distinct, visible choices like changing a background, renaming, or presenting an object, the game externalizes the internal process of scene-building. It relies on the 'Base Reality First' principle, ensuring that characters and relationships are grounded in a tangible, shared environment. The structured turn-taking mitigates virtual lag, allowing the group mind to flourish without conversational overlap.
