# Sevens

> A high-energy, fast-paced counting game where mistakes are celebrated with a joyful lap of honor.

## Overview
Players stand in a circle and pass a count from one to seven using physical shoulder taps to dictate direction. The number seven triggers a double-handed point that redirects the count across the circle, and any mistake is met with a celebratory run around the outside of the group. It transforms errors into moments of high-energy connection and shared laughter.

## Setup
Players stand in a clear, open circle with enough space behind them to safely run or jog around the perimeter. No props or materials are required.

## How to Play
1. Gather the group into a standing circle, ensuring there is a clear path behind the players to run around the outside.
2. Explain the basic counting mechanic: players will count sequentially from one to seven, with each player saying one number in turn.
3. Introduce the direction mechanic: when saying numbers one through six, the player must tap one of their own shoulders. Tapping the left shoulder passes the count to the player on their left; tapping the right shoulder passes it to the right.
4. Introduce the 'seven' rule: the player who receives the number seven must say 'seven' while raising both hands, pointing one hand left or right to indicate direction, and the other hand directly at any player across the circle to pass the next 'one' to them.
5. Establish the 'flub' rule: if a player hesitates, says the wrong number, taps the wrong shoulder, or misses their cue, they must immediately shout 'I flubbed!' and run a quick, celebratory lap around the outside of the circle.
6. While the player is running their lap, the remaining players cheer them on, and the game immediately restarts at 'one' with the person to the runner's left or right.
7. Once the runner returns to their spot, they seamlessly rejoin the circle, ready to play again without any penalty or shame.

## Facilitation Notes
- Encourage players to run their lap with high energy—arms raised, smiling, or high-fiving—to physically reinforce the concept of failing joyfully.
- Watch out for players who hesitate or overthink to avoid making mistakes; coach them to prioritize speed and rhythm over perfection.
- If the group gets stuck on a loop of constant mistakes, slow the initial tempo down slightly, then challenge them to speed up once they find a collective rhythm.
- Ensure the rest of the circle actively cheers for the runner rather than staying silent, which builds the 'Group Mind' and removes any sense of isolation.

## Variations
- Days of the Week: Replace numbers 1-7 with Monday through Sunday, keeping the same shoulder-tap and pointing rules.
- Alphabet Run: Use letters A through G instead of numbers, resetting to A after G is pointed across the circle.
- Category Mashup: Alternate categories on each count (e.g., 1, Tuesday, C, 4, Friday, F, 7), requiring intense focus and rapid cognitive shifting.

## Debrief
- How did it feel to run the lap after making a mistake compared to how you usually feel when messing up?
- How did the group's cheering affect your recovery time and willingness to take risks?
- What does this game teach us about how we should treat mistakes during an active improv scene?

## Safety & Inclusion
Ensure the running path around the circle is completely clear of tripping hazards. For players with mobility limitations, the 'lap' can be modified to a joyful spin in place, a dramatic bow, or a high-energy vocal celebration supported by the group.

## Why It Works
By pairing a cognitive mistake with an immediate, high-energy physical action (the lap) and positive group reinforcement (cheering), the game rewires the brain's threat response to errors. Instead of freezing or spiraling into self-judgment, players practice rapid self-recovery and learn to associate mistakes with playfulness and connection.
