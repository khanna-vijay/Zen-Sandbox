# Soundtrack Roulette

![Soundtrack Roulette — infographic](img/D3_P1_S5_T1_G836__shuffle-mix-tape.jpg){ .game-hero }

> Let random musical tracks dictate the physical, emotional, and verbal landscape of your scenes.

## Overview
Soundtrack Roulette is a performance format where scenes are initiated and guided by random musical tracks suggested by the audience. Players begin in silence, translating the music's tempo, genre, and mood into physical relationships and environment work. As the music fades, dialogue begins mid-action, only to return to physical-only expression when the music swells again.

## What It Trains
- **Domain:** D3 — The Scene
- **Principle(s):** Show, Don't Tell; Start in the Middle; Vulnerability; The Audience Is the Final Scene Partner
- **Skill(s):** World-Building; Physicality & Space Work; Emotional Fluidity; Active Listening; Format Literacy
- **Technique(s):** C.R.O.W. (Character, Relationship, Objective, Where); Object work; Longform vs. shortform mechanics
- **Focus:** mixed

**Objective:** To develop physical world-building, emotional fluidity, and the ability to 'start in the middle' by using external audio cues to establish C.R.O.W. (Character, Relationship, Objective, Where) before any words are spoken.

## Setup
An in-person performance space with a clear stage area and seating for an audience. You will need a sound system connected to a digital music player, plus pens and slips of paper for the audience. Before the session, have the audience write down their favorite songs. A designated technician or facilitator compiles these tracks into a randomized playlist.

## How to Play
1. Collect song suggestions from the audience on slips of paper before the performance begins, and have a technician quickly queue them up in a digital playlist.
2. Two players step onto the stage. The technician plays a random song from the playlist at a moderate-to-high volume.
3. The players immediately begin a silent, physical scene inspired by the music's tempo, mood, and texture, using object work and movement to establish their characters, relationship, and environment (C.R.O.W.).
4. Treat the music as cinematic underscoring; the characters do not hear the music itself, but their emotional states and physical pacing are deeply influenced by it.
5. After 30 to 60 seconds of physical-only play, the technician slowly fades the music down to a low background level.
6. As the music fades, the players immediately begin speaking, launching directly into the middle of an ongoing conversation or conflict that matches their established physical reality.
7. After a minute or two of dialogue, the technician fades the music back up to full volume (optionally accompanied by a lighting shift, such as a colored wash).
8. The players instantly cease all dialogue and return to pure physical expression, heightening the emotional stakes and bringing the scene to a silent, visually striking climax before the track is cut.

## Facilitation Notes
- Side-coaching cue: 'Let the music move your body before it moves your mind. Don't plan a plot; let the rhythm dictate your posture and speed.'
- Common Pitfall: Players pantomiming that they are listening to the radio or dancing to the music. Fix: Remind them that this is a movie soundtrack. The characters are living their lives, unaware of the score, but the audience experiences their emotions through it.
- Side-coaching cue: 'Start in the middle! When the dialogue begins, don't say "Hello, how are you?" Start with a line that assumes you've been talking for ten minutes.'
- Technician Tip: The technician is a vital scene partner. They must actively listen to the scene's energy to time the volume fades and swells effectively.

## Variations
- Genre Swap: Instead of audience-suggested songs, use highly specific cinematic genres (e.g., horror, spaghetti western, sci-fi synth) to push players into distinct stylistic worlds.
- Multi-Song Medley: Run a longer scene where the technician changes the song three or four times, forcing the players to seamlessly transition their emotional states and physical pacing mid-scene.
- Group Ensemble: Expand the scene to four or more players, focusing on group choreography and stage pictures during the high-volume musical segments.

## Debrief
- How did starting with pure physicality change how you established your character's relationship and environment?
- What was the effect of entering the dialogue 'in the middle' rather than building up to it?
- How did the music help you access emotional vulnerability or physical choices you might not normally make?

## Safety & Inclusion
Ensure the physical space is clear of hazards, as players will be moving dynamically with varying levels of speed and intensity. Encourage players to set physical boundaries before the scene, especially since the high-energy music can sometimes lead to rapid physical escalation. Remind players that physical contact must remain safe and consensual, regardless of the music's intensity.

## Why It Works
By stripping away dialogue at the start, players are forced to rely on physical world-building and emotional expression to communicate. This directly trains the 'Show, Don't Tell' principle. When dialogue is introduced, the physical foundation is already so strong that the C.R.O.W. elements are instantly clear to the audience, allowing the scene to start in the middle with high stakes and deep vulnerability.
