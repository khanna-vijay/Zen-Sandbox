# Sound Effects

![Sound Effects — infographic](img/D3_P2_S6_T1_G841__sound-effects.jpg){ .game-hero }

> Two actors perform a scene while off-stage partners vocally generate all their environmental sounds.

## Overview
In this classic comedy game, two performers improvise a grounded scene while two off-stage players provide live, vocal sound effects. The magic of the exercise lies in the immediate, seamless justification of unexpected, delayed, or bizarre sounds, turning auditory surprises into core elements of the scene's reality.

## What It Trains
- **Domain:** D3 — The Scene
- **Principle(s):** Yes, And; Make Your Partner a Genius; Base Reality First; Group Mind
- **Skill(s):** Active Listening; Offer Reception; World-Building; Justification; Support Work
- **Technique(s):** Endowment-acceptance; C.R.O.W. (Character, Relationship, Objective, Where); Justify the absurd; Playing architecture/objects
- **Focus:** comedy_game

**Objective:** To master the art of justification and active listening by treating external, unpredictable auditory inputs as absolute truths that must be integrated into the scene's base reality.

## Setup
A clear performance space for two actors, with two off-stage players positioned at the side where they have a clear, unobstructed view of the actors' physical movements.

## How to Play
1. Select four players: two to act on stage, and two to stand off-stage as the dedicated sound-effect makers.
2. Obtain a simple suggestion of a location and a relationship to establish a grounded base reality.
3. The on-stage actors begin playing a realistic scene, establishing their environment and activities through clear object work.
4. The sound-effect makers closely watch the physical actions of the actors and provide synchronized vocal sound effects for every movement.
5. The sound-effect makers can also introduce unsolicited environmental sounds to challenge the actors and push the narrative forward.
6. The actors must immediately accept every sound effect as a real event in their world, justifying why it happened and letting it alter their emotional state.
7. The actors should actively set up the sound-effect makers by miming actions that demand sounds or verbally prompting them.
8. The scene continues for several minutes, building on the established relationship and the escalating absurdity of the justified sounds, until a natural comedic climax.

## Facilitation Notes
- Coaching cue: 'Don't ignore the sound! If you hear a squeak when you sit down, explain why your chair squeaked or what you sat on.'
- Pitfall: Actors ignoring a delayed or mismatched sound. Fix: Side-coach them to pause and address it: 'What was that noise?'
- Coaching cue: 'Sound makers, watch their hands! Match the physical action first before throwing in wild surprises.'
- Pitfall: Sound makers dominating the scene with constant noise, leaving no room for dialogue. Fix: Remind them to support the scene, not drown it out.

## Variations
- Audience Foley: Use audience members instead of trained improvisers to make the sounds, which increases the unpredictability and requires even higher justification skills from the actors.
- Delayed Reaction: The sound makers intentionally delay their sounds by 2-3 seconds, forcing the actors to justify why their action had a delayed auditory consequence.
- Mismatched Sounds: The sound makers intentionally make sounds that do not match the mimed action, forcing immediate, absurd justification.

## Debrief
- How did justifying a 'mistake' or mismatched sound actually help build the scene's reality?
- What did it feel like to share control of your physical actions with someone off-stage?
- How does active listening change when you have to listen to both your scene partner and the environment?

## Safety & Inclusion
Ensure the off-stage sound makers are physically positioned where they can clearly see the actors' movements without straining. If using audience members, treat their contributions with high respect and never make them the butt of a joke.

## Why It Works
It forces players to practice 'Yes, And' in its purest form. By treating every sound—accidental or intentional—as an absolute truth, players learn that there are no mistakes in improv, only opportunities for creative justification. It builds a strong base reality because the physical environment becomes highly active and consequential.
