# Waking Moments

![Waking Moments — infographic](img/D3_P2_S5_T1_G717__good-morning.jpg){ .game-hero }

> Establish a rich, physical base reality before speaking a single word of dialogue.

## Overview
Two players begin a scene asleep on stage. Upon waking at the facilitator's cue, they must silently establish their characters, relationship, and environment through physical behavior and spatial awareness. Only when the facilitator permits speech do they begin talking, carrying their established physical dynamics and emotional states into a fully realized scene.

## What It Trains
- **Domain:** D3 — The Scene
- **Principle(s):** Base Reality First; Make Your Partner a Genius; The First Thought Is a Gift
- **Skill(s):** World-Building; Active Listening; Physicality & Space Work; Game Identification
- **Technique(s):** C.R.O.W. (Character, Relationship, Objective, Where); Meisner Repetition; Character Walks/Centers; Finding & Playing the Game
- **Focus:** skill_drill

**Objective:** To build a strong, physicalized base reality (C.R.O.W.) and establish character dynamics and environment before relying on verbal exposition.

## Setup
An open performance space with two chairs or space on the floor where two players can comfortably simulate sleeping. The rest of the group observes as the audience.

## How to Play
1. Two players take the stage and position themselves as if they are fast asleep, using chairs or the floor.
2. The facilitator calls out 'Good morning,' signaling the players to 'wake up' and begin the exercise.
3. Players must remain completely silent during the initial waking phase, using only physical movement, facial expressions, and object work to explore their environment.
4. During this silent phase, players must establish their individual physical states (e.g., exhausted, frantic, meticulous) and discover their relationship to each other and the space (C.R.O.W.).
5. Players must actively listen to and mirror or complement each other's physical choices, treating every movement as a gift that defines the shared reality.
6. Once a clear physical dynamic and environment are established, the facilitator calls out 'Speak,' allowing the players to begin verbal dialogue.
7. Upon speaking, players must maintain and escalate the physicalities, emotional temperaments, and environmental details established during the silent phase.

## Facilitation Notes
- Side-coach players to slow down during the silent phase; rushing the waking process often leads to generic scenes.
- If players immediately start talking before the cue, gently remind them: 'Stay in the silence, show us how you feel first.'
- Encourage players to make strong physical choices (e.g., stretching, searching for glasses, checking a watch) that immediately signal character and status.
- Watch for 'talking with hands' (pantomiming words) during the silent phase; coach them to live in the moment rather than trying to mime complex messages.

## Variations
- Varying Environments: The facilitator can suggest a specific sleeping location beforehand (e.g., a camping tent, a luxury hotel, a cramped train cabin) to challenge their spatial awareness.
- Delayed Speech: Extend the silent phase to two full minutes to force deep physical exploration and relationship building before any words are spoken.
- The Third Sleeper: Add a third player to the space to complicate the relationship dynamics and spatial boundaries.

## Debrief
- How did starting in silence change how you listened to your partner's choices?
- What clues did you use to figure out your relationship and the 'Where' before anyone spoke?
- How did maintaining your initial physical state affect the direction and comedy of the scene once dialogue started?

## Safety & Inclusion
Since players start in close proximity simulating sleep, establish boundaries beforehand regarding physical contact. Ensure players agree on comfort levels with touch, or default to a 'no-contact' rule where they sleep in separate, designated personal spaces.

## Why It Works
By stripping away verbal communication at the start, players are forced to rely on physical behavior and environmental interaction to build their base reality. This grounds the scene in a shared physical truth (C.R.O.W.), making the subsequent dialogue naturally flow from an established relationship and environment rather than being invented out of thin air.
