# What's Going On

![What's Going On — infographic](img/D4_P1_S4_T2_G886__what-s-going-on.jpg){ .game-hero }

> Build a vivid, rhythmic world together, one rhyming couplet and collective chorus at a time.

## Overview
A high-energy musical warm-up where players collaborate to describe a single location. Standing in a circle, individuals take turns singing rhyming couplets that paint a sensory picture of the environment, punctuated by a roaring, collective chorus from the entire group. It builds vocal confidence, group cohesion, and strict adherence to a shared musical tempo.

## What It Trains
- **Domain:** D4 — The Ensemble
- **Principle(s):** Group Mind; Commit 100%; The First Thought Is a Gift; Show, Don't Tell
- **Skill(s):** Pacing & Rhythm; Vocal Craft; Unfiltered Spontaneity; World-Building
- **Technique(s):** Timing exercises; Word-association; C.R.O.W. (Character, Relationship, Objective, Where)
- **Focus:** mixed

**Objective:** To develop precise rhythmic timing, group mind, and spontaneous world-building by fitting descriptive imagery into a rigid musical structure.

## Setup
Players stand in a circle in an open space. No props or instruments are required, though a steady physical pulse (like clapping or chest-patting) can be established by the facilitator or the group.

## How to Play
1. Gather the group into a standing circle and establish a steady, moderate four-beat tempo (approx. 90-100 BPM) by having everyone gently tap their chests or sway.
2. Ask the group for a suggestion of a specific, evocative location (e.g., 'a haunted toy store' or 'a deep-sea research lab').
3. The first player sings a rhyming couplet (AABB or AABB-adjacent rhythm) that describes a sensory detail of this location, fitting their words strictly to the established beat.
4. Immediately following the couplet, the entire group enthusiastically sings the shared chorus together: 'I said hey! What's going on?' to the same driving rhythm.
5. Without dropping the beat, the next player in the circle immediately sings their own descriptive, rhyming couplet, expanding on the established environment.
6. The group responds again with the collective chorus: 'I said hey! What's going on?'
7. Continue clockwise around the circle, keeping the tempo steady and the energy high, until every player has contributed a couplet.

## Facilitation Notes
- Keep the beat sacred: If a player struggles to find a rhyme, encourage them to prioritize the rhythm over a perfect rhyme. A bad rhyme delivered on-beat is always better than a perfect rhyme that stalls the song.
- Side-coach sensory details: Remind players to 'show, don't tell' by describing what they see, smell, or feel in the space (e.g., 'slime on the walls' rather than 'this place is scary').
- Pitfall: The tempo dragging. Fix: Have the group maintain a physical bounce or light clap to lock in the tempo, and step in as a facilitator to conduct if the beat starts to slow down.
- Encourage vocal commitment: The chorus should be belted out with full physical and vocal commitment to reset the energy for the next soloist.

## Variations
- Non-Rhyming Atmosphere: Remove the rhyming constraint entirely, focusing purely on rhythm, syllable placement, and rich environmental description.
- Emotional Shift: Change the emotional tone of the location mid-way through (e.g., the haunted toy store suddenly becomes a joyful wonderland), requiring the melody and vocal delivery to shift instantly.
- Double-Time: Increase the tempo of the beat to challenge advanced players' rapid-fire word association and rhythmic precision.

## Debrief
- How did keeping a strict rhythm affect your ability to censor your ideas?
- What did you notice about how the world built up when we focused on sensory details rather than plot?
- How did the collective chorus support the individual soloists when it was their turn to sing?

## Safety & Inclusion
Ensure players know they can pass or sing a very simple one-line description if they feel overwhelmed by the rhyming or musical structure. For hearing-impaired or rhythm-challenged players, a visual physical cue (like a collective downward hand sweep) can help mark the downbeat.

## Why It Works
By combining a rigid rhythmic structure with a highly supportive, repetitive group chorus, the game lowers the stakes of individual failure. The 'Group Mind' is reinforced because everyone must maintain the exact same internal metronome, while the 'First Thought' principle is activated because the relentless beat prevents players from overthinking their rhymes.
