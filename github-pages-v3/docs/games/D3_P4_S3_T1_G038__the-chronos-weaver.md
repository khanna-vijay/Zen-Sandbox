# The Plot Weaver

> Freeze pivotal moments to collectively pitch, vote on, and execute the story's next major turn.

## Overview
A structured narrative game where a live scene is paused at critical turning points so offstage players can pitch potential future consequences. The ensemble votes on the most compelling narrative path, forcing the active players to immediately integrate and justify this new reality. It transforms passive improvisers into active story architects by externalizing the 'if true, what else is true' process.

## What It Trains
- **Domain:** D3 — The Scene
- **Principle(s):** Serve the Story; Yes, And; Group Mind
- **Skill(s):** Narrative Architecture; Game Identification; Justification; Support Work
- **Technique(s):** Story Spine; If this is true, what else is true?; C.R.O.W. (Character, Relationship, Objective, Where); Reincorporation-as-justification
- **Focus:** narrative

**Objective:** To develop a conscious grasp of narrative architecture, cause-and-effect progression, and collaborative story-building by pausing action to deliberately evaluate and select high-stakes plot developments.

## Setup
An open performance space. Two to three players start on stage to perform a scene, while the remaining players stand offstage as the 'Weavers' (story architects). No props or special materials are required.

## How to Play
1. Begin a standard scene with two or three players, focusing on establishing clear characters, relationships, and an initial conflict or dramatic premise.
2. The facilitator monitors the scene closely, looking for a pivotal choice, a major revelation, or a clear shift in character stakes (a 'Narrative Pivot').
3. Upon identifying a pivot, the facilitator calls out 'WEAVE!' and all onstage players immediately freeze in place, holding their physical postures.
4. The facilitator briefly recaps the pivotal moment that triggered the freeze (e.g., 'You just confessed your love to your rival's spouse').
5. All players—both onstage and offstage—individually formulate a potential future consequence using an 'If/Then' structure (e.g., 'If they confessed, then the rival is standing right behind them').
6. The facilitator calls on three to four players to pitch their 'If/Then' proposals, encouraging a mix of internal emotional shifts, external plot twists, and character-driven consequences.
7. The entire group (onstage and offstage) votes on the pitches via a quick show of hands; the facilitator breaks any ties to select the 'Chosen Thread'.
8. The facilitator announces the winning proposal, and on the count of three, the onstage players unfreeze and immediately play out the scene, fully integrating the chosen consequence as their absolute reality.
9. Repeat this cycle two to three times within the same scene, building a layered, highly collaborative narrative arc before calling 'Scene!'

## Facilitation Notes
- Facilitator Tip: Look for 'Narrative Pivots' that represent irreversible choices or high-stakes declarations rather than minor details. Pausing too early prevents momentum; pausing too late makes the story feel bloated.
- Side-Coaching Cue: During pitches, remind players to avoid random non-sequiturs. Every proposal must logically flow from the established reality—ask 'If this is true, what else is honestly true?'
- Common Pitfall: Onstage players ignoring the chosen thread or merely talking about it. Fix: Side-coach them to physically embody and immediately act upon the new reality rather than just discussing it.
- Pacing Tip: Keep the proposal and voting phases fast-paced (under 60 seconds total) to maintain the energy and emotional investment of the frozen players.

## Variations
- Secret Threads: The offstage players vote silently, and only one onstage player is whispered the chosen thread, forcing them to drive the scene toward it while the other player reacts.
- Genre Loom: Restrict all 'If/Then' proposals to fit a specific genre chosen by the audience (e.g., Film Noir, Shakespearean Tragedy, Sci-Fi Epic).
- The Solo Weaver: Instead of a group vote, a single designated offstage 'Director' instantly chooses the next thread to speed up the narrative engine.

## Debrief
- How did pausing the scene to evaluate multiple futures change how you viewed the value of your onstage choices?
- What made a proposed 'If/Then' thread feel satisfying and organic versus forced or disruptive?
- For the onstage players, how did it feel to have your narrative control shared with the offstage group, and how did you justify the sudden shifts?

## Safety & Inclusion
Because players are frozen in place during the proposal phase, ensure players are holding comfortable, sustainable physical postures. If a freeze occurs during an awkward or physically demanding position, invite the players to gently adjust to a neutral standing position while keeping their spatial relationship intact.

## Why It Works
By pausing the scene, the game slows down the rapid cognitive processing required in improv, allowing players to consciously practice narrative design. It externalizes the internal 'Story Spine' and 'if true, what else is true' mechanics, turning subconscious storytelling instincts into a visible, collaborative, and democratic process.
