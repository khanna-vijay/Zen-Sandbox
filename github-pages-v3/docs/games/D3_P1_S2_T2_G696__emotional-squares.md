# Emotional Squares

![Emotional Squares — infographic](img/D3_P1_S2_T2_G696__emotional-squares.jpg){ .game-hero }

> Navigate a stage divided into four distinct emotional zones, justifying sudden shifts in feeling.

## Overview
In this high-energy scene-work game, the stage is divided into an invisible two-by-two grid, with each quadrant assigned a different emotion. As players move through the physical space, they must instantly adopt the emotion of their current quadrant. The comedy and narrative depth emerge as players physically explore the space and justify their rapid emotional transformations.

## What It Trains
- **Domain:** D3 — The Scene
- **Principle(s):** Commit 100%; Yes, And; Show, Don't Tell
- **Skill(s):** Emotional Fluidity; Physicality & Space Work; Active Listening; Offer Reception; Heightening & Exploration; Justification
- **Technique(s):** The Emotional Dial (1→10); Character Walks/Centers; Exploring the 'why'; Justify the absurd
- **Focus:** comedy_game

**Objective:** To develop emotional fluidity, spatial awareness, and justification skills. Players learn to let physical movement drive their emotional state, discovering the 'why' behind their feelings after they begin experiencing them.

## Setup
Define a clear performance area. Mentally divide this space into a two-by-two grid (four equal quadrants: front-left, front-right, back-left, back-right). Ask the audience or workshop group for four distinct, contrasting emotions (e.g., ecstatic joy, deep suspicion, profound grief, intense panic) and assign one to each quadrant. Ensure all players and the audience know which emotion belongs to which zone.

## How to Play
1. Two to four players step onto the stage and receive a simple, non-emotional scene suggestion to establish their initial platform.
2. Players begin the scene, immediately adopting the emotion of whichever quadrant they currently occupy.
3. As players move around the stage to perform object work or interact, they must instantly transition to the emotion of any new quadrant they enter.
4. Players must justify their sudden emotional shifts within the context of the scene, finding logical or absurd reasons why their character's mood just swung dramatically.
5. Multiple players can occupy the same quadrant simultaneously, sharing the same emotional state, or they can occupy different quadrants to create high-contrast interpersonal dynamics.
6. Players should actively explore all four quadrants, varying the pace of their movements and the duration of time spent in each zone to build comedic tension and narrative progression.

## Facilitation Notes
- Side-coaching cue: 'Feel it first, justify it second!' Remind players not to plan their movement based on what makes sense, but to move physically and let the emotion dictate the story.
- Pitfall: Players staying glued to one spot to avoid changing emotions. Fix: Side-coach them to move, or have an external trigger (like a sound or a prompt) force a physical rotation.
- Side-coaching cue: 'Commit 100%!' Encourage players to express the emotions physically through their posture, gestures, and vocal tone, rather than just talking about how they feel.
- Pitfall: The scene becomes a series of disconnected jokes. Fix: Remind players to maintain the core relationship and platform of the scene, using the emotional shifts to deepen the characters' connection.

## Variations
- Emotional Tag-Outs: Other players offstage can tag in, inheriting the physical position and the corresponding emotion of the player they replace.
- Status Squares: Instead of emotions, assign different social status levels (e.g., royalty, subservient, middle-management, rebel) to each quadrant.
- Secret Squares: The audience knows which emotions are in which quadrants, but the players do not. Players must discover the emotions through trial and error based on audience reaction or side-coaching.

## Debrief
- How did changing your physical position on stage affect your internal emotional state?
- What strategies did you use to justify a sudden shift from one extreme emotion to another?
- How did having contrasting emotions in the same scene help build the relationship between the characters?
- In what ways did 'feeling first and finding the why later' make your choices more spontaneous?

## Safety & Inclusion
Since this game involves rapid physical movement and high emotional intensity, remind players to maintain physical safety and respect personal boundaries. Ensure the stage is clear of tripping hazards. If a player has mobility constraints, the quadrants can be adapted to hand gestures, vocal registers, or smaller, localized physical zones.

## Why It Works
This game leverages the physical environment to bypass the analytical mind. By forcing an immediate physical and emotional change based on location, players are compelled to 'show' their feelings through body language and tone before they can intellectually 'tell' the audience why. This naturally trains the skill of justification, as the brain scrambles to find a creative reason for the sudden shift, leading to organic, high-stakes comedic game-play.
