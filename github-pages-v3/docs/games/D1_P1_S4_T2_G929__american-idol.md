# The Ultimate Audition

> Belt out improvised anthems under the hilarious scrutiny of highly opinionated judges.

## Overview
A high-energy comedy game where players step into the high-stakes world of a televised singing competition. Two contestants perform fully improvised songs based on audience suggestions, while a charismatic host and a panel of contrasting judges frame the drama. The focus is on bold vocal choices, extreme commitment, and playful status dynamics.

## What It Trains
- **Domain:** D1 — The Self
- **Principle(s):** Commit 100%; Make Your Partner a Genius; Play for the Back Row
- **Skill(s):** Vocal Craft; Unfiltered Spontaneity; Status Modulation; Stage Presence & Clarity
- **Technique(s):** Vocal characterization; Status Seesaw; Projection
- **Focus:** comedy_game

**Objective:** To develop vocal characterization, uninhibited commitment to musical choices, and status modulation under pressure.

## Setup
Arrange the stage with a clear performance area for the contestants. Position the host to one side, and set up a 'judges' table' (or two chairs) facing the performers. The remaining workshop participants act as the live studio audience.

## How to Play
1. Assign roles to the 4 to 6 players: one Host, two Judges (one hyper-critical, one overly enthusiastic or bizarre), and two Contestants.
2. The Host kicks off the show with high-energy showmanship, introducing the judges and setting a dramatic, televised tone.
3. The Host introduces the first Contestant and asks the audience for a mundane suggestion (such as a household chore or a minor inconvenience) to serve as the title of their song.
4. The first Contestant steps forward and performs a short, fully improvised song, committing 100% to a distinct vocal style, genre, or character voice.
5. Following the performance, the Judges deliver their feedback, contrasting high and low status to create comedic tension.
6. The Host introduces the second Contestant, obtains a different suggestion, and the second Contestant performs their song with equal vocal commitment.
7. The Judges critique the second performance, maintaining their established personas.
8. The Host wraps up the segment by asking the audience to applaud for their favorite, bringing the game to a high-energy conclusion.

## Facilitation Notes
- Encourage contestants to commit to a specific vocal style (e.g., opera, country, heavy metal) immediately, even if they do not know the words.
- Remind judges to play the relationship, not just be mean; the harsh judge should have specific, absurd critiques, while the supportive judge should find genius in the mistakes.
- Side-coach players to 'play for the back row' by projecting their voices and maintaining strong physical presence throughout their songs.
- Pitfall: Contestants getting stuck in their heads trying to write clever lyrics. Fix: Side-coach them to focus on the melody, rhythm, and emotional intensity over perfect rhyming.

## Variations
- Genre Swap: The Host or Judges dictate a specific musical genre (e.g., Broadway, Reggae, Punk) right before the contestant sings.
- Duet Duel: The two contestants must sing a competitive duet together based on a single suggestion, trying to out-sing each other.
- The Silent Accompanist: Add a fifth player who mimes playing an instrument, and the singer must match the tempo and style of the mimed playing.

## Debrief
- How did committing fully to a physical or vocal character help you bypass the fear of singing?
- How did the contrasting status of the judges affect the energy of the performances?
- What strategies did you use to keep the song going when you ran out of lyrical ideas?

## Safety & Inclusion
Ensure players know they do not need to be 'good' singers; the game celebrates bold choices and comedic commitment over vocal perfection. Allow players to opt out of singing roles if they experience severe vocal anxiety, placing them in host or judge roles instead.

## Why It Works
By framing the singing as a character choice within a parody, it lowers the stakes of musical performance. The extreme commitment required to sell a bad song builds trust and vocal confidence, while the structured roles of host and judges provide a safe, supportive comedic framework.
