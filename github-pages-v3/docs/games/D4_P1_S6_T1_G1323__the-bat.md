# The Bat

> An immersive, entirely auditory long-form improv format performed in complete darkness.

## Overview
The Bat is a challenging long-form improv format performed entirely in pitch-black darkness. Without visual cues, the ensemble must rely solely on vocal variety, sound effects, precise verbal edits, and deep active listening. The result is a rich, theater-of-the-mind experience for both the players and the audience.

## What It Trains
- **Domain:** D4 — The Ensemble
- **Principle(s):** Group Mind; Make Your Partner a Genius; The First Thought Is a Gift
- **Skill(s):** Format Literacy; Active Listening; Vocal Craft; Pacing & Rhythm; Peripheral Awareness
- **Technique(s):** Harold; Vocal characterization; Edits (Sweep, Tag-Out, Sound/Light); Thread-tracking drills
- **Focus:** mixed

**Objective:** Develops deep group mind, heightened active listening, vocal craft, and format literacy by stripping away visual communication and forcing players to co-create purely through sound.

## Setup
An in-person performance space that can be made completely dark. Players stand in a safe, stationary line or semi-circle on stage. A suggestion is taken from the audience before the lights are turned off.

## How to Play
1. Obtain a suggestion from the audience while the lights are still on, then transition the room into complete darkness.
2. Begin the performance with an auditory opening, such as a soundscape, word association, or spoken-word poetry inspired by the suggestion.
3. Transition into the first scene using a clear vocal edit, such as a distinct sound effect, a verbal sweep, or a sudden shift in tone.
4. Perform three distinct, unrelated scenes (the first beat) relying entirely on vocal characterization, environment building through sound, and verbal exposition.
5. Execute edits between scenes using auditory cues like clapping, a specific sound effect, or a vocalized fade-out.
6. Introduce an auditory group game between the scene beats, such as a rhythmic chant, a mock radio broadcast, or a shared soundscape.
7. Return to the three scenes for their second and third beats, heightening the themes and connecting the storylines through verbal callbacks.
8. Conclude the piece with a unified auditory climax or a clean vocal edit, then signal the technician to bring the lights back up.

## Facilitation Notes
- Coaching cue: 'Paint the picture with your voice.' Encourage players to describe their actions or environments naturally through dialogue rather than clunky exposition.
- Pitfall: Talking over one another. Fix: Emphasize leaving micro-pauses between lines to allow others to speak, treating silence as a powerful tool.
- Coaching cue: 'Use the space of the room.' Encourage varying vocal projection to simulate physical distance and movement.
- Pitfall: Losing track of the Harold structure. Fix: Keep a strong mental map of the beats; the facilitator can gently hum or make a specific sound if a transition stalls.

## Variations
- The Blindfolded Circle: If a light-controlled room is unavailable, players wear blindfolds in a well-lit room while the audience watches.
- The Radio Play: Focus heavily on live Foley sound effects created by off-scene players to build the physical environment.
- The Monologue Hotspot: Replace the standard opening with a series of spoken-word monologues delivered from the dark.

## Debrief
- How did the absence of sight change your level of focus and listening?
- What vocal techniques did you discover to communicate physical action or spatial distance?
- How did the group mind manifest differently when you could not see your scene partners' physical offers?

## Safety & Inclusion
Because this game is played in complete darkness, players must remain stationary once the lights go out to prevent tripping or collisions. If anyone feels uncomfortable or disoriented in the dark, use blindfolds instead, allowing individuals to opt out or remove their blindfolds at any time.

## Why It Works
Stripping away visual feedback forces improvisers to abandon physical tricks and rely entirely on the auditory channel. This heightens their sensitivity to pacing, tone, and subtext, fostering a profound sense of group mind as they co-create a shared mental space without physical boundaries.
