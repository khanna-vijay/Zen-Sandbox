# Boundary Unpack

> Practice setting, navigating, and respecting boundaries in real-time through low-stakes, character-driven challenges.

## Overview
This structured skill drill places players in scenes where they must navigate ambiguous, socially awkward, or boundary-testing character offers. One player initiates a challenging proposition, while the other practices responding with absolute truthfulness rather than polite compliance, utilizing explicit safety tools like check-ins and cut calls.

## What It Trains
- **Domain:** D2 — The Partner
- **Principle(s):** Consent & Boundaries; Truth Over Pandering
- **Skill(s):** Boundary Navigation; Active Listening; Offer Reception
- **Technique(s):** Check-ins; Cut calls; Negotiating physical contact
- **Focus:** skill_drill

**Objective:** To develop practical boundary navigation, active listening, and offer reception skills, specifically training players to prioritize truth over pandering and confidently use safety overrides.

## Setup
An open, safe physical space. The facilitator prepares a list of unsolicited propositions (socially awkward, slightly intrusive, or physically suggestive character offers that require negotiation). Players stand in a circle or line, ready to step forward in pairs.

## How to Play
1. Pair up two players (Player A and Player B) and place them in the performance space.
2. Assign Player A a simple relationship and setting, such as coworkers at a retreat or strangers at a bus stop.
3. Privately give Player A an unsolicited proposition, such as suggesting an immediate trust fall or proposing they quit their jobs together.
4. Player A initiates the scene, introducing the proposition naturally but clearly as a character choice.
5. Player B responds with absolute honesty, with no obligation to agree or yes-and the action if their character or they as a player feel uncomfortable.
6. Introduce the In-Character Check-In (e.g., Are you serious about that?) and the Out-of-Character Check-In (pausing the scene to ask, Are you comfortable with this direction?).
7. Establish the Cut call: any player or the facilitator can call Cut! at any moment to instantly end the scene with zero explanation required.
8. Implement physical contact rules: if contact is proposed, Player A must ask Tap-in? and Player B must explicitly reply Tap-in! before any physical touch occurs.
9. End the scene once the boundary has been successfully negotiated, a Cut! is called, or the interaction reaches a natural resolution.

## Facilitation Notes
- Safety First: Ensure prompts are socially awkward or intrusive in a character sense, but never genuinely abusive or traumatic. Screen prompts carefully.
- Normalize the Cut: Actively celebrate the first use of a Cut! call to remove any stigma of failure or disruption.
- Avoid Trickery: Coach Player A to make the offer as a genuine character choice, not as a meta-test to trick or trap their partner.
- Focus on the Negotiation: Remind players that a scene's success is measured by the honesty of the boundary negotiation, not by a smooth, agreeable resolution.
- Read Non-Verbal Cues: Encourage initiators to actively watch for physical retreats, closed posture, or hesitant expressions as immediate indicators of a boundary.

## Variations
- Silent Boundaries: Run the scene where Player B can only use non-verbal cues to set boundaries, forcing Player A to rely entirely on visual active listening.
- The Escalator: Player A starts with a mild proposition and slowly increases the awkwardness, requiring Player B to find their specific tipping point for a check-in or cut.

## Debrief
- For the responder: How did it feel to prioritize your character's truth over the traditional pressure to yes-and the offer?
- For the initiator: What physical or verbal cues did you notice that signaled your partner was setting a boundary, and how did you adapt?
- For everyone: How does having explicit tools like Check-in and Cut change your confidence when exploring risky or awkward scene work?

## Safety & Inclusion
This game is highly safety-sensitive. Establish a firm, non-negotiable rule that any player can call Cut! or Check-in at any time without needing to justify it. Ensure the Tap-in? physical consent protocol is strictly enforced before any physical contact occurs.

## Why It Works
By decoupling the concept of yes-and from blind compliance, this drill teaches players that true agreement means agreeing on the reality of the scene, including the reality of a boundary. It builds muscle memory for safety tools in a low-stakes environment, ensuring players can protect their personal and artistic boundaries under pressure.
