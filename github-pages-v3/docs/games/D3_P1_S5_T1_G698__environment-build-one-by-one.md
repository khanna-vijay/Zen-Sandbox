# The Living Blueprint

![The Living Blueprint — infographic](img/D3_P1_S5_T1_G698__environment-build-one-by-one.jpg){ .game-hero }

> Construct a rich, shared physical environment one invisible object at a time.

## Overview
Players collaboratively construct a detailed physical location by adding and interacting with mimed objects in a cumulative sequence. Each player must respect and interact with every previously established object before introducing their own, building a shared spatial memory.

## What It Trains
- **Domain:** D3 — The Scene
- **Principle(s):** Show, Don't Tell; Base Reality First; Group Mind
- **Skill(s):** World-Building; Physicality & Space Work; Peripheral Awareness
- **Technique(s):** C.R.O.W. (Character, Relationship, Objective, Where); Object work; Weight & resistance mime; Thread-tracking drills
- **Focus:** skill_drill

**Objective:** To develop precise physical object work, spatial memory, and a shared commitment to the 'Where' of a scene without relying on verbal explanation.

## Setup
An open playing space representing the stage, with the rest of the group seated as active observers. No physical props are used. The facilitator obtains a suggestion for a specific, object-rich location (e.g., a commercial kitchen, a mechanic's garage, a science lab).

## How to Play
1. The facilitator secures a location suggestion and designates the boundaries of the performance space.
2. The first player enters the space, silently establishes the physical presence of one specific object through detailed mime (e.g., opening a heavy refrigerator door, taking out a jar), interacts with it, and then exits the stage.
3. The second player enters, interacts with the first player's object exactly where it was left, and then introduces a second, new object in a different part of the space before exiting.
4. Each subsequent player enters and must interact with all previously established objects in their correct spatial locations before adding one new object of their own.
5. Players must maintain the physical integrity of the space, respecting the height, weight, volume, and exact coordinates of every object established before them.
6. The game continues until every player has entered, interacted with the cumulative chain of objects, and added the final piece to the environment.

## Facilitation Notes
- Side-coach players to focus on the physical properties of objects: weight, resistance, texture, and size. Remind them that a phone has thickness, and a heavy door requires effort to open.
- Pitfall: Players rushing through the previous objects like a checklist. Fix: Encourage them to find a character-driven reason to interact with each object, making the interactions meaningful rather than mechanical.
- If a player forgets the location of an object, the audience or waiting players can gently point or gesture to guide them, keeping the focus on collective success rather than failure.
- Encourage players to enter with an attitude or physical state (e.g., tired, hurried, sneaky) to show how character and environment influence each other.

## Variations
- Character Pass: Players must interact with the objects while embodying a specific relationship or emotional state related to the location.
- The Living Scene: Once the environment is fully built, two or three players step back into the space and run a fully voiced scene, utilizing the established objects naturally as they talk.
- Speed Build: Run the exercise with a strict time limit per player to encourage rapid, decisive physical choices.

## Debrief
- What helped you remember the location and scale of objects established by others?
- How did treating the invisible objects with weight and resistance make the environment feel more real to you and the audience?
- How does having a fully realized physical environment change the way you approach starting a scene?

## Safety & Inclusion
Ensure the physical space is clear of actual tripping hazards. Players with mobility considerations can establish and interact with objects at heights and positions that are comfortable and accessible for them, and the group must adapt the environment to respect these choices.

## Why It Works
By forcing players to physically engage with what has already been established, this game builds a strong 'Base Reality' and enforces the 'Show, Don't Tell' principle. It trains peripheral awareness and spatial memory, ensuring that the physical world of the scene becomes a tangible, active partner in the improvisation rather than mere background decoration.
