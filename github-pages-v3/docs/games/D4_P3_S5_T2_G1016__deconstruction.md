# Deconstruction

![Deconstruction — infographic](img/D4_P3_S5_T2_G1016__deconstruction.jpg){ .game-hero }

> Dismantle a single rich scene to spin off a web of interconnected stories.

## Overview
This is a classic long-form format where players perform a detailed, grounded opening scene and then systematically deconstruct it to inspire a series of fast-paced, thematic spin-off scenes. The ensemble listens closely to the initial scene's details, relationships, and off-hand mentions, using them as raw material to weave a cohesive, multi-layered narrative tapestry.

## What It Trains
- **Domain:** D4 — The Ensemble
- **Principle(s):** Serve the Piece; Serve the Story
- **Skill(s):** Thematic Synthesis; Format Literacy; Narrative Architecture; World-Building
- **Technique(s):** Weave the threads; Longform vs. shortform mechanics; Platform/Tilt; Edits (Sweep, Tag-Out, Sound/Light)
- **Focus:** mixed

**Objective:** Develops thematic synthesis, narrative architecture, and active listening by training players to identify, extract, and expand upon subtle narrative threads from a single source scene.

## Setup
A performance space with a clear stage area and an off-stage 'backline' where the rest of the ensemble stands. No props or set pieces are needed, though two chairs can be placed on stage for the opening scene.

## How to Play
1. Obtain a single, simple suggestion from the audience (such as an everyday object, a location, or a relationship) to inspire the opening.
2. Two players step forward to perform a substantial, grounded, and character-driven 'source scene' (typically 3 to 5 minutes) rich in details, emotional stakes, and off-hand references to external people, places, or ideas.
3. The remaining players stand on the backline, listening intently to capture specific details: mentioned characters who never appear, background locations, thematic motifs, or unresolved conflicts.
4. As the source scene reaches a natural high point or pause, a player from the backline edits the scene (via a sweep edit or tag-out) to initiate a new, shorter scene based on a specific element from the source.
5. Subsequent scenes are initiated rapidly, drawing from different 'threads' of the source scene, such as exploring a mentioned off-stage character, visiting a referenced location, playing a thematic analogy, or showing a character's backstory.
6. Encourage players to vary the style of the spin-offs—some might be realistic and character-focused, while others might be highly stylized, physical, or abstract representations of the source scene's themes.
7. As the piece progresses, players begin to cross-pollinate the spin-off scenes, bringing characters from different threads into contact with one another or returning to the original source characters to show how they have been affected.
8. The edit pattern accelerates, building to a natural thematic climax where the various threads feel resolved or poetically linked, ending the piece on a strong, unified note.

## Facilitation Notes
- Coaching Cue: 'Listen for the unsaid and the unseen.' Remind players on the backline to write down mental notes of off-hand remarks or background details rather than just waiting for their turn to play.
- Pitfall: Players merely repeating the jokes or plot points of the first scene. Fix: Coach them to explore the consequences or the untold stories of the first scene, rather than just parodying it.
- Coaching Cue: 'Vary the texture.' If the source scene was highly dramatic, encourage a fast-paced, comedic, or highly physical spin-off to break up the rhythm.
- Pitfall: The source scene is too short or lacks detail. Fix: If the opening scene is rushed, freeze the play and instruct the players to slow down, establish a relationship, and name specific details before any edits are allowed.

## Variations
- The Monologue Deconstruction: Start with a personal monologue from a player based on the suggestion, using the details of the monologue as the source material instead of a two-person scene.
- Thematic Echoes: Restrict the spin-offs to purely thematic or emotional echoes rather than literal narrative connections (e.g., if the source scene is about betrayal, all spin-offs explore betrayal in different contexts).
- Time Jump: Require that the spin-offs explicitly take place either 10 years before or 10 years after the events of the source scene, exploring the history and future of the characters and world.

## Debrief
- How did listening to the source scene differ from how you normally listen in a standard scene-to-scene format?
- What strategies did we use to make sure we weren't just repeating the first scene, but actually expanding its world?
- How did the thematic connections emerge naturally versus being forced or planned?

## Safety & Inclusion
Ensure that when exploring off-stage characters or sensitive themes mentioned in the source scene, players maintain respect and avoid punching down or relying on cheap stereotypes for comedic effect.

## Why It Works
It shifts the players' focus from inventing new ideas to mining existing gold. By treating the opening scene as a rich text to be analyzed and exploded, players learn that they already have all the information they need to build a complex, satisfying, and deeply unified long-form piece.
