# Something Old, Something New

> Gift clever clues to make your partner look like a genius in this collaborative guessing game.

## Overview
In this lively guessing game, one player steps out while the remaining four obtain four distinct items from the audience based on a classic four-part adage. When the guesser returns, the players must use physical endowment and verbal clues to help them identify all four items. The focus is entirely on supportive play, ensuring the guesser is set up to succeed through active gifting.

## What It Trains
- **Domain:** D2 — The Partner
- **Principle(s):** Make Your Partner a Genius; Yes, And; Group Mind
- **Skill(s):** Active Gifting; Active Listening; Support Work
- **Technique(s):** Endowment-gifting drills; Give them the answer
- **Focus:** comedy_game

**Objective:** To develop active gifting and endowment skills, training players to make their partner look brilliant by feeding them clear, physical, and contextual clues while maintaining a cohesive scene.

## Setup
Five players stand on stage in front of an audience. No physical props are required. One player is designated as the Guesser and temporarily leaves the room or plugs their ears so they cannot hear the setup.

## How to Play
1. Designate one player as the Guesser and have them step out of earshot of the stage.
2. Solicit four distinct items from the audience, each fitting one of the four categories: 'something old' (e.g., a sundial), 'something new' (e.g., a smart watch), 'something borrowed' (e.g., a neighbor's lawnmower), and 'something blue' (e.g., a sapphire ring).
3. Assign one of these four items to each of the remaining four players on stage.
4. Invite the Guesser back to the stage to initiate a celebratory scene, such as a wedding reception, housewarming, or retirement party.
5. The four players interact with the Guesser one by one or in small groups, using physical object work and dialogue to 'endow' the Guesser with clues about their specific item.
6. Players must actively listen to the scene, stepping in to help their castmates by dropping additional supporting hints if the Guesser is struggling with a particular item.
7. The Guesser must accept these gifts, play along with the scene's reality, and voice their realizations naturally as part of the dialogue.
8. The game successfully concludes once the Guesser has identified all four items through collaborative play.

## Facilitation Notes
- Coaching cue: 'Show, don't just tell! Use your body to demonstrate the weight, size, temperature, and function of the item.'
- Coaching cue: 'Support your team! If a teammate's clue isn't landing, jump into the interaction to offer a different angle or a supportive hint.'
- Pitfall: Players make their clues too obscure or turn the game into a silent charade. Fix: Remind players to use natural dialogue and emotional reactions to endow the Guesser (e.g., reacting with awe at how expensive the 'new' item is).
- Pitfall: The Guesser steps out of character and starts listing random guesses like a game show contestant. Fix: Encourage the Guesser to stay grounded in the scene and only name the item when it makes narrative sense.

## Variations
- Themed Categories: Change the four categories to fit different seasonal themes, such as 'Something Spooky, Something Sweet, Something Broken, Something Cheap' for a holiday-themed show.
- Status Play: Assign high or low status to the guests, which dictates how deferential, demanding, or secretive they are when gifting their clues to the Guesser.
- Monologue Toasts: Instead of a free-form scene, each of the four players delivers a short, character-driven toast to the Guesser, embedding their clues within their speech.

## Debrief
- How did it feel to focus entirely on making the Guesser look smart rather than trying to be funny yourself?
- What types of physical or verbal 'gifts' were the easiest for the Guesser to interpret and build upon?
- How did the players on stage support each other when a clue wasn't immediately understood?

## Safety & Inclusion
Ensure the stage pathway is clear of obstacles for the Guesser entering and exiting. If players choose to physically interact or hand over imaginary items, they should establish clear physical boundaries and respect personal space.

## Why It Works
This game shifts the focus from individual performance to radical support. By framing the guessing mechanic around 'making your partner a genius,' players learn that their primary job is to set up their teammate for success. It relies heavily on active gifting (endowment) and active listening, as players must pay attention to what has already been guessed and adjust their clues dynamically.
