# The Ripple Signal

<div class="game-hero-ph" markdown>🎨 *Infographic coming soon*</div>

> Navigate personal boundaries in real-time using subtle physical signals to co-create safer, more authentic scenes.

## Overview
This is an active, scene-based exercise where players use physical markers like colored cards or stones to communicate comfort levels dynamically. Instead of halting play, these signals invite partners to adjust their physical, emotional, or narrative choices in real-time. The experience shifts the perception of boundaries from restrictive barriers to generative, creative pathways that deepen trust and authenticity.

## What It Trains
- **Domain:** D2 — The Partner
- **Principle(s):** Consent & Boundaries; Truth Over Pandering
- **Skill(s):** Boundary Navigation; Active Listening; Offer Reception
- **Technique(s):** Check-ins; Cut calls; Negotiating physical contact
- **Focus:** mixed

**Objective:** To develop real-time boundary navigation and active listening skills, training players to seamlessly integrate consent check-ins and physical adjustments directly into active scene work without breaking narrative momentum.

## Setup
Set up a moderate, open playing space. Provide each player with three small, easily concealable colored items (such as green, amber, and red cards or stones). Before starting, have players spend two minutes in silent reflection to identify their personal comfort zones for the day (e.g., physical proximity, emotional intensity, or specific themes) without sharing them aloud.

## How to Play
1. Divide players into active pairs, with the remaining players acting as active observers who watch for physical cues.
2. The facilitator provides a high-stakes or emotionally charged scene prompt to initiate play.
3. As the scene progresses, if a player feels their personal comfort zone or boundary being approached, they subtly display their Amber card or stone close to their chest.
4. Upon seeing the Amber signal, the partner must immediately but organically adjust their performance—by shifting physical distance, lowering emotional intensity, or changing the narrative direction—without breaking character.
5. If a player wishes to initiate physical contact, they must explicitly request it in-character, allowing the partner to accept or decline in-character.
6. If a hard boundary is crossed or the Amber signal is missed, the affected player displays the Red card and calls Stop, freezing the scene instantly.
7. Following a Red card freeze, the signaling player requests one small, actionable physical or spatial adjustment without needing to explain why, and the scene immediately resumes with that adjustment.
8. Players may occasionally flash a Green card to offer positive reinforcement when a partner navigates a boundary with exceptional care or makes a highly supportive choice.

## Facilitation Notes
- Side-coach players to keep their eyes open and maintain soft focus: Watch your partner's hands and posture; the signals are invitations, not interruptions.
- Watch out for players who over-apologize or break character to explain themselves when an Amber or Red signal is shown. Coach them to treat the signal as a natural physical prompt, adjusting immediately in-character.
- Encourage authentic choices over pleasing the crowd: Prioritize your partner's comfort over a cheap laugh. Truth over pandering.
- Ensure a strict no-explanation-needed rule for Red cards; the facilitator must validate every signal instantly and move directly to the adjustment to prevent judgment.

## Variations
- Play without physical cards or stones, relying entirely on agreed-upon physical gestures (e.g., a hand to the collarbone for Amber, a raised palm for Red) to make the boundary navigation completely seamless.
- Allow one of the observing players to gently tap in or call a mini-freeze if they notice a player's non-verbal discomfort that the active partner missed, helping to train collective group vigilance.

## Debrief
- How did it feel to use the Amber signal to guide your partner, and did it feel like a restriction or a creative pivot?
- As the partner receiving a signal, how did you balance staying in character while actively adjusting to your partner's comfort level?
- How did the requirement to ask for physical contact in-character change the pacing and emotional depth of the scene?
- In what ways did prioritizing your partner's boundaries lead to more authentic, truthful choices rather than pandering to the audience?

## Safety & Inclusion
This game is highly safety-sensitive. The facilitator must emphasize that using the Red card is a strength, not a failure. Ensure players know they have absolute autonomy over their physical space and are never required to justify or explain their boundaries to the group. For players with visual or physical accessibility needs, replace the colored cards with clear, distinct verbal cues.

## Why It Works
By gamifying the negotiation of boundaries, this exercise removes the stigma of saying no in improv. The Amber signal acts as a real-time check-in that forces active listening and offer reception, turning a boundary into a collaborative offer. This directly supports Truth Over Pandering by proving that respecting a partner's comfort produces deeper, more compelling, and highly authentic dramatic tension than forcing a scene forward at all costs.
