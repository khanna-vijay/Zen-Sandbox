# Follow the Leaver

![Follow the Leaver — infographic](img/D4_P3_S4_T1_G706__follow-the-leaver.jpg){ .game-hero }

> Drive narrative momentum by letting every character's exit edit and launch the next scene.

## Overview
Follow the Leaver is a dynamic narrative editing game where the physical exit of a character serves as the edit for the next scene. Instead of wiping the stage, the ensemble immediately initiates a new scene showing where that departing character went. This creates a continuous, chain-linked sequence of locations and encounters driven entirely by character movement.

## What It Trains
- **Domain:** D4 — The Ensemble
- **Principle(s):** Serve the Piece; Serve the Story
- **Skill(s):** Pacing & Rhythm; Support Work; Narrative Architecture
- **Technique(s):** Edits (Sweep, Tag-Out, Sound/Light); Montage; Platform/Tilt
- **Focus:** mixed

**Objective:** To develop seamless editing skills, narrative pacing, and ensemble support by using physical exits as organic scene transitions.

## Setup
An open performance space with a clear playing area and an offstage area where the rest of the ensemble stands ready to edit. No props or chairs are required.

## How to Play
1. Gather the ensemble in a semi-circle around the playing space, with two players stepping forward to begin an initial, grounded scene.
2. Establish a clear platform, relationship, and environment in the opening scene, allowing the narrative to develop naturally.
3. At a moment of high tension, comedic payoff, or narrative curiosity, one of the active characters must make a physical exit from the scene, stating where they are going or leaving with a clear intention.
4. The moment the character exits the playing space, the remaining player freezes or slips away, and the exiting player immediately enters a new space on stage.
5. Offstage players must instantly support this transition by stepping forward to join the exiting player, establishing the new location that the character just arrived at.
6. The new scene plays out with this character and the new partner(s) until another character (either the original traveler or a new character introduced in this scene) decides to exit.
7. Repeat this cycle, ensuring that every single scene transition is triggered by a character leaving and being followed to their next destination.
8. Continue the chain for five to eight scenes, allowing the narrative thread to wind through various locations before finding a natural, satisfying conclusion for the entire piece.

## Facilitation Notes
- Side-coaching cue: 'Watch the door!' Remind offstage players to be hyper-focused on who is leaving and where they might be going so they can edit instantly.
- Pitfall: The exiting player leaves but has no idea where they are going, leading to a stalled start. Fix: Encourage the exiting player to declare their destination or emotional state as they leave (e.g., 'I'm going to the principal's office!').
- Pitfall: Offstage players wait too long to enter, leaving the exiting player stranded. Fix: Coach the ensemble to treat the exit as a hard edit—step onto the stage the second the player crosses the threshold.
- Side-coaching cue: 'Vary the pace!' Encourage some exits to be fast and urgent, while others are slow and heavy, changing the rhythm of the overall piece.

## Variations
- The Return Loop: After four or five exits, the next exit must lead back to the very first scene, closing the narrative loop.
- Emotional Carryover: The exiting character must carry 100% of their emotional state from the end of the previous scene directly into the next, regardless of how absurd it is for the new location.
- The Tag-Out Hybrid: Offstage players can initiate the exit by entering the scene and physically tagging out a character, sending them on their way to a new location.

## Debrief
- How did relying on exits as edits change the pacing and speed of your scene transitions?
- What cues did you look for to know it was the right time to leave a scene?
- How did following the departing character help build a cohesive, shared narrative universe?

## Safety & Inclusion
Ensure the physical exit pathways are clear of obstacles. Players should be mindful of physical pacing—an exit can be a slow walk or a quick stride, but should never require dangerous running or colliding with offstage players.

## Why It Works
This game works because it replaces the traditional sweep edit with an organic, character-driven transition. By making the exit the edit, players learn to serve the piece rather than just their individual scenes. It forces the offstage ensemble to maintain high focus, instantly supporting the narrative architecture by building the next logical step in a character's journey.
