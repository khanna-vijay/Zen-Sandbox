# The Negotiation Canvas

![The Negotiation Canvas — infographic](img/D2_P1_S6_T1_G323__scene-weavers-the-consent-canvas.jpg){ .game-hero }

> Transform boundary negotiation into a collaborative, generative tool by pausing scenes to check in.

## Overview
A structured scene-work exercise where players actively pause the action to check in with their partners about physical contact, emotional intensity, or narrative direction. By stepping out of character to negotiate comfort levels, players learn to treat boundaries as creative assets rather than scene-stoppers. The experience shifts the focus from maintaining artificial momentum to building deep, mutual trust.

## What It Trains
- **Domain:** D2 — The Partner
- **Principle(s):** Consent & Boundaries; Truth Over Pandering
- **Skill(s):** Boundary Navigation; Active Listening; Offer Reception
- **Technique(s):** Check-ins; Cut calls; Negotiating physical contact
- **Focus:** skill_drill

**Objective:** To develop practical skills in real-time boundary navigation, active listening, and explicit check-ins, ensuring players prioritize personal safety and authentic partner connection over performance pressure.

## Setup
An open, comfortable playing space. Two active players stand in the center, while one to three players observe from the side. No props or materials are required. The facilitator prepares a few low-stakes, relationship-focused scene prompts.

## How to Play
1. Begin a standard two-person scene based on a simple, relationship-driven prompt provided by the facilitator.
2. At any point during the scene—especially when anticipating physical contact, a shift in emotional intensity, or a sensitive topic—either player can call out 'Pause and Check'.
3. Upon hearing 'Pause and Check', both players immediately freeze in place and step out of character to speak directly as themselves.
4. The player who initiated the check-in asks a specific, direct question about their partner's comfort level or proposes a potential physical or narrative choice.
5. The receiving partner must respond honestly with one of three options: a clear 'Yes', a generative 'No' (offering an alternative), or a 'Conditional Yes' (setting specific limits).
6. Once the boundary is established, the players step back into character and resume the scene, either continuing from the exact frozen moment or rewinding slightly to integrate the agreed-upon adjustment.
7. Any player, or the observing facilitator, can call 'Cut!' at any moment for an immediate, non-negotiable end to the scene if a boundary is crossed or if genuine discomfort arises.
8. Measure the success of the scene not by its narrative polish or uninterrupted flow, but by the frequency, clarity, and mutual respect of the check-ins.

## Facilitation Notes
- Model the behavior early by calling a 'Pause and Check' or 'Cut' from the sidelines to show players how simple, low-stakes, and non-disruptive the mechanic actually is.
- Watch for non-verbal cues of discomfort, such as physical tensing, stepping back, or forced laughter, and gently coach players to use these cues as triggers to initiate a check-in.
- Actively praise 'No' and 'Conditional Yes' responses during the exercise to dismantle the cultural pressure in improv to always say 'Yes' to physical or emotional escalation.
- If a player seems hesitant to pause the scene, remind them that a scene with five clear check-ins is far more successful in this drill than a seamless scene with unaddressed tension.

## Variations
- The Silent Cue: Instead of a verbal 'Pause and Check', players use a subtle, pre-agreed physical gesture (like tapping their own collarbone) to signal a need for a quick, quiet verbal check-in.
- The Rewind Practice: When a boundary is set that alters the immediate action, players must explicitly rewind the scene by three lines of dialogue to practice seamlessly finding an alternative narrative path.

## Debrief
- How did it feel to step out of character to ask for permission, and did it disrupt your creative flow as much as you expected?
- What was your experience of receiving a 'No' or a 'Conditional Yes'—did it feel like a rejection, or did it offer a clear path forward?
- How did paying close attention to your partner's physical comfort change the way you listened and reacted in the scene?
- In what ways can we bring this habit of active, low-stakes checking into our regular, unpaused improv scenes?

## Safety & Inclusion
Establish a firm, supportive atmosphere before starting. Emphasize that players have absolute autonomy over their bodies and emotional boundaries. If a 'Cut!' is called, the facilitator must immediately pause the workshop, check in with the affected player privately or gently, and ensure no explanation or justification is demanded from the person who called it.

## Why It Works
This game works because it dismantles the toxic misconception that 'yes-and' requires absolute compliance with every physical or narrative offer. By formalizing the check-in as a core game mechanic, it lowers the social friction of saying 'no' and reframes boundary setting as a collaborative, creative act that actually deepens trust and artistic freedom.
