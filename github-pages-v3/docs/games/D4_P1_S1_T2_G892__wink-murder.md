# Wink Elimination

![Wink Elimination — infographic](img/D4_P1_S1_T2_G892__wink-murder.jpg){ .game-hero }

> A high-stakes game of silent eye contact, dramatic physical reactions, and sharp group observation.

## Overview
Players stand in a circle, trying to identify a secret "eliminator" who knocks out participants with a subtle wink. Those winked at must deliver a highly dramatic, slow-motion physical exit from the circle. It builds intense focus, non-verbal connection, and collective awareness.

## What It Trains
- **Domain:** D4 — The Ensemble
- **Principle(s):** Group Mind; Commit 100%
- **Skill(s):** Peripheral Awareness; Physicality & Space Work; Active Listening
- **Technique(s):** Thread-tracking drills
- **Focus:** connection

**Objective:** To develop peripheral awareness, active listening, and physical commitment by reading subtle cues across a group while maintaining a shared focus.

## Setup
Players stand in a spacious circle facing inward. No props are needed. Ensure the floor is clear of obstacles to allow for safe physical movement.

## How to Play
1. Instruct all players to stand in a circle and close their eyes.
2. The facilitator walks around the outside of the circle and secretly taps one player on the shoulder to designate them as the "eliminator."
3. Instruct everyone to open their eyes and begin making eye contact around the circle, shifting their gaze naturally.
4. The secret eliminator must eliminate other players by making direct eye contact and winking at them subtly.
5. When a player is winked at, they must wait a brief moment (to avoid immediately giving away the culprit) and then perform a highly dramatic, physical, and vocal elimination sequence before stepping out of the circle.
6. Any active player can make an accusation by saying, 'I accuse [Name].' If they are correct, the game ends and the group wins.
7. If an accusation is incorrect, the accuser is immediately eliminated and must dramatically exit the circle.
8. The game continues until the eliminator is successfully identified or all players except the eliminator are eliminated.

## Facilitation Notes
- Coaching cue: 'Keep your eyes moving—don't just stare at one person. Use your peripheral vision to track the whole room.'
- Pitfall: Players dying instantly, which makes it too easy to spot the winker. Fix: Remind players to count to three silently in their heads after being winked at before starting their dramatic exit.
- Coaching cue: 'Commit 100% to your physical exit! Make it a theatrical masterpiece.'
- Pitfall: Accusers shouting out guesses constantly without penalty. Fix: Enforce the rule that a wrong guess results in immediate elimination to raise the stakes and encourage careful observation.

## Variations
- Double Trouble: Appoint two secret eliminators who do not know who each other are. They can accidentally eliminate each other.
- The Detective: One player steps out of the room while the eliminator is chosen. The detective stands in the center of the circle and gets three guesses to find the winker as players are eliminated around them.
- Silent Mode: No vocal accusations or dramatic screams allowed; players must communicate accusations through silent gestures, and eliminations must be entirely physical and silent.

## Debrief
- How did you use your peripheral vision to scan the room without staring directly at people?
- What did it feel like to commit fully to a dramatic physical exit, and how did that affect the energy of the group?
- How does this game show the power of Group Mind and shared focus in an ensemble?

## Safety & Inclusion
Ensure the physical space is clear of obstacles so players can safely fall or move dramatically when eliminated. Offer low-impact physical options (e.g., a dramatic sigh and a slow bow) for players with mobility limitations.

## Why It Works
It forces players to balance direct focus with peripheral awareness. The high stakes of elimination demand total presence, while the dramatic exits require physical commitment and support the group's shared narrative reality.
