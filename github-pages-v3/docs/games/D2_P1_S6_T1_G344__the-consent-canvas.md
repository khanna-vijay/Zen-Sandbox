# The Consent Canvas

<div class="game-hero-ph" markdown>🎨 *Infographic coming soon*</div>

> Navigate physical and emotional boundaries in real time using explicit check-ins and empowered choices.

## Overview
A structured scene-work drill where players collaboratively build a narrative while actively negotiating physical and emotional boundaries. By integrating mandatory verbal check-ins and secret personal boundary prompts, players practice prioritizing personal comfort over narrative momentum. The experience shifts the focus of improv from seamless performance to conscious, respectful partnership.

## What It Trains
- **Domain:** D2 — The Partner
- **Principle(s):** Consent & Boundaries; Truth Over Pandering; Yes, And
- **Skill(s):** Boundary Navigation; Active Listening; Offer Reception
- **Technique(s):** Check-ins; Cut calls; Negotiating physical contact
- **Focus:** skill_drill

**Objective:** To develop real-time boundary navigation and active listening skills by normalizing explicit verbal check-ins, honoring conditional consent, and practicing the non-negotiable 'Cut' call to prioritize personal safety over scene continuity.

## Setup
An open, moderate-sized playing space. The facilitator prepares small, pre-written slips of paper, each containing a subtle, realistic boundary prompt (e.g., 'Avoids direct eye contact,' 'Uncomfortable with touch on the arm'). Players stand in a neutral line, and each secretly draws one boundary slip to keep hidden throughout the round.

## How to Play
1. The facilitator assigns a low-stakes, everyday scene premise to two or three active players (e.g., neighbors borrowing a garden tool).
2. Each player secretly reviews their pre-written boundary slip, which acts as an internal compass for their personal comfort limits during the scene.
3. As the scene begins, players must explicitly ask for verbal consent before initiating any physical contact or introducing sensitive personal topics (e.g., 'May I step closer to look at that?').
4. The receiving player must respond using one of three clear options: 'Yes, and' (full agreement), 'Yes, but' (conditional agreement with a modification), or 'No, thank you' (clear decline).
5. The initiating player must immediately and seamlessly accept the response without questioning, negotiating, or showing frustration, pivoting the scene's direction accordingly.
6. Any player who feels their boundary is being approached or crossed—or who simply feels uncomfortable—can proactively check in with their partner or state their current comfort level.
7. At any point, any player can call 'Cut!' to immediately freeze the scene if a boundary is violated or if they feel genuine discomfort.
8. Upon a 'Cut' call, the facilitator pauses the action, allows the player to briefly state their boundary if they choose, and rewinds or pivots the scene to a safe starting point.

## Facilitation Notes
- Coaching Cue: Remind players that an awkward pause or a 'No, thank you' is a massive win for the scene, not a failure of improvisation.
- Pitfall: Players trying to 'play' their secret boundary dramatically as a character quirk. Fix: Instruct them to treat the boundary as a genuine personal limit, keeping their performance grounded and realistic.
- Coaching Cue: If a player hesitates before touching or speaking, side-coach them to verbalize that hesitation as a check-in: 'Ask first!'
- Pitfall: The initiator trying to persuade the other player after a 'No' or 'Yes, but' response. Fix: Pause the scene immediately and remind the group that consent overrides narrative agreement.

## Variations
- The Silent Canvas: Conduct the scene where check-ins must be done entirely through clear, exaggerated physical gestures and eye contact rather than spoken words.
- The Shared Boundary: The entire ensemble agrees on one collective environmental boundary before the scene starts, practicing group navigation of a shared sensitive space.

## Debrief
- How did it feel to explicitly ask for permission before making a physical or emotional move in a scene?
- For those who declined or modified an offer, did you feel any internal pressure to 'pander' or say yes just to keep the scene moving?
- What did you notice about the pacing and trust in the scene when boundaries were openly negotiated?
- How can we carry the practice of active check-ins into our regular, fast-paced improv scenes?

## Safety & Inclusion
This game is highly safety-sensitive. The facilitator must establish a zero-judgment environment before play begins. Ensure all boundary slips contain low-to-moderate triggers (avoiding severe trauma topics). Emphasize that the 'Cut' call is absolute, requires no explanation, and must be respected instantly by everyone in the room, including the facilitator.

## Why It Works
By gamifying the negotiation of boundaries, this exercise dismantles the toxic improv myth that players must always say 'yes' to physical or emotional escalation. It replaces passive compliance with active, verbalized agency. The secret boundary slips simulate real-world internal discomfort, giving players low-stakes practice in asserting their limits and training partners to receive a 'no' with grace and immediate adaptability.
