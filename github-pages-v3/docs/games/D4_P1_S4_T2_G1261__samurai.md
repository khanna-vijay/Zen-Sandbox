# Samurai Circle

> A high-energy, rhythmic passing game that builds group focus, physical commitment, and split-second timing.

## Overview
Players stand in a circle and pass a virtual energy blade using synchronized physical movements and sharp vocalizations. Each pass requires a three-person coordinated action: one player initiates, and their immediate neighbors strike in unison, before the energy is sent to a new target. It is a fast-paced, high-commitment warm-up that demands intense peripheral awareness and collective rhythm.

## What It Trains
- **Domain:** D4 — The Ensemble

- **Technique(s):** Timing exercises; Object work; Vocal characterization; Gibberish

**Objective:** To develop group mind, split-second timing, and physical/vocal commitment by coordinating rapid, multi-person physical actions without hesitation.

## Setup
Players stand in a clear, open circle with enough space to swing their arms safely without making physical contact. No props are needed.

## How to Play
1. Form a standing circle facing inward, ensuring everyone has enough space to move their arms freely.
2. One player begins as the initiator by raising both hands together above their head (as if holding a vertical energy blade) and making a sharp, loud vocalization (e.g., 'Ha!').
3. Immediately, the two players standing directly to the left and right of the initiator must turn toward them and make a horizontal slashing motion toward the initiator's midsection, accompanied by their own synchronized vocalization (e.g., 'Ho!').
4. The initiator must hold their hands high until their neighbors complete their strike, absorbing the energy.
5. Once the neighbors strike, the initiator quickly brings their hands down, pointing them directly at another player across the circle with a final vocalization (e.g., 'Hi!').
6. The player who was pointed at immediately becomes the new initiator, raising their hands above their head with a 'Ha!', and the cycle repeats with their own immediate neighbors.
7. Keep the momentum going, gradually increasing the speed of the passes while maintaining crisp, committed movements and clear vocalizations.

## Facilitation Notes
- Coaching cue: 'Commit fully to the sound and movement—don't half-heart the gesture!'
- Coaching cue: 'Neighbors, watch your partner's hands. As soon as they go up, you must strike instantly.'
- Pitfall: Players making actual physical contact. Fix: Remind everyone that all movements are mimed and must stop at least a foot away from the target's body.
- Pitfall: The rhythm stalls because neighbors are daydreaming. Fix: Encourage the group to maintain active peripheral vision so they are always ready to support their neighbor.

## Variations
- Silent Blade: Play the entire game in complete silence, relying purely on visual cues, breath, and physical synchronization.
- Sound Shift: Change the vocalizations every round (e.g., using animal sounds, operatic notes, or gibberish words) to challenge vocal flexibility.
- Double Blade: Introduce a second active 'blade' into the circle once the group has mastered the rhythm of a single pass, forcing players to track multiple points of focus.

## Debrief
- How did it feel when you had to support your neighbor without being the center of attention?
- What happened to our collective focus when we increased the speed?
- How does this game demonstrate the concept of 'making your partner look good'?

## Safety & Inclusion
Ensure players maintain physical distance to prevent accidental contact. Encourage participants to adapt the physical gestures (e.g., smaller arm movements or seated participation) to suit their physical comfort and mobility levels. Avoid any culturally insensitive caricatures or mock accents; keep vocalizations abstract, powerful, and self-generated.

## Why It Works
It forces players out of their heads and into their bodies. By requiring three people to coordinate a single action (one initiator, two neighbors), it builds an immediate, shared rhythm that cannot succeed without deep active listening and peripheral awareness.
