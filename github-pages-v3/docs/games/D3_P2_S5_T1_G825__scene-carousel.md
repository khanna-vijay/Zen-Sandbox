# Scene Carousel

![Scene Carousel — infographic](img/D3_P2_S5_T1_G825__scene-carousel.jpg){ .game-hero }

> A fast-paced rotation of two-person scenes focusing on establishing a strong, immediate base reality.

## Overview
Scene Carousel is a high-energy, rapid-fire training drill where players rotate quickly in and out of the performance space to practice launching two-person scenes. By performing multiple short, distinct scenes in quick succession, players build muscle memory for establishing a clear base reality using C.R.O.W. (Character, Relationship, Objective, Where) right from the very first line.

## What It Trains
- **Domain:** D3 — The Scene
- **Principle(s):** Base Reality First; Start in the Middle; Commit 100%; Group Mind
- **Skill(s):** World-Building; Unfiltered Spontaneity; Pacing & Rhythm; Stage Presence & Clarity
- **Technique(s):** C.R.O.W. (Character, Relationship, Objective, Where); First Thought drills; Edits (Sweep, Tag-Out, Sound/Light)
- **Focus:** skill_drill

**Objective:** To develop the ability to rapidly establish a solid base reality (C.R.O.W.) and start scenes in the middle with high commitment and unfiltered spontaneity.

## Setup
An active playing space with two designated 'wings' or side-lines where off-stage players stand. Divide the group of 4 to 12 players equally, with half standing on the left side of the stage and half on the right. No props or chairs are needed.

## How to Play
1. Divide the players into two equal groups, standing on the left and right sides of the stage area, leaving the center stage clear.
2. The facilitator prompts one player from the left and one player from the right to step into the center of the stage.
3. The facilitator provides a simple suggestion (such as a location, an unusual object, or a relationship) to kick off the scene.
4. The two active players immediately initiate a scene, focusing on establishing their C.R.O.W. (Character, Relationship, Objective, Where) within the first three lines of dialogue or physical actions.
5. Players must start in the middle of the action, avoiding long expositions or 'talking head' setups by using physical object work and clear emotional choices.
6. After approximately 60 to 90 seconds, or once a clear base reality is established, the facilitator calls out 'Scene!' or 'Rotate!'.
7. The active players immediately step back to their respective sides, and the next pair (one from each side) steps forward without hesitation.
8. The facilitator provides a new suggestion, and the new pair begins their scene instantly, maintaining a brisk, rhythmic pace.
9. Continue the rotation until all players have cycled through the center stage multiple times, experiencing different scene partners.

## Facilitation Notes
- Side-coach C.R.O.W. elements: If a scene feels vague, call out 'Who are you to each other?' or 'Where are you physically?' to prompt immediate definition.
- Encourage physical initiation: Remind players to start with an action or object work rather than just standing and talking, which instantly helps establish the 'Where'.
- Keep the edits fast: Do not let scenes drag. The goal is the launch and the base reality, not a complete narrative arc. Edit as soon as the platform is clear.
- Pitfall: Players waiting on the sides might overthink their next entrance. Fix: Remind them to stay present, listen to the current scene, and step out with a completely blank slate.

## Variations
- Tag-Team Carousel: Instead of the facilitator editing, off-stage players can clap or yell 'Rotate!' to tag themselves in, taking over the physical posture of the departing player.
- Silent Launch: The first 30 seconds of each new scene must be entirely non-verbal, relying solely on physical environment work and relationship-establishing behavior before any dialogue is spoken.
- C.R.O.W. Challenge: The facilitator calls out a specific letter of the C.R.O.W. acronym (e.g., 'Relationship!') right before the scene starts, and the players must make that element the absolute focus of their first line.

## Debrief
- What strategies helped you establish a clear relationship and setting within the first few seconds of a scene?
- How did starting 'in the middle' of an action affect your commitment and spontaneity?
- What did you notice about the scenes where physical object work was used right away versus scenes that started with just dialogue?

## Safety & Inclusion
Ensure the transition path between the sides and the center stage is clear of tripping hazards. Players with mobility considerations can sit on chairs at the sides and simply stand or roll forward when it is their turn, or the group can adjust the rotation to accommodate physical comfort levels.

## Why It Works
By stripping away the pressure to sustain a long-form narrative, the Carousel format allows players to focus entirely on the critical opening moments of a scene. The rapid repetition builds muscle memory for C.R.O.W., training improvisers to trust their first instincts, commit 100% immediately, and co-create a shared physical and emotional reality with their partner without overthinking.
