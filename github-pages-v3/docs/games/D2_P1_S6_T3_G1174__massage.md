# The Consent Connection

> Practice explicit consent and physical boundary negotiation through a structured, supportive group warm-up.

## Overview
In this physical warm-up, players stand in a circle and offer shoulder contact to the person in front of them. Rather than a passive activity, the focus is placed entirely on active, low-stakes practice of asking for permission, stating preferences, and adjusting touch in real-time.

## What It Trains
- **Domain:** D2 — The Partner
- **Principle(s):** Consent & Boundaries; Group Mind
- **Skill(s):** Boundary Navigation; Peripheral Awareness
- **Technique(s):** Negotiating physical contact; Check-ins
- **Focus:** connection

**Objective:** To develop comfort with negotiating physical boundaries, practicing explicit verbal consent, and building peripheral awareness of partner comfort levels.

## Setup
Players stand in a comfortable circle with enough space to turn and face each other's backs. No props are required.

## How to Play
1. Instruct all players to stand in a circle, then turn 90 degrees to their right so everyone is facing the back of the person in front of them.
2. Introduce the 'Consent Check' rule: no physical contact can begin until the giver explicitly asks for permission and receives a clear response.
3. Have each player ask the person in front of them a specific question, such as: 'May I massage your shoulders?'
4. Instruct the receiver to respond with one of three options: a clear 'Yes', a modified request (e.g., 'Yes, but very light pressure'), or a polite 'No, thank you'.
5. If a receiver says 'No, thank you', the giver must immediately respect this by keeping their hands to themselves, perhaps resting them on their own chest or holding them in a supportive, non-touching gesture near the partner's shoulders.
6. Once consent is established, players begin the shoulder massage, with givers checking in after a few seconds to ask if the pressure is comfortable.
7. After approximately one minute, call out 'Pause and Pivot'. Have all players turn 180 degrees to face the opposite direction.
8. Repeat the exact same consent check and negotiation process with the new person now standing in front of them before any physical contact begins.

## Facilitation Notes
- Coaching cue: 'A "No" is a complete sentence and a gift of clarity. Celebrate boundaries when they are set.'
- Pitfall: Players feeling social pressure to say 'Yes' even if they are uncomfortable. Fix: Explicitly model saying 'No, thank you' during the initial demonstration to normalize it.
- Coaching cue: 'Check in mid-way. Preferences can change, and it is okay to ask for lighter or heavier pressure at any point.'
- Pitfall: Rushing into physical contact out of habit without waiting for the verbal consent check. Fix: Freeze the group immediately if anyone touches before asking, and gently reset the step.

## Variations
- Self-Massage Option: Anyone who prefers not to receive touch from others can perform self-massage on their own shoulders or neck while remaining an active part of the circle.
- The Pressure Dial: Receivers use a scale of 1 to 5 to communicate pressure preferences dynamically during the massage, practicing real-time physical negotiation.
- Non-Contact Energy Wave: Instead of physical touch, players hover their hands two inches away from the other person's back, sending 'warmth' or 'energy' to practice spatial and peripheral awareness without contact.

## Debrief
- How did it feel to explicitly ask for permission before making physical contact?
- For those who set a boundary or requested an adjustment, how did it feel to have that boundary respected immediately?
- How does practicing explicit consent in a simple warm-up help us navigate physical choices and safety in an active, high-energy improv scene?

## Safety & Inclusion
Participation in physical touch must be entirely optional. The facilitator must explicitly state before the game begins that saying 'no' or opting for self-massage is a perfectly valid and supported choice. Ensure the space is physically accessible for all players to stand and turn comfortably.

## Why It Works
This game demystifies consent by turning it into a structured, low-stakes game mechanic. By normalizing boundary-setting and modified agreements in a warm-up, it builds a group culture of safety and trust, which is essential for bold, collaborative physical choices on stage.
