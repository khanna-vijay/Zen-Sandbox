# The Slip Bucket

> Seamlessly justify random audience-written lines to ground an absurd reality.

## Overview
Two players perform a scene while periodically drawing random, pre-written audience suggestions from a bucket. They must read these lines verbatim and immediately justify them within the context of their established scene, turning non-sequiturs into logical plot points.

## What It Trains
- **Domain:** D3 — The Scene
- **Principle(s):** Base Reality First; Yes, And; The Audience Is the Final Scene Partner
- **Skill(s):** Justification; Offer Reception; Heightening & Exploration
- **Technique(s):** Justify the absurd; Endowment-acceptance
- **Focus:** comedy_game

**Objective:** To develop the skill of justification (specifically justifying the absurd) and establishing a strong base reality first, before integrating chaotic external inputs.

## Setup
Before the game, the audience writes random, single-sentence phrases on slips of paper. These are folded and placed in a bucket (or hat) on stage. Two players stand in the performance space, with the bucket easily accessible (either on a stool or held by a host/player).

## How to Play
1. Gather random, single-sentence suggestions from the audience on slips of paper before the scene begins, folding them and placing them in a central bucket.
2. Ask the audience for a simple, grounded suggestion (e.g., a location or relationship) to initiate the scene.
3. Begin the scene by establishing a clear, normal base reality (who, what, where) for at least 30-45 seconds before drawing any slips.
4. At a moment of tension or transition, one player physically walks to the bucket, draws a slip of paper, and reads the line aloud verbatim as their next line of dialogue.
5. The receiving player must immediately accept this line as absolute truth ("Yes, And") and justify why that specific line was said in this context.
6. Continue the scene, alternating who draws slips, ensuring that every absurd line is fully integrated and explored before drawing the next one.
7. Build the scene to a natural climax where the random lines are fully woven into the narrative logic, then edit.

## Facilitation Notes
- Coaching cue: 'Establish the normal first! If the scene is already crazy, the slips won't land.'
- Pitfall: Drawing slips too quickly. Fix: Coach players to breathe and fully explore the consequences of one slip before reaching for another.
- Coaching cue: 'Justify the emotion behind the line, not just the literal words.'
- Pitfall: Treating the slip as a joke or breaking character. Fix: Remind players to stay grounded and treat the slip as a genuine, serious statement from their character.

## Variations
- Pocket Slips: Instead of a central bucket, players stuff their pockets with slips before the scene starts and pull them out dynamically.
- Blind Choice: A third player or host hands them the slips at key moments to keep their hands free for object work.
- Emotional Justification: The player must read the slip in a specific emotion dictated by the other player right before the draw.

## Debrief
- How did establishing a strong base reality first make it easier to justify the weird lines?
- What strategies did you use to make an absurd line sound completely logical in your character's mouth?
- How does treating a random input as a gift rather than an obstacle change the energy of the scene?

## Safety & Inclusion
Ensure audience prompts are vetted briefly if performing in sensitive environments. Remind players they can discard a slip if it contains offensive or incomprehensible handwriting.

## Why It Works
By forcing players to treat random, disconnected text as absolute truth, it bypasses the analytical brain and demands immediate justification. When players commit to making the absurd make sense within a grounded base reality, it creates high-stakes comedy that feels organic rather than forced.
