# Cinematic Cue Cards

<div class="game-hero-ph" markdown>🎨 *Infographic coming soon*</div>

> Navigate sudden cinematic genre shifts by anchoring your scene in a rock-solid base reality.

## Overview
In this high-energy narrative game, players improvise a short film based on an audience suggestion. Throughout the performance, designated audience members trigger sudden cinematic style shifts by raising physical cue cards, forcing the actors to adapt instantly while maintaining their core story.

## What It Trains
- **Domain:** D3 — The Scene
- **Principle(s):** Base Reality First; Serve the Story; The Audience Is the Final Scene Partner
- **Skill(s):** World-Building; Narrative Architecture; Audience-Energy Management
- **Technique(s):** C.R.O.W. (Character, Relationship, Objective, Where); Platform/Tilt
- **Focus:** mixed

**Objective:** To train players in establishing a strong, immediate base reality (C.R.O.W.) that can withstand high-energy, chaotic external disruptions, while practicing narrative architecture and audience interaction.

## Setup
Prepare 4 to 6 large, easily readable cue cards with cinematic tropes (e.g., 'Slow Motion', 'Montage', 'CGI Effects', 'Romantic Interlude', 'Dramatic Death Scene'). Set up a microphone off-stage for a narrator. Distribute the cards to two or three audience members sitting in the front row. Position 2 to 4 active players on stage, with remaining players off-stage ready to support.

## How to Play
1. Distribute the cue cards to front-row audience members and instruct them to hold up a card and shout its title whenever they feel the scene needs a dramatic shift.
2. Ask the audience for a non-existent movie title or a simple genre suggestion to inspire the start of the film.
3. The on-stage players begin the scene, focusing heavily on establishing who they are, their relationship, their objectives, and the physical environment (Where) within the first 30 seconds.
4. Once the base reality is set, the audience members can begin raising their cards at any time.
5. When a card is raised, the off-stage player on the microphone immediately reads it aloud to ensure the entire room hears the cue.
6. The on-stage players must instantly transition their physical and verbal play to match the card's style without breaking character or abandoning the established plot.
7. Players must justify why this cinematic shift is happening within their current story, ensuring the plot continues to move forward rather than resetting.
8. The game concludes once all cue cards have been successfully integrated and the players bring the movie to a satisfying narrative climax and resolution.

## Facilitation Notes
- Coaching Cue: 'Establish the Where and Who first! Don't wait for the cards to start acting.'
- Pitfall: Players immediately jump into high-energy antics before establishing a platform. Fix: Instruct the audience card-holders to wait at least 30 seconds before raising the first card to let the actors build their C.R.O.W.
- Coaching Cue: 'Justify the shift! If it's Slow Motion, why has time slowed down for these characters?'
- Pitfall: The off-stage microphone reader is too slow, causing confusion. Fix: Ensure the off-stage player is highly attentive, treating their role as a live sports commentator who amplifies the audience's input instantly.

## Variations
- Director's Cut: An off-stage director can call 'Cut!' to briefly re-adjust the scene or add a 'behind-the-scenes' commentary before resuming.
- Genre Swap: Instead of cinematic styles, the cards represent completely different theatrical genres (e.g., Shakespeare, Soap Opera, Sci-Fi).
- Silent Film: The actors must play the entire game without speaking, relying purely on physical world-building and exaggerated expressions to convey C.R.O.W.

## Debrief
- How did having a strong initial platform (C.R.O.W.) help you handle the chaotic shifts of the cards?
- What strategies did you use to justify sudden stylistic changes without abandoning your character's objective?
- How did the audience's timing of the cards affect the pacing and energy of your storytelling?

## Safety & Inclusion
Since cards like 'Fight Scene' or 'Romantic Interlude' can encourage physical contact, establish clear boundaries before starting. Remind players to use non-contact stage combat techniques and to respect personal space boundaries during romantic or high-physicality prompts.

## Why It Works
This game works because it gamifies the tension between narrative stability and chaotic disruption. By forcing players to adapt to external prompts, it highlights the necessity of a 'Base Reality First' approach; without a clear platform, the stylistic shifts feel empty, but with a solid C.R.O.W., the shifts become hilarious, justified narrative escalations.
