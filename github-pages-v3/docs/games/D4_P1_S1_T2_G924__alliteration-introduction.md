# Alliterative Name Train

<div class="game-hero-ph" markdown>🎨 *Infographic coming soon*</div>

> Build group memory, physical connection, and focus by chaining names, adjectives, and physical gestures.

## Overview
A classic circle-based icebreaker and focus exercise where players introduce themselves using an alliterative adjective and a distinct physical gesture. Each subsequent player must recall and perform the entire sequence of names, adjectives, and gestures of everyone who came before them before adding their own.

## What It Trains
- **Domain:** D4 — The Ensemble
- **Principle(s):** Group Mind; The First Thought Is a Gift
- **Skill(s):** Peripheral Awareness; Unfiltered Spontaneity; Physicality & Space Work; Active Listening; Single-Partner Empathy & Mirroring
- **Technique(s):** Thread-tracking drills; First Thought drills; Mirror exercise

**Objective:** Develops group mind, active listening, and thread-tracking by requiring players to physically and vocally mirror their peers' choices while maintaining focus under cumulative pressure.

## Setup
Players stand in a clear circle facing inward with enough space to move their arms and take a step forward. No props or materials are required.

## How to Play
1. Gather the group into a standing circle where everyone has a clear line of sight to all other participants.
2. The first player begins by stepping forward slightly, stating an adjective that starts with the same letter as their first name, saying their name, and performing a distinct physical gesture.
3. The second player, moving clockwise, must turn to the first player, repeat their adjective and name, and mirror their physical gesture exactly.
4. The second player then turns back to the circle and introduces themselves with their own unique alliterative adjective, name, and gesture.
5. The third player must repeat player one's adjective, name, and gesture, then player two's adjective, name, and gesture, before adding their own new introduction.
6. Continue around the circle in this cumulative fashion, with each player tracking and performing the entire thread of previous introductions in order.
7. The game concludes when it reaches the first player again, who must successfully perform the name, adjective, and gesture of every single person in the circle in sequence.

## Facilitation Notes
- Coaching cue: Support the person speaking! If someone gets stuck, the whole circle can gently mirror the gesture or whisper the name to help them out.
- Coaching cue: Keep the gestures bold and simple. Tiny movements are hard to see and track; big, physical choices are easier to remember.
- Pitfall: Players overthinking their adjective. Fix: Remind them that the first thought is a gift. It does not have to be a deep truth; Silly Sam or Salty Sam are both perfect.
- Pitfall: The chain becomes too long and stressful for later players. Fix: Encourage the group to physically mirror along silently to keep their muscle memory active, rather than just staring blankly.

## Variations
- Group Chorus: Instead of one person repeating the whole chain alone, the entire circle performs each past step in unison as the active player points to them.
- Speed Run: Once the chain is complete, try to pass the entire sequence around the circle as fast as possible without any pauses.
- Reverse Train: Run the chain counter-clockwise, or have the final player go backward from the person next to them all the way to the start.

## Debrief
- How did it feel when the group supported you when you forgot a step?
- What strategies did you use to remember the names and gestures of people far away from you?
- How did physicalizing the gesture help you remember the verbal name?

## Safety & Inclusion
Ensure players are mindful of physical limitations when choosing their gestures; encourage low-impact, accessible movements that anyone can mirror regardless of mobility.

## Why It Works
By linking a verbal label with a physical action, the game engages multiple cognitive pathways. The cumulative structure builds thread-tracking skills, forcing players to maintain active peripheral awareness and deep listening rather than checking out when it is not their turn.
