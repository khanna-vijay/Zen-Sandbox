# Boundary Blueprints

<div class="game-hero-ph" markdown>🎨 *Infographic coming soon*</div>

> Build a culture of active consent by pausing scenes to negotiate physical boundaries in real-time.

## Overview
This structured exercise trains players to navigate physical contact and personal boundaries dynamically during active scene work. By assigning observing players as safety monitors who can pause the action, the group practices explicit verbal check-ins and learns to prioritize personal safety over comedic momentum. The experience normalizes saying no and gracefully adapting to boundaries without breaking the reality of the performance.

## What It Trains
- **Domain:** D2 — The Partner
- **Principle(s):** Consent & Boundaries; Truth Over Pandering
- **Skill(s):** Boundary Navigation; Active Listening; Peripheral Awareness
- **Technique(s):** Check-ins; Cut calls; Negotiating physical contact
- **Focus:** skill_drill

**Objective:** To develop robust boundary navigation and active listening skills, specifically training players to execute verbal check-ins, assert personal boundaries truthfully, and respect peer boundaries without hesitation.

## Setup
An open playing space with chairs. Provide each player with an index card and a pen. Ensure the group is briefed on the importance of psychological safety and that no is a complete and celebrated answer.

## How to Play
1. Secret Boundary Creation: Each player writes down one specific physical action or type of contact they are uncomfortable with or require explicit permission for on their index card (e.g., touching my hair, whispering in my ear). This card remains secret.
2. Role Assignment: Divide the group of 4 to 6 players. Assign 2 to 3 players to start the scene, and the remaining 1 to 2 players to act as Consent Architects standing on the sidelines.
3. Scene Initiation: Obtain a simple, neutral suggestion that naturally invites physical proximity or collaboration (e.g., packing a fragile box together or sharing a narrow bench). The active players begin a standard scene.
4. The Blueprint Call: The Consent Architects watch closely. If they observe imminent physical contact, a player hesitating, or a potential boundary breach, they must call out Blueprint Moment! to freeze the scene.
5. Architect Explanation: The Architect who called the freeze briefly and neutrally states what they observed (e.g., It looked like Player A was about to place a hand on Player B's shoulder. Let's check in).
6. Verbal Negotiation: The active players step slightly out of character (or remain in character if appropriate) to explicitly negotiate the proposed action. The initiator asks permission (e.g., May I put my hand on your shoulder?).
7. Upholding the Truth: The receiving player must answer honestly. If the action violates their secret boundary, they must say No or I'm not comfortable with that. If it does not, they may consent or negotiate a modified version.
8. Resuming Play: If consent is granted, the action occurs and the scene continues. If consent is denied, the initiator must accept the No gracefully, adapt their physical choices, and continue the scene without showing frustration.
9. The Cut Call: At any point, if a boundary is accidentally crossed or a player feels unsafe, any player (active or observing) can call Cut! to immediately stop the scene. The facilitator will step in to support the players and reset.
10. Rotation: Run the scene for 4 to 5 minutes, then rotate roles so every player experiences being an active performer and a Consent Architect.

## Facilitation Notes
- Side-coaching Cues: Remind the Consent Architects to look for micro-expressions, physical retreats, or sudden freezes in the active players as cues to call a Blueprint Moment!
- Pitfall & Fix: Players might choose highly traumatic boundaries. Fix: Instruct players to choose low-stakes physical boundaries (e.g., no high-fives) that are still real to them, keeping the learning environment safe and manageable.
- Pitfall & Fix: Active players might try to power through a boundary to keep the scene funny. Fix: Side-coach immediately, reminding them that Truth Over Pandering means prioritizing personal safety and authentic boundaries over a cheap laugh.
- Normalize the No: Actively praise players who say No and those who receive a No and pivot smoothly. Frame a boundary assertion as a gift that clarifies the scene's physical reality.
- Neutrality in Intervention: Ensure Consent Architects use objective, non-judgmental language when pausing the scene, focusing strictly on physical actions rather than assuming emotional intent.

## Variations
- Fictional Boundaries: To lower the stakes for beginners, players can write down a fictional character boundary (e.g., My character is terrified of being tapped on the shoulder) instead of a personal one.
- Silent Architects: The Consent Architects use a non-verbal signal (like raising a hand) to pause the scene, training the active players to maintain peripheral awareness of their environment.
- Post-Scene Check-in: Add a mandatory 30-second debrief between the active players immediately after the scene ends to practice post-performance care and validation.

## Debrief
- How did it feel to assert your secret boundary and say No in the middle of an active scene?
- As the initiator, how did receiving a No challenge or expand your choices for where the scene went next?
- For the Consent Architects, what specific physical or non-verbal cues helped you identify when to call a Blueprint Moment!?
- How does practicing explicit verbal check-ins in this drill change how you might approach physical contact in a standard, unscripted performance?

## Safety & Inclusion
This game is highly safety-sensitive. The facilitator must establish a strict, non-judgmental environment before play begins. Emphasize that any Cut! call is absolute, requires no explanation from the person calling it, and must be met with immediate compliance and support from the entire room. Ensure players know they can step out of the exercise at any time without penalty.

## Why It Works
By introducing the Consent Architect role, the game shifts the burden of safety from just the active pair to the entire ensemble, building peripheral awareness. The Blueprint Moment normalizes verbal check-ins as a natural, non-disruptive tool, proving that explicit consent actually enhances creative freedom and trust rather than stalling scenic momentum.
