# Message from Ray

<div class="game-hero-ph" markdown>🎨 *Infographic coming soon*</div>

> Seamlessly justify a sudden, mandatory external interruption without breaking your scene's established reality.

## Overview
Two players establish a grounded, relationship-driven scene. At some point, one player must receive an unexpected message from an offstage character named Ray, forcing both players to immediately justify this sudden interruption and integrate it into their existing reality.

## What It Trains
- **Domain:** D3 — The Scene
- **Principle(s):** Base Reality First; Yes, And
- **Skill(s):** Justification; Offer Reception
- **Technique(s):** Justify the absurd; Endowment-acceptance
- **Focus:** mixed

**Objective:** Develops the ability to establish a firm base reality first, receive unexpected external offers, and use creative justification to make absurd or disruptive events feel entirely logical within the scene.

## Setup
Two players stand in the performance space. No props are needed. The facilitator or players agree on the name of the offstage caller (default is 'Ray') and a simple, grounded starting suggestion.

## How to Play
1. Step 1: Two players begin a scene based on a grounded suggestion, focusing entirely on establishing their relationship, the physical environment, and their current activity.
2. Step 2: Once a stable base reality is established, one player initiates the interruption by announcing they are receiving a message from 'Ray'.
3. Step 3: The player receiving the message chooses how it arrives, whether through a ringing phone, a text message, a carrier pigeon, or a sudden telepathic realization.
4. Step 4: The receiving player delivers the content of Ray's message aloud, introducing a new, potentially bizarre, or inconvenient piece of information to the scene.
5. Step 5: Both players accept this new information as absolute truth, using 'Yes, And' to react honestly to the news.
6. Step 6: The players work together to justify why Ray sent this message and how it directly connects to their current relationship or activity.
7. Step 7: The scene continues with this new justified reality fully integrated, playing out the consequences of the message until a natural conclusion is reached.

## Facilitation Notes
- Coaching cue: 'Don't rush the call! Build the room, the relationship, and the routine first so the interruption has something to disrupt.'
- Pitfall: Players use the call to completely abandon their original scene. Fix: Remind them that Ray's message must explain or complicate their current situation, not replace it.
- Coaching cue: 'Make the message matter. If Ray says he is outside with a truckload of watermelons, how does that affect your current anniversary dinner?'
- Pitfall: The non-receiving player ignores the call or treats it as unimportant. Fix: Encourage the partner to have an immediate, strong emotional reaction to the mention of Ray.

## Variations
- The Secret Line: Instead of a call from Ray, the offstage player or facilitator hands a player a slip of paper with a random, absurd sentence that they must say and immediately justify.
- The Multi-Channel Interruption: Both players receive separate, conflicting messages from Ray at different times and must justify how both messages are simultaneously true.
- The Silent Delivery: The message is delivered via a physical action or mime work (e.g., a brick thrown through a window with a note attached), requiring physical justification before the text is read.

## Debrief
- How did having a strong base reality established beforehand make it easier to justify Ray's sudden message?
- What techniques did you use to make the delivery method of the message fit the environment of your scene?
- How did treating the interruption as highly important, rather than a throwaway joke, deepen the scene's comedy and narrative?

## Safety & Inclusion
Ensure players agree that the 'message' should not introduce real-world trauma or highly sensitive personal triggers unless explicitly negotiated beforehand. Keep the tone of the interruption playful or absurd.

## Why It Works
This game works because it highlights the power of justification. By forcing a highly specific, external constraint into a grounded scene, players learn that any offer—no matter how random—can be seamlessly integrated if they treat it as completely normal and meaningful within their established base reality.
