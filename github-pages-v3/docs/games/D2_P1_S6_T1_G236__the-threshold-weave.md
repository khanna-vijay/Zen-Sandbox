# Hidden Thresholds

![Hidden Thresholds — infographic](img/D2_P1_S6_T1_G236__the-threshold-weave.jpg){ .game-hero }

> Navigate secret personal and character boundaries in-scene to prioritize truth over automatic agreement.

## Overview
In this exercise, players perform scenes while secretly holding cards that dictate specific physical, emotional, or situational boundaries. Scene partners must remain highly observant, picking up on subtle non-verbal cues and adjusting their choices in real time. The resulting play balances the traditional drive to agree with the deeper necessity of honoring personal and character limits.

## What It Trains
- **Domain:** D2 — The Partner
- **Principle(s):** Consent & Boundaries; Yes, And; Truth Over Pandering
- **Skill(s):** Active Listening; Boundary Navigation; Peripheral Awareness
- **Technique(s):** Check-ins; Cut calls; Negotiating physical contact
- **Focus:** connection

**Objective:** To develop advanced boundary navigation and active listening skills. Players learn to execute in-character check-ins, negotiate physical contact dynamically, and confidently prioritize personal safety and artistic truth over polite compliance.

## Setup
A moderate playing space with chairs for the audience/judges. Prepare a deck of 'Threshold Cards' divided into three categories: Player-Centric (non-negotiable personal limits), Character-Centric (fictional traits to resist in-character), and Dynamic (situational triggers). Provide a notepad and pen for the designated observer.

## How to Play
1. Assign two to three players to perform the scene, and one to two players to act as Observers with a notepad.
2. Have each active player secretly draw three Threshold Cards from the deck, keeping their boundaries hidden from their scene partners.
3. Allow players a brief moment to review their cards; if any card feels genuinely distressing or unsafe, they may veto it and draw a replacement without explanation.
4. Begin a standard scene based on a simple relationship and location suggestion.
5. Instruct players to actively pursue their scene objectives while strictly honoring their secret boundaries, using subtle physical redirection, verbal deflections, or in-character check-ins if a partner approaches a threshold.
6. Direct the non-active partners to watch for subtle cues of discomfort (such as tensing, stepping back, or shifting eye contact) and proactively adjust their offers to give their partner space.
7. Establish that any player can call a clear, audible 'CUT!' at any point if a player-level boundary is crossed, immediately halting all action for safety.
8. Have the Observers take silent notes on successful boundary navigation, subtle adjustments, and moments where truth was prioritized over compliance.
9. End the scene naturally or upon a 'CUT!' call, and immediately transition into the structured debriefing process.

## Facilitation Notes
- Coaching Cue: 'Read the body language.' Remind players to treat any physical hesitation or verbal deflection from their partner as a yellow light, prompting them to back off or check in.
- Coaching Cue: 'Truth over pandering.' Remind players that breaking the reality of the scene to protect a boundary is a successful, high-level skill, not a failure of 'yes-and'.
- Pitfall: Players treat the secret boundaries as a joke or a gag. Fix: Before starting, emphasize that this is a high-stakes safety and connection exercise; boundaries must be played with genuine commitment.
- Pitfall: Observers intervene during the scene. Fix: Instruct Observers to remain completely silent and avoid eye contact with active players to prevent accidental signaling.
- Pitfall: Players feel guilty for calling 'CUT!'. Fix: Explicitly celebrate 'CUT' calls during the debrief as courageous acts of self-advocacy that keep the ensemble safe.

## Variations
- Known Thresholds: Play the same setup, but players openly state one of their boundaries before the scene starts, keeping the other two secret, to practice navigating both explicit and implicit limits.
- The Verbal Check-In: Require players to use at least one explicit, in-character verbal check-in (e.g., 'Are you comfortable with me stepping closer?') during the scene to practice active verbal negotiation.

## Debrief
- For the active players: What did it feel like internally to balance the urge to 'yes-and' with the absolute requirement to honor your secret boundary?
- For the partners: What subtle physical or verbal cues did you notice from your partner, and how did you adjust your choices in response?
- For the observers: Where did you see successful, graceful boundary navigation that actually deepened the tension or reality of the scene?
- How can we apply this heightened awareness of unspoken boundaries to our everyday, non-gamified scene work?

## Safety & Inclusion
This game deals directly with consent and boundaries. Ensure the deck of cards is vetted beforehand to exclude highly traumatic triggers. Emphasize that the 'CUT' command is absolute, requires no justification, and must be met with immediate silence and support from the entire room.

## Why It Works
By gamifying boundaries with hidden information, the exercise replicates the real-world ambiguity of human interaction. It forces players to move away from automatic, polite agreement ('pandering') and instead cultivates deep peripheral awareness and active listening. Honoring a boundary, rather than ignoring it for the sake of comedy, ultimately builds trust and creates more grounded, high-stakes theatrical relationships.
