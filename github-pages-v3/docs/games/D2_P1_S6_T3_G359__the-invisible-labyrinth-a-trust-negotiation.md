# The Invisible Labyrinth

![The Invisible Labyrinth — infographic](img/D2_P1_S6_T3_G359__the-invisible-labyrinth-a-trust-negotiation.jpg){ .game-hero }

> Navigate an unseen maze of personal boundaries through explicit, step-by-step consent and active negotiation.

## Overview
In this partner-based exercise, one player acts as a Seeker navigating an imaginary physical space with their eyes closed, while their partner acts as the Guide. The Guide holds a mental map of their own personal comfort boundaries, which serve as the invisible walls of the labyrinth. The Seeker must verbally propose every physical movement or contact, and the Guide must practice absolute honesty in setting boundaries, prioritizing personal comfort over theatrical compliance.

## What It Trains
- **Domain:** D2 — The Partner

- **Focus:** connection

**Objective:** To develop practical skills in negotiating physical contact, practicing proactive verbal check-ins, and honoring personal boundaries by prioritizing truth over pandering in real-time collaborative play.

## Setup
Divide players into pairs. Clear the room of physical obstacles to ensure a safe walking space. Designate one player as the Guide and the other as the Seeker. No props are required, but players need enough space to move around each other safely.

## How to Play
1. Define a starting point and an imaginary exit or treasure point across the playing space.
2. The Guide mentally establishes their own personal comfort boundaries for the day (e.g., specific areas of physical touch, proximity, or emotional vulnerability) which will act as the invisible walls of the labyrinth.
3. The Seeker closes their eyes and must navigate toward the exit, moving only by making explicit, verbal proposals for action or contact.
4. The Seeker's proposals must be highly specific, such as: 'May I place my hand on your shoulder to steady myself?' or 'May I step one foot closer to you?'
5. The Guide must respond with absolute honesty, choosing either enthusiastic consent ('Yes, please'), a clear boundary with an alternative ('No, but you can hold my forearm'), or a direct refusal ('No, please step back').
6. If the Guide sets a boundary or says 'No', the Seeker must immediately cease the action without question or debate, and formulate a new, creative proposal to navigate around the boundary.
7. At any point, either player can call a 'Check-in' to verbally assess comfort levels, or call a 'Labyrinth Reset' to immediately halt all action, step out of character, and recalibrate.
8. The round is complete when the Seeker successfully reaches the designated exit through a continuous chain of mutually consented movements.

## Facilitation Notes
- Coaching Cue: Remind Guides that saying 'No' is a successful move, not a block. It provides the structure that makes the labyrinth a playable game.
- Pitfall & Fix: Seekers might make vague requests. Coach them to be highly specific about touch, detailing the exact body part and action (e.g., 'May I touch your left elbow with my right hand?').
- Coaching Cue: Watch for 'pandering' where a Guide says yes to keep the game moving despite feeling slight discomfort. Intervene and remind them that their comfort is the absolute priority.
- Pitfall & Fix: If a player freezes or feels overwhelmed, encourage them to use the 'Labyrinth Reset' immediately. Normalize this as a positive tool for safety rather than a failure.

## Variations
- Non-Verbal Echoes: Play a round where proposals are made verbally, but the Guide responds using pre-agreed non-verbal physical cues (like a gentle tap or step back) to signal boundaries, training physical sensitivity.
- Multi-Seeker Labyrinth: Two Seekers must navigate the labyrinth together, meaning they must negotiate consent with each other and with the Guide simultaneously.

## Debrief
- For the Guides: How did it feel to prioritize your actual comfort over the desire to be 'agreeable' or keep the game moving?
- For the Seekers: How did encountering a boundary change your creative strategy? Did it feel like a block, or did it open up new pathways?
- How can we apply this level of explicit physical negotiation to our regular, fast-paced scenic improv?

## Safety & Inclusion
This game involves physical contact and boundary exploration. Participation must be entirely voluntary. Establish a clear, non-verbal 'stop' signal (like raising both hands) in addition to the verbal 'Labyrinth Reset'. Ensure players know they can opt out of physical touch entirely, making the labyrinth purely spatial by negotiating physical proximity rather than touch.

## Why It Works
By gamifying the negotiation of boundaries, it strips away the social awkwardness of saying 'no'. The Guide's boundaries literally form the structure of the game, reframing a boundary not as a narrative dead-end, but as the very terrain that makes exploration possible. This builds muscle memory for active listening and consent.
