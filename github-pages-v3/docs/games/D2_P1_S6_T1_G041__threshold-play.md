# Threshold Navigation

> Navigate personal boundaries in real time by making consent the driving force of the scene.

## Overview
A structured partner exercise where players use physical and verbal signals to pause or redirect scenes based on their real-time comfort levels. By integrating explicit boundary-setting mechanics directly into the performance, players learn to prioritize personal safety and authentic communication over narrative momentum. The experience shifts the traditional 'yes-and' instinct from blindly accepting plot offers to enthusiastically honoring the physical and emotional reality of their partner.

## What It Trains
- **Domain:** D2 — The Partner
- **Principle(s):** Consent & Boundaries; Truth Over Pandering
- **Skill(s):** Boundary Navigation; Active Listening
- **Technique(s):** Check-ins; Cut calls; Negotiating physical contact
- **Focus:** skill_drill

**Objective:** To develop practical boundary navigation and active listening skills by practicing real-time check-ins and immediate scene redirection, reinforcing the principle of consent and prioritizing truth over pandering.

## Setup
An open, comfortable playing space. Players stand in a circle for the initial briefing and physical practice, then split into pairs or trios to perform on the stage area while the remaining players observe as supportive active listeners. No props or materials are required.

## How to Play
1. Introduce the 'Pause Threshold' signal: A player places one flat hand on their own chest and immediately states how they feel (e.g., 'I feel hesitant about how close we are standing').
2. Demonstrate the partner's response to a Pause: The partner must immediately halt all action, acknowledge the statement, and offer a collaborative adjustment (e.g., 'Understood. Let me take a step back. Is this distance better?').
3. Introduce the 'Hard Stop Threshold' signal: A player crosses both hands flat over their chest and states a clear boundary (e.g., 'I need to stop this line of conversation'). No justification or explanation is required.
4. Demonstrate the partner's response to a Hard Stop: The partner must immediately stop the action, acknowledge the boundary, and completely pivot the scene's direction, treating the boundary as an absolute, non-negotiable reality.
5. Have the entire group practice both physical gestures and sample 'I feel...' statements in unison to build muscle memory and normalize the mechanics.
6. Invite two players to the stage and provide a neutral, relationship-focused prompt (e.g., 'Two neighbors planning a shared fence' or 'Two coworkers organizing a storage closet').
7. Instruct the active players to improvise the scene while continuously monitoring their own internal comfort levels and actively scanning their partner's non-verbal cues.
8. Direct players to proactively use the Pause or Hard Stop signals the moment they sense their comfort shifting or a boundary being approached.
9. Run the scene for 3 to 5 minutes, allowing multiple pauses or pivots to occur naturally, and instruct the facilitator to immediately freeze the scene if a signal is ignored or negotiated.

## Facilitation Notes
- Side-coaching cue: 'Listen to your gut, not the plot. If you feel a shift, use your hand signal immediately.'
- Pitfall: Players treating the 'Pause' or 'Hard Stop' as an in-character joke or dramatic device. Fix: Pause the scene immediately and remind the group that these signals are always out-of-character, real-world communication tools that must be treated with absolute sincerity.
- Side-coaching cue: 'Yes-and the boundary, not the hazard. The new reality of the scene is the safety of your partner.'
- Pitfall: The responding partner trying to argue, explain, or negotiate a 'Hard Stop'. Fix: Intervene instantly, call a freeze, and instruct the player to simply say 'Understood' and pivot the scene entirely.
- Encourage players to use a wide vocabulary of feelings (e.g., cautious, overwhelmed, curious, guarded) during the Pause verbalization to help partners understand how to adjust.

## Variations
- Non-Verbal Attunement: Run a round where players are not allowed to speak, relying entirely on physical proximity, eye contact, and the physical hand signals to negotiate space and comfort.
- Power Dynamics: Assign roles with inherent power imbalances (e.g., supervisor and employee) to practice setting and respecting boundaries when social pressure is elevated.

## Debrief
- For the person who signaled: How did it feel to prioritize your internal comfort over the momentum of the scene?
- For the responder: What non-verbal cues did you notice in your partner before they officially used a signal, and how did you adapt?
- For the group: How does treating a boundary as the 'new offer' change our understanding of the 'Yes, And' rule?
- How can we carry this heightened awareness of our partners' comfort into our regular, high-energy improv scenes?

## Safety & Inclusion
This exercise is highly safety-sensitive. Participation must be entirely voluntary, and players must be reminded that they have absolute agency over their bodies and comfort. If a player feels overwhelmed, they may step out of the exercise at any time without explanation. Ensure the room is established as a zero-judgment zone before beginning.

## Why It Works
By embedding boundary-setting directly into the physical and verbal mechanics of the game, it removes the social friction of speaking up. It reframes a boundary not as a 'mistake' or a 'scene-killer,' but as a vital, creative offer that the partner must accept and build upon. This builds deep psychological safety and trains improvisers to prioritize their partner's real-world well-being over theatrical performance.
