# Doo-Wop Narrative

> Tell a tragicomic love story through structured 1950s-style vocal harmonies and narrative verses.

## Overview
In this musical narrative game, a lead singer and a backing group collaborate to tell a complete story arc about a beloved object using a classic 1950s doo-wop structure. The players blend vocal harmony, rhythmic support, and structured storytelling into a high-energy, melodramatic performance.

## What It Trains
- **Domain:** D3 — The Scene
- **Principle(s):** Serve the Story; Commit 100%; Group Mind
- **Skill(s):** Narrative Architecture; Vocal Craft; Support Work; Pacing & Rhythm
- **Technique(s):** Story Spine; Vocal characterization
- **Focus:** mixed

**Objective:** To develop narrative architecture and pacing by mapping the classic Story Spine onto a highly structured, rhythmic musical format.

## Setup
Three to five players stand in a line or semi-circle facing the audience. No instruments or props are required. The facilitator obtains a suggestion of a mundane household object to start.

## How to Play
1. Obtain a suggestion of a simple, everyday object from the audience to serve as the subject of the song.
2. The backing singers immediately establish a rhythmic, looping 1950s doo-wop chord progression using nonsense syllables like 'shoo-wop' or 'dum-de-dum'.
3. Verse 1 (The Setup): The lead singer steps forward and sings a verse introducing the suggested object and establishing their deep, exaggerated love for it.
4. Chorus 1: The entire group joins in a high-energy, harmonized chorus celebrating their love for the object.
5. Verse 2 (The Backstory): The lead singer sings a second verse explaining the origin story of how they first acquired this wonderful item.
6. Chorus 2: The group repeats the celebratory chorus to reinforce the emotional baseline of the story.
7. Verse 3 (The Climax/Loss): The lead singer delivers a dramatic verse detailing the tragic moment or fateful night they lost their beloved object, often spoken rhythmically over a quieter backing track.
8. The Bridge (The Despair): The lead singer sings a soulful, high-emotion bridge expressing their deep grief, leading into a modified chorus about how much they miss the object.
9. Verse 4 (The Resolution): The lead singer sings a final verse explaining how they resolved their grief, typically by finding a surprising new replacement object.
10. Final Chorus: The entire group sings a triumphant final chorus celebrating the new object, ending on a big, coordinated harmony.

## Facilitation Notes
- Coaching cue: 'Commit to the melodrama!' The narrative works best when the emotional stakes for the mundane object are ridiculously high.
- Pitfall: Backing singers overcomplicating their harmonies and drowning out the lead. Fix: Remind backing singers to keep their volume lower than the lead and stick to simple, repetitive rhythmic loops.
- Coaching cue: 'Keep the beat steady!' Do not let the tempo drag during the spoken or highly emotional verses.
- If the lead singer gets stuck on a rhyme or narrative beat, backing singers should step up their physical energy and volume to give the lead a moment to recover.

## Variations
- Rotating Lead: Each verse is sung by a different player, rotating who steps up to lead while the others slide back into the harmony group.
- A Cappella Challenge: No instruments or keyboard accompaniment allowed; the backing group must provide all percussion, basslines, and chordal support vocally.
- Genre Swap: Apply the exact same narrative structure to a different musical genre, such as a country ballad, punk rock, or opera.

## Debrief
- How did the strict musical structure help or hinder your ability to advance the story?
- What did the backing singers do to support the emotional shifts of the lead singer, especially during the loss and the bridge?
- How does raising the emotional stakes of a mundane object make a narrative more compelling to watch?

## Safety & Inclusion
Ensure players feel comfortable singing in front of others. Emphasize that vocal quality is secondary to commitment and narrative clarity. Allow players to speak-sing (parlando) if they are anxious about hitting melodic notes.

## Why It Works
This game succeeds because the repetitive chorus structure gives the lead singer time to plan the next narrative beat of the Story Spine. The backing group's physical and vocal support builds a strong sense of Group Mind, ensuring the lead never feels isolated on stage.
