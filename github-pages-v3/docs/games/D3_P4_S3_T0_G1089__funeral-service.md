# Memorial Service

> Uncover a life through heartfelt eulogies and the chaotic flashbacks they inspire.

## Overview
In this structured narrative format, players construct the life story of a recently deceased character. Through a series of eulogies and theatrical flashbacks, the ensemble builds a cohesive, bittersweet, and often hilarious biography of a single character.

## What It Trains
- **Domain:** D3 — The Scene
- **Principle(s):** Serve the Story; Serve the Piece; The First Thought Is a Gift; Make Your Partner a Genius
- **Skill(s):** Narrative Architecture; Format Literacy; Unfiltered Spontaneity; Active Gifting; World-Building
- **Technique(s):** Longform vs. shortform mechanics; Edits (Sweep, Tag-Out, Sound/Light); C.R.O.W. (Character, Relationship, Objective, Where)
- **Focus:** narrative

**Objective:** To develop narrative architecture, edit-by-entrance, and collaborative world-building by weaving monologues and active scenes into a unified story.

## Setup
Place a table or a row of chairs in the center of the stage to represent a casket. One player lies down as the deceased. The remaining players sit or stand in a semi-circle around the casket, acting as mourners.

## How to Play
1. One player volunteers to play the deceased and lies down in the casket (the table or chairs).
2. Ask the group or audience for an unusual occupation, a bizarre hobby, or a peculiar cause of death for the deceased.
3. Play a brief opening scene showing how the character died, featuring the deceased player and one or two other players.
4. Once the character dies in the scene, they return to the casket, and the funeral service officially begins with the remaining players assuming characters who knew them.
5. A player steps up to the downstage podium to deliver a short monologue sharing a specific memory, secret, or defining moment they shared with the deceased.
6. As soon as the eulogist describes a specific event, other players sweep onto the stage to play out that exact scene as a flashback.
7. The flashback scene is played with high commitment, treating the eulogist's words as absolute truth, with the deceased player stepping out of the casket to play their living self.
8. Once the flashback reaches a natural climax, the players edit the scene by returning to their seats, and the eulogist wraps up their speech.
9. Repeat the eulogy and flashback cycle with different players, ensuring each new eulogy builds upon the details established in previous scenes.
10. The game concludes with a brief, collective final moment, such as a group song, a final toast, or a closing remark from the officiant, that honors the established narrative.

## Facilitation Notes
- Coaching Cue: Treat every detail in the eulogy as a gift! If they say you were a terrible unicyclist, show us exactly how terrible you were.
- Pitfall: Players sometimes contradict previous eulogies. Fix: Remind players to yes-and the history. If someone says the deceased was a saint, and the next says they were a thief, find a way to make both true.
- Coaching Cue: Keep flashbacks brief and active. Get in, show the moment described, and get out.
- Pitfall: The deceased player stays frozen during flashbacks. Fix: Encourage the deceased to actively jump out of the casket to play their younger, living self, then immediately return to the casket when the flashback ends.

## Variations
- The Ghostly Observer: The deceased player can speak as a ghost, offering commentary or correcting the mourners' memories between eulogies.
- The Living Will: Introduce a lawyer character who reads a bizarre will, prompting flashbacks based on the inheritances and conditions left behind.
- Chronological Order: Force the eulogies to go in reverse chronological order, starting from the day before death and ending in early childhood.

## Debrief
- How did we ensure that each flashback felt connected to the overall life story rather than just a series of random sketches?
- What was the effect of treating the eulogist's monologue as absolute truth? How did that make your partner look like a genius?
- How did the physical transition between the solemn funeral and the high-energy flashbacks affect the pacing of the piece?

## Safety & Inclusion
Since the theme is a funeral, check in with players beforehand to ensure everyone is comfortable exploring themes of death and grief. Keep the tone light, theatrical, or absurd rather than deeply tragic, unless the group explicitly agrees to explore dramatic territory. Ensure the player lying on the table or chairs is physically comfortable and safe.

## Why It Works
This game relies on a clear narrative engine where monologues serve as direct prompts for scenic action. By alternating between narration (the eulogy) and representation (the flashback), players practice both macro-level storytelling and micro-level scene work. It forces players to listen intently to build a shared history, embodying the principle of serving the story.
