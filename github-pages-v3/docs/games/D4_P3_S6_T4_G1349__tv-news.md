# Broadcast News Desk

![Broadcast News Desk — infographic](img/D4_P3_S6_T4_G1349__tv-news.jpg){ .game-hero }

> Co-create a seamless, fast-paced television news broadcast using structured hand-offs and active gifting.

## Overview
Three players collaborate to perform a complete, multi-segment television news broadcast. One player acts as the studio anchor, another as the on-location field reporter, and the third as a specialist reporter (weather or sports). The team must navigate transitions, callbacks, and shared narrative threads to make the broadcast feel like a cohesive, single piece of media.

## What It Trains
- **Domain:** D4 — The Ensemble
- **Principle(s):** Serve the Piece; Make Your Partner a Genius; Serve the Story
- **Skill(s):** Format Literacy; Pacing & Rhythm; Active Gifting; World-Building
- **Technique(s):** Longform vs. shortform mechanics; Endowment-gifting drills; C.R.O.W. (Character, Relationship, Objective, Where)
- **Focus:** comedy_game

**Objective:** Develops format literacy and pacing by teaching players how to use structural hand-offs and thematic callbacks to serve the overall piece rather than just their individual segments.

## Setup
Three players stand or sit in a line facing the audience. Player A (Anchor) is in the center, Player B (Field Reporter) is on one side, and Player C (Specialist) is on the other. No physical props are required.

## How to Play
1. Assign roles to the three players: Anchor (studio host), Field Reporter (on-location), and Specialist (weather, sports, or entertainment).
2. Obtain a single, mundane suggestion from the audience to serve as the day's breaking news headline.
3. The Anchor begins the scene, establishing the news channel's identity, introducing the breaking news headline, and setting a dramatic or comedic tone.
4. The Anchor seamlessly tosses the broadcast to the Field Reporter, establishing a specific, challenging, or absurd location for them.
5. The Field Reporter accepts the location, describes their immediate environment using vivid world-building, and reports on the chaos before tossing back to the Anchor.
6. The Anchor transitions the broadcast to the Specialist, gifting them a bizarre weather pattern or sports event related to the main headline.
7. The Specialist delivers their report, incorporating physical object work (like a green screen map) and building on the established narrative.
8. The Specialist tosses back to the Anchor, who delivers a final, unifying sign-off that wraps up all three segments into a cohesive conclusion.

## Facilitation Notes
- Coaching cue: 'Listen for the thread! How does the weather report connect to the field reporter's crisis?'
- Pitfall: Players treating their segments as isolated stand-up routines. Fix: Remind them to actively reference and build upon details established by the previous speakers.
- Coaching cue: 'Make your hand-offs clean and high-stakes. Give your partner a specific gift as you throw to them.'
- Pitfall: Long, rambling transitions. Fix: Encourage crisp, professional broadcast jargon ('Reporting live from...', 'Back to you, Dan') to maintain a snappy rhythm.

## Variations
- Technical Difficulties: The facilitator can call out 'Audio delay!' or 'Static!' forcing the players to adjust their physical pacing and mimic satellite lag.
- Split Screen: The Anchor and Field Reporter must maintain eye contact with the 'camera' (audience) while talking to each other, simulating a split-screen broadcast.
- Breaking News Interruptions: The Anchor can interrupt any segment with an urgent, unrelated update that the active reporter must immediately integrate into their current report.

## Debrief
- How did referencing details from previous segments help make the broadcast feel like a single, unified piece?
- What techniques did you use to make your partner look like a professional broadcaster during a hand-off?
- How does understanding the 'format' of a news broadcast help you make quicker choices on stage?

## Safety & Inclusion
Ensure players are mindful of physical comfort if standing for long periods; they can easily perform this seated. Keep the 'breaking news' lighthearted and fictional, avoiding real-world tragedies or sensitive current events.

## Why It Works
This game works because the rigid, highly recognizable structure of a news broadcast provides a safe container for risk-taking. By forcing players to use formal hand-offs, it naturally trains active listening and gifting. Players cannot plan their segments in advance because they must react to the specific prompts and details established by their teammates in real-time, reinforcing the principle of serving the collective piece.
