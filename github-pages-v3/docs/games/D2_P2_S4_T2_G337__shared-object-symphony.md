# Virtual Object Metamorphosis

<div class="game-hero-ph" markdown>🎨 *Infographic coming soon*</div>

> Pass, transform, and elevate imaginary items across screens using precise physical and digital cues.

## Overview
A dynamic virtual warm-up where players collectively shape, pass, and transform an invisible object through their screens. By combining physical mime with strategic video platform features, participants build a shared physical reality across distances. The game emphasizes immediate acceptance of physical offers and creative adaptation in a remote environment.

## What It Trains
- **Domain:** D2 — The Partner
- **Principle(s):** Commit 100%; Yes, And; Group Mind
- **Skill(s):** Physicality & Space Work; Active Listening; Offer Reception; Active Gifting; Peripheral Awareness
- **Technique(s):** Object work; Endowment-acceptance; Endowment-gifting drills; Thread-tracking drills
- **Focus:** skill_drill

**Objective:** To develop rapid offer reception and endowment-acceptance in a virtual space, training players to physically commit to an imaginary object's properties before transforming it into a new offer.

## Setup
Conducted on a video conferencing platform with all participants in Gallery View. Players should position their cameras to show their upper torso and hands, ensuring ample space for physical movement. No physical props are required.

## How to Play
1. Instruct all participants to switch to Gallery View and position themselves so their hands and upper bodies are clearly visible on camera.
2. The facilitator designates a starting player to initiate the game by miming the use of a common, everyday object.
3. The active player must clearly establish the object's physical properties—such as weight, size, temperature, and texture—through committed physical space work.
4. To pass the object, the active player makes direct eye contact with their camera, calls out the recipient's name, and performs an exaggerated physical passing motion toward their screen's edge.
5. Before physically catching the object, the designated recipient must immediately type a confirmation in the chat (e.g., 'Ready to catch!') to synchronize the handoff and bypass audio lag.
6. The facilitator dynamically spotlights both the giver and the receiver during the pass to focus the group's attention on the exchange.
7. The receiver physically catches the object, briefly mirroring its original weight and shape to validate the giver's offer.
8. The receiver then immediately transforms the object into a new item and mimes using it before passing it to a new player.
9. Continue the cycle of passing, chat-confirming, and transforming until every participant has received and modified the object at least twice.

## Facilitation Notes
- Coaching Cue: 'Feel the weight!' Remind players to show, not just tell, by engaging their muscles to convey the object's physical reality.
- Pitfall: Players talking over each other during the pass. Fix: Enforce the 'chat-first' rule strictly; the receiver must type their readiness before speaking or catching.
- Coaching Cue: 'Yes, and the physical properties!' Ensure the receiver fully accepts the size and weight of the incoming object before morphing it into something else.
- Pitfall: The facilitator struggles to spotlight quickly. Fix: Have the facilitator pin the active players locally, or instruct the group to simply rely on Gallery View if live spotlighting causes technical lag.

## Variations
- Thematic Progression: Restrict all transformations to a specific category, such as tools found in a mad scientist's lab or items from a medieval castle.
- Emotional Endowment: When passing the object, the giver also passes an emotional state which the receiver must adopt while using the new object.
- Speed Run: Once the group is comfortable with the mechanics, challenge them to complete a full round of passes in under two minutes, prioritizing rapid physical commitment.

## Debrief
- How did the chat confirmation help overcome the natural lag and disconnect of virtual communication?
- What physical cues made it easiest to understand what object was being passed to you?
- How did it feel to immediately accept someone else's physical offer and transform it? Did you find yourself planning ahead or reacting in the moment?

## Safety & Inclusion
Ensure participants who may have physical limitations or camera constraints can adapt their mime work to smaller, facial, or verbal gestures. If typing in the chat is a barrier, establish a clear visual hand signal as the anticipatory receive cue instead.

## Why It Works
This game succeeds by translating the physical 'Yes, And' of stage work into the digital realm. The requirement to physically receive and mirror the object's properties before transforming it forces players to practice endowment-acceptance. By utilizing the chat tool to mitigate audio latency, it removes technical friction, allowing players to focus entirely on active listening and peripheral awareness.
