# The Obvious Entrance

![The Obvious Entrance — infographic](img/D1_P4_S1_T2_G651__casino-game.jpg){ .game-hero }

> Banish hesitation by launching into scenes with the most immediate, obvious choice possible.

## Overview
A high-energy drill where players leap onto the stage the split-second a location suggestion is uttered. By instantly committing to the most obvious characters and activities associated with that space, players bypass the urge to plan or overthink. The experience is fast-paced, physical, and liberating, proving that you do not need a complex plan to start a successful scene.

## What It Trains
- **Domain:** D1 — The Self
- **Principle(s):** The First Thought Is a Gift; Base Reality First; Start in the Middle
- **Skill(s):** Unfiltered Spontaneity; World-Building
- **Technique(s):** First Thought drills; C.R.O.W. (Character, Relationship, Objective, Where)
- **Focus:** skill_drill

**Objective:** To develop unfiltered spontaneity and rapid world-building by training players to trust their very first instinct as a valuable gift, establishing a clear Who, What, and Where without hesitation.

## Setup
An active performance space with a designated stage area and an off-stage wing area. The group stands in the wings or off-stage, ready to enter. One player acts as the Facilitator or Host standing center stage.

## How to Play
1. Position the group off-stage in the wings, with one person acting as the Host in the center of the playing space.
2. The Host asks the remaining players for a simple location suggestion, such as a hospital, airport, or beach.
3. The moment the suggestion is spoken, two players from the wings must immediately charge onto the stage, aiming to cross paths with the Host as they exit.
4. Upon entering, both players must instantly physicalize the most obvious, cliché, or immediate activity associated with that location.
5. The players immediately speak their first lines, establishing their relationship and the situation right in the middle of the action.
6. Play the scene for only thirty to forty-five seconds—just long enough to establish a solid, obvious base reality—before calling 'Scene!' and resetting with a new Host and new players.

## Facilitation Notes
- Side-coaching cue: 'Don't think, just move! Let your body decide your character before your brain does.'
- Common Pitfall: Players hesitate in the wings waiting for a 'good' idea. Fix: Force them to run onto the stage physically before they even know what they will do, letting the physical environment dictate the choice.
- Side-coaching cue: 'Embrace the cliché! The most obvious choice is the most generous choice for your partner.'
- Common Pitfall: Trying to be clever or subverting the suggestion immediately. Fix: Side-coach them back to the absolute baseline of the location: 'Be a doctor! Be a patient!'

## Variations
- Solo Initiation: A single player runs out on the suggestion and establishes the physical environment solo, with a second player entering a few seconds later to adapt to the established physical work.
- Silent Launch: The first fifteen seconds of the scene must be entirely silent physical action that clearly communicates the location before any dialogue is spoken.
- Three-Player Rush: Three players must enter simultaneously and instantly establish a three-way hierarchy or clear group dynamic based on the location.

## Debrief
- How did it feel to step onto the stage without a pre-planned plot or clever twist?
- Why does choosing the most 'obvious' thing actually make the scene easier for your partner?
- What happened to your physical presence when you had to move before you could think?

## Safety & Inclusion
Ensure the physical pathway from the wings to the stage is completely clear of obstacles. Encourage players to move with high energy but maintain physical control to avoid accidental collisions during rapid entrances.

## Why It Works
This game bypasses the analytical mind by demanding immediate physical action. When players move before they can plan, they rely on their subconscious store of archetypes and environments. Committing to the obvious immediately establishes a shared base reality, giving both players a firm foundation to build upon without the friction of negotiation.
