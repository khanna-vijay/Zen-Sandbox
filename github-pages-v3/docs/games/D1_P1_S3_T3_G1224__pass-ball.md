# The Imaginary Sphere

<div class="game-hero-ph" markdown>🎨 *Infographic coming soon*</div>

> Pass an invisible ball that constantly shifts in weight, size, and physical resistance.

## Overview
Players stand in a circle and pass an imaginary ball to one another. As the facilitator calls out different physical properties—such as extreme weight, zero gravity, or sticky textures—players must instantly adapt their physical commitment to make the object's reality undeniable. It is a high-focus warm-up that builds physical awareness and collaborative play.

## What It Trains
- **Domain:** D1 — The Self
- **Principle(s):** Commit 100%; Make Your Partner a Genius; Show, Don't Tell; Group Mind
- **Skill(s):** Physicality & Space Work; Active Listening; Offer Reception; Peripheral Awareness

**Objective:** To develop physical commitment and spatial awareness by using weight, resistance, and muscle tension to make an invisible object look and feel real to the group.

## Setup
Have all players stand in a circle with enough space to move their arms and shift their weight comfortably. No physical props are needed.

## How to Play
1. Form a standing circle with all participants facing inward.
2. The facilitator initiates the game by miming holding a standard, medium-sized ball and passing it to a specific player in the circle.
3. The receiving player must watch the ball's trajectory, catch it with appropriate physical impact, and then pass it to someone else.
4. Once the basic passing rhythm is established, the facilitator calls out a new physical attribute, such as the ball weighing a hundred pounds.
5. The player currently holding the ball must immediately adjust their muscle tension, posture, and effort to reflect this new weight before passing it.
6. The receiver must match that exact weight and resistance upon catching it, maintaining the physical reality of the object.
7. Continue calling out different attributes every few passes, such as extremely hot, sticky like honey, microscopic and feather-light, or giant and floating.
8. Keep the momentum going, ensuring every player gets to receive and pass the object under different physical constraints.

## Facilitation Notes
- Side-coaching cue: Show us the weight in your knees and spine, not just your hands!
- Pitfall: Players dropping their physical commitment when they aren't holding the ball. Fix: Remind everyone to use active listening and peripheral awareness to track the ball's location and state at all times.
- Side-coaching cue: Don't just name the change—show it through resistance and muscle tension before you let go.
- Pitfall: Rushing the pass without establishing the catch. Fix: Coach players to fully receive the offer (the catch) before they make their own offer (the pass).

## Variations
- Sound and Motion: Add a distinct vocal sound to each pass that matches the physical quality of the object.
- Shape Shifter: Instead of the facilitator changing the properties, the player passing the ball silently changes its shape or weight, and the receiver must discover what it is through physical touch.
- Multi-Ball: Introduce a second, contrasting imaginary ball into the circle at the same time to test peripheral awareness.

## Debrief
- How did changing your muscle tension affect how real the object felt to you and the group?
- What did you have to look for in your partner's body to successfully catch the object they threw?
- How does committing 100% to a physical reality make it easier for your scene partners to support you?

## Safety & Inclusion
Ensure players are mindful of physical limitations; encourage them to scale the physical exertion (like lifting a heavy object) to a level that is safe and comfortable for their own bodies without straining.

## Why It Works
By engaging the entire body in weight and resistance mime, players bypass intellectual overthinking and enter a state of high physical commitment. The shared physical reality relies on mutual agreement (Yes-Anding the physical offer), which instantly builds group mind and trust.
