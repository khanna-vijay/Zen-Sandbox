# Coming Home

<div class="game-hero-ph" markdown>🎨 *Infographic coming soon*</div>

> Step into a familiar person's shoes to explore physical world-building and emotional commitment.

## Overview
A solo physical exploration exercise where players embody a real-life acquaintance returning home after a long day. By focusing on specific object work, environmental details, and physicalized emotions, players build a rich, silent narrative of a character's life and state of mind.

## What It Trains
- **Domain:** D1 — The Self
- **Principle(s):** Commit 100%; Show, Don't Tell
- **Skill(s):** Emotional Fluidity; Physicality & Space Work; World-Building
- **Technique(s):** Character Walks/Centers; Object work; C.R.O.W. (Character, Relationship, Objective, Where)
- **Focus:** skill_drill

**Objective:** To develop deep physical commitment and precise object work by using sensory details and body language to show, rather than tell, a character's internal state and environment.

## Setup
An open, unobstructed studio space where multiple players can work simultaneously. No physical props are required, but players should have enough room to imagine a front door, furniture, and various rooms.

## How to Play
1. Instruct all players to find their own personal space in the room, facing away from each other to minimize self-consciousness.
2. Ask each player to silently select a real person they know well in real life, such as a parent, a roommate, a former boss, or a close friend.
3. Have players close their eyes and briefly visualize this person's typical physical posture, gait, and energy level when they are tired or stressed.
4. Direct players to establish a mental layout of this person's home entrance, including the weight of the front door, the location of light switches, and where they drop their keys or bags.
5. Begin the active phase: players physically enact the moment this person crosses their own threshold after a long, demanding day at work.
6. Guide players to interact with at least three specific imaginary objects in the home with realistic weight, resistance, and placement.
7. Encourage players to let the character's emotional state dictate their physical speed and tension, showing whether the day was a triumph, a disaster, or exhausting.
8. Call a gentle freeze or fade out once players have settled into their character's primary evening routine, such as sitting on the couch or starting dinner.

## Facilitation Notes
- Side-coaching cue: 'Feel the weight of the keys in your hand. Don't just drop them; hear them hit the counter.'
- Side-coaching cue: 'Let your face and shoulders tell us how your day went before you even open the door.'
- Pitfall: Players rushing through the actions. Fix: Slow them down by calling out specific sensory details, like the temperature of the doorknob or the stiffness of their shoes.
- Pitfall: Miming generic actions instead of specific ones. Fix: Remind them of the real person they chose; ask them to recall one unique habit that person actually has.

## Variations
- The Unexpected Guest: Halfway through the arrival, the player discovers an unexpected guest is already sitting in their living room, forcing an instant shift in status and emotion.
- The Opposite Day: Play the same character coming home after the best day of their life, contrasting the physical choices and speed of movement.
- Spoken Subtext: The facilitator taps a player on the shoulder during their routine, and the player must speak one line of internal monologue out loud before continuing silently.

## Debrief
- How did focusing on specific, heavy physical objects help you connect to the character's emotional state?
- What did you discover about your character's day simply by how they opened the door or took off their shoes?
- How does 'showing' an environment through object work feel different than describing it out loud?

## Safety & Inclusion
Ensure players choose a person they feel comfortable embodying, avoiding traumatic or highly stressful personal relationships. Emphasize that physical movements should be adapted to each player's mobility level; a heavy step or exhausted slump can be expressed through small, controlled gestures or facial expressions just as effectively as large physical drops.

## Why It Works
By anchoring the performance in a real person and a familiar physical routine, players bypass the intellectual 'inventing' brain and tap directly into physical memory. Precise object work forces a slow, deliberate pace, which naturally builds a believable environment and reveals deep, unforced character emotions.
