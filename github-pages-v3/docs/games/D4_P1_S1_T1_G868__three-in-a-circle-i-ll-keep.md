# The Living Collage

![The Living Collage — infographic](img/D4_P1_S1_T1_G868__three-in-a-circle-i-ll-keep.jpg){ .game-hero }

> Build three-part stage pictures, keeping one element to inspire the next collaborative scene.

## Overview
Players work together to construct a three-dimensional physical tableau, one element at a time. Once a complete three-part picture is formed, the initiating player selects one element to remain, clearing the rest to spark an entirely new visual environment.

## What It Trains
- **Domain:** D4 — The Ensemble
- **Principle(s):** Group Mind; Yes, And; Base Reality First
- **Skill(s):** Peripheral Awareness; Support Work; Offer Reception; World-Building
- **Technique(s):** Stage-picture exercises; Endowment-acceptance; C.R.O.W. (Character, Relationship, Objective, Where)
- **Focus:** connection

**Objective:** Develops peripheral awareness, physical offer reception, and group mind by training players to visually support an existing stage picture and establish a clear base reality.

## Setup
Players stand in a large circle with an open space in the center. No props or materials are required.

## How to Play
1. Begin with all players standing in a wide circle, leaving the center clear.
2. One player steps into the center, strikes a distinct physical pose, and declares what they are (e.g., 'I am a grandfather clock').
3. A second player quickly enters the space, assumes a complementary pose that connects to the first, and declares their identity (e.g., 'I am the swinging pendulum').
4. A third player enters to complete the picture, posing and declaring a third related element (e.g., 'I am a dusty spiderweb in the corner of the clock').
5. Once all three players are frozen in their tableau, the first player (who initiated the round) surveys the scene and announces which of the other two elements they want to keep (e.g., 'I'll keep the spiderweb').
6. The first player and the unselected player return to the outer circle.
7. The kept player remains in the center, resets their pose, and restates their identity to initiate the next round.
8. Two new players from the circle step in one by one to build a brand-new three-part picture around the kept element.

## Facilitation Notes
- Encourage physical commitment: Players should use their entire bodies to create levels (high, medium, low) rather than just standing and pointing.
- Side-coach speed and instinct: Remind players to jump in without overthinking. The goal is immediate, obvious support rather than clever or convoluted connections.
- Pitfall: Players choosing to keep their own element. Fix: Clarify that the initiator must always choose one of the other two players to keep, ensuring the initiator always gets to step out and rest.
- Pitfall: Disconnected offers. Fix: Remind players to look at the physical stage picture before entering and place themselves in a way that balances the space visually.

## Variations
- Silent Tableau: Play the entire game without verbal declarations, relying purely on physical shapes and spatial relationships to communicate what the objects are.
- Emotional Landscapes: Instead of physical objects, players must represent abstract emotions or atmospheric elements (e.g., 'I am anxiety', 'I am a sudden chill').
- Expanding Collage: Increase the number of players in the center to four or five before the initiator selects one to keep, challenging the group's spatial composition skills.

## Debrief
- How did you decide where to place your body in relation to the players already in the center?
- What did it feel like to have your offer 'kept' and become the foundation for a completely different environment?
- How does making an obvious, simple choice help the next player who enters the space?

## Safety & Inclusion
Ensure players are mindful of physical boundaries and consent when posing near or touching others. Offer modifications for players with limited mobility, allowing them to describe their physical relationship to the picture or use comfortable, seated poses.

## Why It Works
This game builds group mind by stripping away complex narrative and focusing on immediate, physical agreement. By keeping one element and discarding the rest, players practice letting go of their own ideas while discovering how a single offer can be recontextualized in infinite ways, reinforcing the 'Yes, And' philosophy.
