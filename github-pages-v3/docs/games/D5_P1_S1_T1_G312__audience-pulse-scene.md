# Audience Pulse

> Calibrate your scene's energy in real time by treating virtual audience reactions as active offers.

## Overview
A virtual scene-work game where active players perform a scene while the rest of the group acts as a live, reactive audience using video platform emojis. The performers must continuously read the room, calibrating their characters' emotions, status, and choices to match the shifting tide of visual feedback. It transforms passive virtual viewers into an active, collective scene partner.

## What It Trains
- **Domain:** D5 — The Audience
- **Principle(s):** The Audience Is the Final Scene Partner; Yes, And
- **Skill(s):** Room Reading; Audience-Energy Management; Active Listening; Offer Reception
- **Technique(s):** Energy-calibration; Breaking the 4th Wall / Direct Address
- **Focus:** mixed

**Objective:** To develop real-time room reading and energy-calibration skills. Players learn to treat audience reactions not as distractions, but as immediate, actionable offers that dictate the emotional direction of the scene.

## Setup
Conducted on a virtual video platform with gallery view enabled. Active players (2-3) keep their cameras on, while the remaining participants (the audience) keep their cameras on or off but must have their reaction menu ready. No physical props or space are required.

## How to Play
1. Assign 2 to 3 players to be the active performers, and designate everyone else as the active audience.
2. Establish a clear emotional key for the platform's emoji reactions (e.g., Thumbs Up = Agreement/Approval, Heart = Affection/Vulnerability, Laugh = Absurdity/Humor, Surprised = Shock/Plot Twist, Thumbs Down = Disapproval/Conflict).
3. Instruct the audience that their sole job is to react honestly and continuously with emojis based on what the performers say or do.
4. Give the active performers a simple, open-ended relationship prompt (e.g., two business partners launching a bizarre product).
5. Begin the scene. Performers must keep their eyes on the gallery view or reaction overlays to monitor the incoming stream of emojis.
6. Performers must immediately 'Yes, And' the audience's reactions by integrating them into the narrative (e.g., if a wave of Hearts appears, the characters must lean into a tender moment; if Thumbs Down flood the screen, they must immediately pivot or defend their unpopular choice).
7. The facilitator acts as a conductor, occasionally calling out missed trends (e.g., 'The room is shocked, players!') to help the performers calibrate.
8. End the scene after 2 to 3 minutes, rotate performers, and assign a new prompt.

## Facilitation Notes
- Side-coaching cue: 'Read the room! What is the screen telling you right now?'
- Pitfall: Performers get so caught up in their pre-planned narrative that they ignore the emojis. Fix: Pause the scene briefly and ask the performers to describe the dominant emoji on screen, then force them to justify it.
- Pitfall: The audience stops reacting or reacts randomly. Fix: Prompt the audience directly: 'Audience, how do we feel about this betrayal? Show them!'
- Encourage performers to verbally acknowledge the audience's energy (e.g., 'I can feel the tension in the air right now' or 'Clearly, everyone thinks this is a bad idea').

## Variations
- High-Status Hijack: One specific emoji (e.g., Clapping) forces a performer to immediately raise their character's status, while another (e.g., Thumbs Down) forces them to drop it.
- The Silent Ripple: Performers are not allowed to speak; they must conduct a purely physical/silent scene, adjusting their movements and expressions solely based on the audience's emoji feedback.
- Conflicting Currents: Divide the audience into two halves; one half can only use positive emojis, the other only negative. Performers must navigate the polarized room.

## Debrief
- How did it feel to have the audience's immediate, silent feedback guiding your choices?
- What strategies did you use to monitor the screen while staying present in the scene?
- How did calibrating your energy to the audience's reactions change the trajectory of the story?

## Safety & Inclusion
Ensure all participants are comfortable with rapid visual stimuli. If any player has visual processing or attention challenges, the facilitator can verbally narrate the dominant emoji trends to keep them included without overwhelming them.

## Why It Works
This game works because it gamifies the feedback loop between performer and audience. In a virtual space, the lack of physical presence often isolates actors. By translating silent emoji reactions into active narrative offers, it forces players to practice energy-calibration and room reading, proving that the audience is indeed the final scene partner.
