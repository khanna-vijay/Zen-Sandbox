# Bumpity Bump

<div class="game-hero-ph" markdown>🎨 *Infographic coming soon*</div>

> A high-energy circle game of rapid name-recall, peripheral focus, and split-second spontaneity.

## Overview
Players stand in a circle, learning the names of their immediate neighbors. A player in the center challenges individuals by pointing and demanding the name of their left or right neighbor before completing a fast-paced rhythmic phrase. It is a joyful, high-energy warm-up that demands active presence and rapid-fire cognitive processing.

## What It Trains
- **Domain:** D4 — The Ensemble
- **Principle(s):** Group Mind; The First Thought Is a Gift
- **Skill(s):** Peripheral Awareness; Unfiltered Spontaneity
- **Technique(s):** First Thought drills
- **Focus:** connection

**Objective:** To develop peripheral awareness and unfiltered spontaneity by forcing players to maintain connection with their immediate physical surroundings while under light, playful pressure.

## Setup
Have all players stand in a shoulder-to-shoulder circle. Give them 30 seconds to introduce themselves to the players directly to their left and right, ensuring everyone knows their immediate neighbors' names. One player volunteer starts in the center of the circle.

## How to Play
1. Form a standing circle with one player volunteering to start in the center.
2. Allow 30 seconds for all players to turn to their left and right, exchange names, and commit them to memory.
3. The center player approaches any player in the circle, points directly at them, and says either 'Left, bumpity-bump-bump-bump!' or 'Right, bumpity-bump-bump-bump!' at a rapid, steady pace.
4. The targeted player must correctly call out the name of the neighbor on the specified side (left or right) before the center player finishes saying the word 'bump'.
5. If the targeted player successfully names their neighbor in time, the center player must immediately turn and target a different player in the circle.
6. If the targeted player hesitates, says the wrong name, or fails to finish before the phrase ends, they swap places with the center player and become the new challenger.
7. At any point, the center player can call out 'Scramble!', prompting everyone in the circle to run and find a completely new position, after which they have 10 seconds to learn their new neighbors' names before play resumes.

## Facilitation Notes
- Encourage the center player to maintain a fast, rhythmic cadence when saying the phrase to keep the pressure playful and exciting.
- Pitfall: Players in the circle zoning out when the center player is on the opposite side. Fix: Remind players to keep their peripheral vision active, as a scramble can happen at any moment.
- Side-coaching cue: 'Trust your first instinct! Don't overthink the name, just let it fly!'
- Ensure the center player actually points clearly so there is no confusion about who is being targeted.

## Variations
- Double Trouble: The center player can point to someone and say 'Me!', requiring the target to name the center player, or 'You!', requiring the target to say their own name before the phrase ends.
- The Wave: Instead of a single center player, the challenge is passed around the circle sequentially, with each player challenging their neighbor to keep a continuous rhythm going.

## Debrief
- How did the physical pressure of the countdown affect your ability to recall a simple name?
- What strategies did you use to keep track of your changing environment during the scrambles?
- How does this game illustrate the concept of 'the first thought is a gift' when under pressure?

## Safety & Inclusion
Ensure the playing space is clear of tripping hazards before calling a scramble. For players with mobility constraints, the scramble can be modified to a gentle walk, or neighbors can physically swap places with adjacent players rather than running across the circle.

## Why It Works
This game works because it bypasses the analytical brain through rhythmic urgency. By pairing a simple cognitive task with a physical countdown, players must rely on immediate, unfiltered spontaneity. The constant threat of a scramble builds group mind by forcing players to remain highly attuned to their changing physical ensemble.
