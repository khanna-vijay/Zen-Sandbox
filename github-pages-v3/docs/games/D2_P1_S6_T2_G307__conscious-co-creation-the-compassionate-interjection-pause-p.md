# Pause and Pivot

<div class="game-hero-ph" markdown>🎨 *Infographic coming soon*</div>

> Transform boundary-setting into a collaborative, creative redirection that keeps the scene safely moving forward.

## Overview
An active scene-work exercise where players use a specialized verbal cue to instantly freeze play when a personal or narrative boundary is reached. Instead of ending the scene, the players acknowledge the boundary and collaboratively negotiate a creative redirection, resuming play immediately. This shifts the concept of boundary-setting from a disruptive halt to an act of mutual support and conscious co-creation.

## What It Trains
- **Domain:** D2 — The Partner
- **Principle(s):** Consent & Boundaries; Yes, And; Truth Over Pandering

- **Focus:** connection

**Objective:** To develop real-time boundary navigation and active consent practices within active scene work, teaching players to prioritize partner safety over narrative momentum while maintaining collaborative flow.

## Setup
An open playing space with room for two to three players to perform, while the remaining participants observe as an active audience. No props or materials are required.

## How to Play
1. Begin a standard two-player scene based on a simple, low-stakes suggestion or premise, focusing on active listening and establishing a shared reality.
2. At any point during the scene, if a player experiences physical, emotional, or thematic discomfort, or if an offer crosses a personal boundary, they immediately call out 'Pause and Pivot!' in a clear, audible voice.
3. Upon hearing the cue, all players in the scene must instantly freeze in their current physical positions and stop speaking.
4. The player who called the pause states their boundary clearly, concisely, and neutrally, focusing on what needs to change without needing to offer a deep personal justification.
5. The other players in the scene must immediately offer a brief, supportive verbal acknowledgment to validate the boundary, such as 'Heard, thank you' or 'Got it, let's shift'.
6. The players collaboratively negotiate a quick adjustment to the scene's trajectory—such as a slight dialogue shift, a physical adjustment, or a brief jump forward in time—to bypass the boundary while keeping the narrative alive.
7. Once the new direction is agreed upon and the caller confirms they are comfortable, the players release the freeze and resume the scene from the newly established pivot point.

## Facilitation Notes
- Celebrate the call: Actively praise players who call 'Pause and Pivot' to reinforce that setting boundaries is a positive, supportive contribution to the group's trust, not a failure.
- Enforce the 'No Interrogation' rule: Ensure that no one asks the caller to explain why they have the boundary. The boundary must be accepted as a factual parameter of the scene immediately.
- Side-coach the pivot: If players get stuck trying to find a solution, suggest a 'jump cut' (e.g., 'Let's jump to the next morning') to easily bypass the uncomfortable element.
- Watch for silent discomfort: If you notice a player tensing up or looking genuinely uncomfortable but hesitating to call the cue, gently pause the scene yourself and ask, 'Would you like to call a Pause and Pivot here?'
- Keep it brief: The entire pause, acknowledgment, and negotiation process should take less than thirty seconds to keep the energy active and prevent over-intellectualizing.

## Variations
- The Silent Pivot: For advanced players, practice making the pivot non-verbally by reading physical hesitation cues and adjusting the offer immediately without calling the verbal cue, though the verbal option always remains available.
- Audience-Assisted Pivot: If the players on stage are unsure how to pivot, they can look to the observing group, who can quickly pitch a supportive, boundary-respecting alternative to keep the scene moving.

## Debrief
- How did it feel to verbally assert a boundary in the middle of an active scene, and what helped make that moment feel safe?
- For those receiving the call, how did you manage the impulse to justify your original offer and instead focus on supporting your partner?
- In what ways did honoring a boundary actually open up unexpected, creative directions for the story?
- How does this practice change your understanding of the traditional 'Yes, And' rule when it comes to personal comfort?

## Safety & Inclusion
This exercise is highly sensitive and requires a pre-established container of trust. Ensure all participants understand that they have absolute autonomy over their bodies and emotional limits. If a player is unable or prefers not to speak the cue, they may use a clear physical gesture (such as raising a flat hand) to trigger the pause.

## Why It Works
By replacing the traditional, abrupt 'Cut' call with a structured 'Pause and Pivot,' the game reframes boundary-setting as a generative, collaborative act rather than a scene-ending mistake. It teaches players that saying 'no' to a specific action is actually a 'yes' to a safer, more creative partnership, building deep psychological safety and trust.
