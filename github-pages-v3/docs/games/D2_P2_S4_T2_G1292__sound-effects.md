# Foley Artists

<div class="game-hero-ph" markdown>🎨 *Infographic coming soon*</div>

> Bring physical actions to life with live, off-stage sound effects that drive the narrative.

## Overview
In this game, two actors perform a scene while two off-stage players provide all the environmental and physical sound effects. The actors must justify and react to every sound made, while the sound makers must closely watch the actors' physical movements to sync their audio.

## What It Trains
- **Domain:** D2 — The Partner
- **Principle(s):** Yes, And; Make Your Partner a Genius; Show, Don't Tell
- **Skill(s):** Active Listening; Offer Reception; Active Gifting; World-Building; Justification; Support Work
- **Technique(s):** Endowment-acceptance; Endowment-gifting drills; Justify the absurd; Playing architecture/objects
- **Focus:** comedy_game

**Objective:** Develops endowment-acceptance and active listening by forcing actors to instantly justify unexpected auditory offers and integrate them into their physical reality.

## Setup
Two actors stand in the performance space. Two sound makers stand to the side where they can clearly see the actors' movements. No physical props are used.

## How to Play
1. Divide the players into two pairs: two Scene Actors and two Foley Artists.
2. Obtain a simple suggestion of a location or activity to initiate the scene.
3. The Scene Actors begin performing using pantomime and dialogue, but they are strictly forbidden from making non-vocal sound effects themselves.
4. The Foley Artists watch the actors' physical movements intently and immediately provide vocal or physical sound effects to match the pantomimed actions.
5. Foley Artists can also introduce active gifts by making a sound first, such as a phone ringing or a dog barking, which the Scene Actors must immediately accept and justify.
6. The Scene Actors must physically and emotionally react to every sound effect, treating it as absolute truth.
7. Continue the scene for three to five minutes, building a cohesive story where sound and action are perfectly synchronized.

## Facilitation Notes
- Coaching cue: Watch their hands! Remind Foley artists to sync their sounds precisely with the physical point of contact.
- Coaching cue: Say yes to the sound! Remind actors to immediately name or react to unexpected sounds rather than ignoring them.
- Pitfall: Foley artists over-saturating the scene with constant noise. Fix: Coach them to use silence as a tool and let the dialogue breathe.
- Pitfall: Actors ignoring a sound effect because they did not plan for it. Fix: Pause the scene and ask the actor what that sound was and how their character feels about it.

## Variations
- Blind Foley: The Foley artists stand with their backs to the actors, creating random sounds that the actors must physically justify.
- Prop Foley: Provide the sound makers with actual random objects like keys, paper, or cups to create more realistic or absurd sounds.
- Reverse Foley: The sound makers make a sound first, and the actors must instantly create a physical action that matches it.

## Debrief
- How did it feel to have your physical actions instantly endowed with sound?
- For the Foley artists, how did you balance supporting the actors' actions versus offering new narrative directions?
- What strategies did you use to justify a sound that did not match what you originally intended to do?

## Safety & Inclusion
Ensure Foley artists are mindful of volume levels, especially for players with sensory sensitivities. Encourage non-vocal physical sounds like clapping or tapping if vocal strain is a concern.

## Why It Works
This game embodies the principle of Yes, And in its physical form. By separating physical action from sound, it forces both parties into a state of hyper-vigilant active listening and mutual support, creating a tight feedback loop of co-creation.
