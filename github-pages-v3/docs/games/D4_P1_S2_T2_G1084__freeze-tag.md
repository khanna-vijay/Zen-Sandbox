# Freeze Tag

<div class="game-hero-ph" markdown>🎨 *Infographic coming soon*</div>

> Transform physical shapes into brand-new scenes with a single, high-energy tap-out.

## Overview
Two players initiate a highly physical scene in the center of the space. At any moment, an offstage player shouts "Freeze!", steps in, tags one player out, assumes their exact physical posture, and initiates a completely new scene that justifies the frozen physical positions. It is a fast-paced, high-energy game of physical justification and rapid support.

## What It Trains
- **Domain:** D4 — The Ensemble
- **Principle(s):** The First Thought Is a Gift; Base Reality First; Group Mind
- **Skill(s):** Physicality & Space Work; World-Building; Justification; Support Work; Pacing & Rhythm
- **Technique(s):** Character Walks/Centers; C.R.O.W. (Character, Relationship, Objective, Where); Justify the absurd; Tap-ins; Edits (Sweep, Tag-Out, Sound/Light)
- **Focus:** mixed

**Objective:** To develop rapid physical justification, active listening, and seamless support work (tap-ins) while building a shared group mind.

## Setup
A clear stage area with the remaining players forming a line or semi-circle at the back or sides of the playing space, ready to jump in. No props or chairs needed.

## How to Play
1. Two players step into the performance space and begin an active, physical scene, focusing on clear object work and dynamic body positions.
2. The offstage players watch closely, paying attention to the physical shapes and postures of the active players.
3. At any point during the scene, an offstage player yells "Freeze!" in a loud, clear voice.
4. The active players must instantly freeze in their exact physical positions, holding their postures, facial expressions, and gestures perfectly still.
5. The player who called "Freeze" enters the playing space, tags one of the frozen players on the shoulder, and takes their exact physical posture.
6. The tagged-out player exits the stage and joins the offstage line.
7. The remaining original player and the new player must immediately initiate a brand-new scene with completely different characters and a new relationship.
8. The new scene must logically justify the physical positions they are holding (e.g., a raised hand from a high-five in the previous scene becomes a hand reaching for a high shelf in the new scene).
9. The cycle repeats with another offstage player calling "Freeze!" to start the next iteration.

## Facilitation Notes
- Side-coaching cue: "Freeze on a physical shape, not just when someone is standing still!" Encourage players to call freeze when there is dynamic movement or interesting physical postures.
- Pitfall & Fix: Players calling freeze with a pre-planned scene in mind. Fix: Remind players that the physical shape must inspire the new scene. The first line should directly address or justify the physical posture.
- Side-coaching cue: "Commit to the physical shape!" Ensure the entering player matches the exact angle, height, and tension of the person they replaced.
- Pitfall & Fix: Letting scenes run too long or cutting them too quickly. Fix: Coach the sideline to find a rhythm—allow a platform to establish, then edit before the energy dips.

## Variations
- Blind Freeze: The player calling "Freeze" stands with their back to the stage, yells "Freeze!", turns around, and must immediately enter and justify whatever random shape they see.
- Silent Freeze: No dialogue is allowed; players must transition from one physical tableau to another using only physical justification and mime.
- Emotional Freeze: The entering player must adopt the physical shape but also inherit the exact emotional state of the player they are replacing, then justify it in a new context.

## Debrief
- How did it feel to inherit a physical shape without knowing what scene you were going to do?
- What strategies helped you instantly justify a bizarre physical posture?
- How does this game train us to support our scene partners immediately upon entering?

## Safety & Inclusion
While not highly sensitive, players should use a light, respectful tap on the shoulder or back when tagging out. If physical contact is uncomfortable, players can point and say "Tag [Name]" from a safe distance. Ensure the playing space is clear of tripping hazards since players will be freezing mid-motion.

## Why It Works
It forces players out of their heads and into their bodies. By using physical shapes as the starting point (The First Thought Is a Gift), players bypass intellectual planning and rely on immediate physical justification, fostering a strong sense of Group Mind and rapid support.
