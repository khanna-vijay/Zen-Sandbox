# The Contact Compass

![The Contact Compass — infographic](img/D2_P1_S6_T3_G253__physical-yes-and.jpg){ .game-hero }

> Navigate physical contact in real-time scenes through explicit, creative boundary negotiation.

## Overview
A structured scene-work exercise where players explicitly negotiate physical touch in real-time. By transforming consent check-ins into active, creative choices, players learn to establish boundaries without stalling the narrative momentum.

## What It Trains
- **Domain:** D2 — The Partner
- **Principle(s):** Consent & Boundaries; Yes, And; Truth Over Pandering
- **Skill(s):** Boundary Navigation; Active Listening; Offer Reception; Self-Recovery
- **Technique(s):** Check-ins; Cut calls; Negotiating physical contact
- **Focus:** connection

**Objective:** To develop practical boundary navigation and physical contact negotiation skills, ensuring player safety and comfort override scene agreement while maintaining creative momentum.

## Setup
An open, clear playing space. Divide the group into pairs. One player starts as the Physical Initiator (PI), and the other starts as the Boundary Responder (BR).

## How to Play
1. Begin a standard two-person scene based on a simple relationship prompt, such as two long-lost friends meeting at a train station.
2. The Physical Initiator (PI) drives the scene verbally but must explicitly check in before making any physical contact with the Boundary Responder (BR).
3. To initiate touch, the PI must either verbally propose the action and pause (e.g., 'May I put my arm around you?') or initiate a slow, partial movement and freeze just before contact, making eye contact to ask permission non-verbally.
4. The BR must immediately respond to the proposal using one of three explicit choices: 'Yes, and...', 'Let's try this instead...', or 'Cut/No, thank you'.
5. If the BR says 'Yes, and...', they verbally accept the touch, allow the physical contact to complete, and immediately build on the moment physically or verbally.
6. If the BR says 'Let's try this instead...', they propose an alternative physical action (e.g., 'Let's do a high-five instead'). The PI must immediately accept and execute this alternative.
7. If the BR says 'Cut' or 'No, thank you' (or makes a T-sign), the PI must immediately withdraw, accept the boundary without question or justification, and instantly pivot to a non-physical choice to keep the scene moving.
8. After two to three minutes of play, the facilitator calls 'Switch!' so the partners swap roles, allowing both players to practice initiating and responding.

## Facilitation Notes
- Emphasize specificity: Ensure the PI proposes exact physical actions (e.g., 'Can I tap your shoulder?') rather than vague requests (e.g., 'Can I touch you?').
- Normalize the 'No': Actively celebrate when a BR uses 'Cut' or 'Let's try this instead.' Frame boundaries as generative offers that force creative, non-physical scene paths.
- Watch for compliance pressure: If a BR always says 'Yes, and...', gently remind the group that protecting personal boundaries is the ultimate success of this exercise, not pleasing the partner.
- Graceful pivots: Coach PIs to accept a 'Cut' instantly and with zero emotional residue, immediately finding a verbal or environmental way to continue the scene.

## Variations
- Silent Compass: Run the entire exercise in silence, relying purely on physical gestures, freezes, and head nods/shakes to negotiate contact.
- Status Shift: Assign high and low status roles, exploring how status dynamics affect a player's comfort in setting or respecting physical boundaries.

## Debrief
- For Responders: How did it feel to explicitly say 'No' or negotiate an alternative? Did you feel any internal pressure to just say 'Yes'?
- For Initiators: How did waiting for explicit consent affect your creative choices? Did a 'Cut' limit you, or did it push you to find a more interesting non-physical choice?
- For Both: How does making consent explicit actually build deeper trust and freedom between scene partners?

## Safety & Inclusion
This game is highly safety-sensitive. Establish before playing that any player has the absolute right to set a boundary for any reason, without needing to explain why. Respecting a 'Cut' is the highest priority of the exercise. Adjust physical options to accommodate all mobility levels.

## Why It Works
By gamifying the negotiation of physical touch, this exercise removes the stigma of saying 'no' in improv. It reframes boundary-setting not as a scene-blocker, but as a generative platform for creative pivots, aligning physical safety with the core principle of 'Yes, And' by accepting the reality of the players' comfort levels.
