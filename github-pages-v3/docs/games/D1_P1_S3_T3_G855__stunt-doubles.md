# Stunt Doubles

> Elevate simple physical actions into high-stakes, slow-motion physical masterpieces using designated stunt performers.

## Overview
Two actors initiate a grounded, dialogue-driven scene. At key moments of physical action, a facilitator calls for stunt doubles to step in and execute the physical task with extreme, slow-motion theatricality. The original actors then return to deal with the hilarious, high-stakes physical consequences.

## What It Trains
- **Domain:** D1 — The Self
- **Principle(s):** Commit 100%; Show, Don't Tell; Follow the Follower
- **Skill(s):** Physicality & Space Work; World-Building; Support Work
- **Technique(s):** Object work; Weight & resistance mime; Walk-ons
- **Focus:** mixed

**Objective:** To develop physical commitment, weight and resistance mime, and the ability to build a scene's reality through physical consequences rather than just verbal exposition.

## Setup
Four players stand in the playing space. Two players step forward to begin the scene, while the other two stand at the sides of the stage as the designated stunt doubles. No physical props are used; all objects must be mimed.

## How to Play
1. Assign two players as the Lead Actors and two players as the Stunt Doubles. The facilitator acts as the Director.
2. The Lead Actors begin a normal, relationship-focused scene, establishing a clear environment and a simple task.
3. When a Lead Actor initiates a physical action—even a minor one—the Director calls out 'Stunt Doubles!'
4. The Lead Actors instantly freeze or step back, maintaining their physical positions as closely as possible.
5. The Stunt Doubles rush into the exact physical positions of the Lead Actors and execute the initiated action in exaggerated, high-stakes slow-motion.
6. During the stunt, the doubles must use precise weight, resistance, and spatial mime to make the action look incredibly difficult, dangerous, or chaotic.
7. Once the physical climax of the stunt is reached, the Director calls 'Cut!' or 'Scene!' to signal the doubles to exit.
8. The Lead Actors immediately step back into the space, adopting the physical aftermath left by the stunt doubles and continuing the scene, fully justifying the new physical reality.

## Facilitation Notes
- Encourage the Lead Actors to initiate mundane tasks so the Stunt Doubles have room to escalate them into epic physical struggles.
- Side-coach the Stunt Doubles to use slow-motion movement. Slowing down the action forces them to focus on weight, tension, and precise object work.
- Pitfall: Lead Actors ignoring the stunt's outcome. Fix: Remind the Leads that they must inherit the exact physical state and mess left behind by their doubles.
- Ensure the Stunt Doubles work together, using 'Follow the Follower' to coordinate their slow-motion movements and reactions without speaking.

## Variations
- Sound Effects: Have the offstage players or the audience provide slow-motion sound effects for the stunt sequence.
- Emotional Doubles: Instead of physical stunts, the doubles step in to express the subtextual, extreme emotional reactions of the characters in slow-motion choreography.
- Tag-Team: Allow the players themselves to call 'Stunt Double!' when they want to hand off a physical challenge to their partner's double.

## Debrief
- How did slowing down the physical actions help you discover details about the environment and objects?
- Leads, how did inheriting a chaotic physical state change the direction and stakes of your scene?
- How did the Stunt Doubles use physical commitment to make everyday objects feel heavy, dangerous, or real?

## Safety & Inclusion
Since this game involves high physical energy and simulated falls or chaos, players should establish physical boundaries beforehand. Remind Stunt Doubles to perform slow-motion falls with proper control, prioritizing personal safety and avoiding actual physical contact unless explicitly agreed upon.

## Why It Works
By separating the verbal narrative from the physical action, players can commit 100% to their physical choices without the distraction of dialogue. The slow-motion mechanic forces actors to employ weight and resistance mime, making the imaginary world feel tangible. When the original actors inherit the physical consequences, it reinforces the principle of 'Show, Don't Tell' and builds deep narrative support.
