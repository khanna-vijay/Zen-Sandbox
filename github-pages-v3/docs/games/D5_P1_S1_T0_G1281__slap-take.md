# The Reaction Strike

![The Reaction Strike — infographic](img/D5_P1_S1_T0_G1281__slap-take.jpg){ .game-hero }

> Listen to the room and turn audience laughter into sudden, justified physical comedy.

## Overview
Two players perform a scene where the audience's laughter acts as a physical trigger. Every time the audience laughs, the player who did not cause the laugh must immediately deliver a safe, non-contact stage slap to the funny partner, who must then justify the physical reaction within the story.

## What It Trains
- **Domain:** D5 — The Audience
- **Principle(s):** The Audience Is the Final Scene Partner; Make Your Partner a Genius; Commit 100%
- **Skill(s):** Room Reading; Active Listening; Physicality & Space Work
- **Focus:** comedy_game

**Objective:** To develop acute room reading and active listening by treating the audience's laughter as an active, physical cue that directly impacts the scene's reality.

## Setup
An open performance space with a clear boundary between the stage and the audience. No props are required, but players must be trained in basic non-contact stage combat safety before beginning.

## How to Play
1. Two players stand on stage and obtain a simple suggestion (e.g., a location or relationship) to initiate a scene.
2. The players begin a grounded, character-driven scene, focusing on their relationship and environment.
3. The audience is instructed to react naturally and laugh whenever they find something genuinely amusing.
4. The instant the audience laughs, the player who did not cause the laugh must deliver a safe, non-contact stage slap to the player who made them laugh.
5. The player who was 'slapped' must physically react to the simulated impact, selling the physical comedy of the moment.
6. The slapped player must immediately justify why their character was just struck within the context of the scene's narrative.
7. The scene continues seamlessly, with both players remaining highly sensitive to the audience's vocal reactions for the next trigger.
8. The facilitator edits the scene after three to four successful cycles of laughter, reaction, and justification.

## Facilitation Notes
- Side-coach players to keep the scene grounded at the start; trying too hard to force laughs makes the physical reactions feel unearned and chaotic.
- Ensure the stage slap is strictly non-contact. Coach the 'knap'—the sound of the slap made by the slapper clapping their own hands safely away from the partner's face.
- Remind players to listen to the audience as their third scene partner. If they miss a laugh, gently call out 'Strike!' to prompt the reaction.
- Watch out for players anticipating the laugh and breaking character. Encourage them to stay committed to the reality of the scene until the exact moment of the laugh.

## Variations
- The Compliment Strike: Instead of a physical slap, the partner must deliver an over-the-top, poetic compliment, which the receiver must justify.
- The Freeze Strike: The player who caused the laugh must freeze in a ridiculous pose, and the partner must physically manipulate them like a statue.
- The Sound Effect: The partner must make a specific cartoon sound effect, which the laugh-maker must physically embody and justify.

## Debrief
- How did having to listen to the audience change how you delivered your lines or physicalized your character?
- What did it feel like to justify a sudden physical 'slap' within a realistic scene?
- How does treating the audience's laughter as a physical cue help us become more present in the room?

## Safety & Inclusion
Physical contact is strictly forbidden. Players must agree on a safe distance (at least arm's length plus a foot) and use classic stage combat techniques (the 'knap' sound created by the slapper clapping their own hands, while the slap motion misses the face entirely). Offer a non-physical alternative, such as a dramatic gasp or a verbal 'How dare you!', if any player is uncomfortable with simulated violence.

## Why It Works
It forces players to step out of their internal monologue and actively monitor the energy of the room. By turning the audience's laughter into a physical trigger, it cements the principle that the audience is an active participant in the scene. The requirement to justify the slap keeps the scene narrative-focused rather than just a series of cheap jokes.
