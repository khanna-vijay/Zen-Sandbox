# News Desk

![News Desk — infographic](img/D2_P3_S5_T1_G783__newsreader.jpg){ .game-hero }

> Elevate your partners by tossing them absurd, high-stakes live news reporting assignments.

## Overview
Two anchors sit at a news desk, introducing field reporters scattered around the room. By tossing to these reporters with highly specific, imaginative endowments, the anchors set their partners up for comedic success. The reporters must instantly accept these gifts, paint the scene with physical and verbal details, and throw the broadcast back to the desk.

## What It Trains
- **Domain:** D2 — The Partner
- **Principle(s):** Make Your Partner a Genius; Yes, And; Show, Don't Tell; Group Mind
- **Skill(s):** Offer Reception; Active Gifting; World-Building; Support Work; Pacing & Rhythm
- **Technique(s):** Endowment-acceptance; Endowment-gifting drills; Endowment chains; Walk-ons; Edits (Sweep, Tag-Out, Sound/Light)
- **Focus:** comedy_game

**Objective:** To practice active gifting and endowment, learning how to set up a partner with a clear, fun, and supportive premise while practicing rapid offer acceptance.

## Setup
Place two chairs at the front of the room to serve as the news desk. The remaining players stand in the space behind or around the desk, acting as field reporters waiting for their cue.

## How to Play
1. Designate two players to sit at the news desk as the anchors, while the rest of the players stand in the performance space as field reporters.
2. The two anchors begin the broadcast with a brief, high-energy news introduction, establishing their anchor personas and a general tone.
3. One anchor tosses the broadcast to a field reporter by naming them, specifying their bizarre location, and describing the strange situation they are covering.
4. The designated reporter immediately steps forward, adopts the physical reality of the endowed location, and delivers a short, high-energy report that validates and expands on the anchor's setup.
5. The reporter concludes their segment by tossing the broadcast back to the anchors with a witty sign-off.
6. The anchors react briefly to the report, validating the reporter's contribution, and then the other anchor tosses to a different reporter with a brand-new, highly specific endowment.
7. Rotate anchors and reporters after a few rounds so everyone gets a turn both gifting and receiving.

## Facilitation Notes
- Coaching cue: 'Give them a gift they can play with!' Encourage anchors to avoid generic setups like 'Let's go to John with the weather.' Instead, use: 'Let's go to John, who is currently battling a swarm of aggressive butterflies.'
- Pitfall: Anchors asking questions instead of endowing (e.g., 'What's happening over there, Sarah?'). Fix: Instruct anchors to make statements that establish the reality for the reporter, giving them a solid foundation to build upon.
- Coaching cue: 'Show us the environment!' Encourage reporters to use physical object work and sensory reactions to sell the location the anchors gave them.
- Pitfall: Reporters denying the endowment or ignoring the physical constraints. Fix: Remind reporters to 'Yes, And' the physical reality immediately—if you are in a swamp, stand like you are sinking in mud.

## Variations
- Emotional Endowments: The anchors endow not just the location, but the reporter's emotional state or a secret they are keeping (e.g., 'Let's go to Dave, who is trying very hard not to cry during this puppy parade').
- Technical Difficulties: The facilitator can call out 'audio delay' or 'frozen feed,' forcing the reporter and anchors to play with physical and timing constraints.
- Breaking News: A reporter can interrupt the anchors with an urgent, self-generated breaking news flash, reversing the flow of gifting.

## Debrief
- How did it feel to receive a highly specific endowment versus a generic one?
- What strategies did the anchors use to make their reporters look brilliant?
- How did physicalizing the environment help you generate dialogue as a reporter?

## Safety & Inclusion
Ensure that physical endowments are mindful of players' physical mobility. If a reporter is endowed with a highly physical action, they can translate this into verbal descriptions or minor physical choices rather than actual strenuous movements.

## Why It Works
This game forces players to practice 'making your partner a genius' by giving them clear, actionable setups. Instead of leaving the reporter with a blank slate, the anchor provides a rich platform of details (location, stakes, character). The reporter's job is simplified to reacting and expanding, demonstrating how active gifting reduces cognitive load and fuels comedic chemistry.
