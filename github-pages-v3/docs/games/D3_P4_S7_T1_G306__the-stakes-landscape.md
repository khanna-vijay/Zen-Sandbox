# Stakes Topography

![Stakes Topography — infographic](img/D3_P4_S7_T1_G306__the-stakes-landscape.jpg){ .game-hero }

> Navigate physical stage zones to dynamically escalate, de-escalate, and resolve narrative tension in real time.

## Overview
A physicalized narrative exercise where the stage is divided into distinct zones representing different levels of dramatic tension. As players move through the space, they must instantly adapt their characters' emotional urgency and stakes to match their physical location. This spatial mapping forces improvisers to embody narrative structure and master the art of justification.

## What It Trains
- **Domain:** D3 — The Scene
- **Principle(s):** Serve the Story; Show, Don't Tell; Yes, And
- **Skill(s):** Raising the Stakes; Narrative Architecture; Justification; Stakes / The 'Want'; Emotional Fluidity; Offer Reception
- **Technique(s):** Stakes-escalation reps; Platform/Tilt; Justify the absurd; What do they stand to lose? reps; The Emotional Dial (1→10); Endowment-acceptance
- **Focus:** narrative

**Objective:** To develop a visceral, physical understanding of narrative pacing and stakes-escalation reps, training players to dynamically raise, lower, and justify dramatic tension through spatial movement.

## Setup
Divide the playing area into five distinct, labeled zones using floor tape or physical markers: 1) Neutral Ground (low stakes/exposition), 2) Dilemma Alley (complication/obstacle), 3) Crisis Point (high stakes/urgency), 4) Consequence Junction (fallout/ripple effects), and 5) Resolution Nook (catharsis/closure). Set up a whiteboard visible to all players listing these zones and their corresponding stakes levels.

## How to Play
1. Two players enter the 'Neutral Ground' zone and begin a scene, establishing their characters, relationship, and a basic platform.
2. As the scene progresses, players may physically step into an adjacent zone. The moment a player crosses a boundary, both players must immediately shift the scene's dramatic tension to match that zone's description.
3. If a player enters 'Dilemma Alley', they must introduce a clear problem or personal obstacle; if they enter 'Crisis Point', they must escalate the situation to an urgent, high-stakes emergency requiring immediate action.
4. To help players align, any player can tap the floor twice to initiate a 'Stakes Check', asking: 'What specifically are the stakes here, now?' The partner must instantly answer in character, clarifying the current dramatic urgency.
5. After players demonstrate comfort with self-directed movement, the facilitator introduces guided shifts by calling out 'SHIFT!' and pointing to a specific zone, forcing the players to move instantly and justify the sudden transition.
6. Players must use physical body language, emotional shifts, and dialogue to organically justify why their characters are suddenly experiencing the stakes of the new zone.
7. The scene concludes once the players navigate through a complete narrative arc, ending in either 'Resolution Nook' or 'Consequence Junction', and the facilitator calls 'Scene'.

## Facilitation Notes
- Side-coaching cue: 'Don't explain the move—show the impact!' Encourage players to let their physical posture and emotional intensity shift before they even speak a line in the new zone.
- Pitfall: Players treat the zones as literal locations (e.g., thinking 'Crisis Point' is a burning building). Fix: Remind them that zones represent psychological and narrative stakes, not physical geography. A crisis can happen in a kitchen.
- If players flatline or resist escalating, use the 'SHIFT!' command to force them into 'Crisis Point' and coach them to make a bold, irreversible choice.
- Ensure the 'Stakes Check' is used as an active narrative tool, not a pause button. The response should be delivered with emotional commitment, keeping the scene's momentum alive.

## Variations
- Blind Topography: The players do not know which zones represent which stakes; the facilitator secretly assigns them, and players must discover the stakes of each zone through trial and error based on facilitator side-coaching.
- Silent Stakes: Run the entire exercise in gibberish or complete silence, forcing players to communicate the shifting levels of stakes purely through physical tension, pacing, and facial expressions.
- Multi-Character Migration: Introduce a third or fourth player who enters the landscape in a different zone, forcing the onstage players to balance multiple levels of stakes simultaneously.

## Debrief
- How did physically moving your body change your emotional access to high-stakes choices?
- What did you notice about the transition between 'Crisis Point' and 'Consequence Junction'—how does fallout differ from active panic?
- How did the 'Stakes Check' help you and your partner get back on the same page when the narrative felt muddy?
- How can we apply this spatial awareness of stakes to a standard scene without physical tape on the floor?

## Safety & Inclusion
Ensure the physical boundaries are clearly marked and free of tripping hazards. Since high-stakes zones can elicit intense emotional responses, remind players that they can always choose to de-escalate or step back to 'Neutral Ground' if the emotional intensity becomes genuinely uncomfortable.

## Why It Works
By mapping abstract narrative concepts to physical space, this game leverages embodied cognition. Moving physically forces a shift in the player's physiology, making it easier to access heightened emotional states. The requirement of instant justification prevents overthinking, training the brain to find immediate, logical connections between physical action and narrative consequence.
