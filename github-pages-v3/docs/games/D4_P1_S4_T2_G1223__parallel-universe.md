# Simultaneous Worlds

<div class="game-hero-ph" markdown>🎨 *Infographic coming soon*</div>

> Two distinct scenes share the same stage space, weaving together through rhythm and timing.

## Overview
Four players split into two pairs, each establishing a completely different scene in a unique location. Both scenes occur simultaneously in the same physical space, requiring players to share the stage dynamically without ever acknowledging the other pair's characters or environment.

## What It Trains
- **Domain:** D4 — The Ensemble
- **Principle(s):** Group Mind; Make Your Partner a Genius; Base Reality First
- **Skill(s):** Pacing & Rhythm; Peripheral Awareness; Active Listening; World-Building
- **Technique(s):** Timing exercises; Thread-tracking drills; Meisner Repetition; C.R.O.W. (Character, Relationship, Objective, Where)
- **Focus:** mixed

**Objective:** Develops group mind, peripheral awareness, and precise comedic timing by training players to share stage time, balance dialogue, and use physical space cooperatively without direct interaction.

## Setup
Four players stand on stage, divided into two pairs (Pair A and Pair B). The facilitator or audience provides two distinct, contrasting locations (e.g., a quiet library and a chaotic commercial kitchen). No props or special set pieces are needed.

## How to Play
1. Divide the four players into two pairs and assign each pair one of the two contrasting locations.
2. Instruct both pairs to begin their scenes at the same time, sharing the physical stage space.
3. Players must establish their own base reality, characters, and relationship, completely ignoring the physical presence of the other pair.
4. Only one scene should have active dialogue at any given moment; while Pair A speaks, Pair B must engage in silent, active object work or slow-tempo physical action.
5. Pairs must pass the focus back and forth organically, using physical cues, pauses, and emotional shifts rather than verbal hand-offs.
6. Encourage players to physically weave through each other's spaces, creating visual near-misses and interesting stage pictures without breaking character.
7. The game continues for three to five minutes, building both narratives to a natural, synchronized conclusion.

## Facilitation Notes
- Side-coach the silent pair to keep their physical actions high-stakes so they remain compelling even without dialogue.
- Pitfall: Both pairs talking at once. Fix: Freeze or slow down one pair physically when the other starts speaking, emphasizing active listening.
- Encourage accidental thematic or rhythmic connections—if Pair A says 'It's getting hot in here,' Pair B might react to their own environment's temperature.
- Remind players to use the entire stage; they shouldn't just stick to their own half of the space, but rather occupy the same physical footprint.

## Variations
- Emotional Mirroring: The emotional tone of one scene must instantly influence or mirror the emotional tone of the other, even though the contexts remain different.
- Physical Freeze-Tag: When one pair freezes completely, the other pair must immediately pick up their dialogue, creating a rapid-fire edit style.
- Soundtrack Sharing: Play a single background music track that both scenes must adapt to, matching their pacing and rhythm to the music.

## Debrief
- How did you determine when it was your turn to speak without looking directly at the other pair?
- What did you have to do physically to keep your scene active and interesting while remaining silent?
- How did the presence of the other scene subconsciously affect your own scene's pacing and emotional choices?

## Safety & Inclusion
Since players are moving through the same physical space while ignoring each other, establish a rule of soft focus and physical safety to avoid actual collisions. Players should maintain peripheral awareness of physical bodies even while pretending they aren't there.

## Why It Works
This game forces players to step out of their individual heads and tap into a shared group rhythm. By stripping away the ability to verbally negotiate stage time, players must rely on breath, physical tension, and auditory cues to pass the focus. This builds a deep sense of ensemble trust and teaches the value of silence and active physical support.
