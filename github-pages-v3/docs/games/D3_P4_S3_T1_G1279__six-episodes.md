# Six-Scene Saga

<div class="game-hero-ph" markdown>🎨 *Infographic coming soon*</div>

> Rapidly outline and perform a complete six-part narrative arc under intense time pressure.

## Overview
A collaborative storytelling exercise where a small ensemble has exactly sixty seconds to map out a six-scene narrative arc based on a major objective. Once the timer stops, the players must immediately perform the six distinct scenes to resolve the story, relying on rapid agreement and narrative pacing.

## What It Trains
- **Domain:** D3 — The Scene
- **Principle(s):** Serve the Story; Yes, And; Group Mind
- **Skill(s):** Narrative Architecture; Offer Reception; Thematic Synthesis
- **Technique(s):** Story Spine; Yes, And… sentence games; Weave the threads
- **Focus:** narrative

**Objective:** Develops narrative architecture, rapid offer acceptance, and thematic synthesis by forcing players to quickly agree on a story structure and execute it without overthinking.

## Setup
A semi-circle of 3 to 8 players facing a performance space. A facilitator with a stopwatch or timer is required. No props or special materials are needed.

## How to Play
1. The facilitator provides a major, goal-oriented prompt, such as launching a community garden or building a rocket.
2. The facilitator starts a 60-second timer, signaling the players to begin planning.
3. During this minute, the ensemble must collaboratively outline exactly six distinct scenes that will tell the complete story from beginning to resolution.
4. Players must rapidly pitch and accept ideas without debating, ensuring they establish a clear narrative progression.
5. When the 60 seconds expire, the facilitator calls time, and all planning must immediately cease.
6. The players then perform the six scenes in chronological order, with players stepping forward to initiate each scene.
7. Each scene should be relatively brief, focusing on a specific beat of the planned narrative.
8. The facilitator or an off-stage player calls 'Scene!' or 'Edit!' to transition between the six chapters until the story is fully resolved.

## Facilitation Notes
- Coaching cue: 'Say yes to the first structure proposed! Don't look for the perfect story, look for the next logical step.'
- Pitfall: Spending the entire 60 seconds debating the first scene. Fix: Remind the group at the 30-second mark that they need to have at least 3 or 4 scenes mapped out.
- Coaching cue: 'Keep the scenes focused on the planned beat. Don't introduce massive new subplots that derail the six-part structure.'
- Pitfall: Players forgetting who is in which scene during the performance. Fix: Encourage them to trust the group mind to step in and fill gaps dynamically.

## Variations
- Silent Planning: The 60-second planning phase must be done entirely through pantomime or written keywords on a whiteboard, forcing physical and visual agreement.
- The Story Spine Constraint: Force the six scenes to strictly align with the classic Story Spine steps (Once upon a time, Every day, Until one day, Because of that, Because of that, Until finally).
- Blind Chapters: Each player is assigned a chapter number (1-6) and plans their chapter in secret, then they must perform them in order and try to make them connect logically on the fly.

## Debrief
- How did the time constraint affect your willingness to accept your teammates' ideas?
- What strategies did you use to ensure the story actually resolved by the sixth scene?
- How did it feel to perform a scene when the planning was minimal or incomplete?

## Safety & Inclusion
Ensure the prompt is collaborative and positive. If players have physical limitations, allow them to perform scenes seated or use verbal narration to bridge physical gaps.

## Why It Works
By restricting the planning time to one minute, the game bypasses the analytical brain and forces players into a state of radical acceptance. The six-scene constraint provides a clear container that helps players understand pacing, climax, and resolution without getting bogged down in endless exposition.
