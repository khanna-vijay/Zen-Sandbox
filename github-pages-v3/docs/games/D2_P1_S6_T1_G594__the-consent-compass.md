# The Consent Compass

> Navigate personal boundaries and physical contact in real-time using four explicit meta-communication tools.

## Overview
A structured scene-work drill designed to normalize active boundary negotiation and real-time check-ins. Players perform a standard scene but are equipped with four distinct verbal commands to pause, adjust, or redirect the action based on physical, emotional, or narrative comfort. This experience transforms boundary management from an emergency intervention into a collaborative, creative dialogue.

## What It Trains
- **Domain:** D2 — The Partner
- **Principle(s):** Consent & Boundaries; Truth Over Pandering
- **Skill(s):** Boundary Navigation; Active Listening
- **Technique(s):** Check-ins; Cut calls; Negotiating physical contact
- **Focus:** skill_drill

**Objective:** Develops boundary navigation, active listening, and the ability to prioritize personal safety and character truth over automatic compliance.

## Setup
A clear, open playing space with moderate room for movement. No props are required, though the facilitator may write the four key phrases on a whiteboard as a visual reference.

## How to Play
1. Divide the group into a performing set of two to three active players, with the remaining players acting as active observers.
2. Obtain a simple, low-stakes suggestion from the group to initiate a standard, character-driven scene.
3. Instruct players that they must actively use four specific verbal commands during the scene to manage boundaries and narrative direction.
4. Introduce 'Bearing Check!' as a proactive tool: a player pauses the scene briefly to ask their partner about their comfort level regarding an upcoming physical, emotional, or thematic choice.
5. Introduce 'Hard Reset!' as an immediate stop: if a boundary is crossed or a player feels genuine discomfort, they call this out to freeze the scene, state the boundary, and either rewind to a safer point or pivot to a new setup.
6. Introduce 'Physical Bearing!' as a touch negotiation tool: when physical contact is initiated or imminent, the receiving player uses this phrase to verbally and physically accept, modify, or decline the touch.
7. Introduce 'True North!' as an authenticity tool: if a player feels pressured to agree to a premise that violates their character's integrity or their own gut instinct, they call this out to state their honest response and redirect the scene's reality.
8. Run the scene for three to five minutes, encouraging players to actively experiment with calling out these commands, while the partner must immediately pause, listen, and adapt.

## Facilitation Notes
- Coaching Cue: Remind players that calling a 'Compass Point' is a sign of high-level collaboration and skill, not a failure or a 'block' of the scene.
- Pitfall & Fix: Players may hesitate to use the tools due to the traditional 'yes-and' pressure. Fix: The facilitator should actively side-coach or pause the scene to suggest a 'Bearing Check' when they notice physical tension or hesitation.
- Coaching Cue: Keep the meta-pauses brief. State the boundary or check-in in one to two sentences, get the response, and immediately resume the scene with the new parameters.
- Pitfall & Fix: Players might use 'True North' to simply avoid difficult narrative choices rather than protecting boundaries. Fix: Clarify that 'True North' is for honoring personal comfort or deep character integrity, not for avoiding creative challenges.

## Variations
- Silent Signals: Replace the verbal commands with distinct, low-profile physical gestures (e.g., a hand over the heart for a check-in) to practice non-verbal boundary navigation.
- Observer Intervention: Allow the observing players to call out 'Bearing Check!' on behalf of the active players if they notice a potential boundary issue, helping build collective safety awareness.

## Debrief
- How did it feel to explicitly pause the scene to check in with your partner, and did it disrupt or actually deepen your connection?
- For those who used 'Physical Bearing,' how did negotiating touch in real-time affect your sense of physical safety on stage?
- Did you experience any internal resistance to calling a 'Hard Reset' or 'True North'? What does that tell us about the pressure to always agree in improv?
- How can we carry the spirit of these explicit check-ins into our regular, un-gimmicked scene work?

## Safety & Inclusion
This game is highly safety-sensitive. Establish a firm, non-judgmental atmosphere before playing. Ensure all participants understand that they have absolute autonomy over their bodies and emotional limits. If a 'Hard Reset' is called, the facilitator must support the player immediately without requiring deep personal explanations, and the group must respect the boundary without debate.

## Why It Works
By formalizing boundary negotiation into explicit, gamified mechanics, it removes the social pressure of 'blocking' or being 'difficult.' It teaches players that consent is a dynamic, continuous dialogue that actually enhances creative trust and allows for bolder, more authentic choices because players know their boundaries are secure.
