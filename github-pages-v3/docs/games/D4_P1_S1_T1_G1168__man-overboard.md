# All Hands on Deck

![All Hands on Deck — infographic](img/D4_P1_S1_T1_G1168__man-overboard.jpg){ .game-hero }

> A high-energy physical reaction game that builds rapid group cohesion and sharp spatial awareness.

## Overview
Players move dynamically through a shared space and must instantly react to physical commands called out by the facilitator. These commands require players to either strike individual poses or rapidly self-organize into specific multi-person physical shapes. It is a fast-paced, high-energy warm-up that demands instant commitment and constant connection with the rest of the ensemble.

## What It Trains
- **Domain:** D4 — The Ensemble
- **Principle(s):** Group Mind; Commit 100%
- **Skill(s):** Peripheral Awareness; Physicality & Space Work; Active Listening
- **Technique(s):** Stage-picture exercises
- **Focus:** connection

**Objective:** To develop peripheral awareness, rapid physical commitment, active listening, and group mind by forcing players to coordinate non-verbally and assemble physical pictures under time pressure.

## Setup
A large, open room free of obstacles. All players stand scattered throughout the space, facing the facilitator.

## How to Play
1. Explain the premise: the room is a ship, the facilitator is the Captain, and players must instantly execute physical commands.
2. Introduce the solo commands: 'Captain's Coming' (stand at attention and salute) and 'Hit the Deck' (drop safely into a plank position or touch the floor).
3. Introduce the multi-player commands: 'Rowboat' (two players sit front-to-back and mimic rowing), 'Crow's Nest' (three players stand back-to-back with arms linked), and 'Sea Monster' (four players connect to form a waving, multi-limbed creature).
4. Instruct players to begin moving around the space at a brisk walk, filling all empty spaces and keeping their eyes up.
5. Call out a command unexpectedly. Players must immediately execute the action or find nearby partners to form the designated group shape.
6. Identify the last player(s) to complete the action, or any players left without a group; these players step to the sidelines to assist the facilitator.
7. Resume movement, gradually increasing the speed of the players' walking and the frequency of the commands.
8. Continue play until only a small group of players remains, celebrating the final survivors.

## Facilitation Notes
- Side-coach players to partner with the closest available people rather than running across the room to find specific friends.
- If players are hesitant, encourage 100% physical commitment by reminding them that a confident, incorrect shape is better than a hesitant, correct one.
- To keep eliminated players engaged, have them join you on the sidelines as 'co-captains' to help call out commands and spot the last players to form groups.
- Pitfall: Players colliding due to reckless running. Fix: Remind players that peripheral awareness means moving fast while remaining completely safe and in control of their bodies.

## Variations
- Silent Ship: The facilitator uses distinct, silent hand gestures instead of verbal commands, forcing players to keep their eyes on the leader at all times.
- Crew Takeover: The first player eliminated becomes the new Captain, taking over the role of calling out commands for the rest of the round.
- Non-Elimination: Instead of players dropping out, any players who are last or left without a group must perform a quick, five-second 'victory dance' before play immediately resumes.

## Debrief
- How did you use your peripheral vision to find partners without shouting or waving your arms?
- What did it feel like to commit physically to a shape without having time to think or plan?
- How does this game show the difference between looking at someone and actually being aware of them in space?

## Safety & Inclusion
Because this game involves rapid movement and physical contact, establish clear boundaries before starting. Offer low-impact alternatives for players with mobility limitations (e.g., touching knees instead of dropping to the floor for 'Hit the Deck'). Remind players to respect personal boundaries and use light, safe contact when linking up for group shapes.

## Why It Works
This game bypasses the analytical, overthinking brain by demanding immediate physical action. By forcing players to look outward to find partners and fill gaps, it builds 'Group Mind' and trains the ensemble to move and react as a single, cohesive organism.
