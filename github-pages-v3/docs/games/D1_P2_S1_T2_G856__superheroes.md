# Unlikely Superheroes

> Save the world from absurd crises using highly specific, completely useless superpowers.

## Overview
A high-energy narrative game where a team of bizarre, audience-suggested superheroes attempts to solve a trivial global crisis. Each hero enters with absolute confidence, tries a ridiculous solution based on their name, fails spectacularly, and summons the next hero until the final player helps resolve the catastrophe.

## What It Trains
- **Domain:** D1 — The Self
- **Principle(s):** Commit 100%; Fail Joyfully; The First Thought Is a Gift; Yes, And; Make Your Partner a Genius
- **Skill(s):** Unfiltered Spontaneity; Offer Reception; Active Gifting; Justification; Support Work
- **Technique(s):** First Thought drills; Endowment-acceptance; Endowment-gifting drills; Justify the absurd; Walk-ons
- **Focus:** comedy_game

**Objective:** To practice unfiltered spontaneity and joyful failure by committing to absurd character concepts and celebrating unsuccessful attempts before finding a collaborative resolution.

## Setup
Players stand in a line-of-sight offstage or at the back of the playing space. One player starts on stage. The facilitator asks the audience for a minor, mundane crisis and a ridiculous superhero name for the first player.

## How to Play
1. The first player enters the stage with high energy, introducing themselves as the audience-suggested superhero and declaring their mission to solve the mundane crisis.
2. The player immediately attempts a solution using their specific, absurd superpower, describing and physically committing to the action.
3. The player must fail in their attempt, justifying why their power did not work in a fun, dramatic way.
4. Realizing they cannot do it alone, the player calls out to the audience for a new superhero name to assist them.
5. The next player in line immediately enters the stage as that new superhero, fully committing to the physical and vocal traits implied by the name.
6. The new hero attempts to solve the problem using their own power, building on what has already happened, but also fails and justifies the failure.
7. This cycle repeats for subsequent players, with each new hero entering, attempting, failing, and summoning the next.
8. Once the final player is summoned, they enter and collaborate with the existing heroes to find a ridiculous, successful resolution to the crisis, ending the scene on a high note.

## Facilitation Notes
- Side-coach players to fail with enthusiasm. The failure is the comedy; there is no shame in a power not working.
- Encourage immediate physical choices upon entry. A superhero's posture and movement should instantly reflect their name.
- Pitfall: Players trying to think of the perfect solution. Fix: Remind them that the first thought is the best thought, and the more illogical the solution, the better.
- Ensure players justify why the previous failure happened, weaving a loose, silly narrative thread rather than just resetting.

## Variations
- The I Know What to Do Drill: A two-player version where one player is the hero who repeatedly says I know what to do! and proposes solutions, while the other player plays the sidekick or obstacle explaining why it fails, forcing rapid-fire spontaneous ideas.
- Reverse Resolution: The final hero's attempt makes the problem worse, and they must collectively celebrate the absolute destruction of the world as a joyful failure.

## Debrief
- How did knowing you had to fail change your level of pressure when pitching your first idea?
- What did you notice about the speed of your choices when you could not plan your superhero's power in advance?
- How did we use justification to make our partners' failed attempts feel important and logical to the story?

## Safety & Inclusion
Ensure physical offers and superhero movements respect personal space boundaries on a crowded stage. If players choose highly physical powers, they should mime them safely without making physical contact with other players unless explicitly consented to.

## Why It Works
By framing failure as a narrative requirement, this game bypasses the analytical brain's fear of making a bad choice. Players are forced to rely on their first instinct (unfiltered spontaneity) and must enthusiastically support and justify the absurd realities established by their teammates.
