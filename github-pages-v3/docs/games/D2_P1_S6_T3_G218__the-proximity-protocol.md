# The Proximity Protocol

<div class="game-hero-ph" markdown>🎨 *Infographic coming soon*</div>

> Negotiate physical and emotional intimacy step-by-step using an explicit, structured scale of consent.

## Overview
This exercise provides a structured, step-by-step framework for players to practice negotiating physical closeness and emotional intimacy in real-time. By moving through a defined scale of proximity, players learn to explicitly propose, accept, decline, or modify physical contact within a scene. The focus is on prioritizing personal boundaries and authentic comfort over the traditional theatrical impulse to always agree.

**Objective:** To develop practical boundary navigation and physical negotiation skills, training players to prioritize consent and authentic comfort ('Truth Over Pandering') over automatic agreement.

## Setup
Arrange the space with clear boundaries. Write the Proximity Scale (Levels 0 to 6) on a board visible to all players. Have two active players stand at least six feet apart in the playing area, with one to three players observing as active safety monitors.

## How to Play
1. Introduce the Proximity Scale: Level 0 (Distant Observation, 6+ feet), Level 1 (Verbal Engagement, 3-6 feet), Level 2 (Shared Space, within 3 feet), Level 3 (Light/Brief Contact, e.g., handshake), Level 4 (Sustained Contact, e.g., holding hands), Level 5 (Intimate Contact, e.g., deep embrace), and Level 6 (Pre-choreographed actions only, off-limits for spontaneous play).
2. Assign a scenario to two active players that naturally invites physical or emotional escalation (e.g., two estranged relatives stuck in a small space, or two partners solving a delicate puzzle).
3. Begin the scene at Level 0 or Level 1, establishing the characters and their immediate environment without physical contact.
4. To advance to a higher proximity level, a player must make an explicit proposal, either out-of-character ('I would like to move to Level 3 to comfort you') or clearly in-character ('May I step closer and take your hand?').
5. The receiving partner must respond with absolute honesty, choosing to accept, decline, counter-propose ('I am not ready for Level 4, but we can stay at Level 3'), or set conditions ('Yes, but do not touch my shoulders').
6. If a proposal is declined, the proposing player must immediately accept the boundary without negotiation, and the scene continues at the current agreed-upon level.
7. Any player, observer, or the facilitator can call 'Cut' at any moment if they sense discomfort, a boundary violation, or need to pause for safety.
8. If 'Cut' is called, play stops instantly, the group conducts a brief, non-judgmental check-in, and the scene either resets to a lower level or ends.

## Facilitation Notes
- Celebrate the 'No': Actively praise players who decline proposals or set firm boundaries, reinforcing that a boundary is a gift to the scene's safety.
- Watch for Non-Verbal Cues: Monitor players for physical signs of hesitation, tension, or pulling back, and be ready to call 'Cut' to check in if they seem to be pandering.
- Transition from Meta to In-Character: Start beginners with explicit meta-proposals ('I propose Level 3') before letting them transition to seamless, in-character requests.
- Enforce Level 6 Boundaries: Remind players that Level 6 actions (like lifts or intense physical struggles) are strictly off-limits for spontaneous negotiation and must be choreographed beforehand.

## Variations
- De-escalation Drill: Challenge players to actively propose moving down the scale (e.g., moving from Level 4 back to Level 2) to practice comfortable physical retreat.
- Silent Negotiation: Run the scene entirely in gibberish or silence, forcing players to rely on highly clear physical gestures to propose and negotiate proximity levels.

## Debrief
- How did it feel to explicitly say 'no' or set a condition, and did you feel any pressure to 'yes-and' the physical contact?
- How did respecting a boundary or a refusal change the dynamic and narrative tension of the scene?
- What strategies did you use to make your proximity proposals clear and justified within the story?

## Safety & Inclusion
This game is highly safety-sensitive. Establish a clear, physical 'Cut' gesture (like crossing arms in a 'T') for anyone who cannot speak. Ensure players know they have absolute autonomy over their bodies, and that refusing physical contact is the highest form of successful play in this exercise.

## Why It Works
By gamifying the physical distance between players and requiring explicit verbal checkpoints, this exercise bypasses the subconscious pressure to please the partner or the audience. It reframes boundary setting not as a narrative block, but as a compelling, realistic dramatic tension that builds trust and physical safety.
