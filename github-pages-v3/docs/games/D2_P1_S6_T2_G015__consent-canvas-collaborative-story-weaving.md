# Boundary Canvas

<div class="game-hero-ph" markdown>🎨 *Infographic coming soon*</div>

> Navigate hidden personal boundaries in real-time scenes using proactive check-ins and immediate rewinds.

## Overview
Boundary Canvas is an interactive skill drill where players collaboratively build scenes while navigating secret, individual boundary cards. Players must balance narrative momentum with active listening, using proactive check-ins to gauge comfort. When a boundary is crossed, players call a freeze, renegotiate the interaction, and rewind the action to find a mutually agreeable path forward.

## What It Trains
- **Domain:** D2 — The Partner
- **Principle(s):** Consent & Boundaries; Truth Over Pandering
- **Skill(s):** Boundary Navigation; Active Listening
- **Technique(s):** Check-ins; Cut calls; Negotiating physical contact
- **Focus:** skill_drill

**Objective:** To develop advanced boundary navigation and active listening skills by training players to prioritize personal comfort over narrative compliance, utilizing explicit 'Cut' calls and real-time renegotiation to adapt scenes dynamically.

## Setup
Arrange the space for a standard performance area with the remaining players seated as active observers. Prepare a set of index cards ('Boundary Cards') beforehand, each containing a specific physical, emotional, or social limit (e.g., 'No physical contact without verbal consent', 'No high-status shouting', 'No invading personal space within two feet'). Provide pens and blank cards for players to write their own if preferred.

## How to Play
1. Distribute one secret Boundary Card to each player; players must keep their assigned boundary hidden from their scene partners throughout the round.
2. Select two to three players to enter the performance space and provide a low-stakes, collaborative scene prompt (e.g., organizing a shared garage or planning a community event).
3. Begin the scene, instructing players to actively pursue their characters' objectives while remaining highly sensitive to their partner's verbal and non-verbal comfort cues.
4. If a player intends to initiate an action that might approach a boundary (such as physical touch, intense emotional escalation, or personal space invasion), they must perform a brief, in-character or out-of-character verbal check-in.
5. If a player's secret boundary is crossed, or if any player feels genuine discomfort, they must immediately call 'Cut!' to freeze the scene.
6. Upon hearing 'Cut!', all players freeze, and the player who called it briefly states the boundary that was crossed without judgment or apology.
7. The facilitator guides a brief, 30-second renegotiation where the players agree on an alternative choice or physical adjustment that respects the boundary.
8. Rewind the scene's action by a few seconds, resuming play from just before the boundary was crossed, incorporating the newly negotiated agreement into the narrative.
9. Run the scene for 5-7 minutes, allowing multiple 'Cut' calls and rewinds as necessary, focusing on the process of adaptation rather than a polished performance.

## Facilitation Notes
- Side-coaching cue: 'Watch the physical distance—check in before you close the gap.' Encourage players to look for micro-expressions, shifts in posture, or vocal tension.
- Pitfall: Players apologizing excessively after a 'Cut' call, which stalls the momentum and adds shame. Fix: Frame 'Cut' calls as neutral, helpful data points. Instantly transition the group into the 30-second renegotiation.
- Side-coaching cue: 'Prioritize your boundary over the story.' Remind players of 'Truth Over Pandering'—it is always better to pause the scene than to ignore a boundary for the sake of a joke or plot point.
- Pitfall: Players making their secret boundaries too extreme or impossible to play with (e.g., 'No one can look at me'). Fix: Ensure the pre-written cards represent realistic, navigable boundaries that require communication rather than total isolation.

## Variations
- Open Boundaries: Play the same structure, but players state their boundaries openly before the scene starts, focusing purely on the physical execution of check-ins and adjustments.
- Audience Cut: Allow observing players to call 'Cut!' if they observe a boundary being crossed that the active players missed, training collective vigilance and care.
- Emotional Escalation: Increase the scene complexity by introducing high-stakes emotional prompts, requiring players to navigate intense verbal boundaries under pressure.

## Debrief
- How did it feel to hold a secret boundary that you were absolutely required to protect, even if it disrupted the scene's flow?
- What subtle physical or vocal cues did you notice that signaled your partner was approaching a boundary before they actually crossed it?
- How did the process of calling 'Cut!' and rewinding change your perspective on 'Yes, And' when personal comfort is at stake?
- In what ways can we bring this practice of proactive check-ins and graceful adaptation into our standard, non-templated improv scenes?

## Safety & Inclusion
Because this game explicitly deals with boundary navigation, establish a firm baseline of psychological safety before play. Ensure players know they can write their own boundary cards to reflect real-life comfort levels. Emphasize that calling 'Cut!' is a highly valued skill and a gift to the ensemble, never a failure or an interruption. If a player feels overwhelmed, they may step out of the scene at any time without explanation.

## Why It Works
This game works because it gamifies the mechanics of consent, removing the social stigma of saying 'no' in a collaborative environment. By mandating secret boundary cards, it forces players to actively read their partners rather than operating on autopilot. The 'Cut and Rewind' mechanic directly reinforces 'Truth Over Pandering' by proving that a scene can pause, adjust, and continue successfully without losing its creative integrity, thereby building trust and safety within the ensemble.
