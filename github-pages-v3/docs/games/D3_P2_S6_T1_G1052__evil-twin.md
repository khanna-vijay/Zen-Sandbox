# Evil Twin

<div class="game-hero-ph" markdown>🎨 *Infographic coming soon*</div>

> Justify your worst impulses when your malicious double sabotages a polite, high-stakes scene.

## Overview
Four players collaborate on a scene where two main characters try to navigate a polite, high-stakes social situation. Meanwhile, their off-stage 'evil twins' periodically freeze the action, tag in to commit a socially disastrous act, and tag out, leaving the original characters to justify the absurd behavior and repair the damage.

## What It Trains
- **Domain:** D3 — The Scene
- **Principle(s):** Yes, And; Base Reality First; Follow the Follower
- **Skill(s):** Offer Reception; Justification; Support Work
- **Technique(s):** Endowment-acceptance; Justify the absurd; Tap-ins
- **Focus:** comedy_game

**Objective:** To develop advanced justification skills by forcing players to accept, integrate, and rationalize highly disruptive, absurd choices while maintaining a grounded base reality.

## Setup
Four players stand in the playing space. Two players step forward to begin a grounded scene. The other two players stand on the wings, designated as the 'evil twins' (one assigned to each active player). No props or special staging are required.

## How to Play
1. Assign two players to start a scene in a polite, high-stakes setting, such as a job interview, meeting future in-laws, or a first date.
2. Assign the remaining two players to stand on the sidelines as the 'evil twins,' each paired with one of the active players.
3. The active players begin a normal, grounded scene, establishing a clear base reality, relationship, and polite social boundaries.
4. At any point, an evil twin can shout 'Freeze!', step into the scene, and physically tag out their corresponding active player.
5. The evil twin must immediately perform one highly inappropriate, rude, or 'evil' action or line of dialogue that disrupts the polite dynamic.
6. Immediately after delivering the disruptive offer, the evil twin tags their counterpart back in and returns to the sidelines.
7. The returned active player must immediately accept the absurd behavior as their own action, justify why they did it, and attempt to repair the social damage while keeping the scene grounded.
8. The scene continues with the other active player reacting to the justification, until another freeze is called by either twin.

## Facilitation Notes
- Side-coach the active players to avoid denying the behavior (e.g., do not say 'I don't know why I just did that' or 'That wasn't me'). They must fully own the action.
- Encourage the evil twins to make surgical, specific disruptions rather than chaotic, scene-ending choices. One sharp, terrible line is better than a long monologue.
- Remind the non-disrupted active player to react realistically to the shock of the action, helping to establish the high stakes of the base reality.
- Pitfall: The scene becomes too wacky too fast. Fix: Remind players to return to the polite, grounded base reality immediately after the justification.

## Variations
- Angel and Devil: Instead of just an evil twin, one sideline player is an 'angel' (making overly saintly, submissive, or honest offers) and the other is a 'devil' (making malicious offers).
- Internal Monologue: The sideline players tag in to reveal the character's deep, dark, unspoken thoughts, which the active player must then accidentally speak aloud and justify.

## Debrief
- How did establishing a strong, polite base reality at the start make the 'evil' interruptions more impactful?
- What strategies did you use to justify an absurd action without breaking the reality of the scene?
- How does fully owning and justifying a mistake or wild offer help move a scene forward rather than stalling it?

## Safety & Inclusion
Ensure players establish boundaries beforehand regarding physical touch during tags and the nature of the 'evil' actions, avoiding offensive stereotypes, sensitive personal topics, or unwanted physical contact.

## Why It Works
This game works because it gamifies the core improv principle of 'Yes, And' under extreme pressure. By forcing players to take responsibility for choices they didn't make, it strips away the fear of making mistakes and trains the brain to find logical, character-driven justifications for the most absurd behaviors, ultimately strengthening the scene's foundation.
