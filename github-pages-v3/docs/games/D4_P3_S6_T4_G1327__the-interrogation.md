# The Interrogation

<div class="game-hero-ph" markdown>🎨 *Infographic coming soon*</div>

> A non-linear narrative format exploring a tragic friendship through the lens of a police investigation.

## Overview
The Interrogation is a structured longform format where players alternate between a high-stakes police questioning room and theatrical flashbacks. By jumping back and forth in time, the ensemble pieces together the complex history of a victim and their accused best friend. The focus is not on solving a crime, but on building a rich, emotional portrait of a relationship.

## What It Trains
- **Domain:** D4 — The Ensemble
- **Principle(s):** Serve the Piece; Serve the Story; The Audience Is the Final Scene Partner
- **Skill(s):** Format Literacy; Pacing & Rhythm; Narrative Architecture; World-Building; Room Reading
- **Technique(s):** Longform vs. shortform mechanics; Edits (Sweep, Tag-Out, Sound/Light); Story Spine; C.R.O.W. (Character, Relationship, Objective, Where)
- **Focus:** narrative

**Objective:** Develops format literacy, narrative architecture, and pacing by balancing high-tension framing scenes with character-driven, non-linear flashbacks.

## Setup
Set up two distinct performance areas on stage: one side is the dimly lit interrogation room (with a chair for the suspect and space for two detectives), and the other side is the open playing space for flashbacks. No physical props are required.

## How to Play
1. Cast the core roles: one player as the deceased victim, one as the victim's best friend (the suspect), and two as the interrogating detectives. The remaining players form the ensemble.
2. Gather audience suggestions for the names of the victim and the suspect, and the duration of their friendship.
3. Begin the performance in the interrogation room with the detectives questioning the suspect, establishing the immediate tension and the timeline of the questioning.
4. Trigger a flashback when a detective asks a question about the past; the suspect answers briefly, and the action immediately transitions to the flashback area to play out that memory.
5. Perform the flashback scene focusing on the relationship dynamics between the suspect and the victim, utilizing the ensemble to play secondary characters.
6. Return to the interrogation room when the flashback reaches an emotional peak, with the detectives editing the scene by interrupting with a new question.
7. Vary the timelines by jumping chronologically in both the interrogation (e.g., hours vs. days of questioning) and the flashbacks (e.g., childhood vs. the day before the tragedy).
8. Conclude the performance when the detectives ask the ultimate question: 'Did you do it?' Cut to black or call 'scene' just as the suspect inhales to answer, leaving the mystery unresolved.

## Facilitation Notes
- Coaching Cue: 'Focus on the relationship, not the crime!' Remind players that this is a character study, not a procedural whodunit.
- Pitfall: Getting bogged down in finding clues or proving innocence. Fix: Side-coach the detectives to ask emotional, personal questions rather than forensic ones.
- Coaching Cue: 'Play with the interrogation timeline.' Remind the suspect they can show physical exhaustion or changing attitudes to indicate how much time has passed in custody.
- Pitfall: Flashbacks that go on too long. Fix: Teach the detectives to aggressively edit by interrupting with a new question from the interrogation desk when the flashback's point has been made.

## Variations
- The Ghost's Perspective: The victim sits in the interrogation room as an invisible ghost, reacting silently to the suspect's lies or truths.
- Multiple Suspects: The detectives alternate between interrogating two different suspects who have conflicting memories of the victim, creating a Rashomon-style narrative.
- The Audio Tape: Play the interrogation scenes as voiceovers from off-stage while the ensemble physically mimics the interrogation or the memories on stage.

## Debrief
- How did the non-linear structure affect how we developed the characters' relationship?
- What strategies did the detectives use to successfully edit and pace the flashbacks?
- How does letting go of 'solving the mystery' free us up to make more interesting narrative choices?

## Safety & Inclusion
While this game deals with a murder premise, it should avoid graphic descriptions of violence or trauma. Establish boundaries during the setup that the focus is on the emotional life of the characters, and keep the cause of death abstract or non-violent if requested by any player.

## Why It Works
This format works because the interrogation frame provides an incredibly strong, built-in narrative engine. The detectives act as live, on-stage directors who can edit scenes, prompt new directions, and control the pacing, which helps competent players practice format literacy and structural control without needing external direction.
