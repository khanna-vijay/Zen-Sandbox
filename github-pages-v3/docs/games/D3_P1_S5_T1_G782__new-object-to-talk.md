# Object-First Dialogue

![Object-First Dialogue — infographic](img/D3_P1_S5_T1_G782__new-object-to-talk.jpg){ .game-hero }

> Establish a new physical object in the space before speaking every single line of dialogue.

## Overview
This scene-work drill requires players to physically interact with or discover a new object in their environment before delivering any line of dialogue. This constraint forces players to ground their scenes in a rich, tactile physical space rather than relying on disembodied talking heads.

## What It Trains
- **Domain:** D3 — The Scene
- **Principle(s):** Show, Don't Tell; Base Reality First
- **Skill(s):** World-Building; Physicality & Space Work
- **Technique(s):** C.R.O.W. (Character, Relationship, Objective, Where); Object work
- **Focus:** skill_drill

**Objective:** To develop physical world-building, establish the 'Where' of C.R.O.W., and practice showing rather than telling by prioritizing spatial awareness and object work over verbal exposition.

## Setup
Two players stand in the performance space. The rest of the group acts as an active audience. No physical props are used; all objects must be mimed using space work. Get a simple location suggestion from the group.

## How to Play
1. Pair up two players and place them in the performance space.
2. Obtain a location suggestion from the audience to set the scene.
3. Player A begins the scene by physically interacting with a mimed object in the environment before speaking their first line.
4. Once the physical action is clearly established, Player A delivers their line, integrating the object into their dialogue or character's focus.
5. Player B must then discover and interact with a different new object before they are allowed to speak their response.
6. The scene continues in this alternating fashion, with every single line of dialogue preceded by the physical creation of a new object or a distinct new interaction with the environment.
7. Players must maintain the physical integrity of previously established objects, ensuring they do not walk through tables or drop items invisibly.

## Facilitation Notes
- Side-coaching cue: 'Touch first, talk second!' Remind players to pause and physically engage before opening their mouths.
- Pitfall: Players making tiny, insignificant gestures just to get to speak. Fix: Coach them to make the object work substantial, clear, and honest to the environment.
- Side-coaching cue: 'Remember where you put that!' Ensure players respect the physical layout they are building so the space remains consistent.
- Pitfall: Forgetting the relationship while focusing too much on objects. Fix: Encourage players to let the object reveal how they feel about the other character.

## Variations
- Object Hand-off: Players must interact with the same object the other player just used, changing its state or passing it, before introducing a new one.
- Silent Build: The first two minutes of the scene are completely silent, focusing entirely on building a complex physical environment together before dialogue is permitted under the same rules.
- Emotional Objects: Every new object introduced must carry an emotional charge or trigger a specific reaction from the character.

## Debrief
- How did forcing yourself to interact with an object first change the pace and tone of your dialogue?
- Did you find it easier or harder to establish the 'Where' of the scene compared to a standard verbal start?
- How did the physical environment help you discover your character's relationship and objectives?

## Safety & Inclusion
Ensure the performance space is clear of actual physical hazards so players can move freely and safely mime their environment without tripping. Encourage players to be mindful of their partners' physical boundaries when passing or interacting with mimed objects.

## Why It Works
By placing a physical gatekeeper before verbal expression, this exercise disrupts the habit of rapid-fire, head-up talking. It anchors the players in their bodies and the immediate environment, naturally generating the 'Where' and 'Character' elements of C.R.O.W. while giving the brain a physical task that reduces overthinking.
