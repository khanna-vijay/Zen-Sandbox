# Family Portraits

<div class="game-hero-ph" markdown>🎨 *Infographic coming soon*</div>

> Create instant, expressive group snapshots that reveal relationships, status, and story through physical stage pictures.

## Overview
In this physical ensemble warm-up, players collaborate to build a series of frozen group photographs based on a prompt. By paying close attention to spatial relationships, levels, and physical offers, the group co-creates a cohesive narrative snapshot without speaking.

## What It Trains
- **Domain:** D4 — The Ensemble
- **Principle(s):** Group Mind; Follow the Follower; Make Your Partner a Genius; Commit 100%
- **Skill(s):** Peripheral Awareness; Support Work; Single-Partner Empathy & Mirroring; Physicality & Space Work
- **Technique(s):** Stage-picture exercises; Character Walks/Centers
- **Focus:** connection

**Objective:** Develops peripheral awareness, physical storytelling, and group mind by training players to read and support the physical offers of others to build a balanced, expressive stage picture.

## Setup
A clear playing space where a group of players can stand together, with the remaining players acting as the audience/observers who will swap in. No props are needed.

## How to Play
1. Divide the group so that a subset of players (4 to 6) step into the playing space, while the rest observe from the edge.
2. The facilitator calls out a specific family theme or archetype, such as 'a family of competitive synchronized swimmers' or 'a family of high-tech software developers.'
3. One by one, players enter the frame to build the portrait, with the first player entering, striking a distinct physical pose that establishes a character, and freezing.
4. Subsequent players enter one at a time, observing the existing physical shapes, levels, and emotional offers, and positioning themselves to complement, contrast, or react to the established characters.
5. Players must use their peripheral awareness to balance the stage picture, ensuring diverse physical levels (sitting, standing, kneeling) and clear spatial relationships (who is close, who is isolated).
6. Once all players are in the frame and frozen, the facilitator 'takes the photo' by counting down '3, 2, 1, click!'
7. The facilitator asks the frozen players to hold their pose while the observers briefly decode the relationships shown, identifying dynamics like the favorite child or the rebel.
8. Clear the stage, rotate players, and repeat the process with a brand-new prompt.

## Facilitation Notes
- Coaching cue: 'Look at the empty spaces! If everyone is standing, who can take a low level?'
- Coaching cue: 'Show us the relationship. Are you leaning towards someone, or pulling away in disgust?'
- Pitfall: Players entering with a pre-planned idea instead of reacting to what is already there. Fix: Remind them to 'follow the follower' and let their character be defined by how they can support the existing players.
- Pitfall: Clumping together in a flat line. Fix: Encourage depth by utilizing the foreground, midground, and background of the playing space.

## Variations
- Action Portrait: After the photo is taken, the facilitator says 'Action!' and the family comes to life for ten seconds of improvised movement and dialogue before freezing again.
- The Black Sheep: Designate one player to secretly be the outsider of the family, and the group must physically convey this dynamic without speaking.
- Evolution of a Family: Create three portraits of the same family over different eras (e.g., 1920s, 1980s, 2020s) to show how the relationships have shifted over time.

## Debrief
- How did you decide where to place yourself in relation to the other players?
- What physical cues made it easy to understand the relationships and status in the portrait?
- How does paying attention to the overall stage picture help us tell a story without words?

## Safety & Inclusion
Ensure players are mindful of physical boundaries and consent when posing close to one another. Offer alternatives for players with mobility limitations, such as providing chairs or allowing them to set the focal point of the portrait from a comfortable position.

## Why It Works
It forces players to step out of their own heads and look at the entire group composition. By focusing on physical levels, proximity, and body language, players practice making their partner a genius by instantly defining their partner's character through their own physical reactions.
