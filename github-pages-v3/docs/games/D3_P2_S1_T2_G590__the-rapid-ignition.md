# The Instant Spark

<div class="game-hero-ph" markdown>🎨 *Infographic coming soon*</div>

> Ignite a compelling scene dynamic and establish the game within the first two exchanges.

## Overview
A high-speed, high-repetition drill where pairs of improvisers rapidly construct a scene's core conflict. Player A establishes a rich character baseline through physical, emotional, and motivational choices, while Player B enters to immediately complicate that reality. By cutting the action after just one exchange, players learn to launch scenes with high stakes and clear comedic or dramatic engines without getting bogged down in exposition.

## What It Trains
- **Domain:** D3 — The Scene
- **Principle(s):** Base Reality First; Start in the Middle; Yes, And; Make Your Partner a Genius
- **Skill(s):** Game Identification; Stakes / The 'Want'; World-Building; Justification; Active Listening; Offer Reception; Active Gifting; Physicality & Space Work
- **Technique(s):** If this is true, what else is true?; C.R.O.W. (Character, Relationship, Objective, Where); Endowment-acceptance; Endowment-gifting drills; Character Walks/Centers
- **Focus:** skill_drill

**Objective:** To master the rapid establishment of a scene's Base Reality and immediately identify the Game of the scene using the principle of 'If this is true, what else is true?'.

## Setup
An open performance space. The facilitator gathers the group (ideally 8-12 players) in a semi-circle facing the stage area. No props or chairs are required. The facilitator will solicit a simple, generic location suggestion from the group to serve as the backdrop for each round.

## How to Play
1. The facilitator obtains a simple, everyday location suggestion (e.g., a laundromat, a dentist's waiting room) to set the scene's physical context.
2. Player A steps into the performance space alone, immediately embodying a distinct physical posture, a clear emotional state, and an active, immediate objective.
3. Player A must express these three elements through their body language, vocal tone, and their very first line of dialogue (e.g., frantically searching the floor while muttering, 'I can't lose this ring before the proposal!').
4. Player B enters the space, immediately observing and validating Player A's physical and emotional reality.
5. In their opening line, Player B must both justify Player A's behavior and introduce a direct obstacle or a conflicting personal objective (e.g., 'I found the ring, and I'm keeping it to pay for my own wedding!').
6. Player A responds with one line, reacting directly to the new obstacle and escalating their emotional stakes.
7. Player B delivers one final line, cementing the pattern of their conflicting dynamic (the 'game' of the scene).
8. The facilitator immediately calls 'Cut!' to end the scene, preventing the players from wandering into narrative exposition.
9. The facilitator provides brief, targeted feedback on how clearly the game was established, and the next pair steps up to repeat the process with a new location.

## Facilitation Notes
- Side-coaching cue: Encourage Player A to make bold, physical choices before they even speak. A slumped spine or a manic pace tells the audience who they are instantly.
- Common Pitfall: Player B enters with an unrelated conflict that ignores Player A's established reality. Fix: Remind Player B to 'Yes, And' the physical and emotional state first before introducing the twist. The obstacle must feel organic to what is already true.
- Side-coaching cue: Keep the cuts ruthless. Do not let the scene run past the second exchange. The learning happens in the rapid setup and immediate reset, not in the long-form resolution.
- Common Pitfall: Players setting vague, long-term objectives (e.g., 'I want to be happy'). Fix: Coach them to choose immediate, physical wants (e.g., 'I need to clean this specific spot on the floor right now').

## Variations
- Silent Ignition: Run the entire exercise without spoken dialogue, relying purely on physical objectives, body language, and spatial obstacles to establish the game.
- Blind Entrances: Player B turns their back or closes their eyes until Player A has fully established their physical and emotional state, forcing Player B to read the physical offers instantly upon turning around.
- The Third Beat: Allow the scene to continue for one additional beat where players must apply 'If this is true, what else is true?' to escalate the established conflict to an absurd degree.

## Debrief
- How did having a clear physical and emotional state make it easier to discover your character's immediate objective?
- For Player B, what clues did you look for to ensure your obstacle felt like a natural extension of Player A's reality?
- How does establishing a clear conflict in the first two lines prevent a scene from stalling or turning into polite conversation?

## Safety & Inclusion
Ensure players are mindful of physical boundaries when establishing high-energy physicalities or lunging for imaginary objects. Encourage vocal and physical choices that do not require actual physical contact or invading a partner's personal space without consent.

## Why It Works
This game works because it forces players to bypass intellectual planning and rely on immediate physical and emotional offers. By combining a clear character baseline (Base Reality) with an immediate obstacle, the scene's 'Game' is instantly generated. The strict two-exchange limit prevents players from overthinking or writing the plot, training the muscle of rapid, intuitive justification and active gifting.
