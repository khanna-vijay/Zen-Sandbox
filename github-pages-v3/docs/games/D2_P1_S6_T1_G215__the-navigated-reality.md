# Navigated Realities

> Integrate explicit verbal consent and boundary negotiation directly into the flow of active scene work.

## Overview
A structured scene-work exercise where players must explicitly check in with their partners before introducing major narrative shifts, emotional escalations, or physical contact. By normalizing 'yes,' 'no,' and 'negotiate' as active, character-driven choices, players learn to treat boundaries as creative springboards rather than narrative dead ends.

## What It Trains
- **Domain:** D2 — The Partner
- **Principle(s):** Consent & Boundaries; Truth Over Pandering
- **Skill(s):** Boundary Navigation; Offer Reception

**Objective:** To build practical skills in real-time boundary navigation, explicit verbal check-ins, and prioritizing personal/character truth over people-pleasing within a narrative scene.

## Setup
Players stand in a neutral playing space. The facilitator establishes a clear, universal 'Cut' or 'Pause' signal that any player can use at any time to immediately halt action. No props or special materials are required.

## How to Play
1. Two to four players step forward to begin a standard, character-driven scene, establishing a clear relationship and setting.
2. Before initiating any significant plot development, major emotional escalation, or physical contact, the initiating player must pause and perform an explicit, in-character verbal check-in.
3. The check-in must be a direct, specific question regarding the partner's comfort level, such as asking permission to step closer, touch a shoulder, or reveal a sensitive piece of character history.
4. The receiving player must respond immediately with one of three clear options: 'Yes' (full consent), 'No' (boundary set), or 'Negotiate' (conditional consent with a modified alternative).
5. If the response is 'Yes,' the action proceeds exactly as proposed, maintaining the scene's momentum.
6. If the response is 'No,' the initiating player must immediately accept the boundary without question, pivot their focus, and find a creative new direction for the scene.
7. If the response is 'Negotiate,' the players briefly align on a modified action (e.g., holding hands instead of a hug) and then continue the scene with the new agreement.
8. At any point, any player or the facilitator can call 'Cut' to immediately stop the scene for real-world safety or comfort, bypassing all in-character mechanics.

## Facilitation Notes
- Frame 'No' as a gift: Remind players that a boundary is not a rejection of them as an actor; it is a creative redirection that forces deeper character work and unexpected narrative paths.
- Address the 'Yes-And' trap: Actively coach players to avoid 'pandering'—saying yes to things they or their characters are uncomfortable with just to keep the scene moving.
- Monitor trigger thresholds: Watch for moments where players skip check-ins during rapid emotional escalation. Gently pause the scene and ask, 'What was the check-in there?' to build muscle memory.
- Keep check-ins concise: Encourage players to make their check-ins brief and integrated into the dialogue so they do not stall the scene's momentum.

## Variations
- Non-Verbal Echoes: Play the same game but use subtle, pre-agreed physical gestures or eye-contact cues instead of verbal check-ins to negotiate boundaries.
- The Hidden Boundary: Give one player a secret personal boundary card (e.g., 'No physical touch' or 'No shouting') that the other player must discover and navigate entirely through active check-ins.

## Debrief
- How did it feel to explicitly ask for or deny consent while remaining in character?
- When you said 'No' or negotiated, did you base it on your character's perspective, your personal comfort, or a creative instinct?
- How did receiving a 'No' change your creative momentum? Did it feel like a block or an invitation to explore a new path?
- How does this exercise redefine the traditional improv rule of 'Yes, And' when it comes to physical and emotional safety?

## Safety & Inclusion
This game is highly safety-sensitive. Establish a firm, non-negotiable 'Cut' rule before playing. Ensure players know they can step out of the scene at any time without explanation. Emphasize that personal comfort always overrides character choices, and no player should ever feel pressured to consent to physical touch or intense emotional themes.

## Why It Works
By codifying consent as an explicit, mandatory mechanic, the game strips away the social anxiety of boundary-setting. It transforms 'No' from a narrative block into a generative pivot point, proving that respecting boundaries actually drives compelling, high-stakes storytelling.
