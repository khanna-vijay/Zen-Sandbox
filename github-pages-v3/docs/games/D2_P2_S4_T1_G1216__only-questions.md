# Questions Only

> Build a rich narrative world using nothing but questions while the audience monitors your grammar.

## Overview
In this fast-paced, high-energy elimination game, players improvise a scene speaking exclusively in questions. If a player stumbles, hesitates, or makes a statement, the audience playfully calls them out, and a teammate must instantly step in to inherit their character and keep the story moving.

## What It Trains
- **Domain:** D2 — The Partner
- **Principle(s):** Yes, And; Make Your Partner a Genius; Serve the Story; The Audience Is the Final Scene Partner
- **Skill(s):** Active Listening; Offer Reception; World-Building; Support Work; Audience-Energy Management
- **Technique(s):** Last Word Response; Yes, And… sentence games; C.R.O.W. (Character, Relationship, Objective, Where); Tap-ins
- **Focus:** mixed

**Objective:** To develop active listening, rapid offer reception, and the ability to 'Yes, And' under strict linguistic constraints, transforming questions from passive stalls into active world-building tools.

## Setup
Two lines of players stand on opposite sides of the stage, leaving the center clear. The remaining players or spectators act as the audience. No props or special materials are required.

## How to Play
1. Have the first player from each line step into the center of the stage to begin the scene.
2. Ask the audience for a simple suggestion, such as a location or a relationship, to establish the platform.
3. Begin the scene, ensuring that every single line of dialogue spoken by the active players is phrased as a grammatically correct question.
4. Ensure each question accepts the premise of the previous line ('Yes, And') and adds new, concrete information to advance the plot.
5. If a player makes a statement, repeats a question, hesitates for more than two seconds, or asks a non-sequitur, the audience and off-stage players shout 'Out!'
6. The eliminated player must immediately exit to the back of their line.
7. The next player in that line must instantly step into the scene, taking over the exact character, physical posture, and emotional state of the player who was just eliminated.
8. The scene continues seamlessly from the moment of interruption, with the incoming player immediately asking a question to maintain the narrative momentum.

## Facilitation Notes
- Coaching cue: 'Load your questions with information!' Encourage players to avoid empty questions like 'What is that?' and instead use descriptive ones like 'Why are you holding that glowing green vial?'
- Pitfall: Players often 'wimp' or stall by asking repetitive questions. Fix: Remind them to treat every line as a statement with a question mark at the end.
- Coaching cue: 'Inherit the physical life!' Remind incoming players to match the physical stance and emotional energy of the person they are replacing to maintain narrative continuity.
- If the audience is hesitant to call out mistakes, the facilitator should loudly lead the first few 'Out!' calls to establish a playful, high-energy boundary.

## Variations
- New Character Entry: Instead of inheriting the eliminated player's character, the incoming player enters the scene as a brand-new character, adjusting the narrative dynamic.
- Non-Elimination Practice: Play the game as a standard two-person scene without elimination, focusing purely on the depth of the relationship and world-building through questions.
- Genre Questions: Force the players to ask questions that fit a specific genre, such as Shakespearean drama, Film Noir, or a Sci-Fi epic.

## Debrief
- How did it feel to receive an offer framed as a question? Did it feel like a gift or a demand?
- What strategies did you use to ensure your questions actually advanced the scene instead of stalling it?
- How did inheriting another player's physical posture and character affect your immediate choices when entering the scene?

## Safety & Inclusion
Ensure the elimination call is kept playful and supportive rather than punitive. If players have mobility constraints, adapt the transition mechanic so players can tag in safely without needing to rush or run onto the stage.

## Why It Works
By restricting the grammatical form to questions, this game forces players to bypass their analytical filters. It highlights how questions can either build a world when loaded with details, or stall a scene when empty, making players highly conscious of how they deliver and receive offers.
