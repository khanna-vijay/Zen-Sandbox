# That's Right, Bob

<div class="game-hero-ph" markdown>🎨 *Infographic coming soon*</div>

> Pitch an absurd imaginary product by enthusiastically agreeing with and building upon your partner's wild claims.

## Overview
Two players act as co-hosts on a live home shopping television network, selling a bizarre, imaginary product to the viewing audience. Every single line of dialogue must begin with the phrase 'That's right, [Partner's Name],' forcing both players to immediately validate the previous statement before adding a new, heightened detail. The result is a fast-paced, high-energy exercise in radical agreement and collaborative world-building.

**Objective:** To master the core mechanics of 'Yes, And' by practicing immediate offer reception, active listening, and collaborative heightening under a playful linguistic constraint.

## Setup
Two players stand side-by-side facing the front of the room, as if looking directly into a television camera. No physical props are used; all products and their features are entirely mimed and described. The facilitator or audience provides a simple, mundane object as a starting inspiration.

## How to Play
1. Two players stand side-by-side, adopting the high-energy, cheerful personas of home shopping channel hosts.
2. The facilitator or audience suggests a mundane, everyday item to serve as the base product (e.g., a standard pencil, a coffee mug, or a pair of socks).
3. Player A initiates the pitch by introducing the product with an unusual, heightened feature, addressing Player B by name (e.g., 'Today we have this standard pencil, Bob, but it never needs sharpening because it writes in pure liquid gold!').
4. Player B must immediately respond by saying, 'That's right, [Player A's Name],' and then add a new detail that builds directly on what Player A just said.
5. Player A responds in kind: 'That's right, [Player B's Name],' validating Player B's addition and heightening it further.
6. Both players must use physical object work (miming) to demonstrate the product's features as they are described, reacting physically to each other's verbal offers.
7. The pitch continues back and forth, with each line escalating the product's absurdity, utility, or physical effects, until the facilitator calls 'scene' or a natural climax is reached.

## Facilitation Notes
- Coaching Cue: 'Listen to the very last word your partner said, and use that specific detail to inspire your next claim!' This prevents players from planning ahead.
- Pitfall: Players saying 'That's right, Bob, but...' or immediately changing the subject. Fix: Remind them that 'That's right' means 100% agreement; they must accept the premise fully before adding their 'And.'
- Coaching Cue: 'Show, don't just tell! If your partner says the vacuum has a high-powered jet engine, let your body react to the pull of that engine.'
- Pitfall: Listing unrelated features (e.g., 'It makes toast! And it flies! And it plays music!'). Fix: Encourage logical heightening—if it writes in liquid gold, where does the gold come from, and what does it feel like to hold?

## Variations
- The Physical Toll: The product has physical side effects or requirements that the hosts must physically endure while maintaining their cheerful, professional TV personas.
- The Dissatisfied Customer: A third player calls in as a customer with a bizarre complaint, and the hosts must 'Yes, And' the complaint as if it is a fantastic hidden feature of the product.
- Rapid Fire: Set a strict timer for 60 seconds, forcing the players to escalate the pitch at a breakneck pace.

## Debrief
- How did starting every sentence with 'That's right' change your instinct to control or block the scene?
- What happened when you let your partner's physical miming dictate what you said next?
- How does building on the last detail mentioned create a more cohesive world than jumping to brand-new ideas?

## Safety & Inclusion
Ensure players are mindful of the physical space when engaging in high-energy miming or physical comedy, keeping movements controlled to avoid accidental collisions.

## Why It Works
By forcing players to verbally agree ('That's right') before they can speak their own thoughts, the game bypasses the analytical brain's tendency to reject or pivot away from unexpected ideas. It transforms the 'Yes, And' principle into a literal, rhythmic linguistic structure, making active listening and immediate offer reception mandatory for the scene to progress.
