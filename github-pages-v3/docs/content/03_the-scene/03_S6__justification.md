# 🧠 Justification

> *The judgment of which technique to use, when, and how much.*

![Square infographic explaining the improv skill “Justification”](../../assets/infographics/D3.S6.jpg){ .infographic }

## 🧠 What it is

At its core, **justification** is the real-time judgment of *why* something is happening on stage. When a scene partner makes a bizarre choice, misspeaks, or initiates with an unusual behavior, justification is the cognitive leap required to make that absurdity make perfect sense. It is the active decision to frame an oddity as a foundational truth of the scene, rather than a mistake to be swept under the rug. As a skill, it asks you to constantly evaluate the emerging reality and decide: *What kind of person would do this? What shared history makes this normal? What emotional state makes this the only logical reaction?*

This is genuinely difficult because it runs counter to our everyday social instincts. In real life, when someone does something nonsensical, we point it out, correct it, or distance ourselves from it. On stage, the novice instinct is to do exactly that—to play the "voice of reason," call the other character crazy, or ask, "Why are you doing that?" Justification requires you to suppress the urge to fix the anomaly. Instead, you must do the heavy lifting of retrofitting the universe around it. It demands that you treat the absurd as absolute truth, inventing a cohesive internal logic on the fly without breaking the reality of the scene or killing the comedy.

!!! abstract "The core equation"
    **Absurd Action + Grounded Justification = The Game of the Scene.** 
    Without the absurd action, the scene is mundane. Without the justification, the scene is confusing and chaotic. The skill lies in providing the exact right amount of logic to make the weirdness sustainable.

## 👀 The litmus test

Justification is often introduced in Improv 101 as a simple mechanical rule: "Give a reason for what you are doing." But inventing a reason is merely a technique. The *skill* of justification is the real-time, high-pressure judgment of **which** reason to give, **when** to give it, and **how much** weight it needs to carry to serve the scene. 

The true litmus test for this skill occurs in the three seconds immediately following an accident, a bizarre line of dialogue, or a wildly out-of-character choice. When the unexpected happens, the improviser is placed under immense live pressure and must instantly decide how to treat the anomaly. 

!!! example "In a scene: The 'Mom' Slip"
    Player A accidentally calls their romantic date (Player B) "Mom."
    
    * **Lacking the skill:** Player A panics, breaks character, or defensively explains it away: *"Sorry, I meant Mary. I'm just tired."* The anomaly is killed; the scene deflates.
    * **Applying the skill:** Player A leans in and justifies the slip to fuel the scene: *"Look, ever since you started cutting my steak for me and telling me I'm a handsome boy, the lines have blurred."* The anomaly is justified; a comedic game is born.

This is why justification lives in the "Brain" layer of the framework. It requires you to read the room and diagnose what the scene needs in a split second. A master of this skill doesn't just explain things to make logical sense; they justify things to make *theatrical* sense. 

The ultimate litmus test is simple: Does your justification apologize for the weirdness, or does it build a world where the weirdness is the most important thing happening?

## ⚙️ How it works in a scene

Justification operates as a continuous, high-speed sense-making loop. It is the improviser’s internal engine for turning random collisions, mistakes, and wild offers into inevitable realities. 

In the heat of a scene, this cognitive loop fires in three distinct, rapid-fire stages:

**1. Perceive the Anomaly (The "What")**  
Your radar catches a friction point. It might be a bizarre statement from your partner ("I buttered the cat"), a physical mistake (you trip over an imaginary chair), or a mismatched emotional reaction (your partner starts sobbing when handed a slice of cake). The novice ignores the anomaly; the skilled improviser flags it as the most important thing in the room.

**2. Interrogate the Reality (The "Why")**  
In a fraction of a second, your brain asks the foundational question of improv: *If this unusual thing is true, why does it make perfect sense?* You rapidly scan the established **Platform** (the who, what, and where) and the character's psychology to find a logical or emotional anchor. You aren't looking for an excuse to dismiss the anomaly; you are looking for a reason to *elevate* it.

**3. Execute the Grounding (The "How")**  
You deploy a line of dialogue, a physical action, or an emotional shift that locks the reason into the scene. You weave the absurdity into the fabric of the narrative or use it to frame the **Game of the Scene**. 

!!! example "In a scene: The Cognitive Loop"
    **The Trigger:** Player A enters the scene and accidentally trips over their own feet, stumbling hard.  
    **The Perception:** Player B notices the stumble. It wasn't planned, but it happened.  
    **The Decision:** Player B's brain races: *Why is my partner stumbling? We established we are on a pirate ship. What makes you stumble on a ship? Rough seas or a peg leg.*  
    **The Execution:** Player B grabs a nearby chair, mimes holding onto a railing, and yells, "Hold fast, Captain! The starboard waves are getting violent!"  
    *(Player B has just justified the physical mistake by altering the environment, giving the scene immediate stakes and physical reality.)*

!!! tip "On stage: Justify actively, not defensively"
    A weak justification sounds like an apology or a defensive explanation ("I buttered the cat because I was confused"). A strong justification is active and drives the scene forward. It treats the absurdity as a deliberate, necessary step in the character's plan. 

This loop applies just as heavily to your *own* choices as it does to your partner's. Often, an improviser will blurt out a line from their subconscious that makes no immediate sense. The skill of justification is trusting that your brain gave you a gift, and retroactively building the bridge to make your own wild statement true.

## 📈 The maturity arc

As an improviser develops, their ability to justify shifts from a reactive panic-response to a proactive, scene-building tool. Because justification is the connective tissue of **The Scene**, its maturity arc is deeply tied to how an improviser handles game, narrative, and stakes.

| Stage | **Game Identification** | **Narrative Architecture** | **Stakes / The "Want"** | **Engine Selection** *(meta)* |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **1 Novice** | Tries to spot the unusual thing but misses it live | Tries to tell a story but it wanders / no change | Plays activities with no reason to care | Doesn't yet know two engines exist |
| **2 Adv. Beginner** | Names the game *after* the scene | Hits Story-Spine beats when prompted | States a "Want" when reminded | Recognizes "this is a game scene" vs. "story scene" in review |
| **3 Competent** | Identifies the game during the scene | Builds Platform then Tilts deliberately | Establishes what's at risk for the character | Chooses an engine consciously at scene start |
| **4 Proficient** | Frames the game with the first unusual line | Story arc and character change feel inevitable | Stakes are felt, not stated; they fuel the scene | Switches engines mid-scene as the scene demands |
| **5 Master** | Finds, plays, *and intentionally breaks* the game | Architects a full arc with consequence and change in real time | Makes the audience genuinely care about absurd people | Reads what the scene needs and serves it invisibly — game *or* story, never forced |

!!! abstract "How Justification drives this evolution"
    While the table above tracks the overall architecture of the scene, **justification** is the underlying skill that makes each leap possible. Here is what that looks like in practice:

*   **The Novice (Unjustified Action):** The improviser does things on stage, but doesn't know *why*. They wash dishes or argue with their scene partner, but there is no underlying philosophy or emotional reason driving the behavior. The scene wanders because nothing is anchored.
*   **The Advanced Beginner (Reactive Justification):** The improviser begins to explain their actions, but usually defensively or after the fact. If their scene partner points out they are holding a spoon weirdly, they invent a frantic, often overly-complicated excuse. They can see the "game" in the notes session, but struggle to justify it in real time.
*   **The Competent (Deliberate Justification):** The improviser uses justification to build the **Platform**. They actively establish *why* the characters care about the current situation. When a **Tilt** (the unusual thing) occurs, they don't panic; they consciously provide a reason for it, turning a mistake into a feature.
*   **The Proficient (Structural Justification):** Justification becomes instantaneous. The moment an unusual line is spoken, the proficient improviser immediately frames it with a grounded, character-driven *reason*. They don't just state their "want"—their deep justification makes the stakes palpable to the audience. The story feels inevitable because every action is perfectly justified by the character's established worldview.
*   **The Master (Invisible Justification):** The master uses justification to make the impossible feel real. They can play the most absurd, heightened character imaginable, yet provide an internal logic so airtight and empathetic that the audience genuinely cares about them. The justification is no longer a mechanical tool; it is the living, breathing psychology of the scene.

## 🧰 The techniques that train it

Justification is not an innate talent; it is a cognitive reflex. You build this reflex by isolating the mechanics of sense-making and practicing them until your brain automatically seeks the "why" behind every "what." 

Two core techniques act as the primary training weights for this skill, teaching your brain not just how to explain an action, but how to choose the most satisfying explanation for the scene.

### 1. Justify the absurd
This technique involves taking a bizarre, nonsensical, or accidental offer and grounding it with a rock-solid emotional or logical reason. Practicing this forces the improviser to abandon the panic of "this makes no sense" and replace it with the curiosity of "under what circumstances *would* this make perfect sense?"

*   **How it trains the brain:** It builds the judgment of **emotional grounding**. It teaches you that you don't need to invent a sci-fi universe to explain a weird action; you just need a recognizable human motivation. It calibrates your internal dial so you know *how much* reality to inject into a crazy situation to keep the audience invested.

    !!! example "In a scene"
        **The Absurd Offer:** Player A enters walking on their hands.  
        **The Weak Response (No Justification):** "Wow, you're walking on your hands! You're an alien!" *(Fails to ground the scene).*  
        **The Justified Response:** "Look, I know the floor is freshly waxed, but you're going to have to use your feet eventually, Brenda." *(Grounds the absurdity in a relatable, mundane reality).*

### 2. Reincorporation-as-justification
When faced with a strange behavior, a novice's instinct is to invent a brand-new piece of information to explain it. **Reincorporation-as-justification** is the deliberate practice of looking *backward* into the scene's existing details to find the answer. 

*   **How it trains the brain:** It builds the judgment of **economy and elegance**. By forcing yourself to use what is already on stage, you train your brain to stop over-inventing. It teaches you *where* to look for your justifications—realizing that the most satisfying answers are usually hiding in the first three lines of the scene.

    !!! tip "On stage"
        If your scene partner suddenly starts crying, don't invent a sudden tragedy off-stage. Look at what just happened. Did you just hand them a cup of coffee? Justify the tears with the coffee: *"I'm sorry, it's just... this is exactly how my mother used to stir in the cream."* You have justified the emotion using the immediate physical reality.

Together, these techniques train the improviser's judgment. **Justify the absurd** teaches you *how deep* to go to make the audience care, while **Reincorporation-as-justification** teaches you *how efficiently* you can tie the scene together without adding unnecessary clutter.

## 🔀 Dialing it up vs. down

Justification is the tether that keeps your audience from floating away into pure nonsense. But like any tether, if you pull it too tight, the scene can't move. Knowing how much justification to apply is a delicate balancing act between grounding the reality and maintaining the scene's momentum.

**When to dial it UP (Lean into Justification)**  
You need more justification when the scene threatens to break the audience's trust or suspension of disbelief. 
*   **Grounding wild moves:** If a scene partner makes a bizarre, seemingly random choice (e.g., suddenly licking the floor), dialing up your justification provides the logical or emotional "why" that makes the absurd choice brilliant ("I told you, the baby dropped a single grain of sugar and the ants are coming!").
*   **Saving accidents:** When a prop is dropped, a name is forgotten, or a line is flubbed, high justification weaves the mistake seamlessly into the reality of the scene, turning an error into a gift.
*   **Raising the stakes:** Deepening the *reason* behind a character's unusual behavior naturally raises the emotional stakes, shifting the scene from a fleeting joke to a compelling narrative.

!!! example "In a scene: Dialing it up"
    **Player A:** *(Accidentally knocks over a chair while entering)* "Whoops."  
    **Player B:** *(Dialing up justification)* "I see the ghost of your Victorian husband is still following you, Helen. Tell him to have a seat."   
    *The accident is now the foundation of the scene's reality.*

**When to dial it DOWN (Exercise Restraint)**  
More justification is not always better. Over-explaining can drain the energy from a scene and kill the pacing.
*   **The "Wizard's Curse" trap:** If the premise of the scene is inherently magical or absurd (e.g., you are playing two talking dogs), you do not need to spend three minutes explaining *how* you learned to talk. Just be the dogs. The audience accepts the base reality; don't bore them by justifying it.
*   **When it becomes "talking head" improv:** If you find yourself standing in a semi-circle explaining why things are happening rather than *doing* things, your justification dial is too high. 
*   **When the game is already flying:** If the comedic engine is humming and the audience is laughing at the pattern, stop explaining the pattern. Just play it.

!!! warning "Watch out: Defensive Justification"
    Sometimes improvisers over-justify because they are afraid of looking silly. They will say, "Why are we doing this crazy thing? We must be dreaming!" This is **defensive justification**—it attempts to apologize for the improv rather than committing to it. Dial it down and trust the reality.

**Balancing neighboring skills**  
Justification sits directly between **Commitment** and **Pacing**. If you have high commitment but zero justification, you get "wacky town"—loud, fast, and meaningless. If you have high justification but low pacing, you get a dry, theatrical lecture. The master improviser uses just enough justification to make the absurd feel real, then immediately hits the gas on the scene's engine.

## 🤝 How it serves the domain

The domain of **The Scene** asks us to architect compelling, cohesive realities in real time, fluidly navigating between the two primary engines of improv: **Game** (comedic pattern) and **Narrative** (story and change). **Justification** is the structural mortar that holds both of these architectures together. 

Without it, a scene is just a sequence of random, wacky events. With it, the scene develops an ironclad internal logic that makes the audience invest in the outcome.

Here is how the skill of justification directly fuels the two engines of the scene:

* **In the Game Engine (Grounding the Absurd):** Game relies on a pattern of unusual behavior. If a character does something bizarre for no reason, the scene devolves into "crazy town"—a world without rules where nothing matters. Justification provides the character's internal philosophy. When you justify *why* your character is doing the unusual thing, you ground the absurdity in a relatable human perspective. This gives you a solid, believable platform from which to heighten the game even further.
* **In the Narrative Engine (Creating Causality):** A compelling story requires cause and effect. Justification is the mechanism that turns a loose string of events ("this happened, *and then* this happened") into a driving narrative ("this happened, *therefore* this happened"). By justifying your scene partner's offers and your own reactions, you establish character motivations, define the **Stakes** (what is at risk), and clarify the **Want** (what the character desires).

!!! abstract "The Architectural Glue"
    If the domain of The Scene is about building a house without a blueprint, justification is the physics that keeps it standing. It ensures that every new brick (offer) added to the structure makes sense in relation to the ones already laid, regardless of whether you are building a comedic funhouse (Game) or a dramatic arc (Narrative).

!!! example "In a scene"
    **The Offer:** A character walks into a corporate board meeting wearing a full scuba suit.
    
    **Without Justification (Random):** "I'm a fish now! Blub blub!" *(The scene loses reality; the audience stops caring because nothing matters.)*
    
    **With Justification (Architecting the Scene):** "I know the CEO said we're 'diving deep' into the Q3 financials today, and I wanted to show I'm a team player." *(The absurd is grounded, a character trait—extreme literalism or desperate sycophancy—is established, and the scene's engine is primed.)*

Ultimately, good judgment in justification means knowing *what kind* of reason the scene needs right now. It allows improvisers to take wild leaps of faith, knowing they have the skill to make those leaps make perfect sense by the time they land.

## ⚠️ Common failure modes & how to grow

Justification is a delicate balancing act. When it fails, the scene either grinds to a halt under the weight of real-world logic, or spins out of control into chaotic meaninglessness. 

Here are the most common ways justification goes wrong:

*   **The Logic Trap (Over-justification):** Stopping the scene dead to explain the physics, logistics, or complex backstory of an absurdity. The improviser acts like a detective solving a plot hole rather than a character living in a moment.
*   **Crazy Town (Under-justification):** Piling weirdness on top of weirdness without any grounded reality. If a character has a third arm, and the scene partner ignores it to talk about an alien invasion, nothing matters and the audience stops caring.
*   **The "Oops" (Apologetic Justification):** Explaining away the unusual thing as a mere mistake or accident. This instantly kills the potential for a repeating game because accidents rarely happen the exact same way twice.

!!! example "In a scene: The 'Oops' vs. The 'Philosophy'"
    **Player A:** "Why did you bring a live penguin to our wedding?"  
    **The "Oops" (Weak):** "Oh no! I thought I grabbed the ring bearer pillow. My mistake, I'll put him outside." *(The game is dead; the problem is solved.)*  
    **The Philosophy (Strong):** "Because marriage is a frozen wasteland, Susan, and we must learn to waddle through it together." *(The game is born; the character's worldview justifies the absurdity.)*

### How to grow at every stage

To advance your justification skills, look at where you currently sit in your scene-work maturity and focus on the specific next step:

*   **Stage 1 (Novice):** You often miss the unusual thing live, playing activities with no reason to care. 
    *   *Next step:* Stop ignoring the weirdness. When something odd happens, simply state **why** your character is doing it. Give yourself a basic, grounded reason.
*   **Stage 2 (Advanced Beginner):** You can name the game *after* the scene, but during the scene, you only state a "Want" when reminded.
    *   *Next step:* Connect your justification to the **Stakes**. Don't just explain *what* is happening; justify why it *matters* to your character. Let your justification reveal what is at risk.
*   **Stage 3 (Competent):** You identify the game live and build platforms deliberately, choosing your engine (Game or Story) at the start.
    *   *Next step:* Use justification to fuel your chosen engine. If it's a game scene, justify to heighten the comedic pattern. If it's a story scene, justify to deepen the character's emotional arc.
*   **Stage 4 (Proficient):** You frame the game with the first unusual line, and your stakes are felt, not stated.
    *   *Next step:* Practice switching engines mid-scene. Use a deep, emotional justification to pivot a silly, game-heavy scene into a grounded, narrative story beat (or vice versa) as the scene demands.
*   **Stage 5 (Master):** You architect full arcs and break games intentionally.
    *   *Next step:* Make your justifications entirely invisible. Your reasons for doing absurd things should feel so deeply baked into the character's worldview that the audience genuinely cares about these absurd people without ever noticing the technique.

!!! tip "On stage: Justify the philosophy, not the physics"
    Don't explain *how* a dog learned to speak English. Explain *why* the dog is using its newfound voice to critique your dating life. Ground the emotion, not the mechanics.

## 📚 References & Further Reading

### Foundational sources
*   **Charna Halpern, Del Close, and Kim "Howard" Johnson, *Truth in Comedy: The Manual of Improvisation* (1994)** — Establishes the core tenet that "everything is justified" on stage, demanding that improvisers play the reality of absurd situations honestly rather than defensively explaining them away or going for cheap jokes.
*   **Keith Johnstone, *Impro: Improvisation and the Theatre* (1979)** — Explores the mechanics of accepting offers and incorporating mistakes, teaching improvisers to treat every accidental slip as a deliberate choice that must be justified to build the narrative.
*   **Viola Spolin, *Improvisation for the Theater* (1963)** — The root text of American improv, which frames improvisation as real-time problem solving, requiring actors to justify their physical actions and environment to create a shared reality.

### Practitioner guides & manuals
*   **Matt Besser, Ian Roberts, and Matt Walsh, *The Upright Citizens Brigade Comedy Improvisation Manual* (2013)** — The definitive textbook on using justification to frame the "Game of the Scene," famously codifying the improviser's ultimate diagnostic question: "If this unusual thing is true, what else is true?"
*   **Mick Napier, *Improvise: Scene from the Inside Out* (2004)** — Warns against falling into "justification land" (where actors defensively apologize for weirdness) and instead advocates for the active, confident justification of your own bizarre choices to establish context and power.
*   **Will Hines, *How to Be the Greatest Improviser on Earth* (2016)** — Offers highly practical, modern advice on how to ground absurd premises, emphasizing that justification is about making the audience believe the character's internal logic, no matter how flawed it is.

### Lineage & teachers
*   **Upright Citizens Brigade (UCB)** — The theater and training center that elevated justification from a general acting tool to the primary mechanical engine for finding, framing, and heightening comedic game.
*   **The Annoyance Theatre** — Founded by Mick Napier in Chicago, this school emphasizes committing to strong, unusual personal choices and justifying them internally, prioritizing bold action over polite, logical scene-building.
*   **iO Theater (formerly ImprovOlympic)** — The Chicago institution where Del Close developed the philosophy of "playing to the top of your intelligence," which requires improvisers to justify absurdities using genuine intellect and emotional truth rather than cartoonish logic.

### Research & theory
*   **Leon Festinger, *A Theory of Cognitive Dissonance* (Stanford University Press, 1957)** — The foundational psychological theory explaining how humans retroactively invent logic to resolve conflicting behaviors or absurdities, perfectly mirroring the improviser's real-time task on stage.
*   **Michael S. Gazzaniga, *The Mind's Past* (University of California Press, 1998)** — Explores the brain's "left-brain interpreter," the neurological mechanism that constantly confabulates post-hoc justifications to make sense of our random actions, proving that justification is a hardwired human reflex.
*   **Karl E. Weick, *Sensemaking in Organizations* (SAGE Publications, 1995)** — Examines how groups retroactively assign meaning to unexpected events to create a cohesive shared reality, directly paralleling the cognitive loop of justification between scene partners.

### Talks, videos & courses
*   **UCB Training Center, *Improv 101 & 201 Core Curriculum*** — The standardized live courses where the mechanics of "justifying the unusual thing" and establishing a "base reality" are drilled as the foundational skills of modern scene work.
*   **Matt Besser, *Improv4Humans* (Earwolf Podcast Network, 2011–Present)** — A long-running audio masterclass in real-time justification, where Besser frequently stops scenes to discuss why a choice needs to be grounded, how an absurdity should be justified, or why a player failed to support a weird move.
*   **Mick Napier, *Annoyance Theatre Directing Notes* (unverified)** — Anecdotal but widely shared audio recordings and notes from Napier's rehearsals, focusing on his philosophy of "doing something, and then justifying it later" to bypass the analytical brain.

### Communities & adjacent reading
*   **Improv Resource Center (IRC) Forums** — A historical online hub where practitioners from UCB, iO, and Annoyance debated the nuances of justification, specifically the line between "grounding" a scene and "explaining away" the fun.
*   **Gary Klein, *Sources of Power: How People Make Decisions* (MIT Press, 1998)** — A study of naturalistic decision-making under pressure (firefighters, pilots), offering insight into how improvisers rapidly pattern-match and justify anomalies in high-stakes, split-second environments.

## 💬 Quotes & Anecdotes

!!! quote "— Del Close and Charna Halpern, *Truth in Comedy* (1994)"
    The actor's business is to justify... Whenever someone makes what appears to be a mistake on stage, the others will immediately justify it and weave it into the pattern of the entire work.

!!! quote "— Mick Napier, *Improvise: Scene from the Inside Out* (2004)"
    Another very common example of justification in improvisation is the calling-someone-crazy thing. It happens in a scene where someone is exhibiting behavior that the other person can't figure out. The scene goes on for a bit (probably unfunnily), and the confused improviser blurts out something like, "Well, you're just crazy." Ultimate justification. I don't understand your improvisation behavior (and I probably haven't created any powerful choice for myself either), therefore I am going to accuse you of being crazy. I'm going to justify your behavior, explain why you are behaving that way. You must be crazy. It happens a lot. It's ironic because in improvisation we hope for crazy. We want elevated theatrical absurd behavior; we just don't want it called out.

!!! quote "— Matt Besser, Ian Roberts, and Matt Walsh, *The Upright Citizens Brigade Comedy Improvisation Manual* (2013)"
    Repeatedly answering the question "If this unusual thing is true, then what else is true?" creates a comic pattern. Each answer to this question (or similar, related versions of this question) is called a "Game move."

### Where it comes from
The concept of justification as an active tool to make sense of the absurd has deep roots in the teachings of Del Close. To explain how improvisers must treat stage accidents, Close famously cited a Sufi saying about a master weaver who "incorporated the mistakes of his students into a larger pattern." 

Later, the specific mechanical phrasing of justification—*"If this is true, what else is true?"*—was codified by the Upright Citizens Brigade (UCB) as the foundational cognitive leap required to find and play the "Game of the Scene."

### A telling example
**The "Calling Someone Crazy" Trap**  
As Mick Napier points out in the quote above, the weakest form of justification is defensive. If Player A walks on stage and starts licking the walls, Player B's novice instinct is to say, "You're crazy, stop that!" This technically justifies the behavior (they are insane) but it kills the scene by framing the anomaly as a problem to be fixed. 

A skilled improviser justifies the behavior *actively* to build the world: "The snozzberry wallpaper tastes like snozzberries, doesn't it?" Now, the absurdity is a foundational truth of the scene's reality, not a mistake to be swept away.
